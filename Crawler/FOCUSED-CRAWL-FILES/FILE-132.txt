      <p>
       <b>
        Dysnomia
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        Δυσνομία
       </i>
       )—officially
       <b>
        (136199) Eris I Dysnomia
       </b>
       —is the only known
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moon
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       (the most massive known
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       ). It was discovered in 2005 by
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">
        Mike Brown
       </a>
       and the laser guide star adaptive optics team at the
       <a href="/wiki/W._M._Keck_Observatory" title="W. M. Keck Observatory">
        W. M. Keck Observatory
       </a>
       , and carried the provisional designation of
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/2005 (2003 UB
         <sub>
          313
         </sub>
         ) 1
        </span>
       </b>
       until officially named
       <i>
        Dysnomia
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_8747_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_8747-8">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (from the Ancient Greek word
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Δυσνομία
       </span>
       meaning anarchy/lawlessness) after the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dysnomia_(mythology)" title="Dysnomia (mythology)">
        daughter
       </a>
       of the Greek goddess
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(mythology)" title="Eris (mythology)">
        Eris
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysnomia_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       During 2005, the
       <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">
        adaptive optics
       </a>
       team at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_telescopes" title="Keck telescopes">
        Keck telescopes
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">
        Hawaii
       </a>
       carried out observations of the four brightest
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       objects (
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Makemake" title="Makemake">
        Makemake
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       ), using the newly commissioned
       <a href="/wiki/Laser_guide_star" title="Laser guide star">
        laser guide star
       </a>
       adaptive optics system. Observations taken on 10 September revealed a
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moon
       </a>
       in orbit around Eris, provisionally designated
       <span class="nowrap">
        S/2005 (2003 UB
        <sub>
         313
        </sub>
        ) 1
       </span>
       . In keeping with the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Xena" title="Xena">
         Xena
        </a>
       </i>
       nickname that was already in use for Eris, the moon was nicknamed "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gabrielle_(Xena)" title="Gabrielle (Xena)">
        Gabrielle
       </a>
       " by its discoverers, after Xena's sidekick.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC-Zabarenko_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ABC-Zabarenko-9">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC-Ingham_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ABC-Ingham-10">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">
        Properties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysnomia_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Dysnomia has an estimated diameter of 700 km ± 115 km with an albedo of 0.04 (+0.02 -0.01).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_Butler_2018_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown_Butler_2018-5">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In its discovery images Dysnomia was ~60 times fainter (or 4.43 magnitudes) than Eris in the
       <a href="/wiki/K_band_(infrared)" title="K band (infrared)">
        K band
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-johnston-11">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_8610_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_8610-12">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and later observations with the Hubble Space Telescope found it to be 500 times fainter in the visible band.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dysnomia_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dysnomia-7">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This indicates a very different, and quite redder, spectrum, indicating a significantly darker surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sicardy_Ortiz_et_al._2011_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sicardy_Ortiz_et_al._2011-13">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its diameter reveals it to be a rather large Trans-Neptunian object.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOCool4_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOCool4-14">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Of the Solar System's dwarf planets and minor planet moons, only Charon is definitely larger than Dysnomia.
      </p>
      <p>
       Combining Keck and Hubble observations, the satellite was used to determine the mass of Eris, and orbital parameters were estimated. Its orbital period is calculated to be
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001157740000000000♠
        </span>
        15.774
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.002
        <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">
         d
        </a>
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_Schaller_2007_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown_Schaller_2007-4">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These observations indicate that Dysnomia has a circular orbit around Eris, with a radius of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7007373500000000000♠
        </span>
        37
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         350
        </span>
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        140 km
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_Schaller_2007_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown_Schaller_2007-4">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This shows that the mass of Eris is 1.27 times that of Pluto.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_Schaller_2007_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown_Schaller_2007-4">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">
        Formation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysnomia_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Astronomers now know that the four brightest
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       objects (KBOs) have satellites. Among the fainter members of the belt only about 10% are known to have satellites. This is thought to imply that collisions between large KBOs have been frequent in the past. Impacts between bodies of the order of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7006100000000000000♠
        </span>
        1000 km
       </span>
       across would throw off large amounts of material that would coalesce into a moon. A similar mechanism is thought to have led to the formation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       when
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       was struck by a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        giant impactor
       </a>
       early in the history of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       Mike Brown, the moon's discoverer, chose the name
       <i>
        Dysnomia
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Δυσνομία
       </span>
       ) due to a number of associations it had for him. Dysnomia, the daughter of Eris, fits the general historically established pattern of naming moons after lesser gods associated with the primary god (hence,
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       's largest moons are named after lovers of Jupiter, both male and female, while
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       's are named after his fellow Titans). Also, the English translation of "Dysnomia", "lawlessness", echoes
       <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Lawless" title="Lucy Lawless">
        Lucy Lawless
       </a>
       , the actress famous for starring in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Xena:_Warrior_Princess" title="Xena: Warrior Princess">
         Xena: Warrior Princess
        </a>
       </i>
       on television. Before receiving their official names, Eris and Dysnomia were nicknamed "Xena" and "Gabrielle", and Brown decided to retain that connection.
      </p>
      <p>
       Brown also notes that Pluto owes its name in part to its first two letters, which form the initials of
       <a href="/wiki/Percival_Lowell" title="Percival Lowell">
        Percival Lowell
       </a>
       , the founder of the observatory where its discoverer,
       <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Tombaugh" title="Clyde Tombaugh">
        Clyde Tombaugh
       </a>
       , was working, and the person who inspired the search for "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planet_X" title="Planet X">
        Planet X
       </a>
       ".
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Christy" title="James Christy">
        James Christy
       </a>
       , who discovered
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       , followed the principle established with
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       by choosing a name which shared its first four letters with his wife's name, Charlene. "Dysnomia", similarly, has the same first letter as Brown's wife, Diane,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SkyTel_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SkyTel-15">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Brown uses the nickname "Dy"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈdaɪ/
       </span>
       for the moon, which he pronounces the same as his wife's nickname, Di. Because of this, Brown pronounces the full name
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">
            aɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       , with a long "y".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pluggdTV-Hammer_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pluggdTV-Hammer-16">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
