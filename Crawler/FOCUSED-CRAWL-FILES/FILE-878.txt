      <p>
       "
       <b>
        The Whole of the Moon
       </b>
       " is a song by
       <a href="/wiki/The_Waterboys" title="The Waterboys">
        the Waterboys
       </a>
       which was released as a single from their album
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/This_Is_the_Sea" title="This Is the Sea">
         This Is the Sea
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1985. It is a classic of the band's repertoire and has been consistently played at live shows ever since its release. Written and produced by
       <a href="/wiki/Mike_Scott_(musician)" title="Mike Scott (musician)">
        Mike Scott
       </a>
       , the subject of the song has inspired some speculation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-disc-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The single was not a big success when initially released in 1985, only making the lower ends of the chart, although reached number 12 on the Australian chart. Subsequently it became one of The Waterboys' best-known songs and their most commercially successful. It is arguably the band's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signature_song" title="Signature song">
        signature song
       </a>
       and was the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivor_Novello_Award" title="Ivor Novello Award">
        Ivor Novello Award
       </a>
       winner "Best Song Musically and Lyrically" in 1991.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bbc-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">
        Lyrics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Whole_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lyrics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The subject of the lyrics has inspired speculation, some of which has been rebutted by the writer. The song apparently began as a "scribble on the back of an envelope on a wintry
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
        New York
       </a>
       street",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recnotes_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-recnotes-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       after Scott's girlfriend asked him if it was difficult to write a song.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Like The Waterboys' first single "A Girl Called Johnny", the song is a tribute to an inspirational figure or figures. In each line, the singer describes his own perspective and immediately contrasts it with that of the song's subject, summarizing the difference with the line "I saw the crescent / You saw the whole of the moon".
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">
        Allmusic
       </a>
       instead suggests that its subject is a number of people who inspired Scott, including writer
       <a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">
        C. S. Lewis
       </a>
       and the musician
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_(artist)" title="Prince (artist)">
        Prince
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amgtwotm_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-amgtwotm-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Scott himself says that he "couldn't have written" the song without having read
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_Helprin" title="Mark Helprin">
        Mark Helprin
       </a>
       's novel
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter%27s_Tale_(Helprin)" title="Winter's Tale (Helprin)">
         Winter's Tale
        </a>
       </i>
       , but goes on to state that the song is not about Helprin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recnotes_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-recnotes-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The official Waterboys website's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequently_Asked_Questions" title="Frequently Asked Questions">
        Frequently Asked Questions
       </a>
       clarifies that Scott has said that the song's subject is "a composite of many people", including C. S. Lewis, but explicitly states that it is not about Prince.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Musician
       <a href="/wiki/Nikki_Sudden" title="Nikki Sudden">
        Nikki Sudden
       </a>
       , with whom Scott had collaborated before forming The Waterboys, has claimed that the song was written about himself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">
        Music
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Whole_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The band members at the time were Mike Scott,
       <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Thistlethwaite" title="Anthony Thistlethwaite">
        Anthony Thistlethwaite
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Karl_Wallinger" title="Karl Wallinger">
        Karl Wallinger
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Roddy_Lorimer" title="Roddy Lorimer">
        Roddy Lorimer
       </a>
       . Drummer
       <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Wilkinson" title="Kevin Wilkinson">
        Kevin Wilkinson
       </a>
       had left the band by the time "The Whole of the Moon" was recorded and drums were played by session player, Chris Whitten. Demoed but not finished at the beginning of the recording sessions, the song was eventually completed in May 1985.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recnotes_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-recnotes-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WholeoftheMoon2.jpeg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="313" data-file-width="317" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9b/WholeoftheMoon2.jpeg/220px-WholeoftheMoon2.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/9b/WholeoftheMoon2.jpeg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WholeoftheMoon2.jpeg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         1991 alternate sleeve
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A feature of "The Whole of the Moon" is the trumpet work on the recording, courtesy of the classically trained Lorimer. Lorimer spent three days with Scott working on the song's arrangement and "went home with a tape of the song and thought about a more classical approach. After a while sitting at the piano I came up with the idea of antiphonal trumpets. A piccolo trumpet on the left answered a piccolo on the right and then the same again, growing by adding a B
       <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">
        <span class="music-flat">
         ♭
        </span>
       </span>
       trumpet below each side of the stereo picture. Mike loved it, except the slightly jazzy chords I had used on the run down at the very end, which he simplified. I used the same classical approach later in the song, mixing two classical-type trumpets behind a later verse."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lorimer also contributes
       <a href="/wiki/Falsetto" title="Falsetto">
        falsetto
       </a>
       background vocals to the song, while Thistlethwaite, another brass section member, performs a saxophone solo near the end. Wallinger provided synthesizer, synth bass and backing vocals.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Releases">
        Releases
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Whole_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Releases">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       It was first released as a seven and 12-inch single, which reached number 26 on the United Kingdom singles chart. The single also contained a live recording of "The Girl in the Swing", from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Waterboys_(album)" title="The Waterboys (album)">
         The Waterboys
        </a>
       </i>
       , the band's first album, an extended mix of "Spirit", and a song titled "Medicine Jack". Following various sell-out tours by the band from 1986-90 the song appeared on the group's greatest hits package
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Best_of_the_Waterboys_81%E2%80%9390" title="The Best of the Waterboys 81–90">
         The Best of the Waterboys 81–90
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1991.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-disc-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That year, this song was re-released as a single (7", 12" and CD) from the album and was a big hit, peaking at number three on the
       <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">
        UK Singles Chart
       </a>
       and receiving an Ivor Novello Award as "Best Song Musically and Lyrically" in 1991.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-bbc-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The second single release had different B sides from the 1985 version. "Golden Age" was on the 7" B side and the 12" had "The Golden Age Medley", which included "A Golden Age", "Higher In Time" (fast), "High Far Soon" and "Soon As I Get Home".
      </p>
      <p>
       Including the 2004 remastered album, the song has been officially released four times and appears on the following Waterboys albums:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/This_Is_the_Sea" title="This Is the Sea">
          This Is the Sea
         </a>
        </i>
        (1985)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Best_of_The_Waterboys_81%E2%80%9390" title="The Best of The Waterboys 81–90">
          The Best of The Waterboys 81–90
         </a>
        </i>
        (1991)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         The Whole of the Moon: The Music of Mike Scott &amp; The Waterboys
        </i>
        (1998)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         This Is the Sea
        </i>
        - special remastered 2CD edition (2004)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Karma_to_Burn_(The_Waterboys_album)" title="Karma to Burn (The Waterboys album)">
          Karma to Burn
         </a>
        </i>
        (live) (2005)
       </li>
      </ul>
