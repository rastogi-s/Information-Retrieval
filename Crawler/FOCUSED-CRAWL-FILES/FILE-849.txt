   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Culmination
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       , the
       <b>
        culmination
       </b>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">
        constellation
       </a>
       is its
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_(astronomy)" title="Transit (astronomy)">
        transit
       </a>
       over an observer's
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(astronomy)" title="Meridian (astronomy)">
        meridian
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgc_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cgc-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fof_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-fof-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_day" title="Sidereal day">
        sidereal day
       </a>
       , an astronomical object crosses the meridian twice: once at its
       <b>
        upper culmination
       </b>
       , when it is approximately at its highest point as seen from the Earth, and once at its
       <b>
        lower culmination
       </b>
       , its approximately lowest point. Often, culmination is used to mean upper culmination.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgc_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-cgc-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fof_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-fof-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altitude_(astronomy)" title="Altitude (astronomy)">
        altitude
       </a>
       of an object in degrees at its upper culmination is equal to (90 − L + D), where L is the observer's
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and D is the object's
       <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">
        declination
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cases">
        Cases
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Culmination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cases">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The three cases are dependent on the observer's latitude and the declination of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">
        celestial body
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The object is above the
        <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
         horizon
        </a>
        even at its lower culmination; i.e. if |declination + latitude| &gt; 90° (i.e. if in
        <a href="/wiki/Absolute_value" title="Absolute value">
         absolute value
        </a>
        the declination is more than the colatitude, in the corresponding hemisphere)
       </li>
       <li>
        The object is below the horizon even at its upper culmination; i.e. if |declination − latitude| &gt; 90° (i.e. if in absolute value the declination is more than the colatitude, in the opposite hemisphere)
       </li>
       <li>
        The upper culmination is above and the lower below the horizon, so the body is observed to rise and set daily; in the other cases (i.e. if in absolute value the declination is less than the
        <a href="/wiki/Colatitude" title="Colatitude">
         colatitude
        </a>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The third case applies for objects in a part of the full sky equal to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosine" title="Cosine">
        cosine
       </a>
       of the latitude (at the equator it applies for all objects, because the sky turns around the horizontal north-south line; at the poles it applies for none, because the sky turns around the vertical line). The first and second case each apply for half of the remaining sky.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Period">
        Period
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Culmination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Period">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The time from one upper culmination to the next is approximately 24 hours, and between an upper to a lower culmination is approximately 12 hours. The movement of the Earth on its orbit and
       <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">
        proper motion
       </a>
       of the celestial body affect the time between successive upper culminations. Because of the
       <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">
        proper
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Improper_motion" title="Improper motion">
        improper
       </a>
       motions of the sun, one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_day" title="Solar day">
        solar day
       </a>
       (the time between two upper culminations of the sun) is longer than one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_day" title="Sidereal day">
        sidereal day
       </a>
       (the time between two like culminations of any
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_star" title="Fixed star">
        fixed star
       </a>
       )
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Mean" title="Mean">
        mean
       </a>
       difference is 1/365.24219 because the Earth needs 365.24219 days for its orbit around the Sun. (see also
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_day" title="Sidereal day">
        sidereal day
       </a>
       )
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sun">
        Sun
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Culmination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sun">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Generally, the sun is visible at its upper culmination (at noon) and not visible at its lower culmination (at midnight). But near the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Pole
       </a>
       during the winter of that hemisphere, the sun is below the horizon at both of its culminations.
      </p>
      <p>
       Assume that on a given summer day the declination of the Sun is +20°. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complementary_angles" title="Complementary angles">
        complementary angle
       </a>
       of 70° (from the sun to the pole) is added or subtracted from the observer's latitude to find the upper and lower culminations:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        At a latitude of 52°N, the upper culmination is at 58°, in the south, and the lower culmination is −18°, below the horizon, in the north. This is calculated as 52°+70°=122° (the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supplementary_angles" title="Supplementary angles">
         supplementary angle
        </a>
        being 58°) for the upper, and 52°-70°=−18° for the lower.
       </li>
       <li>
        At a latitude of 80°N the upper culmination is at 30°, in the south, and the lower at 10°, also above the horizon (
        <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">
         midnight sun
        </a>
        ), in the north.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Polaris">
        Polaris
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Culmination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Polaris">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In most of the northern hemisphere,
       <a href="/wiki/Polaris" title="Polaris">
        Polaris
       </a>
       , the "North Star," and the rest of the stars of the constellation
       <a href="/wiki/Ursa_Minor" title="Ursa Minor">
        Ursa Minor
       </a>
       can be seen rotating around the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_pole" title="Celestial pole">
        celestial pole
       </a>
       and are visible at both culminations, as long as the sky is dark enough. Such stars, which never set at the observer's location, are described as
       <a href="/wiki/Circumpolar_star" title="Circumpolar star">
        circumpolar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
