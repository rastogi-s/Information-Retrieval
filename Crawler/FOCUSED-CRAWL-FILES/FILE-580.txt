      <p>
       <b>
        Moon Express
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        MoonEx
       </b>
       ), is an American privately held early stage company formed by a group of
       <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">
        Silicon Valley
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">
        space
       </a>
       entrepreneurs, with the goal of ultimately
       <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">
        mining
       </a>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       for natural resources of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_value" title="Economic value">
        economic value
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat20110408a_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lat20110408a-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrown2011_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown2011-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Express&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In August 2010,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChow2011_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChow2011-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_D._Richards" title="Robert D. Richards">
        Robert D. Richards
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SuccessfulFlight_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SuccessfulFlight-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Naveen_Jain" title="Naveen Jain">
        Naveen Jain
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ForbesMoon_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ForbesMoon-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Barney_Pell" title="Barney Pell">
        Barney Pell
       </a>
       co-founded Moon Express, a
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain_View,_California" title="Mountain View, California">
        Mountain View, California
       </a>
       -based company that plans to offer commercial lunar robotic transportation and data services with a long-term goal of mining the Moon for resources,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuffingtonPost_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HuffingtonPost-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       including elements that are rare on Earth, including
       <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">
        niobium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">
        yttrium
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium" title="Dysprosium">
        dysprosium
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat20110408a_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-lat20110408a-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hindu.com_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hindu.com-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moon Express based itself at the NASA Ames Research Center from 2010 to 2015, and the company announced its relocation to Florida's Cape Canaveral in 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On June 30, 2011, the company held its first successful test flight of a prototype lunar lander system called the Lander Test Vehicle (LTV) that was developed in partnership with NASA.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SuccessfulFlight_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-SuccessfulFlight-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On September 11, 2011, Moon Express announced that it had set up a robotics lab for a lunar probe named the "Moon Express Robotics Lab for INnovation" (MERLIN) and hired several engineering students who had successfully competed at the
       <a href="/wiki/FIRST_Robotics_Competition" title="FIRST Robotics Competition">
        <i>
         FIRST
        </i>
        Robotics Competition
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In mid-2012, Moon Express announced that it will work with
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.iloa.org/" rel="nofollow">
        International Lunar Observatory Association (ILOA)
       </a>
       to put a shoebox-sized astronomical telescope on the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additional details were released in July 2013, including that there would be two telescopes: a 2 meters (6 ft 7 in)
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">
        radio telescope
       </a>
       as well as an
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">
        optical telescope
       </a>
       . The preferred location is 5 kilometers (3.1 mi)-high
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malapert" title="Malapert">
        Malapert
       </a>
       crater, with plans to land the mission no earlier than 2018.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired20130718_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wired20130718-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By 2012, MoonEx had 20 employees. In December 2012, MoonEx acquired one of the other Google Lunar X-Prize teams,
       <a href="/wiki/Rocket_City_Space_Pioneers" title="Rocket City Space Pioneers">
        Rocket City Space Pioneers
       </a>
       , from
       <a href="/wiki/Dynetics" title="Dynetics">
        Dynetics
       </a>
       for an undisclosed sum. The new agreement makes
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tim_Pickens&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tim Pickens (page does not exist)">
        Tim Pickens
       </a>
       , the former lead of the RCSP team, the Chief Propulsion Engineer for MoonEx.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsw20121220_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nsw20121220-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In September 2013, MoonEx added
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Spudis" title="Paul Spudis">
        Paul Spudis
       </a>
       as Chief Scientist and Jack Burns as Science Advisory Board Chair.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GLXP20130905_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GLXP20130905-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In October and November, 2013, Moon Express successfully conducted several free flight tests of its flight software utilizing the NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Mighty_Eagle" title="Mighty Eagle">
        Mighty Eagle
       </a>
       lander test vehicle, under a Reimbursable Space Act Agreement with the NASA Marshall Space Flight Center.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span id="MX-1">
       </span>
       In December 2013, MoonEx unveiled the
       <b>
        MX-1
       </b>
       lunar lander, a
       <a href="/wiki/Toroid" title="Toroid">
        toroidal
       </a>
       robotic lander that uses
       <a href="/wiki/High-test_peroxide" title="High-test peroxide">
        high-test
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">
        hydrogen peroxide
       </a>
       as its
       <a href="/wiki/Rocket_propellant" title="Rocket propellant">
        rocket
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Monopropellant" title="Monopropellant">
        propellant
       </a>
       to support
       <a href="/wiki/VTVL" title="VTVL">
        vertical landing
       </a>
       on the lunar surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pa20131205_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pa20131205-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On April 30, 2014
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       announced that Moon Express Inc. was one of the three companies selected for the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_CATALYST" title="Lunar CATALYST">
        Lunar CATALYST
       </a>
       initiative.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20140430_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20140430-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon Express "MX-1" spacecraft is designed to be launched as a secondary payload and to fly to the Moon from GEO.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FL_TD_20141103_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FL_TD_20141103-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In December 2014, Moon Express successfully conducted flight tests of its "MTV-1X" lander test vehicle at the Kennedy Space Center Shuttle Landing Facility, becoming the first private company (and GLXP team) to demonstrate a commercial lunar lander.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In October 2015, Moon Express announced a launch contract with
       <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Lab" title="Rocket Lab">
        Rocket Lab
       </a>
       to launch three Moon Express robotic spacecraft to land on the Moon, with two launches manifested in 2017, utilizing an Electron launch vehicle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In July 2016, Moon Express announced it would be taking over Cape Canaveral Launch Complexes 17 and 18 as its new permanent home.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On July 20, 2016, the
       <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">
        Federal Aviation Administration
       </a>
       approved Moon Express plans for a mission to deliver commercial payloads to the Moon in late 2017, making Moon Express the first private company to receive government approval for a commercial space mission beyond traditional Earth orbit under the requirements of the Outer Space Treaty.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj.com_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wsj.com-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On July 12, 2017, Moon Express unveiled the robotic rocket architecture and plans for regular deliveries to the Moon and back.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On October 31, 2017 NASA announced that the Lunar CATALYST agreement had been extended for 2 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCAT_20171031_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LCAT_20171031-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_regime_for_lunar_resource_extraction">
        Legal regime for lunar resource extraction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Express&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal regime for lunar resource extraction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although the requisite legal regime to enable mining of lunar resources is not fully in place,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSI_SM14a_20101109_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSI_SM14a_20101109-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       major world space agencies, including
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       , have put in place a coordination framework for encouraging the type of commercial activity proposed by Moon Express.
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        "Entrepreneurs are thinking about further commercial expansion into space. As space exploration extends to the Moon and Mars, there will be potential opportunities for companies to provide ... space-based resource extraction and processing capabilities. For example, Moon rocks are rich in oxygen that might be exploited to provide life support systems for lunar operations. Liquid oxygen can also be used as a rocket propellant – and it might be more economical to manufacture it in space than to lift it off the Earth. Mining the Moon might also yield
        <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">
         titanium
        </a>
        – a strong but light metal favoured for high-end aerospace applications. Finally, the Moon’s known abundance of
        <a href="/wiki/Helium-3" title="Helium-3">
         Helium-3
        </a>
        could prove valuable if fusion reactors ever become feasible in the future.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csa2007_25-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-csa2007-25">
          [25]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        "For business to be confident about investing, it needs the certainty of a long-term commitment to space exploration, the opportunity to introduce its ideas into government thinking, and the rule of law. This means common understanding on such difficult issues as property rights and technology transfer. The Coordination Mechanism foreseen as part of the Global Exploration Strategy will provide a forum to discuss these important issues."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csa2007_25-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-csa2007-25">
          [25]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The views of Moon Express on the legal and political realities of lunar resource utilization were articulated in an April 2011
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">
        Los Angeles Times
       </a>
       article where Naveen Jain was interviewed:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        The idea of exploiting the Moon's resources for private gain is not likely to be a concern, Jain said. The United States, he said, 'has already brought back Moon rocks to our country without any other country fighting war over it.' 'I also think that the Moon will be treated no differently than the
        <a href="/wiki/International_waters" title="International waters">
         international water
        </a>
        in our oceans,' he added. 'In this case, no one really owns the water but any company or country can mine the resources … from the international water as long as they follow certain safety/moral guidelines.' Jain also noted that 'there is strong legal precedent and consensus of 'finders keepers' for resources that are liberated through private investment, and the same will be true on the Moon. You don't have to own land to have ownership of resources you unlock from it. Moon Express will use existing precedents of peaceful presence and exploration set by the U.S. government 40 years ago.'
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat20110408a_1-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-lat20110408a-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">
        Outer Space Treaty
       </a>
       of 1967, ratified by 100 nations, including the United States, forbids countries from claiming sovereignty over any part of the Moon, but does not prevent private companies from building or staking claims on the Moon. Mining could fall under similar legal parameters as fishing in international waters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeattleTimes_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SeattleTimes-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On May 23, 2017, the Senate Subcommittee on Space, Science, and Competitiveness held a hearing titled, "Reopening the American Frontier: Exploring How the Outer Space Treaty Will Impact American Commerce and Settlement in Space." This hearing examined U.S. government obligations under the Outer Space Treaty on its 50th anniversary, specifically compliance with Article VI of the Treaty that requires governments to authorize and continually supervise the activities of non-government entities. This hearing also explored the Treaty’s potential impacts on expansion of the nation’s commerce and settlement in space. Bob Richards testified in this hearing with other commercial space proponents.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>

       for data purchase that could be worth up to
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">

        <a href="#cite_note-SuccessfulFlight-4">
         [4]

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Robotic_spacecraft">
        Robotic spacecraft
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Express&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Robotic spacecraft">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       All Moon Express robotic spacecraft are environmentally green, use eco-friendly fuels, advanced carbon composites and silicates and a Moon Express PECO rocket engine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fleet_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fleet-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The PECO main engine uses (unknown) as a fuel and hydrogen peroxide as an oxidiser. The reusable landing thrusters use hydrogen peroxide as a monopropellant.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       MX-1 – A single engine robotic spacecraft. The MX-1 has a 30 kg payload capacity.
      </p>
      <p>
       MX-2 – A two-stage robotic spacecraft with a 30 kg payload capacity.
      </p>
      <p>
       MX-5 – A 5-engine, platform configuration that can include MX-1 or MX-2 staged system. The MX-5 has a 150 kg payload capacity.
      </p>
      <p>
       MX-9 – A 9-engine platform configuration, designed for sample return. The MX-9 has over a 500 kg payload capacity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fleet_30-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fleet-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rocket">

       <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Lab" title="Rocket Lab">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contract_2015_32-0">

       <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Lab#Electron" title="Rocket Lab">
        Electron

       rocket system to launch three missions. Two launches are for 2018, with the third to be scheduled at a later date.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contract_2015_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-contract_2015-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <p>
       The company is also a competitor in the
       <a href="/wiki/Google_Lunar_X_Prize" title="Google Lunar X Prize">

       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IndiaWest_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IndiaWest-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of October 2015, there are 16 teams competing for the Google Lunar XPRIZE, two of which have launch contracts for 2017 (now 2018) (SpaceIL and Moon Express). This prize will award $20 million to the first team to put a robotic spacecraft on the Moon and deliver data, images and video from the landing site and 500 meters away.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ForbesMoon_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ForbesMoon-5">

         [35]
        </a>

        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On January 23, 2018, X Prize founder and chairman Peter Diamandis stated "After close consultation with our five finalist Google Lunar X Prize teams over the past several months, we have concluded that no team will make a launch attempt to reach the moon by the March 31, 2018, deadline.".

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_mission_plans">
        Other mission plans
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Express&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other mission plans">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In addition to participation in the Google Lunar XPRIZE, with a planned "maiden technology demonstrator flight" in 2015,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pa20131205_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-pa20131205-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       MoonEx is planning to place the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_Lunar_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International Lunar Observatory (page does not exist)">
        International Lunar Observatory
       </a>
       (ILO) on the Moon as early as 2018. The plan calls for placement of both a 2 meters (6 ft 7 in)
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">
        radio telescope
       </a>
       as well as an
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">
        optical telescope
       </a>
       at the South Pole of the Moon. The preferred location, as of July 2013
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Moon_Express&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is 5 kilometers (3.1 mi)-high
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malapert" title="Malapert">
        Malapert crater
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired20130718_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-wired20130718-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       First mission planned is the
       <i>
        Lunar Scout
       </i>
       expedition. This is a technology demonstration flight that will include payloads including the International Lunar Observatory, “MoonLight” by the INFN National Laboratories of Frascati and the University of Maryland, a Celestis memorial flight and the attempt to win the $20M Google Lunar XPRIZE.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Second mission is the
       <i>
        Lunar Outpost
       </i>
       expedition. This mission will include developing a permanent lunar outpost on the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_38-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Third mission,
       <i>
        Harvest Moon
       </i>
       , will be a sample return mission.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_38-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
