    Moons of Haumea

      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System#Trans-Neptunian_region" title="Solar System">
        outer Solar System
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       has two known
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moons
       </a>
       , Hiʻiaka and Namaka, named after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawaiian_mythology" title="Hawaiian mythology">
        Hawaiian
       </a>
       goddesses. These small moons were discovered in 2005, from observations of Haumea made at the large telescopes of the
       <a href="/wiki/W._M._Keck_Observatory" title="W. M. Keck Observatory">
        W. M. Keck Observatory
       </a>
       in Hawaii.
      </p>
      <p>
       Haumea's moons are unusual in a number of ways. They are thought to be part of
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea_family" title="Haumea family">
        its extended collisional family
       </a>
       , which formed billions of years ago from icy debris after a large impact disrupted Haumea's
       <a href="/wiki/Volatiles" title="Volatiles">
        ice
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       . Hiʻiaka, the larger, outermost moon, has large amounts of pure water ice on its surface, which is rare among
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       objects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barkume2006_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barkume2006-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Namaka, about one tenth the mass, has an orbit with surprising dynamics: it is unusually
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentric
       </a>
       and appears to be greatly influenced by the larger satellite.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Haumea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Two small
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellites
       </a>
       were discovered around
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       (which was at that time still designated 2003 EL
       <sub>
        61
       </sub>
       ) through observations using the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W.M._Keck_Observatory" title="W.M. Keck Observatory">
        W.M. Keck Observatory
       </a>
       by a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caltech" title="Caltech">
        Caltech
       </a>
       team in 2005. The outer and larger of the two satellites was discovered January 26, 2005,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and formally designated S/2005 (2003 EL
       <sub>
        61
       </sub>
       ) 1, though nicknamed "
       <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_the_Red-Nosed_Reindeer" title="Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer">
        Rudolph
       </a>
       " by the Caltech team.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The smaller, inner satellite of Haumea was discovered on June 30, 2005, formally termed S/2005 (2003 EL
       <sub>
        61
       </sub>
       ) 2, and nicknamed "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blitzen_the_Reindeer" title="Blitzen the Reindeer">
        Blitzen
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RagozzineDPS08_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RagozzineDPS08-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On September 7, 2006, both satellites were numbered and admitted into the official minor planet catalogue as (136108) 2003 EL
       <sub>
        61
       </sub>
       I and II, respectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       The permanent names of these moons were announced, together with that of 2003 EL
       <sub>
        61
       </sub>
       , by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       on September 17, 2008: (136108) Haumea I Hiʻiaka and (136108) Haumea II Namaka.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iaunews_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-iaunews-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Each moon was named after a daughter of
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea_(mythology)" title="Haumea (mythology)">
        Haumea
       </a>
       , the Hawaiian goddess of fertility and childbirth.
       <a href="/wiki/Hi%CA%BBiaka" title="Hiʻiaka">
        Hiʻiaka
       </a>
       is the goddess of
       <a href="/wiki/Hula" title="Hula">
        dance
       </a>
       and patroness of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Island_of_Hawaii" title="Big Island of Hawaii">
        Big Island of Hawaii
       </a>
       , where the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea_Observatory" title="Mauna Kea Observatory">
        Mauna Kea Observatory
       </a>
       is located.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-usgs-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/N%C4%81maka" title="Nāmaka">
        Nāmaka
       </a>
       is the goddess of water and the sea; she cooled her sister
       <a href="/wiki/Pele_(deity)" title="Pele (deity)">
        Pele
       </a>
       's lava as it flowed into the sea, turning it into new land.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craig_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-craig-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In her legend, Haumea's many children came from different parts of her body.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craig_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-craig-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The dwarf planet
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       appears to be almost entirely made of rock, with only a superficial layer of ice; most of the original icy mantle is thought to have been blasted off by the impact that spun Haumea into its current high speed of rotation, where the material formed into the small
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       objects in
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea_family" title="Haumea family">
        Haumea's collisional family
       </a>
       . There could therefore be additional outer moons, smaller than Namaka, that have not yet been detected. However,
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        HST
       </a>
       observations have confirmed that no other moons brighter than 0.25% of the brightness of Haumea exist within the closest tenth of the distance (0.1% of the volume) where they could be held by Haumea's gravitational influence (its
       <a href="/wiki/Hill_sphere" title="Hill sphere">
        Hill sphere
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ragozzine&amp;Brown2009_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ragozzine&amp;Brown2009-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This makes it unlikely that any more exist.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_properties">
        Surface properties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Haumea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surface properties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Hiʻiaka is the outer and, at roughly 350 km in diameter, the larger and brighter of the two moons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2006-discovery_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2006-discovery-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Strong absorption features observed at 1.5, 1.65 and 2
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">
        µm
       </a>
       in its
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       spectrum are consistent with nearly pure crystalline water ice covering much of its surface. The unusual spectrum, and its similarity to absorption lines in the spectrum of Haumea, led Brown and colleagues to conclude that it was unlikely that the system of moons was formed by the gravitational capture of passing Kuiper belt objects into orbit around the dwarf planet: instead, the Haumean moons must be fragments of Haumea itself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-largest_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-largest-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The sizes of both moons are calculated with the assumption that they have the same infrared
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       as Haumea, which is reasonable as their spectra show them to have the same surface composition. Haumea's albedo has been measured by the
       <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">
        Spitzer Space Telescope
       </a>
       : from ground-based telescopes, the moons are too small and close to Haumea to be seen independently.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraser09_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fraser09-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Based on this common albedo, the inner moon, Namaka, which is a tenth the mass of Hiʻiaka, would be about 170 km in diameter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnston_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Johnston-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       (HST) has adequate angular resolution to separate the light from the moons from that of Haumea. Photometry of the Haumea triple system with HST's
       <a href="/wiki/Near_Infrared_Camera_and_Multi-Object_Spectrometer" title="Near Infrared Camera and Multi-Object Spectrometer">
        NICMOS camera
       </a>
       has confirmed that the spectral line at 1.6 µm that indicates the presence of water ice is at least as strong in the moons' spectra as in Haumea's spectrum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraser09_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fraser09-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The moons of Haumea are too faint to detect with telescopes smaller than about 2 metres in
       <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">
        aperture
       </a>
       , though Haumea itself has a visual magnitude of 17.5, making it the third-brightest object in the Kuiper belt after
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Makemake" title="Makemake">
        Makemake
       </a>
       , and easily observable with a large amateur telescope.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_characteristics">
        Orbital characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Haumea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbital characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Haumea_moons.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="400" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Haumea_moons.svg/300px-TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Haumea_moons.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Haumea_moons.svg/450px-TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Haumea_moons.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Haumea_moons.svg/600px-TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Haumea_moons.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Haumea_moons.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A view of the orbits of Hiʻiaka (blue) and Namaka (green)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Hiʻiaka orbits Haumea nearly circularly every 49 days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2006-discovery_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2006-discovery-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Namaka orbits Haumea in 18 days in a moderately elliptical,
       <a href="/wiki/Osculating_orbit" title="Osculating orbit">
        non-Keplerian
       </a>
       orbit, and as of 2008 was inclined 13° with respect to Hiʻiaka, which
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        perturbs
       </a>
       its orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RagozzineDPS08_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-RagozzineDPS08-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because the impact that created the moons of Haumea is thought to have occurred in the early history of the Solar System,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownBarkume2007_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BrownBarkume2007-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       over the following billions of years it should have been
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">
        tidally damped
       </a>
       into a more circular orbit. Namaka's orbit has likely been disturbed by
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonances
       </a>
       with the more-massive Hiʻiaka due to converging orbits as they moved outward from Haumea due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">
        tidal dissipation
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RagozzineDPS08_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-RagozzineDPS08-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They may have been caught in and then escaped from orbital resonance several times; they currently are in or at least close to an 8:3
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        resonance
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RagozzineDPS08_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-RagozzineDPS08-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This resonance strongly perturbs Namaka's orbit, which has a current
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precession_(astronomy)#Anomalistic_precession" title="Precession (astronomy)">
        precession
       </a>
       of its
       <a href="/wiki/Argument_of_periapsis" title="Argument of periapsis">
        argument of periapsis
       </a>
       by about −6.5° per year, a precession period of 55 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ragozzine&amp;Brown2009_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ragozzine&amp;Brown2009-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At present, the orbits of the Haumean moons appear almost exactly edge-on from Earth, with Namaka having periodically
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occulted
       </a>
       Haumea from 2009 to 2011.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU8949_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAU8949-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-events09_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-events09-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Observation of such transits would provide precise information on the size and shape of Haumea and its moons, as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_eclipses_on_Pluto" title="Solar eclipses on Pluto">
        happened in the late 1980s
       </a>
       with Pluto and
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The tiny change in brightness of the system during these occultations required at least a
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope#Astronomical_research_telescopes" title="Optical telescope">
        medium
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">
        aperture
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_optical_telescopes" title="List of optical telescopes">
        professional telescope
       </a>
       for detection.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FabryckyDPS08_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FabryckyDPS08-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hiʻiaka last occulted Haumea in 1999, a few years before its discovery, and will not do so again for some 130 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shadows_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-shadows-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, in a situation unique among
       <a href="/wiki/Regular_moon" title="Regular moon">
        regular satellites
       </a>
       , the great
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precession_(astronomy)#Anomalistic_precession" title="Precession (astronomy)">
        torquing
       </a>
       of Namaka's orbit by Hiʻiaka preserved the viewing angle of Namaka–Haumea transits for several more years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RagozzineDPS08_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-RagozzineDPS08-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FabryckyDPS08_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FabryckyDPS08-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Namaka is the smaller, inner moon of the dwarf planet Haumea. It is named after
       <a href="/wiki/N%C4%81maka" title="Nāmaka">
        Nāmaka
       </a>
       , the goddess of the sea in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawaiian_mythology" title="Hawaiian mythology">
        Hawaiian mythology
       </a>
       and one of the daughters of
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea_(mythology)" title="Haumea (mythology)">
        Haumea
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Haumea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Namaka was discovered on 30 June 2005 and announced on 29 November 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_8636_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_8636-26">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was nicknamed "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blitzen_the_Reindeer" title="Blitzen the Reindeer">
        Blitzen
       </a>
       " by the discovery team before being assigned an official name.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Haumea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Namaka is only 1.5% as bright as its parent dwarf planet Haumea
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barkume2006_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barkume2006-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and is about 0.05% its mass. If it turns out to have a similar
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       , it would be about 170 km in diameter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston_25-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-johnston-25">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Photometric observations indicate that its surface is made of water ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barkume2006_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barkume2006-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Mutual events between 2009 and 2011
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       were expected to improve the knowledge of the orbits and masses of the components of the Haumean system,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RagozzineBrown2009_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RagozzineBrown2009-28">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but interpreting those observations was greatly complicated by the unexpected non-tidally locked spin state of Hiʻiaka. Further observations of Hiʻiaka might allow to determine its rotation period and spin state more precisely, at which point it should be possible to remove its effect from the data obtained in 2009.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>

      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">
        Discovery and naming
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Haumea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Hiʻiaka was the first
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       discovered around Haumea. It is named after one of the daughters of
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea_(mythology)" title="Haumea (mythology)">
        Haumea
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hi%CA%BBiaka" title="Hiʻiaka">
        Hiʻiaka
       </a>
       , the patron goddess of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Island_of_Hawaii" title="Big Island of Hawaii">
        Big Island of Hawaii
       </a>
       , though at first it had gone by the nickname "
       <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_the_Red-Nosed_Reindeer" title="Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer">
        Rudolph
       </a>
       " by its discovery team. It orbits once every
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7006424396800000000♠
        </span>
        49.12
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.03 d
       </span>
       at a distance of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7007498800000000000♠
        </span>
        49
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         880
        </span>
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        198
        <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">
         km
        </a>
       </span>
       , with an eccentricity of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6998513000000000000♠
        </span>
        0.0513
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.0078
       </span>
       and an inclination of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7002126356000000000♠
        </span>
        126.356
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.064°
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics_2">

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

         ]

      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Size_and_brightness">
        Size and brightness
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Haumea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Size and brightness">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Its measured brightness is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7000590000000000000♠
        </span>
        5.9
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.5%
       </span>
       , translating into a diameter of about 22% of its primary, or in the range of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005320000000000000♠
        </span>
        320 km
       </span>
       , assuming similar albedo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RagozzineBrown2009_28-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-RagozzineBrown2009-28">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To put this in perspective, this would make it larger than all but
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_notable_asteroids#Largest_by_diameter" title="List of notable asteroids">
        four
       </a>
       of the asteroids, after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_Ceres" title="1 Ceres">
        1 Ceres
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/2_Pallas" title="2 Pallas">
        2 Pallas
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">
        4 Vesta
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/10_Hygiea" title="10 Hygiea">
        10 Hygiea
       </a>
       . In spite of its relatively large size, however, lightcurve studies suggest that Hi'iaka is not a gravitationally collapsed spheroid; they further suggest that Hi'iaka is not tidally locked and has a rotation period of about 9.8 hours.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mass">
        Mass
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Haumea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mass">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The mass of Hiʻiaka is estimated to be
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7019179000000000000♠
        </span>
        (1.79
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.11)
        <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         19
        </sup>
        <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">
         kg
        </a>
       </span>
       using precise relative
       <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">
        astrometry
       </a>
       from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_Telescope" title="Hubble Telescope">
        Hubble Telescope
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_Telescope" title="Keck Telescope">
        Keck Telescope
       </a>
       and applying
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-body_problem" title="3-body problem">
        3-body
       </a>
       , point-mass model to the Haumean system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RagozzineBrown2009_28-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-RagozzineBrown2009-28">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spectrum_and_composition">
        Spectrum and composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Haumea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Spectrum and composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The near infrared spectrum of Hiʻiaka is dominated by water-ice absorption bands, which means that its surface is made mainly of water ice. The presence of the band centered at
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6994165000000000000♠
        </span>
        1.65 μm
       </span>
       indicates that the surface water ice is primarily in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">
        crystalline
       </a>
       form. Currently it is unclear why water ice on the surface has not turned into
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous" title="Amorphous">
        amorphous
       </a>
       form as would be expected due to its constant irradiation by
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">
        cosmic rays
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dumas2011_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dumas2011-33">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
