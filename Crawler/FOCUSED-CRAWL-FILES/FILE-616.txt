   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Human Lunar Return study
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       began its "
       <b>
        Human Lunar Return study
       </b>
       " in September 1995 to identify ways it could conduct future
       <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">
        human spaceflight
       </a>
       missions to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . The final Human Lunar Return (HLR) briefing took place on August 7, 1996. The study was seen as laying "the foundation for human space activity over the next three decades."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radiation_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-radiation-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">
        Mission
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Lunar_Return_study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The study called for a mission lasting 16 days, 10 of which would be spent on the lunar surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radiation_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-radiation-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The study baselined a lightweight architecture including an open-cockpit
       <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">
        lunar lander
       </a>
       weighing 4,565 kilograms (10,064 lb) including fuel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nss_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nss-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The lunar habitat was designed to have an inflatable hull and was scheduled to be delivered in advance of the crew.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astronautix_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-astronautix-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To protect against
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">
        cosmic rays
       </a>
       and a possible
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_particle_event" title="Solar particle event">
        solar particle event
       </a>
       , the hull of the lunar habitat to be at minimum 5 grams per square centimetre (1.1 oz/in
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ) thick and filled with either water or
       <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">
        polyethylene
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radiation_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-radiation-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Components and crew for the mission would have been transported to the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">
        International Space Station
       </a>
       (ISS) by two
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">
        Space Shuttle
       </a>
       flights. The HLR schedule called for the first mission to depart from the ISS in August 2001.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nss_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nss-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One of the primary goals of the mission was to bring 300 kilograms (660 lb) of payload from the lunar surface back to earth to be studied.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astronautix_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-astronautix-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cost">
        Cost
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Lunar_Return_study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cost">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The projected cost of the mission over the five year development timeline ranged between $2.5
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astronautix_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-astronautix-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and $4 billion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nss_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-nss-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mission required two shuttle and three
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket)" title="Proton (rocket)">
        Proton
       </a>
       launches to land two
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        astronauts
       </a>
       and a small habitat structure at
       <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_(crater)" title="Aristarchus (crater)">
        Aristarchus
       </a>
       crater.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astronautix_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-astronautix-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
