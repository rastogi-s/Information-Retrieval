      <p>
       <b>
        Moon Knight
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">
        fictional
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">
        superhero
       </a>
       appearing in
       <a href="/wiki/American_comic_book" title="American comic book">
        American comic books
       </a>
       published by
       <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">
        Marvel Comics
       </a>
       . Created by
       <a href="/wiki/Doug_Moench" title="Doug Moench">
        Doug Moench
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Don_Perlin" title="Don Perlin">
        Don Perlin
       </a>
       , the character first appeared in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Werewolf_by_Night" title="Werewolf by Night">
         Werewolf by Night
        </a>
       </i>
       #32 (August 1975).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_history">
        Publication history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Publication history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The character debuted in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Werewolf_by_Night" title="Werewolf by Night">
         Werewolf by Night
        </a>
       </i>
       #32 (August 1975), written by
       <a href="/wiki/Doug_Moench" title="Doug Moench">
        Doug Moench
       </a>
       with art by
       <a href="/wiki/Don_Perlin" title="Don Perlin">
        Don Perlin
       </a>
       , as a villain hired by the
       <a href="/wiki/Committee_(comics)" title="Committee (comics)">
        Committee
       </a>
       to capture the title character for them in a two-part story continuing in #33 where he eventually has a change of heart and helps the Werewolf escape, briefly fighting alongside him. Moon Knight later returned in the form of a demonic apparition taking on his appearance in #37 (March 1976) to battle the Werewolf once again. Editors
       <a href="/wiki/Marv_Wolfman" title="Marv Wolfman">
        Marv Wolfman
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Len_Wein" title="Len Wein">
        Len Wein
       </a>
       liked the character, prompting them to grant him a solo spot in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Spotlight" title="Marvel Spotlight">
         Marvel Spotlight
        </a>
       </i>
       #28–29 (June/August 1976),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       again written by Doug Moench with art by Don Perlin. The story, along with the Bill Mantlo-penned
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectacular_Spider-Man" title="Spectacular Spider-Man">
         Spectacular Spider-Man
        </a>
       </i>
       #22 and #23 (September/October 1978), recast Moon Knight as a hero and his villainous first appearance as a cover to infiltrate the Committee. Subsequent appearances came in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Two-in-One" title="Marvel Two-in-One">
         Marvel Two-in-One
        </a>
       </i>
       #52, written by
       <a href="/wiki/Steven_Grant" title="Steven Grant">
        Steven Grant
       </a>
       with art by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Craig_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jim Craig (comics) (page does not exist)">
        Jim Craig
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Defenders_(comic_book)" title="The Defenders (comic book)">
         The Defenders
        </a>
       </i>
       #47–51, which had him briefly join the Defenders during their war against the Zodiac Cartel.
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon Knight then gained a backup strip in
       <i>
        Hulk! Magazine
       </i>
       #11–15, #17–18, and #20, which saw the character first drawn with artist
       <a href="/wiki/Bill_Sienkiewicz" title="Bill Sienkiewicz">
        Bill Sienkiewicz
       </a>
       on issues #13-15, 17-18, and #20 as well as a black and white story in the magazine publication
       <i>
        Marvel Preview
       </i>
       #21. Sienkiewicz's eclectic art style helped shed the early perception of Moon Knight as a mere
       <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">
        Batman
       </a>
       clone.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        Hulk
       </i>
       backups and
       <i>
        Marvel Preview
       </i>
       issue, which were all written by Doug Moench, provided Moon Knight with a partial origin story and introduced one of his most notable recurring villains: Randall Spector, who would later become
       <a href="/wiki/Shadow_Knight_(comics)" title="Shadow Knight (comics)">
        Shadow Knight
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon Knight received his first ongoing series in 1980, with Doug Moench and Bill Sienkiewicz as its main creative team. The character received a complete origin story, and most of his notable recurring villains were introduced; particularly his arch enemy
       <a href="/wiki/Bushman_(comics)" title="Bushman (comics)">
        Bushman
       </a>
       . Early sales were good for the book, leading to Marvel, as of issue #15, moving the title from newsstand distribution and making it one of its flagship titles for a group of books only available in comic shops. A companion mini-series was also released,
       <i>
        Moon Knight: Special Edition
       </i>
       , which reprinted the
       <i>
        Hulk
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Marvel Preview
       </i>
       Moon Knight stories in color and in comic format, as opposed to their original magazine format. Sienkiewicz left the series after issue #30, though continued to contribute covers until the final issue (#38).
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1985, Marvel followed up the series with
       <i>
        Moon Knight – Fist Of Khonshu
       </i>
       by Alan Zelenetz and Chris Warner, a six issue mini-series that established Moon Knight as suffering from schizophrenia due to the stress of his various aliases. Moon Knight appeared in
       <i>
        Marvel Fanfare
       </i>
       for two issues (#30 and #38) and in the pages of
       <i>
        West Coast Avengers
       </i>
       (#21–41 and
       <i>
        Annual
       </i>
       s #1–3), with the character written by Steven Englehart.
      </p>
      <p>
       With the arrival of John Byrne onto the title, Moon Knight was written out of the
       <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_Avengers" title="West Coast Avengers">
        West Coast Avengers
       </a>
       and after a guest spot in
       <i>
        Punisher Annual
       </i>
       #2 (part of the "Atlantis Attacks" storyline), the character was given a new ongoing title in 1989,
       <i>
        Marc Spector: Moon Knight
       </i>
       . The series was originally written by Chuck Dixon, who left the title after issue #24. Dixon left the book with several storylines unresolved (most notably, the fate of Moon Knight's errant sidekick, the second Midnight) and the plotline with the sidekick was resolved in the pages of
       <i>
        The Amazing Spider-Man
       </i>
       #353–358, written by Al Milgrom.
      </p>
      <p>
       The series was canceled with #60 (March 1994), with four of the last six issues drawn by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Platt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stephen Platt (page does not exist)">
        Stephen Platt
       </a>
       , who was hired by Image Comics based on the strength of his work on the series. Two one-shots,
       <i>
        Marc Spector: Moon Knight Special Edition
       </i>
       #1 and
       <i>
        Moon Knight: Divided We Fall,
       </i>
       were published during the run of the title.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1998, writer
       <a href="/wiki/Doug_Moench" title="Doug Moench">
        Doug Moench
       </a>
       , artist
       <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Edwards" title="Tommy Edwards">
        Tommy Edwards
       </a>
       , and inker Robert Campanella brought the deceased hero back in a four-part
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mini-series" title="Mini-series">
        mini-series
       </a>
       called the
       <i>
        Resurrection Wars
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1999, Moench and artist
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_Texeira" title="Mark Texeira">
        Mark Texeira
       </a>
       worked together on another four-part mini-series called "High Strangeness" which was nominated for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comics_Buyer%27s_Guide_Fan_Award" title="Comics Buyer's Guide Fan Award">
        Comics Buyer's Guide Fan Award
       </a>
       for Favorite Limited Series. The title of the story was mistakenly given as "High Strangers" on the covers of the mini-series. The correct title of the story, "High Strangeness", appeared on the title page of each issue.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <i>
        Moon Knight
       </i>
       ongoing series was launched in April 2006, written by
       <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Huston" title="Charlie Huston">
        Charlie Huston
       </a>
       with art by
       <a href="/wiki/David_Finch_(comics)" title="David Finch (comics)">
        David Finch
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of #14 of this series,
       <a href="/wiki/Mike_Benson_(screenwriter)" title="Mike Benson (screenwriter)">
        Mike Benson
       </a>
       took over writing duties
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with Huston acting as story-outline adviser according to Benson in an interview with Marvel published as a one-page excerpt in various Marvel comic books throughout late 2007 and early 2008.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Milligan" title="Peter Milligan">
        Peter Milligan
       </a>
       also wrote a 2008 seasonal one-shot titled "Moon Knight: Silent Knight" with artist
       <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Campbell" title="Laurence Campbell">
        Laurence Campbell
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 2006 series ended with #30 (July 2009), and only one Annual in the series was printed in 2008.
      </p>
      <p>
       That series was followed by a ten-issue maxi-series titled
       <i>
        Vengeance of the Moon Knight
       </i>
       , beginning in September 2009, written by
       <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Hurwitz" title="Gregg Hurwitz">
        Gregg Hurwitz
       </a>
       and drawn by Jerome Opena.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After
       <i>
        Vengeance of the Moon Knight
       </i>
       was canceled, Moon Knight was placed in the team book
       <i>
        Secret Avengers
       </i>
       (appearing for the title's first 21 issues), in the
       <i>
        Shadowland
       </i>
       three issue storyline and in a 2010 relaunch of
       <i>
        Heroes for Hire
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It was announced at the New York Comic Con that 2011 would see the launch of a new
       <i>
        Moon Knight
       </i>
       series by
       <a href="/wiki/Brian_Michael_Bendis" title="Brian Michael Bendis">
        Brian Michael Bendis
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Alex_Maleev" title="Alex Maleev">
        Alex Maleev
       </a>
       . The series, which saw Moon Knight replace his multiple personalities with heroes such as Spider-Man, Captain America, and Wolverine, was canceled after 12 issues due to poor sales.
      </p>
      <p>
       In March 2014, Marvel launched a new ongoing series as part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Marvel_NOW!" title="Marvel NOW!">
        Marvel NOW!
       </a>
       initiative that introduced a new suit-and-tie costume for the character, with a revolving door of creative teams that included

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khonshu_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Khonshu (Marvel Comics)">

        </i>
        Volume 3 (2006)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       This volume opens with Marc Spector's early retirement which comes after a brutal battle with
       <a href="/wiki/Bushman_(comics)" title="Bushman (comics)">
        Bushman
       </a>
       . Although his body is broken after a tremendous fall and both knees shattered, Moon Knight finally defeats his nemesis
       <a href="/wiki/Bushman_(comics)" title="Bushman (comics)">
        Bushman
       </a>
       by carving off his face with a crescent moon dart. Spector's is then haunted by a spiritual appiration of Khonshu, who chose a faceless Bushman as his ethereal representation. Marc Spector's back ground is
       <a href="/wiki/Retroactive_continuity" title="Retroactive continuity">
        updated
       </a>
       , so he fought in the
       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">
        Gulf War
       </a>
       and that his time as a mercenary was during the 1990s. It is also revealed that Frenchie is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homosexual" title="Homosexual">
        homosexual
       </a>
       and in love with Marc Spector; which is why he stuck around for so long.
      </p>
      <p>
       The new Secret Committee then hires
       <a href="/wiki/Profile_(comics)" title="Profile (comics)">
        The Profile
       </a>
       , an amoral character who can analyze a persona past and future habits with a glance, to help them entrap Moon Knight. He escapes after the plan collapses because they authorized a mugging of Frenchie too soon. Profile then becomes a reluctant source of information for Spector himself, even giving him advise on rekindling his relationship with Marlene.
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon Knight eventually gets drawn into the
       <a href="/wiki/Civil_War_(comics)" title="Civil War (comics)">
        Superhero Registration Act
       </a>
       as he investigates a string of murders perpetrated by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midnight_(Jeff_Wilde)" title="Midnight (Jeff Wilde)">
        Midnight
       </a>
       , his former sidekick. Other Marvel heroes take notice of his violent return to vigilantism.
       <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">
        Spider-Man
       </a>
       attempts to contact Moon Knight but is rebuked.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Captain_America" title="Captain America">
        Captain America
       </a>
       pays him a visit to deliver a warning and they quarrel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Punisher" title="Punisher">
        Punisher
       </a>
       and Moon Knight have a lengthy conversation about the nature of their vigilantism and their shared past.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moon Knight is forced into a final confrontation with his cybernetically enhanced former sidekick Midnight, seemingly killing him for good.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man" title="Iron Man">
        Iron Man
       </a>
       also investigates Moon Knight's activities by placing him under close surveillance. Finding his mental condition unstable,
       <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man" title="Iron Man">
        Iron Man
       </a>
       decides that arresting Moon Knight under the Registration Act might make his mental instability worse. Moon Knight begrudgingly applies for registration after much prodding from Khonshu, not wanting the law to keep him from his work. The new law requires him to submit to a psychiatric exam. The psychiatrist controlling the exam, along with the government and Tony Stark, has no intention of granting Marc Spector approval for registration. After speaking with Spector's repressed alter egos Jake Lockley and Steven Grant, the psychiatrist begins the process of officially turning him down, suggesting possible future imprisonment. Subconsciously Spector breaks the doctor's will by speaking in the voice of Khonshu and pointing out the doctor's own
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-social_behavior" title="Anti-social behavior">
        antisocial
       </a>
       tendencies, as told to him earlier by the Profile. The psychiatrist not only approves his application, but bows to worship him as well. However, later on, Marc meets the Profile with their dialogue suggesting that the personalities above were just an act to be approved for registration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
        <a href="#cite_note-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon Knight's shows little regard for his newly licensed super hero status or any passion to the people around him, further alienating himself. His former nemesis
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Spectre_(Carson_Knowles)" title="Black Spectre (Carson Knowles)">
        Black Spectre
       </a>
       returns. Carson Knowles, recently released from prison, attempts to frame his murders on Moon Knight. He steals some Stark nanotechnology and plans to unleash it on the city. Moon Knight pushes Knowles off a building apparently to his death.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Stark" title="Tony Stark">
        Tony Stark
       </a>
       , as the head of the Initiative, revokes his superhero registration, but buries the fact that Spectre had stolen Stark Tech.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
        <a href="#cite_note-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>

       <a href="/wiki/Thunderbolts_(comics)" title="Thunderbolts (comics)">
        Thunderbolts
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Norman_Osborn" title="Norman Osborn">
        Norman Osborn
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Maria_Hill" title="Maria Hill">
        Maria Hill
       </a>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commission_on_Superhuman_Activities" title="Commission on Superhuman Activities">

       </a>

        <a href="#cite_note-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>

Khonshu appears and informs Spector he doesn't need him anymore, as he now has other worshipers. Spector returns to his Moon Knight costume to aid Frenchie DuChamp in gaining revenge on the
       <a href="/wiki/Whyos" title="Whyos">
        Whyos
       </a>
       gang for attacking his restaurant and injuring Frenchie's lover Rob, only to find the Whyos' attack was designed to draw Spector into another conflict with the Thunderbolts when he is ambushed by
       <a href="/wiki/Mac_Gargan#Venom" title="Mac Gargan">

       </a>

        <a href="#cite_note-57">
         [57]
        </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Bullseye_(comics)" title="Bullseye (comics)">
        Bullseye
       </a>
       is released to kill Moon Knight, as Spector prepares to go out with a bang.

        <a href="#cite_note-58">
         [58]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon Knight is next seen battling Bullseye in the streets of NYC. He eventually leads Bullseye to a bunker/warehouse where he has planted several explosives. Bullseye narrowly escapes as Moon Knight ignites the explosives. Later that day two press conferences are held: one by Norman Osborn to announce the Thunderbolts' success and Moon Knight's death and the other held by Tony Stark who denounces the methods used by the Thunderbolts. At the end of the issue it is revealed that Moon Knight has faked his death, escapes from the warehouse through a secret passage in the floor, and is hiding in Mexico. It is also revealed that the Marc Spector persona has "died" and that Jake Lockley is now in control.

        <a href="#cite_note-59">
         [59]

         [60]

       <a href="/wiki/Toltec" title="Toltec">
        Toltec
       </a>

Frank Castle's reasoning was that he knew Moon Knight would find himself somehow involved with the very cartel Frank had been pursuing; however, the Toltec god's avatar made quick work of the cartel.

         [61]

Lockley knows that Osborn had been behind the group of Thunderbolts sent to kill him, and that a deranged psychotic was not fit to run the U.S.A.'s national security agencies, so using some spoils from the Cartel, Moon Knight decided it was time for a comeback.

While trying to walk the path of a hero he makes a bold return taking on many criminals but killing none of them; now the people of New York begin to see him as a hero and not a murderous vigilante much to Norman Osborn's disdain. Jake's personality has been one of struggle against the inner demon trying to get him to kill while juggling sobriety.

        <a href="#cite_note-62">
         [62]

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons_to_Batman">
        Comparisons to Batman
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Comparisons to Batman">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Huston" title="Charlie Huston">
        Charlie Huston
       </a>
       , writer of the 2006 re-launch of
       <i>
        Moon Knight
       </i>
       , attempted to answer the criticism that Moon Knight is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ersatz" title="Ersatz">
        ersatz
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">
        Batman
       </a>
       in an interview with Comixfan.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95">
        <a href="#cite_note-95">
         [95]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The interviewer noted that the comparison is not baseless, as both Moon Knight and the Dark Knight are wealthy, "normal" humans that use gadgetry to fight crime.
      </p>
      <p>
       Huston accepted that the two characters had their similarities, but went on to contrast the two by noting in particular differences in origin, motives, and personality. "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruce_Wayne" title="Bruce Wayne">
        Bruce Wayne
       </a>
       ", he said, "fights crime to avenge the murders of his parents", whereas Moon Knight "beats up whoever has it coming because he believes he is the avatar of the Egyptian god of vengeance and it helps him to feel better about all the people he killed when he was a mercenary." Thus, while Batman is motivated by vengeance for wrong done to his parents, Marc Spector is motivated by vengeance as a concept. Huston further notes that Bruce Wayne, Batman's alter ego, takes on other personalities merely to aid in his fight. However, Moon Knight has three alter egos which aid him as much in dealing with personal demons as fighting law-breakers, and which have taken a further psychological toll of causing
       <a href="/wiki/Dissociative_identity_disorder" title="Dissociative identity disorder">
        dissociative identity disorder
       </a>
       . In the question of his sanity,
       <a href="/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">
        Spider-Man
       </a>
       remarked "Mooney. Rhymes with loony."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96">
        <a href="#cite_note-96">
         [96]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

He has some degree of superhuman strength during the peak of a lunar cycle, but he only gets superhuman strength from the lunar cycle. It's not known how much of this strength is mystical and how much is simply the result of self-hypnosis due to his psychological instability. Due to his multiple personalities, he is also resistant to some psychic attacks and sometimes receives prophetic visions.       <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sculpture">
        Sculpture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Knight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Sculpture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Numerous commercially released sculptures have been created of the character Moon Knight. The first was a mini bust created by Bowen Designs in the late 1990s. It features a him in his white costume from the waist up. The second rendition was another mini bust from Diamond Select in 2006. It depicted only his torso and head, and featured a darker costume and white cape. In 2006 Bowen Designs released a full body statue of Moon Knight sculpted and designed by the Kucharek Brothers. This statue came in two versions. The first was painted a dark silver, and the second, which was far more rare, was painted white. In 2013 Gentle Giant released yet another mini bust of Moon Knight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132">
        <a href="#cite_note-132">
         [132]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This sculpture featured a waist up depiction of moon knight with his arms prominently displayed.
      </p>
