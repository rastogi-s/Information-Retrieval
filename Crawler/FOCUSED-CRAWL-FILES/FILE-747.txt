      <p>
       <b>
        Moon Township
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Township_(Pennsylvania)" title="Township (Pennsylvania)">
        township
       </a>
       along the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">
        Ohio River
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">
        Allegheny County
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">
        Pennsylvania
       </a>
       , United States. Moon is a part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Metro_Area" title="Pittsburgh Metro Area">
        Pittsburgh Metro Area
       </a>
       and is located 12 miles (19 km) northwest of
       <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">
        Pittsburgh
       </a>
       . The origin of its name is unknown; a few historians suggest it was derived from a crescent-shaped bend in the river. The population was 24,185 at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Census_2010" title="United States Census 2010">
        2010 census
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="Early_history_.281756-1773.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_(1756-1773)">
        Early history (1756-1773)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history (1756-1773)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The initial settlement of Moon Township was a direct result of the westward expansion of English settlers and traders who arrived in the Ohio Valley in the early to mid-18th century. During the
       <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">
        French and Indian War
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">
        Seven Years' War
       </a>
       ), the Iroquois, who controlled the land for hunting grounds through right of conquest, ceded large parcels of southwestern Pennsylvania lands through treaty or abandonment to settlers. In some cases, the land was already occupied by squatters who were to be forced off of the land.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the face of this turmoil, Indian settlements of the south bank of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">
        Ohio River
       </a>
       typically relocated to more populous areas of the north bank in the current locales of
       <a href="/wiki/Sewickley,_Pennsylvania" title="Sewickley, Pennsylvania">
        Sewickley
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ambridge,_Pennsylvania" title="Ambridge, Pennsylvania">
        Ambridge
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       On the southern banks of the Ohio, political disputes among settlers clouded the disposition of lands. Generally, the Pennsylvania Land Office apportioned land to owners through grants. But, some of the land encompassing what is now the Coraopolis Heights, Thorn Run valley, and Narrows Run valley were claimed through the process of "Tomahawk Improvements", a non-specific and oftentimes contested method. Settlement processes were often convoluted because of differences among land policies of the several colonies claiming the land, specifically Pennsylvania and
       <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">
        Virginia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Each colony had its own means of either granting or restricting settlement opportunities. Each settler claiming land in what is now Moon Township had to go through a multi-level process of application for grant, warrant of property, and survey to ensure the physical boundaries of the property, and patent approval whereby the applicant paid for the land and title was conferred.
      </p>
      <p>
       On April 3, 1769,
       <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Montour" title="Andrew Montour">
        Andrew Montour
       </a>
       , an Indian interpreter who had provided service to the
       <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">
        English
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Settler" title="Settler">
        settlers
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">
        French and Indian War
       </a>
       , was granted one of the first land patents for approximately 350 acres (1.4 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ) of what later became the borough of
       <a href="/wiki/Coraopolis,_Pennsylvania" title="Coraopolis, Pennsylvania">
        Coraopolis
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neville_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Neville Township, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">
        Neville Island
       </a>
       . In 1773, the settler John Meek was awarded a 400-acre (1.6 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ) land grant from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Colony" title="Virginia Colony">
        Virginia
       </a>
       above the river bottom and between the Thorn Run and Montour Run valleys, and "Moon Township" was born.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Settlement_times_.281773-1799.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Settlement_times_(1773-1799)">
        Settlement times (1773-1799)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Settlement times (1773-1799)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The settlers Robert Loudon and John Vail were awarded grants to a total of 600 acres (2.4 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ). Loudon's tract was situated on the Coraopolis Heights adjacent to the Meek grant. Vail's grant was established between the current Thorn Run and Narrows Run valleys (although the exact location is open to some interpretation).
      </p>
      <p>
       Three other early grants were warranted by either Virginia or Pennsylvania land speculators. The boundaries of these land tracts are hard to identify, and the names of the original grantees are contested. But historians believe that they encompassed about 700 acres (2.8 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ) or so, and were occupied by anonymous squatters. Given that the history is somewhat hazy, it remains that in abandoning their lands, the unidentified squatters ceded any potential claims to settlers who would otherwise improve and/or cultivate the land.
      </p>
      <p>
       As the 18th century drew to a close, abandoned lands were taken up by new settlers who were drawn to the region by the fertility of the soil. This round of pioneers were, by and large, wealthier than their predecessors and had the means to develop the broken and hilly areas into plots suitable for farming.
      </p>
      <p>
       At this time Moon Township occupied an enormous tract of land - possibly 145 square miles (380 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ). The sheer difficulty of residents performing their civic duties (e.g., report to assigned polling places or attend jury trials) made it necessary for local governing authorities to parcel out the land into smaller municipalities. So, in 1790, the current Fayette Township was portioned off from Moon Township, to be followed by
       <a href="/wiki/Findlay_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Findlay Township, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">
        Findlay
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Crescent_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Crescent Township, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">
        Crescent
       </a>
       townships, respectively.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1900s_to_present">
        1900s to present
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1900s to present">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1943, the
       <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">
        federal government
       </a>
       designed and built a housing plan known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mooncrest" title="Mooncrest">
        Mooncrest
       </a>
       for defense workers. Mooncrest residents produced
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armor_plates" title="Armor plates">
        armor plates
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munitions" title="Munitions">
        munitions
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">
        ships
       </a>
       at the nearby Dravo Corp. during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       . Operated by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">
        U.S. Air Force
       </a>
       after 1945, homes were sold to private investors in the mid-1950s.
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon became home to Pittsburgh's modern-day airport in 1951, replacing the
       <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_County_Airport" title="Allegheny County Airport">
        Allegheny County Airport
       </a>
       as the main terminal for the region. The area developed mainly due to the airport. Prior to this time, the western hills of Allegheny County consisted largely of rolling farms and small residential developments. On April 1, 1956,
       <a href="/wiki/TWA_Flight_400" title="TWA Flight 400">
        TWA Flight 400
       </a>
       crashed on takeoff from the airport, killing 22 persons just past the east end of the runway, which lies in Moon Township.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">
        Cold War
       </a>
       , Moon Township was the location of
       <a href="/wiki/Project_Nike" title="Project Nike">
        Nike
       </a>
       Site PI-71, which was a battery of Nike Ajax and/or Nike Hercules surface-to-air missiles, used by US armed forces for high – and medium-altitude air defense. The former missile site is now a nature preserve.
      </p>
      <p>
       Development of
       <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Route_60" title="Pennsylvania Route 60">
        Pennsylvania Route 60
       </a>
       (now
       <a href="/wiki/Interstate_376" title="Interstate 376">
        Interstate 376
       </a>
       ) to the Pittsburgh airport, plus the addition of the
       <a href="/wiki/Interstate_376" title="Interstate 376">
        Parkway West
       </a>
       from Pittsburgh and nearby exits of
       <a href="/wiki/Interstate_79" title="Interstate 79">
        Interstate 79
       </a>
       , allowed Moon to become the area's crossroads for transportation via air and road.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1991, the relocation of the landside terminal of the
       <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_International_Airport" title="Pittsburgh International Airport">
        Pittsburgh International Airport
       </a>
       to nearby
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Findlay_Township,_Pennsylvania" title="Findlay Township, Pennsylvania">
        Findlay Township
       </a>
       resulted in a loss in traffic to the township. Moon experienced a significant loss of tax revenues but has since rebounded as the cargo area for the airport.
      </p>
      <p>
       A large part of the airport's runways and facilities are located within the boundary of Moon Township, although the terminal and about half of the airport's land area are in
       <a href="/wiki/Findlay_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Findlay Township, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">
        Findlay Township
       </a>
       , to the west.
      </p>
      <p>
       The township is home to the
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.afreserve.com/bases.asp?id=71" rel="nofollow">
        Air Force Reserve 911th Airlift Wing
       </a>
       , which was established in 1943. Moon is also home to the 171st Air Refueling Wing of the Pa. Air National Guard. Additionally, the Army has its 99th Regional Readiness Command, built in the late 1990s in Moon Township.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the loss of the airport terminal, the township has shifted its focus from airport commerce to corporate development, residences and university hub. The main campus of
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Morris_University" title="Robert Morris University">
        Robert Morris University
       </a>
       is also located within the township.
      </p>
      <p>
       Major corporation headquarters like
       <a href="/wiki/Nova_Chemicals" title="Nova Chemicals">
        Nova Chemicals
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Ground" title="FedEx Ground">
        FedEx Ground
       </a>
       , First Health/Coventry and the consumer division of
       <a href="/wiki/GlaxoSmithKline" title="GlaxoSmithKline">
        GlaxoSmithKline
       </a>
       are located in Moon.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_and_development">
        Expansion and development
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Expansion and development">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Growth is expected to continue in Moon in the near future due to many new construction projects such as the redevelopment of University Boulevard and conversion of the West Hills Shopping Center into a new shopping plaza.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ground was broken in late 2006 on the new Cherrington Parkway extension. The extension, anticipated to be opened in early 2008, will create additional shovel-ready land for Class A office space, for corporate development.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As a result of Robert Morris University, the college feeds much of the economy along the township's University Boulevard area.
      </p>
      <p>
       Several new businesses have opened recently, including the new
       <a href="/wiki/DoubleTree" title="DoubleTree">
        DoubleTree
       </a>
       Hotel and restaurants including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primanti%27s" title="Primanti's">
        Primanti's
       </a>
       . A new
       <a href="/wiki/Walgreens" title="Walgreens">
        Walgreens
       </a>
       has been completed.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="US_Airways_Development">
        US Airways Development
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: US Airways Development">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Moon Township residents have a sort of love/hate relationship when it comes to US Airways. Love, because our hometown airline was born and grew through many iterations here. Hate because, ultimately, our hometown airline abandoned us several times - and always to our detriment and shame.
      </p>
      <p>
       US Airways was born in Pittsburgh in 1939 as All-American Aviation, an airmail service that was the brainchild of the duPont family, and helmed by brothers Richard and Alexis Felix duPont - with the supervision of CEO Steven Gardner. Once passenger airline service became the vogue, the duPonts decided to haul people instead of boxes and letters and, in 1953, Allegheny Airlines was born.
      </p>
      <p>
       Allegheny Airlines was one of the fastest growing airlines between the late 50's and 70s'. During that time, Allegheny Airlines had absorbed two regional airlines, Lake Central and Mowhawk. This sudden growth was a burden on the airline employees, who often had to make creative rather than operational decisions when encountering a traveler. Hence, Allegheny Airlines was colloquially called "Agony Airlines."
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1978, the Airline Deregulation Act was passed. The next year Allegheny Airlines renamed itself USAir.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the 80's, USAir went on a shopping spree. Within two years, they had bought up both Piedmont and Pacific Southwest Airlines, bringing their total number of flights to about 5,000 daily.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1987, with major subsidies from both Allegheny County and the State of Pennsylvania, USAir commanded the building of a 1.2 billion dollar expansion (rebuilding) of Pittsburgh International Airport. Where, it was said, USAir would retain a commanding presence and an airline hub for at least 45 years.
      </p>
      <p>
       Even before their custom-made airport was finished, USAir became US Airways and promised that the hub status of PIT airport would remain in place.
      </p>
      <p>
       After years of declining its operations in Moon Township, US Airways announced Feb. 20, 2007, it would build its flight operations center on a piece of land adjacent to the Pittsburgh International Airport and
       <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Route_60" title="Pennsylvania Route 60">
        Business Route 60
       </a>
       (now
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_376_Business" title="Interstate 376 Business">
        Interstate 376 Business
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The center would retain 450 high paying jobs and increase it by 150 for a total of 600 jobs. The facility was closed following the 2015 merger of US Airways and American Airlines, and replaced by a different facility in
       <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth,_Texas" title="Fort Worth, Texas">
        Fort Worth, Texas
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-post-gazette.com_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-post-gazette.com-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="West_Hills_Shopping_Center">
        West Hills Shopping Center
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: West Hills Shopping Center">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The West Hills Shopping Center, once the heart of Moon's commercial business, was sold to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">
        Wal-Mart
       </a>
       for $4.7 million and announced to the public on January 3, 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Wal-Mart officials announced their plans to build a supercenter location on the site of what was the West Hills Shopping Center.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The company also purchased two adjacent parcels of land along Brodhead Road. Preparation of the site began in the second half of 2015. The store opened in fall of 2016.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="University_Boulevard">
        University Boulevard
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: University Boulevard">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On the morning of August 14, 2003, the former Beers School and Narrows Run Roads (from the I-376 Business Loop route to Thorn Run Road) became known as University Boulevard, a move that helped to promote the township as the home of
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Morris_University" title="Robert Morris University">
        Robert Morris University
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The new road name also depicts the township's efforts to re-emerge as a business-dominant community. Since the 2003 renaming, township officials have researched various zoning ordinances to piece together Moon's main business corridor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Explore_Our_Universe">
        Explore Our Universe
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Explore Our Universe">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Playing off the township's unique name, supervisors in 2005 gave Moon a new slogan, "Explore Our Universe." "The slogan is a play not only on the township's lunar name but also on
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Morris_University" title="Robert Morris University">
        Robert Morris University
       </a>
       and the University Boulevard business corridor, which township officials would like investors and consumers to explore a little more thoroughly," wrote the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette" title="Pittsburgh Post-Gazette">
         Pittsburgh Post-Gazette
        </a>
       </i>
       in 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Education">
        Education
       </span>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxes">
        Taxes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Taxes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       School tax millage rate- The Moon Area School District (shared with Moon Township and Crescent Township) in 2017 was 20.30. This ranked 24th highest/most expensive out of Allegheny County's 45 school districts [between Allegheny Valley SD (23rd highest) and Plum Borough SD (25th highest)].
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>

      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Surrounding_and_adjacent_communities">
        Surrounding and adjacent communities
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Surrounding and adjacent communities">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Moon Township has six land borders, including
       <a href="/wiki/Crescent_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Crescent Township, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">
        Crescent Township
       </a>
       to the north-northwest,
       <a href="/wiki/Hopewell_Township,_Beaver_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Hopewell Township, Beaver County, Pennsylvania">
        Hopewell Township
       </a>
       (Beaver County) to the northwest,
       <a href="/wiki/Findlay_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Findlay Township, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">
        Findlay Township
       </a>
       to the west and southwest,
       <a href="/wiki/North_Fayette_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="North Fayette Township, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">
        North Fayette Township
       </a>
       to the south,
       <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Robinson Township, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">
        Robinson Township
       </a>
       to the southeast, east and northeast, and the borough of
       <a href="/wiki/Coraopolis,_Pennsylvania" title="Coraopolis, Pennsylvania">
        Coraopolis
       </a>
       to the north-northeast. Across the Ohio River to the northwest, a section of Moon runs adjacent with (from north to south)
       <a href="/wiki/Edgeworth,_Pennsylvania" title="Edgeworth, Pennsylvania">
        Edgeworth
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sewickley,_Pennsylvania" title="Sewickley, Pennsylvania">
        Sewickley
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osborne,_Pennsylvania" title="Osborne, Pennsylvania">
        Glen Osborne
       </a>
       . The Sewickley Bridge is the direct link between Moon Township and Sewickley.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="National_recognition">
        National recognition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: National recognition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 2007, Moon Township was honored with several honors as one of the country's best places to live.
      </p>
      <p>
       BusinessWeek.com ranked Moon one of five best affordable suburbs in the Northeast.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-businessweek-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moontwp_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moontwp-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Township officials had no idea the community received the award until Greg Smith, the township's manager, found the report online.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The recognition includes the 15108 zip code covering
       <a href="/wiki/Coraopolis,_Pennsylvania" title="Coraopolis, Pennsylvania">
        Coraopolis
       </a>
       borough,
       <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Kennedy Township, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">
        Kennedy
       </a>
       and Moon townships.
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon also was included in the 2007 "
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Best_Places_To_Raise_Your_Family&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Best Places To Raise Your Family (page does not exist)">
        Best Places To Raise Your Family
       </a>
       " published by Wiley Publishing, Inc. Moon is featured on pages 166-167. This listing included
       <a href="/wiki/Coraopolis,_Pennsylvania" title="Coraopolis, Pennsylvania">
        Coraopolis
       </a>
       and Moon as the rankings are based on zip code.
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon was nominated as a runner up in the list of top Pittsburgh suburbs to raise a family in 2013.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heartlandluxuryhomes_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-heartlandluxuryhomes-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        Presidential visits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

       <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">

       </a>
       made a surprise visit to Moon a day after pardoning President
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" title="Richard M. Nixon">

       </a>
       on Sept. 9, 1974.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1994, President
       <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">
        Bill Clinton
       </a>
       greeted Prime Minister John Major of Great Britain at a hangar at the 911th Air Wing of the Air force Reserve at the Pittsburgh International Airport in Moon Township.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPI_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UPI-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>

        <a href="#cite_note-pittsburghmagazine-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A day after securing the Democratic nomination for president, then-Vice President
       <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">
        Al Gore
       </a>
       held a rally at Moon's high school gym on March 16, 2000. In September 2009, President Obama visited Pittsburgh for the G-20 conference.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Republican presidential nominee
       <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">
        Donald Trump
       </a>
       held a rally on June 11, 2016 at the Moon Township airplane hangar.
      </p>

      <p>
       As of the
       <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">
        census
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GR2-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of 2000, there were 22,290 people, 8,445 households, and 5,767 families residing in the township. The
       <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">
        population density
       </a>
       was 939.1 people per square mile (362.5/km²). There were 9,200 housing units at an average density of 387.6/sq mi (149.6/km²). The racial makeup of the township was 93.17%
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">
        White
       </a>
       , 3.58%
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">
        African American
       </a>
       , 0.06%
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">
        Native American
       </a>
       , 1.94%
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">
        Asian
       </a>
       , 0.02%
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">
        Pacific Islander
       </a>
       , 0.25% from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">
        other races
       </a>
       , and 0.97% from two or more races.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">
        Hispanic
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">
        Latino
       </a>
       of any race were 0.99% of the population.
      </p>
      <p>
       There were 8,445 households, out of which 31.2% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 58.9% were
       <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">
        married couples
       </a>
       living together, 7.1% had a female householder with no husband present, and 31.7% were non-families. 26.8% of all households were made up of individuals, and 6.7% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.44 and the average family size was 2.99.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the township the population was spread out, with 22.1% under the age of 18, 10.6% from 18 to 24, 29.9% from 25 to 44, 24.6% from 45 to 64, and 12.8% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 38 years. For every 100 females there were 97.3 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 95.1 males.
      </p>
      <p>
       The median income for a household in the township was $57,173, and the median income for a family was $68,256. Males had a median income of $48,444 versus $31,073 for females. The
       <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">
        per capita income
       </a>
       for the township was $26,457. About 2.2% of families and 4.2% of the population were below the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">
        poverty line
       </a>
       , including 3.6% of those under age 18 and 3.5% of those age 65 or over.
      </p>
