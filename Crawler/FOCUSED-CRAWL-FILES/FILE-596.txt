      <p>
       <b>
        Cassini
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_craters" title="Lunar craters">
        lunar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       that is located in the Palus Nebularum, at the eastern end of
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Imbrium" title="Mare Imbrium">
        Mare Imbrium
       </a>
       . The crater are named after astronomers
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_Cassini" title="Giovanni Cassini">
        Giovanni
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cassini" title="Jacques Cassini">
        Jacques
       </a>
       Cassini. To the northeast is the
       <a href="/wiki/Promontorium_Agassiz" title="Promontorium Agassiz">
        Promontorium Agassiz
       </a>
       , the southern tip of the
       <a href="/wiki/Montes_Alpes" title="Montes Alpes">
        Montes Alpes
       </a>
       mountain range. South by south-east of Cassini is the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Theaetetus_(crater)" title="Theaetetus (crater)">
        Theaetetus
       </a>
       . To the northwest is the lone peak
       <a href="/wiki/Mons_Piton" title="Mons Piton">
        Mons Piton
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       From that location the Earth would mainly appear in the lunar sky at around 50 degrees above the southern horizon. Also the sun rises during the third quarter of Earthshine and the sun sets during its first quarter.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cassini_(lunar_crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The floor of Cassini is flooded, and is likely as old as the surrounding
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        mare
       </a>
       . The surface is peppered with a multitude of impacts, including a pair of significant craters contained entirely within the rim. Cassini A is the larger of these two, and it lies just north-east of the crater center, its depth is more than 2.5 km and has the elevation of 4,487 meters below mean level. A hilly ridge area runs from this inner crater toward the south-east. Near the south-west rim of Cassini is the smaller crater Cassini B whose depth is about 2 km (elevation: -4,400 meters). These elevations were recently scanned by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The walls of this crater are narrow and irregular in form but remain intact despite the lava flooding. Beyond the crater rim is a significant and irregular outer
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rampart" title="wikt:rampart">
        rampart
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For unknown reasons, this crater was omitted from early maps of the Moon. This crater is not of recent origin, however, so the omission was most likely an error on the part of the map-makers.
      </p>
