   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Chaldene
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    </div>

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Chaldene
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         kal-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          DEE
         </span>
         -nee
        </i>
       </a>
       ; Greek:
       <i>
        Χαλδηνή
       </i>
       ), also known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter XXI
        </span>
       </b>
       , is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       . It was discovered by a team of astronomers from the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">
        University of Hawaii
       </a>
       led by
       <a href="/wiki/Scott_S._Sheppard" title="Scott S. Sheppard">
        Scott S. Sheppard
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       , in 2000, and given the temporary designation
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/2000 J 10
        </span>
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Chaldene is about 3.8 kilometres in diameter, and orbits Jupiter at an average distance of 22,713 Mm in 699.327 days, at an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       of 167° to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       (169° to Jupiter's equator), in a retrograde direction and with an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of 0.2916.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was named in October 2002 after Chaldene, the mother of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solymos" title="Solymos">
        Solymos
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It belongs to the
       <a href="/wiki/Carme_group" title="Carme group">
        Carme group
       </a>
       , made up of irregular retrograde moons orbiting Jupiter at a distance ranging between 23 and 24 Gm and at an inclination of about 165°.
      </p>
