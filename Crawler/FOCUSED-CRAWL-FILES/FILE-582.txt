      <p>
       <b>
        Smart Lander for Investigating Moon
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        SLIM
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">
        lunar lander
       </a>
       being developed by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency">
        Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency
       </a>
       (JAXA). Unlike lunar landers of the past which have landed "where it is easy to land", SLIM will land "where it is wanted to land".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of 2016, the probe is planned to be launched in 2019.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaxa63_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-jaxa63-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-japanrealtime_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-japanrealtime-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_Lander_for_Investigating_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       SLIM is Japan's first major lunar surface mission, and will demonstrate precise, pinpoint lunar landing. During its descent to the Moon, the lander will recognize
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_craters" title="Lunar craters">
        lunar craters
       </a>
       by applying technology from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facial_recognition_systems" title="Facial recognition systems">
        facial recognition systems
       </a>
       , and determine its current location from utilizing observation data collected by the
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
        SELENE
       </a>
       (Kaguya) lunar orbiter mission. SLIM aims to
       <a href="/wiki/Soft_landing_(rocketry)" title="Soft landing (rocketry)">
        soft land
       </a>
       with an error range of 100 m (330 ft).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-japanrealtime_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-japanrealtime-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In comparison, the error range of the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       Eagle lunar module was an elliptic which was 20 km (12 mi) wide in downrange and 5 km (3.1 mi) wide in crossrange.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaxa63_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-jaxa63-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to Yoshifumi Inatani, deputy director general of the JAXA
       <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Space_and_Astronautical_Science" title="Institute of Space and Astronautical Science">
        Institute of Space and Astronautical Science
       </a>
       (ISAS), by succeeding in this extremely precise landing, it will lead to enhancing the quality of space exploration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       SLIM is planned to be launched together with the
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_Astronomy_Recovery_Mission" title="X-ray Astronomy Recovery Mission">
        XARM
       </a>
       space telescope, and is to land near the
       <a href='/wiki/Marius_Hills#Marius_Hills_"Hole"' title="Marius Hills">
        Marius Hills Hole
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_lava_tube" title="Lunar lava tube">
        lunar lava tube
       </a>
       entrance discovered by Kaguya.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The expected cost for developing this project is 18 billion yen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_Lander_for_Investigating_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The proposal which later came to be known as SLIM existed as early as back in 2005, as the
       <i>
        Small Lunar Landing Experiment Satellite
       </i>
       <span style="font-weight: normal">
        (
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         小型月着陸実験衛星
        </span>
        )
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 27 December 2013, ISAS called for proposals for its next "Competitively-Chosen Medium-Sized Focused Mission", and SLIM was among the seven proposals submitted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaxa63_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-jaxa63-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In June 2014, SLIM passed the semi-final selection along with the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Demonstration_and_Experiment_of_Space_Technology_for_INterplanetary_voYage&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Demonstration and Experiment of Space Technology for INterplanetary voYage (page does not exist)">
        DESTINY
       </a>
       technology demonstration mission, and in February 2015 SLIM was ultimately selected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From April 2016, SLIM gained project status within JAXA.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In May 2016,
       <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Electric" title="Mitsubishi Electric">
        Mitsubishi Electric
       </a>
       (MELCO) was reportedly awarded the contract for building the spacecraft.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       SLIM is scheduled to be the second Japanese lunar lander to operate from the Moon's surface; on 27 May 2016
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       announced that the
       <a href="/wiki/OMOTENASHI" title="OMOTENASHI">
        OMOTENASHI
       </a>
       (Outstanding MOon exploration TEchnologies demonstrated by NAno Semi-Hard Impactor)
       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">
        CubeSat
       </a>
       lander jointly developed by JAXA and the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">
        University of Tokyo
       </a>
       will be launched as a secondary payload on
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">
        Space Launch System
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Mission_1" title="Exploration Mission 1">
        Exploration Mission 1
       </a>
       . OMOTENASHI will deploy a mini lunar lander weighing 1 kg.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2017, because of funding difficulties arising from XARM's development cost, SLIM's launch was switched from a dedicated
       <a href="/wiki/Epsilon_(rocket)" title="Epsilon (rocket)">
        Epsilon
       </a>
       flight to a rideshare
       <a href="/wiki/H-IIA" title="H-IIA">
        H-IIA
       </a>
       flight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nikkeiepsilon_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nikkeiepsilon-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The resulting cost savings will be transferred to develop other satellites that are behind schedule due to XARM.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nikkeiepsilon_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nikkeiepsilon-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
