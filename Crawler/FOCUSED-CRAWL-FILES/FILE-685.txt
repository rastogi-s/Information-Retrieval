      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Destination Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       (a.k.a.
       <i>
        <b>
         Operation Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       ) is a 1950 American
       <a href="/wiki/Technicolor" title="Technicolor">
        Technicolor
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">
        space exploration
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">
        science fiction film
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">
        independently made
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/George_Pal" title="George Pal">
        George Pal
       </a>
       , directed by
       <a href="/wiki/Irving_Pichel" title="Irving Pichel">
        Irving Pichel
       </a>
       , that stars
       <a href="/wiki/John_Archer_(actor)" title="John Archer (actor)">
        John Archer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Warner_Anderson" title="Warner Anderson">
        Warner Anderson
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tom_Powers" title="Tom Powers">
        Tom Powers
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dick_Wesson_(actor)" title="Dick Wesson (actor)">
        Dick Wesson
       </a>
       . The film was distributed in the United States and the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eagle-Lion_Classics" title="Eagle-Lion Classics">
        Eagle-Lion Classics
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       With
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       , George Pal produced the first major U.S. science fiction film to deal with the dangers inherent in human space travel and the possible difficulties of America's first lunar mission landing on and safely returning from the closest heavenly body to Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       The film's premise is that U.S. private industry will mobilize, finance, and manufacture the first spacecraft to the Moon, while making the assumption that the U.S. government will then be forced to purchase or lease this new technology to remain the dominant power in space and on the Moon. Industrialists are shown cooperating to support the private venture. In the final scene, as the crew approaches the Earth, the traditional "The End"
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Title_card" title="Title card">
        title card
       </a>
       heralds the dawn of the coming
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Age" title="Space Age">
        Space Age
       </a>
       : "This is THE END...of the Beginning".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warren_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Warren-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
        Plot
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When their latest rocket test fails and government funding collapses,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_scientist" title="Rocket scientist">
        rocket scientist
       </a>
       Dr. Charles Cargraves (Warner Anderson) and space enthusiast General Thayer (Tom Powers) enlist the aid of aircraft
       <a href="/wiki/Magnate" title="Magnate">
        magnate
       </a>
       Jim Barnes (John Archer). With the necessary millions raised privately from a group of patriotic U.S. industrialists, Cargraves, Warner and Barnes build an advanced
       <a href="/wiki/Single-stage-to-orbit" title="Single-stage-to-orbit">
        single-stage-to-orbit
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_power" title="Atomic power">
        atomic powered
       </a>
       spaceship, named
       <i>
        Luna
       </i>
       , at their desert manufacturing and launch facility; the project is soon threatened by a ginned-up public uproar over "radiation safety". The three idealists circumvent legal efforts to stop their expedition by simply launching the world's first Moon mission well ahead of schedule; as a result, they must quickly substitute Joe Sweeney (Dick Wesson) as their expedition's
       <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
        radar
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
        radio
       </a>
       operator.
      </p>
      <p>
       On their way to the Moon, they are forced to go outside
       <i>
        Luna
       </i>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_gravity" title="Zero gravity">
        zero gravity
       </a>
       , wearing magnetic boots to stay on the hull to free a frozen piloting radar antenna greased-up by the inexperienced Sweeney hours before the launch. In the process, they carelessly lose one of the crew overboard, untethered in
       <a href="/wiki/Free_fall" title="Free fall">
        free fall
       </a>
       . He is cleverly retrieved by using the nozzle of a large oxygen cylinder as an improvised rocket motor. After achieving orbit around the Moon, the crew begins the complex landing procedure, using too much fuel during the
       <i>
        Luna
       </i>
       <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">
        '
       </span>
       s descent phase.
      </p>
      <p>
       Safely on the Moon, they explore the lunar surface, reporting back by radio how their view of the Earth looks contrasted against the black lunar sky; one crew member photographs another pretending to hold up the Earth like a modern
       <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(mythology)" title="Atlas (mythology)">
        Atlas
       </a>
       . The story takes a serious turn when they calculate the mass needed to lighten their spaceship in the Moon's one-sixth gravity in order to get home safely with their remaining fuel. No matter how much non-critical equipment they remove and leave on the lunar surface, the hard numbers radioed from Earth continue to point to one conclusion: Someone will have to stay behind on the Moon if the other three crew are to return safely to Earth. With time running out for their return launch window, the crew engineers their way home. They first jettison the ship's heavy radio equipment, losing contact with Earth, and finally their sole remaining space suit. An oxygen tank is used as a tethered, suspended weight to pull the space suit outside through the open
       <a href="/wiki/Airlock" title="Airlock">
        airlock
       </a>
       , which is then remotely closed and resealed. Their critical take-off weight finally achieved, and with all her crew safely aboard,
       <i>
        Luna
       </i>
       blasts off from the Moon for home.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
        Development
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Pal commissioned an initial
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        screenplay
       </a>
       from screenwriters James O'Hanlon and Rip Van Ronkel, but
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       writer
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
        Robert A. Heinlein
       </a>
       contributed significantly to
       <i>
        Destination Moon'
       </i>
       s final
       <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">
        screenplay
       </a>
       , also serving as the film's technical adviser. Certain story elements from his 1947
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juvenile_novel" title="Juvenile novel">
        juvenile novel
       </a>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Ship_Galileo" title="Rocket Ship Galileo">
         Rocket Ship Galileo
        </a>
       </i>
       were adapted for use in the film's final screenplay. Heinlein also published a tie-in
       <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">
        novella
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(story)" title="Destination Moon (story)">
         Destination Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       , based on the screenplay. The film's storyline also resembles portions of Heinlein's novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Sold_the_Moon" title="The Man Who Sold the Moon">
         The Man Who Sold the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       , which he wrote in 1949 but did not publish until 1951, a year after the Pal film opened.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warren_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Warren-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">
        '
       </span>
       s matte paintings, used for the departure of the
       <i>
        Luna
       </i>
       from Earth, its approach to the Moon, the spaceship's landing on its surface, and showing the panoramic Lunar landscape, are by noted astronomical artist
       <a href="/wiki/Chesley_Bonestell" title="Chesley Bonestell">
        Chesley Bonestell
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Director">
        Director
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Director">
         edit
        </a>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Irving_Pichel" title="Irving Pichel">
        Irving Pichel
       </a>
       was selected to direct the film, his 30th since 1932. Pichel began his Hollywood career as an actor during the 1920s and early 1930s, in such films as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dracula%27s_Daughter" title="Dracula's Daughter">
         Dracula's Daughter
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Story_of_Temple_Drake" title="The Story of Temple Drake">
         The Story of Temple Drake
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koszarski_1977_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Koszarski_1977-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Pichel had been
       <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_blacklist" title="Hollywood blacklist">
        blacklisted
       </a>
       after he was subpoenaed by the
       <a href="/wiki/House_Un-American_Activities_Committee" title="House Un-American Activities Committee">
        House Un-American Activities Committee
       </a>
       in 1947, despite having never been called to testify before HUAAC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buhle_2002_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buhle_2002-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He would go on to direct five more films after
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       before his death in 1954.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBride_2003_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-McBride_2003-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Woody_Woodpecker">
        Woody Woodpecker
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Woody Woodpecker">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Cartoon character
       <a href="/wiki/Woody_Woodpecker" title="Woody Woodpecker">
        Woody Woodpecker
       </a>
       's creator
       <a href="/wiki/Walter_Lantz" title="Walter Lantz">
        Walter Lantz
       </a>
       and producer George Pal had been close friends ever since Pal left Europe and arrived in Hollywood. As a result, out of friendship and good luck, Pal always tried to include Woody in all his film productions. (On the commentary track of the Special Collector's DVD Edition of George Pal's science fiction film
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_the_Worlds" title="War of the Worlds">
         War of the Worlds
        </a>
       </i>
       (1953), actors
       <a href="/wiki/Ann_Robinson" title="Ann Robinson">
        Ann Robinson
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gene_Barry" title="Gene Barry">
        Gene Barry
       </a>
       point out that Woody can be seen in a tree top, center screen, near the beginning of the film.) George Pal incorporates Woody in
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       as a vital part of its unfolding storyline.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adamson_1985_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Adamson_1985-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In a cartoon shown within the film, Woody explains the scientific principles behind space travel and then a trip to the Moon. This engaging cartoon is shown to a gathering of U.S. industrialists, who it is hoped will patriotically finance such a daring venture before an (unnamed) non-western power can do so successfully. The Woody cartoon actually serves the purpose of explaining, in layman's terms, to the average 1950 moviegoing audience, the practical details of a manned space expedition to the Moon and how it might be accomplished.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lev_2003_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lev_2003-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        Destination Moon
       </i>
       ultimately became the "second" space adventure film of the post-World War II era. Piggybacking on the growing publicity and expectation surrounding the Pal film,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lippert_Pictures" title="Lippert Pictures">
        Lippert Pictures
       </a>
       ' small budget ($94,000) and quickly shot (18 days)
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rocketship_X-M" title="Rocketship X-M">
         Rocketship X-M
        </a>
       </i>
       , about the first spaceship to land on distant
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       , opened in movie theaters 25 days before the Pal feature.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warren_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Warren-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reaction">
        Critical reaction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Critical reaction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Bosley_Crowther" title="Bosley Crowther">
        Bosley Crowther
       </a>
       in his review of
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       , opined, "... we've got to say this for Mr. Pal and his film: they make a lunar expedition a most intriguing and picturesque event. Even the solemn preparations for this unique exploratory trip, though the lesser phase of the adventure, are profoundly impressive to observe."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In a later appraisal in a
       <i>
        Time Out
       </i>
       review, editor John Pym saw
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       as having both good and bad aspects, "... characteristically thin on plot and characterization, high on patriotism, and impressive in its colour photography and special effects; a true precursor to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_(film)" title="Star Wars (film)">
         Star Wars
        </a>
       </i>
       ."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In his autobiography
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">
        Isaac Asimov
       </a>
       called it "the first intelligent science-fiction movie made."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">
        Awards and honors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       won the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Visual Effects">
        Academy Award for Visual Effects
       </a>
       in the name of the effects director,
       <a href="/wiki/Lee_Zavitz" title="Lee Zavitz">
        Lee Zavitz
       </a>
       . The film was also nominated for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Art_Direction" title="Academy Award for Best Art Direction">
        Academy Award for Best Art Direction
       </a>
       , by
       <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Fegt%C3%A9" title="Ernst Fegté">
        Ernst Fegté
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/George_Sawley" title="George Sawley">
        George Sawley
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes_staff_2010_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NYTimes_staff_2010-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At the
       <a href="/wiki/1st_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="1st Berlin International Film Festival">
        1st Berlin International Film Festival
       </a>
       it won the Bronze Berlin Bear Award, for "Thrillers and Adventure Films."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIFF_staff_2013_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BIFF_staff_2013-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retro_Hugo_Award" title="Retro Hugo Award">
        Retro Hugo Awards
       </a>
       : A special 1951
       <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Dramatic_Presentation" title="Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation">
        Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation
       </a>
       was retroactively awarded to
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/59th_World_Science_Fiction_Convention" title="59th World Science Fiction Convention">
        59th World Science Fiction Convention
       </a>
       (2001) exactly 50 years later for being one of the science fiction films eligible in calendar year 1950. (50 years, 75 years, or 100 years govern the time periods when a Retro Hugo can be awarded by a
       <a href="/wiki/Worldcon" title="Worldcon">
        Worldcon
       </a>
       for the years prior to 1953 when the Hugos were established and first awarded.)
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">
        Adaptations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Adaptations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Episode 12 of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dimension_X_(radio_program)" title="Dimension X (radio program)">
         Dimension X
        </a>
       </i>
       radio series was called
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       and was based on Heinlein's final draft of the film's shooting script. During the broadcast on June 24, 1950, the program was interrupted by a news bulletin announcing that
       <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">
        North Korea
       </a>
       had declared war on
       <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">
        South Korea
       </a>
       , marking the beginning of the
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">
        Korean War
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBC_1950_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NBC_1950-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
        Robert A. Heinlein
       </a>
       published an
       <a href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(short_story)" title="Destination Moon (short story)">
        adaptation
       </a>
       in the September 1950 issue of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Short_Stories_(magazine)" title="Short Stories (magazine)">
         Short Stories
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine.
      </p>
      <p>
       A highly condensed version of the Dimension X
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       radio play was adapted by Charles Palmer and was released by
       <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">
        Capitol Records
       </a>
       for children, who had become familiar with their recordings through a
       <a href="/wiki/Bozo_the_Clown" title="Bozo the Clown">
        Bozo the Clown
       </a>
       -approved record series. The series featured 7-inch, 78-rpm recordings and full-color booklets which children could follow as they listened to the stories. The
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       record was narrated by Tom Reddy, and Billy May composed the incidental and background music. The record's storyline took considerable liberties with the film's plot and characters, although the general shape of the film story remained.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KRW_staff_2005_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-KRW_staff_2005-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1950,
       <a href="/wiki/Fawcett_Publications" title="Fawcett Publications">
        Fawcett Publications
       </a>
       released a 10-cent
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       film tie-in
       <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">
        comic book
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">
        DC Comics
       </a>
       also published a comic book preview on the Pal film; it was the cover feature of DC's brand new science fiction
       <a href="/wiki/Anthology" title="Anthology">
        anthology
       </a>
       comic book
       <a href="/wiki/Strange_Adventures" title="Strange Adventures">
        <i>
         Strange Adventures
        </i>
        # 1 (September 1950).
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warren_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Warren-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The same year,
       <i>
        Tintin Magazine
       </i>
       released a serialization from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="The Adventures of Tintin">
         The Adventures of Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       stories titled
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       by the Belgian cartoonist
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herge" title="Herge">
        Herge
       </a>
       . It ran from 30 March 1950 to 7 September 1950.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
