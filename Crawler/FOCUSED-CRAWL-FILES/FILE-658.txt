   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Moon landing conspiracy theories in popular culture
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The notion that the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">
        Moon landings
       </a>
       were
       <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">
        hoaxes
       </a>
       perpetrated by
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       and other agencies has appeared many times in popular culture. Not all references to
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing_conspiracy_theories" title="Moon landing conspiracy theories">
        Moon landing conspiracy theories
       </a>
       are in support of them, but the ideas expressed in them have become a popular
       <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">
        meme
       </a>
       to reference, both in humor and sincerity.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Precursors_in_other_media">
        Precursors in other media
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_landing_conspiracy_theories_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Precursors in other media">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1956
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Gunn_(author)" title="James Gunn (author)">
        James E. Gunn
       </a>
       wrote a
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       story entitled "Cave of Night" in which the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">
        United States Air Force
       </a>
       fakes the first manned American
       <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">
        spaceflight
       </a>
       . When lack of funds precludes a survivable manned mission, the mission is faked to spur funding for a real space program. The Air Force launches the craft carrying a transmitter relaying prerecorded messages from the pilot. The Air Force later claims that the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        astronaut
       </a>
       died in
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       , and that his body will remain in orbit until the craft disintegrates in the atmosphere. The conspiracy is nearly exposed by a radio reporter who sees the astronaut on Earth after his supposed "death," but he is forced to destroy his evidence by the government. "Cave of Night" was adapted for radio and broadcast as an episode of the popular program
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/X_Minus_One" title="X Minus One">
         X Minus One
        </a>
       </i>
       on February 1, 1956, a full five years before
       <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">
        Yuri Gagarin
       </a>
       's first manned spaceflight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_print">
        In print
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_landing_conspiracy_theories_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In print">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">
         President
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">
         Bill Clinton
        </a>
        in his 2004 autobiography,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/My_Life_(Bill_Clinton_autobiography)" title="My Life (Bill Clinton autobiography)">
          My Life
         </a>
        </i>
        , states: "Just a month before, Apollo 11 astronauts Buzz Aldrin and Neil Armstrong had left their colleague, Michael Collins, aboard spaceship Columbia and walked on the moon...The old carpenter asked me if I really believed it happened. I said sure, I saw it on television. He disagreed; he said that he didn't believe it for a minute, that 'them television fellers' could make things look real that weren't. Back then, I thought he was a crank. During my eight years in Washington, I saw some things on TV that made me wonder if he wasn't ahead of his time."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clinton2004_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-clinton2004-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Norman_Mailer" title="Norman Mailer">
         Norman Mailer
        </a>
        in 1969 wrote: "Besides - the event [Apollo 11 moonwalk] is obdurate on the surface and a mystery beneath. It’s not at all easy to comprehend. Like an adolescent married before he can vote, and trying to react the congratulations "You’re a married man", a remark which has no reality to the brand-new groom, so America and the world were in a round of congratulations - we had landed a man on the moon. The event was so removed, however, so unreal, that no objective correlative existed to prove it had not been an event staged in a television studio—the greatest con of the century—and indeed a good mind, product of the iniquities, treacheries, gold, passions, invention, deception, and rich worldly stink of the Renaissance could hardly deny that the event if bogus was as great a creation in mass hoodwinking, deception, and legerdemain as the true ascent was in discipline and technology. Indeed, conceive of the genius of such a conspiracy. It would take criminals and confidence men mightier, more trustworthy and more resourceful than anything in this century or the ones before. Merely to conceive of such men was the surest way to know the event was not staged. Yes, the century was a giant and a cretin. Man had become a Herculean embodiment of the Vision, but the brain on the top of the head was as small as a transistorized fist, and the chambers of the heart had shrunk to the dry hard seeds of some hybrid future."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mailer1970_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-mailer1970-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_film">
        In film
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_landing_conspiracy_theories_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In film">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In the 1971
        <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">
         James Bond
        </a>
        film
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Diamonds_Are_Forever_(film)" title="Diamonds Are Forever (film)">
          Diamonds Are Forever
         </a>
         ,
        </i>
        a brief scene depicts the action taking place in a Moon setting where astronauts are being trained.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Agent 007 steals what appears to be a Moon buggy from the model set, and drives it off to escape from an enemy compound. This scene may have helped to spread the idea of the Moon landings being a hoax. However, model sets like the one in the film had been used prior to the moon landing in order to simulate conditions.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaysing2002_5-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-kaysing2002-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The 1978 film
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Capricorn_One" title="Capricorn One">
          Capricorn One
         </a>
        </i>
        portrayed a fictional NASA attempt to fake a landing on the planet
        <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
         Mars
        </a>
        , in a plot inspired by Apollo hoax theories.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In 2002,
        <a href="/wiki/William_Karel" title="William Karel">
         William Karel
        </a>
        released a
        <a href="/wiki/Mockumentary" title="Mockumentary">
         spoof documentary
        </a>
        film,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)" title="Dark Side of the Moon (film)">
          Dark Side of the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        , 'exposing' how
        <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">
         Stanley Kubrick
        </a>
        was recruited to fake the Moon landings, and featured interviews with, among others, Kubrick's widow and a number of American statesmen including
        <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">
         Henry Kissinger
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Donald_Rumsfeld" title="Donald Rumsfeld">
         Donald Rumsfeld
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        It was an elaborate joke: interviews and other footage were presented out of context and in some cases completely staged, with actors playing interviewees who had never existed—and named, in many cases, after characters from Kubrick's films; this was one of many clues included to reveal the joke to the alert viewer.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In the 2004 film
        <i>
         Man on the Moon
        </i>
        , Richard Fortunato fictionally explores the links between Apollo 11, the
        <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
         Vietnam War
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">
         Richard Nixon
        </a>
        , and a Russian spy in an effort to explain an alleged staged moon landing.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In the 2009 film
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Futurama:_Into_the_Wild_Green_Yonder" title="Futurama: Into the Wild Green Yonder">
          Futurama: Into the Wild Green Yonder
         </a>
        </i>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fry_(Futurama)" title="Fry (Futurama)">
         Fry
        </a>
        reads Richard Nixon's mind and learns that the president staged the Moon landing, but it took place on the planet
        <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
         Venus
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The 2012 documentary
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Room_237" title="Room 237">
          Room 237
         </a>
        </i>
        featured
        <a href="/wiki/Film_analysis" title="Film analysis">
         film analysis
        </a>
        by fans of Stanley Kubrick's 1980 film
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Shining_(film)" title="The Shining (film)">
          The Shining
         </a>
        </i>
        , connecting Kubrick's film with the
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
         Apollo 11
        </a>
        Moon landing and other major events.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
        (See 2012 video by Michael Wysmierski below.)
       </li>
       <li>
        In the 2014 film
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_(film)" title="Interstellar (film)">
          Interstellar
         </a>
        </i>
        , the US Government deliberately promotes Moon landing conspiracy theories to discredit
        <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
         NASA
        </a>
        and encourage people to seek Earth-based solutions to an ongoing global famine; this is an example of a
        <a href="/wiki/Post-truth_politics" title="Post-truth politics">
         post-truth
        </a>
        society.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the 2015 comedy film
        <a href="/wiki/Moonwalkers_(film)" title="Moonwalkers (film)">
         Moonwalkers
        </a>
        , a CIA agent tries to hire Stanley Kubrick for the Apollo 11 moon landing scene.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The 2016 film
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Operation_Avalanche_(film)" title="Operation Avalanche (film)">
          Operation Avalanche
         </a>
        </i>
        is about CIA agents who, when they find NASA is incapable of meeting the 1969 deadline for the Apollo 11 moon landing, simulate the landing with a film crew.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="On_television">
        On television
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [

        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_landing_conspiracy_theories_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: On television">

      <ul>
       <li>
        In the

        episode "
        <a href="/wiki/Roswell_That_Ends_Well" title="Roswell That Ends Well">

       </li>

         <a href="/wiki/MythBusters" title="MythBusters">
