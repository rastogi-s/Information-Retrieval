      <p>
       <b>
        Lunar pareidolia
       </b>
       refers to the
       <a href="/wiki/Pareidolia" title="Pareidolia">
        pareidolic
       </a>
       images seen by humans on the face of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . The Moon's surface is a complex mixture of dark areas (the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        lunar
        <i>
         maria
        </i>
       </a>
       , or "seas") and lighter areas (the highlands). Many cultures have seen shapes in these dark and light areas that have reminded them of people, animals or objects, often related to their
       <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">
        folklore
       </a>
       ; the most famous are the
       <a href="/wiki/Man_in_the_Moon" title="Man in the Moon">
        Man in the Moon
       </a>
       in Western folklore and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_Rabbit" title="Moon Rabbit">
        Moon Rabbit
       </a>
       of Asia and the Americas. Other cultures perceive the silhouette of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chang%27e_(mythology)" title="Chang'e (mythology)">
        woman
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">
        frog
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Moose" title="Moose">
        moose
       </a>
       , a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Bison" title="American Bison">
        buffalo
       </a>
       , or a
       <a href="/wiki/Dragon" title="Dragon">
        dragon
       </a>
       (with its head and mouth to the right and body and wings to the left) in the full moon. Alternatively, the vague shape of the overall dark and light regions can resemble a
       <a href="/wiki/Taijitu" title="Taijitu">
        Yin Yang symbol
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Man_in_the_Moon">
        The Man in the Moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Man in the Moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Man_in_the_Moon" title="Man in the Moon">
        Man in the Moon
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Man_in_the_Moon_with_key.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="594" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d4/Man_in_the_Moon_with_key.jpg/200px-Man_in_the_Moon_with_key.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d4/Man_in_the_Moon_with_key.jpg/300px-Man_in_the_Moon_with_key.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d4/Man_in_the_Moon_with_key.jpg/400px-Man_in_the_Moon_with_key.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Man_in_the_Moon_with_key.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         One possible interpretation of the Man in the Moon as seen from the Northern Hemisphere
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Man in the Moon is an imaginary figure resembling a human face, head or body, that observers from some cultural backgrounds typically perceive in the bright disc of the
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Rabbit.2Fhare">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rabbit/hare">
        Rabbit/hare
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rabbit/hare">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_rabbit" title="Moon rabbit">
        Moon rabbit
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rabbit_in_the_moon_standing_by_pot.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="500" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Rabbit_in_the_moon_standing_by_pot.png/150px-Rabbit_in_the_moon_standing_by_pot.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Rabbit_in_the_moon_standing_by_pot.png/225px-Rabbit_in_the_moon_standing_by_pot.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Rabbit_in_the_moon_standing_by_pot.png/300px-Rabbit_in_the_moon_standing_by_pot.png 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rabbit_in_the_moon_standing_by_pot.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Rabbit with a pot – making medicine,
         <a href="/wiki/Mochi" title="Mochi">
          mochi
         </a>
         , or
         <a href="/wiki/Tteok" title="Tteok">
          tteok
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culture_of_China" title="Culture of China">
        Chinese culture
       </a>
       , the rabbit in the moon (a companion of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chang%27e_(mythology)" title="Chang'e (mythology)">
        Chang'e
       </a>
       ) is pounding medicine. Similarly, in
       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
        Japan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">
        Korea
       </a>
       , popular culture sees a rabbit making
       <a href="/wiki/Mochi" title="Mochi">
        mochi
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tteok" title="Tteok">
        tteok
       </a>
       , respectively, in the moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">
        Pre-Columbian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">
        Mesoamerica
       </a>
       the rabbit is often associated with the moon, for example,
       <a href="/wiki/Tecciztecatl" title="Tecciztecatl">
        Tecciztecatl
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">
        Aztec
       </a>
       moon god, was pictured as an anthropomorphic rabbit; there is also a myth involving
       <a href="/wiki/Quetzalcoatl" title="Quetzalcoatl">
        Quetzalcoatl
       </a>
       and the moon rabbit.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Female_figures">
        Female figures
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Female figures">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabethan_England" title="Elizabethan England">
        Elizabethan England
       </a>
       , the spots of the Moon were supposed to represent a witch carrying sticks of wood on her back, or an old man with a lantern (which was illustrated by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">
        Shakespeare
       </a>
       in his comedy
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night's Dream">
         A Midsummer Night's Dream
        </a>
       </i>
       ). A more recent Western image is the profile of a coiffed woman wearing a jeweled pendant, the jewel being the crater
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tycho_(crater)" title="Tycho (crater)">
        Tycho
       </a>
       , which at full moon is very bright and has bright radiating lines (
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_system" title="Ray system">
        rays
       </a>
       ). In New Zealand
       <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_mythology" title="Māori mythology">
        Māori legend
       </a>
       , the moon shows a woman with a local tree, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Myoporum_laetum#Maori_legend" title="Myoporum laetum">
         Ngaio
        </a>
       </i>
       . However, throughout
       <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">
        Melanesia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">
        Polynesia
       </a>
       , the moon is seen to be a cook over a three-stone fire.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Mythology" title="Chinese Mythology">
        Chinese Mythology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e" title="Chang'e">
        Chang'e
       </a>
       (various spellings) lives on the moon. She was mentioned in the conversation between Houston Capcom and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       crew just before the first moon landing:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Space_Center" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center">
         Houston
        </a>
        : Among the large headlines concerning Apollo this morning there's one asking that you watch for a lovely girl with a big rabbit. An ancient legend says a beautiful Chinese girl called
        <i>
         Chang-o
        </i>
        has been living there for 4000 years. It seems she was banished to the moon because she stole the pill for immortality from her husband. You might also look for her companion, a large Chinese rabbit, who is easy to spot since he is only standing on his hind feet in the shade of a cinnamon tree. The name of the rabbit is not recorded.
        <br/>
        <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
         Aldrin
        </a>
        : Okay, we'll keep a close eye for the bunny girl.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         NASA Capcom and Apollo 11 crew, NASA
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
          <a href="#cite_note-2">
           [2]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Toad">
        Toad
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Toad">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       "The toad was seen by Chinese Taoists as ... the moon, representing Chang-O, the transformed wife of Yi, the excellent archer. ... According to Robert M. DeGraaff, in some representations of the moon-toad, the
       <i>
        ling chih
       </i>
       fungus is shown growing out of the creature's forehead."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ali">
        Ali
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ali">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shia_Muslims" title="Shia Muslims">
        Shia Muslims
       </a>
       believe that the name of
       <a href="/wiki/Ali" title="Ali">
        Ali
       </a>
       Ibne-Abi Talib (
       <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">
        Muhammad
       </a>
       's son in law) is written on the moon. This interpretation has roots in several
       <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">
        hadith
       </a>
       by Muhammad where he compares Ali to the moon and himself to the sun. There are also other esoteric interpretations of this analogy in Islamic philosophy.
      </p>
