   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

         <a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Calendario_Lunar_2017.png" title="commons:File:Calendario Lunar 2017.png">
          lunar calendar
         </a>
         for 2017

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        lunar calendar
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">
        calendar
       </a>
       based upon the
       <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">
        monthly
       </a>
       cycles of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        phases
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Synodic_month" title="Lunar month">
        synodic months
       </a>
       ), in contrast to
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">
        solar calendars
       </a>
       , whose annual cycles are based only directly upon the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_year" title="Solar year">
        solar year
       </a>
       . The most commonly used calendar, the
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian calendar
       </a>
       , is a solar calendar system that originally evolved out of a lunar calendar system. A purely lunar calendar is also distinguished from a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">
        lunisolar calendar
       </a>
       , whose
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">
        lunar months
       </a>
       are brought into alignment with the solar year through some process of
       <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)" title="Intercalation (timekeeping)">
        intercalation
       </a>
       . The details of when months begin varies from calendar to calendar, with some using
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Crescent" title="Crescent">
        crescent
       </a>
       moons and others employing detailed calculations.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since each
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">
        lunation
       </a>
       is approximately ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        29
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </span>
       days (29 days, 12 hours, 44 minutes, 3 seconds, or 29.530588 days), it is common for the months of a lunar calendar to alternate between 29 and 30 days. Since the period of twelve such lunations, a
       <b>
        lunar year
       </b>
       , is only 354 days, 8 hours, 48 minutes, 34 seconds (354.367056 days), purely lunar calendars lose around 11 days per year relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian calendar
       </a>
       . In purely lunar calendars like the
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
        Islamic calendar
       </a>
       , the lack of intercalation causes the lunar months to cycle through all the seasons of the Gregorian year over the course of a
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Islamic_years#Modern" title="List of Islamic years">
        33 lunar-year cycle
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian calendar
       </a>
       is in common and legal use in most countries, traditional lunar and lunisolar calendars continue to be used throughout the
       <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">
        Old World
       </a>
       to determine
       <a href="/wiki/Religious_festival" title="Religious festival">
        religious festivals
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/National_holiday" title="National holiday">
        national holidays
       </a>
       . Such holidays include
       <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">
        Ramadan
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
        Islamic calendar
       </a>
       ); the
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">
        Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_New_Year" title="Japanese New Year">
        Japanese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_New_Year" title="Korean New Year">
        Korean
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vietnamese_New_Year" title="Vietnamese New Year">
        Vietnamese
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongolian_New_Year" title="Mongolian New Year">
        Mongolian New Year
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">
        Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_calendar" title="Japanese calendar">
        Japanese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_calendar" title="Korean calendar">
        Korean
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_calendar" title="Vietnamese calendar">
        Vietnamese
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_calendar" title="Mongolian calendar">
        Mongolian calendars
       </a>
       ); the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nepali_New_Year" title="Nepali New Year">
        Nepali New Year
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nepali_calendar" title="Nepali calendar">
        Nepali calendar
       </a>
       ); the
       <a href="/wiki/Mid-Autumn_Festival" title="Mid-Autumn Festival">
        Mid-Autumn Festival
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Chuseok" title="Chuseok">
        Chuseok
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">
        Chinese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_calendar" title="Korean calendar">
        Korean calendars
       </a>
       );
       <a href="/wiki/Loi_Krathong" title="Loi Krathong">
        Loi Krathong
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Thai_lunar_calendar" title="Thai lunar calendar">
        Thai calendar
       </a>
       );
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunuwar" title="Sunuwar">
        Sunuwar
       </a>
       calendar;
       <a href="/wiki/Diwali" title="Diwali">
        Diwali
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">
        Hindu calendars
       </a>
       ); and
       <a href="/wiki/Rosh_Hashanah" title="Rosh Hashanah">
        Rosh Hashanah
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">
        Hebrew calendar
       </a>
       ).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The earliest known lunar calendar was found at
       <a href="/wiki/Warren_Field" title="Warren Field">
        Warren Field
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
        Scotland
       </a>
       and has been dated to
       <abbr title="circa">
        c.
       </abbr>
       <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
        8000
       </span>
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         BC
        </span>
       </span>
       , during its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesolithic_Scotland" title="Mesolithic Scotland">
        Mesolithic period
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some scholars argue for lunar calendars still further back—
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Rappenglueck&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Michael Rappenglueck (page does not exist)">
        Rappenglück
       </a>
       in the marks on a
       <abbr title="circa">
        c.
       </abbr>
       <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
        17,000
       </span>
       year-old
       <a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">
        cave painting
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">
        Lascaux
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Marshack" title="Alexander Marshack">
        Marshack
       </a>
       in the marks on a
       <abbr title="circa">
        c.
       </abbr>
       <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
        27,000
       </span>
       year-old bone baton—but their findings remain controversial.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunisolar_calendars">
        Lunisolar calendars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lunisolar calendars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">
        Lunisolar calendar
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Most calendars referred to as "lunar" calendars are in fact
       <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">
        lunisolar calendars
       </a>
       . Their months are based on observations of the lunar cycle, with
       <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)" title="Intercalation (timekeeping)">
        intercalation
       </a>
       being used to bring them into general agreement with the solar year. The solar "
       <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_calendar" title="Egyptian calendar">
        civic calendar
       </a>
       " that was used in
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">
        ancient Egypt
       </a>
       showed traces of its origin in the earlier lunar calendar, which continued to be used alongside it for religious and agricultural purposes. Present-day lunisolar calendars include the
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">
        Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">
        Hindu
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Thai_lunar_calendar" title="Thai lunar calendar">
        Thai
       </a>
       calendars.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_month" title="Synodic month">
        Synodic months
       </a>
       are 29 or 30 days in length, making a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_year" title="Lunar year">
        lunar year
       </a>
       of 12 months about 11 days shorter than a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_year" title="Solar year">
        solar year
       </a>
       . Some lunar calendars do not use intercalation, such as most
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
        Islamic calendars
       </a>
       . For those that do, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">
        Hebrew calendar
       </a>
       , the most common form of intercalation is to add an additional month every second or third year. Some lunisolar calendars are also calibrated by annual natural events which are affected by lunar cycles as well as the solar cycle. An example of this is the lunar calendar of the
       <a href="/wiki/Banks_Islands" title="Banks Islands">
        Banks Islands
       </a>
       , which includes three months in which the edible
       <a href="/wiki/Palolo_worm" title="Palolo worm">
        palolo worm
       </a>
       mass on the beaches. These events occur at the last quarter of the lunar month, as the reproductive cycle of the palolos is synchronized with the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Start_of_the_lunar_month">
        Start of the lunar month
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Start of the lunar month">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lunar and lunisolar calendars differ as to which day is the first day of the month. In some lunisolar calendars, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">
        Chinese calendar
       </a>
       , the first day of a month is the day when an astronomical
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       occurs in a particular time zone. In others, such as some
       <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">
        Hindu calendars
       </a>
       , each month begins on the day after the full moon or the new moon. Others were based in the past on the first sighting of a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        lunar crescent
       </a>
       , such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">
        Hebrew calendar
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hijri_calendar" title="Hijri calendar">
        Hijri calendar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Length_of_the_lunar_month">
        Length of the lunar month
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Length of the lunar month">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The length of each lunar cycle varies slightly from the average value. In addition, observations are subject to uncertainty and weather conditions. Thus to avoid uncertainty about the calendar, there have been attempts to create fixed arithmetical rules to determine the start of each calendar month.
      </p>
      <p>
       The average length of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_month" title="Synodic month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       is 29.530589 days. Thus it is convenient if months generally alternate between 29 and 30 days (sometimes termed respectively “
       <i>
        hollow
       </i>
       ” and “
       <i>
        full
       </i>
       ”). The distribution of
       <i>
        hollow
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        full
       </i>
       months can be determined using
       <a href="/wiki/Continued_fraction" title="Continued fraction">
        continued fractions
       </a>
       , and examining successive approximations for the length of the month in terms of fractions of a day. In the list below, after the number of days listed in the numerator, an integer number of months as listed in the denominator have been completed:

      <p>
       These fractions can be used to construct a lunar calendar, or in combination with a solar calendar to produce a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">
        lunisolar calendar
       </a>
       . A 49 month cycle was proposed as the basis of an alternative
       <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">
        Easter computation
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Isaac Newton
       </a>
       around 1700.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Tabular_Islamic_calendar" title="Tabular Islamic calendar">
        tabular Islamic calendar
       </a>
       's 360 month cycle is equivalent to 24×15 months, minus a correction of one day.
      </p>
