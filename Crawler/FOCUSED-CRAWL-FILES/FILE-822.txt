    Lunar distance (navigation)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

         <b>
          Finding Greenwich time while at sea using a lunar distance
         </b>
         . The lunar distance is the angle between the Moon and a star (or the Sun). The altitudes of the two bodies are used to make corrections and determine the time.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_navigation" title="Celestial navigation">
        celestial navigation
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        lunar distance
       </b>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_navigation#Angular_measurement" title="Celestial navigation">
        angle
       </a>
       between the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and another
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">
        celestial body
       </a>
       . The
       <b>
        lunar distances method
       </b>
       uses this angle, also called a
       <b>
        lunar
       </b>
       , and a
       <a href="/wiki/Nautical_almanac" title="Nautical almanac">
        nautical almanac
       </a>
       to calculate
       <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Mean_Time" title="Greenwich Mean Time">
        Greenwich time
       </a>
       . By comparing that calculated time to the measured local time, the
       <a href="/wiki/Navigator" title="Navigator">
        navigator
       </a>
       can determine
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       . The method was published in 1763 and used until about 1850 when it was superseded by the
       <a href="/wiki/Marine_chronometer" title="Marine chronometer">
        marine chronometer
       </a>
       . A similar method uses the positions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Galilean_moons" title="Galilean moons">
        Galilean moons
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">
        Purpose
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(navigation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purpose">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_navigation" title="Celestial navigation">
        celestial navigation
       </a>
       , knowledge of the time at
       <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">
        Greenwich
       </a>
       and the measured positions of one or more
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_object" title="Celestial object">
        celestial objects
       </a>
       allows the navigator to calculate
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_222_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_222-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Reliable
       <a href="/wiki/Marine_chronometer" title="Marine chronometer">
        marine chronometers
       </a>
       were unavailable until the late 18th century and not affordable until the 19th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_221_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_221-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_1851_pg_195_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Taylor_1851_pg_195-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watchmakers_and_their_Work,_pg_230_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Watchmakers_and_their_Work,_pg_230-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After the method was first published in 1763 by British astronomer royal
       <a href="/wiki/Nevil_Maskelyne" title="Nevil Maskelyne">
        Nevil Maskelyne
       </a>
       , for about a hundred years (until about 1850)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       mariners lacking a chronometer used the method of lunar distances to determine Greenwich time as a key step in determining longitude. Conversely, a mariner with a chronometer could check its accuracy using a lunar determination of Greenwich time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_221_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_221-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The method saw usage all the way up to the beginning of the 20th century on smaller vessels that could not afford a chronometer or had to rely on the this technique for correction of the chronometer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowditch_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bowditch-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Method">
        Method
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(navigation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Method">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">
        Summary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(navigation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Summary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The method relies on the relatively quick movement of the moon across the background sky, completing a circuit of 360 degrees in 27.3 days (the sidereal month), or 13.2 degrees per day. In an hour then, it will move about half a degree,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_222_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_222-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       roughly its own
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        diameter
       </a>
       , with respect to the background stars and the Sun. Using a
       <a href="/wiki/Sextant" title="Sextant">
        sextant
       </a>
       , the navigator precisely measures the angle between the moon and another
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">
        body
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_222_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_222-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That could be the Sun or one of a selected group of bright stars lying close to the Moon's path, near the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Regulus" title="Regulus">
        Regulus
       </a>
       was particularly commonly used. At that moment, anyone on the surface of the earth who can see the same two bodies will observe the same angle (after correcting for
       <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">
        parallax
       </a>
       error). The navigator then consults a prepared table of lunar distances and the times at which they will occur.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_222_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_222-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sources_of_lunar_distance_tables_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sources_of_lunar_distance_tables-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By comparing the corrected lunar distance with the tabulated values, the navigator finds the Greenwich time for that observation. Knowing Greenwich time and local time, the navigator can work out longitude.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_222_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_222-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Local time can be determined from a sextant observation of the altitude of the Sun or a star.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_226_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_226-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_230_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_230-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Then the longitude (relative to Greenwich) is readily calculated from the difference between local time and Greenwich Time, at 15 degrees per hour.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_practice">
        In practice
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(navigation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In practice">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Having measured the lunar distance and the heights of the two bodies, the navigator can find Greenwich time in three steps.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Step one – Preliminaries
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Almanac tables predict lunar distances between the centre of the Moon and the other body (published between 1767 and 1906 in Britain).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
        However, the observer cannot accurately find the centre of the Moon (or Sun, which was the most frequently used second object). Instead, lunar distances are always measured to the sharply lit, outer edge ("limb") of the Moon (or of the Sun). The first correction to the lunar distance is the distance between the limb of the Moon and its center. Since the Moon's apparent size varies with its varying distance from the Earth, almanacs give the Moon's and Sun's
        <b>
         semidiameter
        </b>
        for each day.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutton_12-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Dutton-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Additionally the observed altitudes are cleared of dip and semidiameter.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Step two – Clearing
       </dt>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Clearing
        </b>
        the lunar distance means correcting for the effects of
        <a href="/wiki/Parallax#Lunar_parallax" title="Parallax">
         parallax
        </a>
        and atmospheric refraction on the observation. The almanac gives lunar distances as they would appear if the observer were at the center of a transparent Earth. Because the Moon is so much closer to the Earth than the stars are, the position of the observer on the surface of the Earth shifts the relative position of the Moon by up to an entire degree.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
         <a href="#cite_note-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The clearing correction for parallax and refraction is a relatively simple trigonometric function of the observed lunar distance and the altitudes of the two bodies.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Navigators used collections of mathematical tables to work these calculations by any of dozens of distinct clearing methods.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Step three – Finding the time
       </dt>
       <dd>
        The navigator, having cleared the lunar distance, now consults a prepared table of lunar distances and the times at which they will occur in order to determine the Greenwich time of the observation.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_222_1-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_222-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sources_of_lunar_distance_tables_7-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-sources_of_lunar_distance_tables-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        These tables were the high tech wonder of their day. Predicting the position of the moon years in advance requires solving the
        <a href="/wiki/Three-body_problem" title="Three-body problem">
         three-body problem
        </a>
        , since the earth, moon and sun were all involved.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euler" title="Euler">
         Euler
        </a>
        developed the numerical method they used, called
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euler%27s_method" title="Euler's method">
         Euler's method
        </a>
        , and received a grant from the
        <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Longitude" title="Board of Longitude">
         Board of Longitude
        </a>
        to carry out the computations.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Having found the (absolute) Greenwich time, the navigator either compares it with the observed local apparent time (a separate observation) to find his longitude, or compares it with the Greenwich time on a chronometer (if available) if one wants to check the chronometer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_222_1-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_222-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Errors">
        Errors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(navigation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Errors">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Almanac_error">
        Almanac error
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(navigation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Almanac error">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the early days of lunars, predictions of the Moon's position were good to approximately half an arc-minute
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       , a source of error of up to approximately 1 minute in Greenwich time, or one quarter degree of longitude. By 1810, the errors in the almanac predictions had been reduced to about one-quarter of a minute of arc. By about 1860 (after lunar distance observations had mostly faded into history), the almanac errors were finally reduced to less than the error margin of a sextant in ideal conditions (one-tenth of a minute of arc).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_distance_observation">
        Lunar distance observation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(navigation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lunar distance observation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The best
       <a href="/wiki/Sextant" title="Sextant">
        sextants
       </a>
       at the very beginning of the lunar distance era could indicate angle to one-sixth of an arc-minute
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       and later sextants (after c. 1800) to 0.1 arc-minutes, after the use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Vernier_scale" title="Vernier scale">
        vernier
       </a>
       was popularized by its description in English in the book
       <i>
        Navigatio Britannica
       </i>
       published in 1750 by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Barrow_(historian)" title="John Barrow (historian)">
        John Barrow
       </a>
       , the mathematician and historian. In practice at sea, actual errors were somewhat larger. Experienced observers can typically measure lunar distances to within one-quarter of a minute of arc under favourable conditions,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       introducing an error of up to one quarter degree in longitude. If the sky is cloudy or the Moon is "New" (hidden close to the glare of the Sun), lunar distance observations could not be performed.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Total_error">
        Total error
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(navigation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Total error">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A lunar distance changes with time at a rate of roughly half a degree, or 30 arc-minutes, in an hour.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norie_1828_pg_222_1-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Norie_1828_pg_222-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The two sources of error, combined, typically amount to about one-half arc-minute in Lunar distance, equivalent to one minute in Greenwich time, which corresponds to an error of as much as one-quarter of a degree of Longitude, or about 15 nautical miles (28 km) at the equator.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">
        In literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(navigation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Captain
       <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Slocum" title="Joshua Slocum">
        Joshua Slocum
       </a>
       , in making the first solo
       <a href="/wiki/Circumnavigation" title="Circumnavigation">
        circumnavigation
       </a>
       in 1895–1898, somewhat anachronistically used the lunar method along with
       <a href="/wiki/Dead_reckoning" title="Dead reckoning">
        dead reckoning
       </a>
       in his
       <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">
        navigation
       </a>
       . He comments in
       <a href="/wiki/Sailing_Alone_Around_the_World" title="Sailing Alone Around the World">
        Sailing Alone Around the World
       </a>
       on a sight taken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">
        South Pacific
       </a>
       . After correcting an error he found in his
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Log_tables" title="Log tables">
        log tables
       </a>
       , the result was surprisingly accurate:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slocam_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-slocam-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        I found from the result of three observations, after long wrestling with lunar tables, that her longitude agreed within five miles of that by dead-reckoning. This was wonderful; both, however, might be in error, but somehow I felt confident that both were nearly true, and that in a few hours more I should see land; and so it happened, for then I made out the island of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nukahiva" title="Nukahiva">
         Nukahiva
        </a>
        , the southernmost of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marquesas" title="Marquesas">
         Marquesas
        </a>
        group, clear-cut and lofty. The verified longitude when abreast was somewhere between the two reckonings; this was extraordinary. All navigators will tell you that from one day to another a ship may lose or gain more than five miles in her sailing-account, and again, in the matter of lunars, even expert lunarians are considered as doing clever work when they average within eight miles of the truth...
       </p>
       <p>
        The result of these observations naturally tickled my vanity, for I knew it was something to stand on a great ship’s deck and with two assistants take lunar observations approximately near the truth. As one of the poorest of American sailors, I was proud of the little achievement alone on the sloop, even by chance though it may have been...
       </p>
       <p>
        The work of the lunarian, though seldom practised in these days of chronometers, is beautifully edifying, and there is nothing in the realm of navigation that lifts one’s heart up more in adoration.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
