      <p>
       <b>
        Elara
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">
            ɛ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">
            ər
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          EL
         </span>
         -ər-ə
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Ελάρα
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dillon_Perrine" title="Charles Dillon Perrine">
        Charles Dillon Perrine
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">
        Lick Observatory
       </a>
       in 1905.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvard_1905_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvard_1905-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perrine_1905_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Perrine_1905-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is the eighth largest moon of Jupiter and is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Elara_(mythology)" title="Elara (mythology)">
        Elara
       </a>
       , one of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       's lovers and the mother of the giant
       <a href="/wiki/Tityos" title="Tityos">
        Tityos
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_2846_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_2846-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Elara did not receive its present name until 1975; before then, it was simply known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter VII
        </span>
       </b>
       . It was sometimes called "
       <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">
        Hera
       </a>
       "
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaposchkin_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gaposchkin-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       between 1955 and 1975. It has a mean radius of just 43 km, thus it is 2% of the size of
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       . However it is half the size of
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_(moon)" title="Himalia (moon)">
        Himalia
       </a>
       , so it is the second biggest moon in the
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_group" title="Himalia group">
        Himalia group
       </a>
       . It might be a captured type C or D asteroid, for it reflects very little light.
      </p>
      <p>
       Elara belongs to the Himalia group, five moons orbiting between 11 and 13 Gm from Jupiter at an inclination of about 27.5°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_2000_3-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson_2000-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its orbital elements are as of January 2000. They are continuously changing due to
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       and planetary perturbations.
      </p>

      </p>

       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In February and March 2007, the
       <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
        New Horizons
       </a>
       spacecraft to
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       captured Elara in several LORRI images from a distance of five million miles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
