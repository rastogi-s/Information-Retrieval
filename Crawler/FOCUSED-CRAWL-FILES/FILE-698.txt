      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Moonbase 3
        </b>
       </i>
       is a British
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       programme that ran for six episodes in 1973.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was a co-production between the
       <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
        BBC
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">
        20th Century Fox
       </a>
       and the American
       <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">
        ABC network
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#320_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#320-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Created by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">
         Doctor Who
        </a>
       </i>
       producer
       <a href="/wiki/Barry_Letts" title="Barry Letts">
        Barry Letts
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Script_editor" title="Script editor">
        script editor
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Terrance_Dicks" title="Terrance Dicks">
        Terrance Dicks
       </a>
       as a realistic alternative strand of TV science-fiction,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it was not a commercial or critical success (Dicks himself has stated in a foreword to a collection of
       <a href="/wiki/Tom_Baker" title="Tom Baker">
        Tom Baker
       </a>
       -era
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       scripts that they "overdid the grimness and forgot about the sense of wonder that science fiction is all about").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It starred
       <a href="/wiki/Donald_Houston" title="Donald Houston">
        Donald Houston
       </a>
       as Director David Caulder, who is appointed to the position after the previous director was killed while returning to Earth.
       <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Bates" title="Ralph Bates">
        Ralph Bates
       </a>
       was Michel Lebrun, the Deputy Director, who was concerned about keeping to the rules.
       <a href="/wiki/Fiona_Gaunt" title="Fiona Gaunt">
        Fiona Gaunt
       </a>
       played Doctor Helen Smith, the base's
       <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">
        psychiatrist
       </a>
       , and Barry Lowe played Tom Hill, the head of the base technical section.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The programme was notable for its combination of realistic
       <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">
        spaceflight
       </a>
       procedures, ensured by hiring BBC technical adviser
       <a href="/wiki/James_Burke_(science_historian)" title="James Burke (science historian)">
        James Burke
       </a>
       , and its strong character-based writing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although very dated in terms of its looks and assumptions about the future, it remains well regarded in retrospect.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mausoleum_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mausoleum-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Concept_and_setting">
        Concept and setting
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonbase_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concept and setting">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Moonbase 3
       </i>
       was set in the year 2003 – some 30 years into the future at time of broadcast – and dramatised life in the enclosed environment of the titular
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_moon" title="Colonization of the moon">
        moonbase
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Five world powers have colonised the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       : America (Moonbase 1, commanded by Bill Jackson), Russia (Moonbase 2), Europe (Moonbase 3), China (Moonbase 4, commanded by General Cheng
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#2_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#2-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       (Moonbase 5).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#1_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#1-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The European Moonbase 3 has been in existence for 8 years at the time the series starts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#1_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#1-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       With oversight provided by the European Space Assembly and the European Aeronautics and Space Administration, Moonbase 3 is a shoestring operation when compared with the Russian and American efforts and much of base director David Caulder's job is to stave off budget cuts or a complete shutdown in the face of sceptical bureaucrats.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Alongside technical problems such as stranded astronauts,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#5_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#5-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       explosive decompressions
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#2_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#2-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and failed experiments,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#3_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#3-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#4_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#4-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the inhabitants of the moonbase must also deal with psychological problems arising from the cramped, dangerous environment they live in.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_1-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In "Departure and Arrival", a mental breakdown suffered by a shuttle pilot has tragic consequences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#1_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#1-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "Achilles Heel" and "Outsiders" deal with the fallout from crew members' difficulty with living up to the standards they have set for themselves.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#3_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#3-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#4_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#4-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "Behemoth" and "View of a Dead Planet" deal with forms of mass hysteria.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#2_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#2-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#6_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-M3#6-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <dl>
       <dt>
        Dr David Caulder (
        <a href="/wiki/Donald_Houston" title="Donald Houston">
         Donald Houston
        </a>
        )
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Appointed Director of Moonbase 3 following the death of his predecessor, David Caulder is a scientist, academic and administrator.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#1_6-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#1-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        A lecturer at the
        <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">
         University of Oxford
        </a>
        , he rose to prominence when he was appointed
        <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_(education)" title="Chancellor (education)">
         Chancellor
        </a>
        of the University during a period of student unrest and managed to calm the situation down, earning him a reputation as a "Welsh wizard".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#1_6-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#1-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The Director-General of the European space effort describes Caulder as a "militant liberal, passionately committed to the middle of the road".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#1_6-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#1-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Caulder sees himself as a buffer, who deals with the political aspects of running the moonbase, giving the others the room to perform the vital tasks of keeping Moonbase 3 operations going.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#5_7-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#5-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Dr Michel Lebrun (
        <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Bates" title="Ralph Bates">
         Ralph Bates
        </a>
        )
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Deputy Director Michel Lebrun is noted for his rigid adherence to the rules and procedures, making him a somewhat inflexible individual who has difficulty understanding the human dimension involved when dealing with other base personnel.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#3_8-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#3-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
         Frenchman
        </a>
        is slightly resentful of Caulder's appointment to the position of Director, feeling that he has been overlooked for the job though Helen Smith reminds him that he is, perhaps, too young to take up the position at this time.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#1_6-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#1-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        However, when he is ordered to relieve Caulder of command during the attempted rescue of Tom Hill in the episode "Castor and Pollux", he remains loyal to Caulder and carries out the rescue as planned despite his misgivings about the risks.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#5_7-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#5-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>

        <a href="/wiki/Fiona_Gaunt" title="Fiona Gaunt">
         Fiona Gaunt
        </a>

       </dt>
       <dd>
        Helen Smith is the base psychologist responsible for preparing regular psychological record reports (or PSIs), assessing the mental state of all personnel.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#1_6-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#1-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        An expert in behavioural studies in an alien environment – her
        <a href="/wiki/Thesis" title="Thesis">
         thesis
        </a>
        was titled "Human beings adjust" – she acts as a sounding board, enabling individual crew members to adapt to the situation of life on the Moon.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#3_8-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#3-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        However, she develops a trend of engaging in romantic relationships with her most troubled patients as seen in "Achilles Heel" and "Outsiders".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#3_8-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#3-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#4_9-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#4-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>

       <dt>
        Tom Hill (Barry Lowe)
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Forty-two-year-old Tom Hill is director of operations on the moonbase responsible for flight control and base maintenance.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#2_5-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#2-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>

         <a href="#cite_note-M3#1-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        An experienced astronaut, he became a minor celebrity six years before the events of the series take place when he rescued an escaped cat from a spacelab.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#1_6-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-M3#1-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        A down-to-earth no-nonsense type, who dislikes having his work interfered with by management, he generally tries to maintain an informal, casual air, calling all female staff as "love" and calling Lebrun "Mickey boy", a moniker Lebrun hates.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3#1_6-10">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
        Production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonbase_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonbase_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Terrance Dicks and Barry Letts had been working together, as script editor and producer respectively, on
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       since 1970. Dicks had begun his television writing career on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Avengers_(TV_series)" title="The Avengers (TV series)">
         The Avengers
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crossroads_(soap_opera)" title="Crossroads (soap opera)">
         Crossroads
        </a>
       </i>
       before joining
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       as its script editor in 1968.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sixties_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sixties-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Former actor Barry Letts had changed career into television direction in 1967 and had worked on series such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Z-Cars" title="Z-Cars">
         Z-Cars
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Newcomers_(UK)" title="The Newcomers (UK)">
         The Newcomers
        </a>
       </i>
       before being asked to take over as producer of
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       in 1970, where he first met Dicks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seventies_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Seventies-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1972, looking to move on from
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       , the pair started considering a number of ideas for other shows they could collaborate on.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWSE#2_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWSE#2-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Their first idea arose from the successful collaboration they had with the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">
        Royal Navy
       </a>
       on the
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       serial
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sea_Devils" title="The Sea Devils">
         The Sea Devils
        </a>
       </i>
       which led to them considering pitching a serial set on a
       <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">
        frigate
       </a>
       only to find they were too late – the BBC had just commissioned a series,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Warship_(TV_series)" title="Warship (TV series)">
         Warship
        </a>
       </i>
       , with that very premise.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In late 1972, they developed the concept of
       <i>
        Moonbase 3
       </i>
       , as an anthology series set on a lunar colony that would "provide intelligent, realistic drama rather than Science Fantasy",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVZ#20_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TVZ#20-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and submitted a pilot script, titled "Departure and Arrival", to
       <a href="/wiki/Shaun_Sutton" title="Shaun Sutton">
        Shaun Sutton
       </a>
       , the Head of Drama at the BBC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#246_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#246-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The series was formally commissioned in December 1972 and would be made, as a co-production between the BBC and 20th Century Fox and the ABC network in America, during the break in production between Seasons 10 and 11 of
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#320_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#320-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#246_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#246-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production_2">
        Production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonbase_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The first
       <i>
        Moonbase 3
       </i>
       script to be formally commissioned was "View of a Dead Planet" on 15 December 1972. This was written by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arden_Winch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arden Winch (page does not exist)">
        Arden Winch
       </a>
       , a respected writer who had written for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Wednesday_Play" title="The Wednesday Play">
         The Wednesday Play
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#320_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#320-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Two scripts, "Behemoth" and "Outsiders", were commissioned from
       <a href="/wiki/John_Brason" title="John Brason">
        John Brason
       </a>
       , who had written for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Colditz_(TV_series)" title="Colditz (TV series)">
         Colditz
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Special_Branch_(TV_series)" title="Special Branch (TV series)">
         Special Branch
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The final two scripts, "The Dark Side of the Moon" and "The Gentle Rain" (later renamed "Castor and Pollux" and "Achilles Heel" respectively), were commissioned from
       <a href="/wiki/John_Lucarotti" title="John Lucarotti">
        John Lucarotti
       </a>
       , a prolific writer whose credits included
       <i>
        The Avengers
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Troubleshooters" title="The Troubleshooters">
         The Troubleshooters
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#320_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#320-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lastly, Letts and Dicks retrospectively commissioned themselves in late May 1973 for their pilot script "Departure and Arrival".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#320_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#320-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In order to achieve a high level of realism, Letts and Dicks hired a technical advisor, James Burke, a science correspondent who was well known to television viewers thanks to his work as anchorman on the television coverage of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">
        Apollo moon landings
       </a>
       and for presenting science series such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tomorrow%27s_World" title="Tomorrow's World">
         Tomorrow's World
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        The Burke Special
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Burke decided that 2003 would be a realistic date for bases to have been established on the Moon, telling the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Radio_Times" title="Radio Times">
         Radio Times
        </a>
       </i>
       that "Men won't go back to the Moon until the 1990s. Neither the Russians nor the Americans have any plans at present and no-one else can afford it. [...] As for the base itself – it should look exactly as it would if they built one tomorrow. [...] They'd be small, supporting 30 or 40 men and running on a shoestring. [...] It'll be like life on a nuclear submarine".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bygrave-1973_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bygrave-1973-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Cast as David Caulder was Donald Houston, an experienced character actor, who had appeared in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/633_Squadron" title="633 Squadron">
         633 Squadron
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Longest_Day_(film)" title="The Longest Day (film)">
         The Longest Day
        </a>
       </i>
       , and was known for playing authority figures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ralph Bates, who was cast as Michel Lebrun, had first made a name for himself playing the Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Caligula" title="Caligula">
        Caligula
       </a>
       in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granada_Television" title="Granada Television">
        Granada Television
       </a>
       series
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Caesars_(TV_series)" title="The Caesars (TV series)">
         The Caesars
        </a>
       </i>
       but was best known as a regular actor in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hammer_Film_Productions" title="Hammer Film Productions">
        Hammer horror
       </a>
       stable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Fiona Gaunt, playing Helen Smith, had been in a television adaptation of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/War_and_Peace_(1972_TV_series)" title="War and Peace (1972 TV series)">
         War and Peace
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while Barry Lowe, playing Tom Hill, had been a regular on the police drama
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Z-Cars" title="Z-Cars">
         Z-Cars
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Two directors were assigned to the series –
       <a href="/wiki/Ken_Hannam" title="Ken Hannam">
        Ken Hannam
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Barry" title="Christopher Barry">
        Christopher Barry
       </a>
       . Hannam had previously directed such series as
       <i>
        Colditz
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Paul_Temple" title="Paul Temple">
         Paul Temple
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while Barry had worked extensively on
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       , having directed some 31 episodes at the time, including the stories
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_D%C3%A6mons" title="The Dæmons">
         The Dæmons
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Mutants" title="The Mutants">
         The Mutants
        </a>
       </i>
       for Letts and Dicks,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as well as episodes of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Out_of_the_Unknown" title="Out of the Unknown">
         Out of the Unknown
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Paul Temple
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Onedin_Line" title="The Onedin Line">
         The Onedin Line
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hannam and Barry alternated directing the episodes between them with filming on
       <i>
        Moonbase 3
       </i>
       beginning on 24 April 1973 at the BBC film studios in
       <a href="/wiki/Ealing_Studios" title="Ealing Studios">
        Ealing
       </a>
       . The Ealing filming mainly centred around the scenes set on the lunar surface which proved a difficult experience for the actors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#320_2-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#320-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mausoleum_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mausoleum-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Star Donald Houston told the
       <i>
        Radio Times
       </i>
       that the spacesuits got "hot and claustrophobic. In the end they had to have oxygen standing by. [...] the dust rose in clouds and the cameramen all wore surgical masks. The actors just choked".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bygrave-1973_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bygrave-1973-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Recalling his guest appearance on
       <i>
        Moonbase 3
       </i>
       , in the episode "Behemoth",
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Miles_(English_actor)" title="Peter Miles (English actor)">
        Peter Miles
       </a>
       told
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/TV_Zone" title="TV Zone">
         TV Zone
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1991 that he was "asked to be like a gazelle and leap as I came down the hillock in full astronaut gear. [...] The tech crew wondered why I was steaming up furiously in my astronaut's head. They'd forgotten to put air holes into the helmet. [...] I said, 'Help help! Help help! I'm not breathing folks!' They took the helmet off before drilling the holes or I wouldn't be here now".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Filming continued at Ealing until 30 May 1973
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#320_2-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#320-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       before production moved to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Television_Centre" title="BBC Television Centre">
        BBC Television Centre
       </a>
       for the remaining scenes, mostly those set inside the moonbase, which would be recorded on
       <a href="/wiki/Videotape" title="Videotape">
        videotape
       </a>
       beginning on 18 June 1973.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mausoleum_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mausoleum-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The episodes were recorded in a different order than that in which they were broadcast with "Departure and Arrival" recorded first followed by "Castor and Pollux", "Behemoth", "View of a Dead Planet", "Outsiders" and, lastly, "Achilles Heel" with recording wrapping on 15 August 1973.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mausoleum_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mausoleum-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Music was provided by
       <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Simpson" title="Dudley Simpson">
        Dudley Simpson
       </a>
       who, at this time, was the regular composer on
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Simpson composed the main title theme that accompanied the opening and closing credits as well as approximately 60 minutes of incidental music.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mausoleum_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mausoleum-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission_and_audience_reaction">
        Transmission and audience reaction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonbase_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transmission and audience reaction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Moonbase 3
       </i>
       was promoted by the
       <i>
        Radio Times
       </i>
       on the week of broadcast with a two-page article by Mike Bygrave, titled "The Facts of Life on the Moon", that interviewed James Burke about his ideas of life on the moon and also spoke to star Donald Houston, dresser Leslie Hallam and costume designer Dee Kelly about their experiences making the show.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bygrave-1973_20-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bygrave-1973-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Broadcast on Sunday nights at 7:25pm on
       <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">
        BBC One
       </a>
       , audience reaction to the series was disappointing with the debut episode garnering under 6 million viewers and ratings slipping as low as 2 million in subsequent weeks before stabilising at 4 million.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#320_2-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#320-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A BBC Audience Research report slated the series as "banal, predictable and slow".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#320_2-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#320-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Reviewing "Departure and Arrival" in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">
         The Observer
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Clive_James" title="Clive James">
        Clive James
       </a>
       described the plot as "the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMS_Amethyst_(U16)" title="HMS Amethyst (U16)">
        Yangtze Incident
       </a>
       plus liquid oxygen".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Archive_status">
        Archive status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonbase_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Archive status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       As was normal procedure at the BBC at the time,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the original
       <a href="/wiki/PAL" title="PAL">
        PAL
       </a>
       master tapes of the series were wiped some time after broadcast and, for many years,
       <i>
        Moonbase 3
       </i>
       was believed to be lost forever. However, in 1993,
       <a href="/wiki/NTSC" title="NTSC">
        NTSC
       </a>
       copies of all six episodes were found in co-producer Fox's archives and returned to the BBC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mausoleum_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mausoleum-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The series was subsequently released on
       <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">
        VHS
       </a>
       videotape over three volumes in 1994 by BBC Video and on
       <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">
        DVD
       </a>
       in 2002 by Second Sight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DWM#320_2-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-DWM#320-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
        Legacy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonbase_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Terrance Dicks has felt that
       <i>
        Moonbase 3
       </i>
       was ultimately a failure: "The trouble was we built a too restrictive format for ourselves"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and that the series "lacked a sense of wonder and outrageousness".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVZ#35_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TVZ#35-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Academic Peter Wright has said about
       <i>
        Moonbase 3
       </i>
       that its "appeal to realism resulted in a disquieting sense of claustrophobia and isolation that undermined the optimism of its premise and captured the general mood of insularity felt (and often desired) in Britain in the early 1970s".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Moonbase 3
       </i>
       , although not directly influential, can be seen as an antecedent of similar realistic, near-future, British space series such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Cops" title="Star Cops">
         Star Cops
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Space_Island_One" title="Space Island One">
         Space Island One
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
