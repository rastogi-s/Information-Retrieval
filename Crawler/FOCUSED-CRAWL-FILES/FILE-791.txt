      <p>
       <b>
        Carpo
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="/ɑːr/: 'ar' in 'far'">
            ɑːr
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          KAR
         </span>
         -poh
        </i>
       </a>
       ; Greek:
       <i>
        Καρπώ
       </i>
       ), also
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter XLVI
        </span>
       </b>
       , is a
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       . It was discovered by a team of astronomers from the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">
        University of Hawaii
       </a>
       led by
       <a href="/wiki/Scott_S._Sheppard" title="Scott S. Sheppard">
        Scott S. Sheppard
       </a>
       in 2003, and was provisionally designated as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/2003 J 20
        </span>
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       until it received its name in early 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Carpo is about 3 kilometres (1.9 miles) in diameter, and orbits Jupiter at an average distance of 17.145 Gm (just over ten million miles) in 458.625 days, at an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       of 56° to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       (55° to Jupiter's equator), and with a high
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of 0.4316. All of the moons further from Jupiter than Carpo are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It was named in March 2005 after
       <a href="/wiki/Horae#Descriptions" title="Horae">
        Carpo
       </a>
       , one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Horae" title="Horae">
        Horae
       </a>
       , and a daughter of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       (Jupiter).
      </p>
      <p>
       Like
       <a href="/wiki/Themisto_(moon)" title="Themisto (moon)">
        Themisto
       </a>
       , this moon seems to be the lone member of a unique class, which makes it particularly interesting. The orbital
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       of satellites such as this one is limited by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kozai_effect" title="Kozai effect">
        Kozai effect
       </a>
       , discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Yoshihide_Kozai" title="Yoshihide Kozai">
        Yoshihide Kozai
       </a>
       in 1962. This effect induces a periodic exchange between the inclination and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of the orbit; if the inclination is large enough, the eccentricity can in turn grow so large that the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">
        periapsis
       </a>
       of the satellite (called the
       <i>
        perizene
       </i>
       in the case of moons of Jupiter) would be in the immediate vicinity of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galilean_moon" title="Galilean moon">
        Galilean moons
       </a>
       (Io, Europa, Ganymede and Callisto). The satellite would eventually collide with one of these, or a close encounter would eject it altogether from the Jovian system. The periapsis precession period (P
       <sub>
        w
       </sub>
       ) is 6.8 million years long.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-JPL-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
