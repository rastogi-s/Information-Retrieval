   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Canada lunar sample displays
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Canada lunar sample displays
       </b>
       are two
       <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_plaque" title="Commemorative plaque">
        commemorative plaques
       </a>
       consisting of small fragments of Moon specimen brought back with the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        Apollo 17
       </a>
       lunar missions and given in the 1970s to the people of
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       by United States President
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">
        Richard Nixon
       </a>
       as goodwill gifts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canada_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_11">
        Apollo 11
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canada_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Apollo 11">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11_lunar_sample_display" title="Apollo 11 lunar sample display">
        Apollo 11 lunar sample display
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Apollo 11 Canadian moon rocks commemorative podium plaque display consists of four "moon rock" rice-size particle specimens that were collected by Apollo 11 astronauts
       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
        Buzz Aldrin
       </a>
       in 1969 and a small Canadian flag that went to the Moon and back.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The four Moon rocks weigh about 0.05 grams total and are entirely enveloped in a clear plastic button the size of a coin which is mounted to a wooden board approximately a foot square on a small
       <a href="/wiki/Podium" title="Podium">
        podium
       </a>
       pedestal display. The small podium plaque display also has mounted on it a small
       <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Canada" title="Flag of Canada">
        Canadian flag
       </a>
       that had been taken to the moon and back on Apollo 11, which lies directly below the Moon rocks. The small podium plaque display was given to the people of Canada as a gift by President Richard Nixon. Similar lunar sample displays were also distributed to all the states of the United States and all the countries of the world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div style="clear:both;">
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_17">
        Apollo 17
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canada_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apollo 17">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17_lunar_sample_display" title="Apollo 17 lunar sample display">
        Apollo 17 lunar sample display
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Canada Apollo 17 lunar sample display commemorative wooden plaque (10 by 14 inches) consists of one Moon rock particle specimen that was cut from
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_basalt_70017" title="Lunar basalt 70017">
        lunar basalt 70017
       </a>
       and a Canadian flag. The
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       70017 was collected by Apollo 17 astronaut
       <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">
        Harrison Schmitt
       </a>
       on the Moon in 1972. Once lunar basalt 70017 was brought back to Earth from the Moon, the basalt Moon rock was cut up into small fragments of approximately 1 gram. The specimen was enveloped in a plastic ball and mounted on the wooden plaque along with the Canadian flag which had been taken to the Moon and back by the crew of Apollo 17. The wooden plaque display was then distributed in 1973 by President Richard Nixon to the country of Canada as he did that year to all the countries of the world and all the states of the United States (the same as for the Apollo 11 plaque display gifts). This was done as a goodwill gesture to promote peace and harmony.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canada_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CanadianMuseumofNature2010-05-19.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1943" data-file-width="3277" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/CanadianMuseumofNature2010-05-19.JPG/220px-CanadianMuseumofNature2010-05-19.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/CanadianMuseumofNature2010-05-19.JPG/330px-CanadianMuseumofNature2010-05-19.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/CanadianMuseumofNature2010-05-19.JPG/440px-CanadianMuseumofNature2010-05-19.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CanadianMuseumofNature2010-05-19.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         National Museum of Natural Sciences in
         <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">
          Ottawa
         </a>
         housed in the Victoria Memorial Museum building.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1972 a 10-day international "Youth Science Tour" took place in the United States with 80 "youth ambassadors" ages 17 to 21 from all the countries of the United Nations participating.
       <a href="/wiki/Jaymie_Matthews" title="Jaymie Matthews">
        Jaymie Matthews
       </a>
       , age 13 at the time (falsely identifying himself as 17), of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chatham,_Ontario" title="Chatham, Ontario">
        Chatham, Ontario
       </a>
       , was the Canadian "youth ambassador" representative whose reward for winning an essay contest on "The Importance of Space Flight to Mankind" was to go to the
       <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">
        United States national capital
       </a>
       . There Matthews met President Nixon and vice-president
       <a href="/wiki/Spiro_Agnew" title="Spiro Agnew">
        Spiro Agnew
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UBC_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UBC-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From Washington, D.C. the "youth ambassadors" were flown to
       <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">
        Orlando, Florida
       </a>
       and put up in a luxury hotel. They watched the launch of Apollo 17 in Florida from
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_Control_Center" title="Mission Control Center">
        Mission Control Center
       </a>
       . Matthews not only saw the launch but the landing of Apollo 17 on the Moon, which was memorialised by astronauts
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_A._Cernan" title="Eugene A. Cernan">
        Eugene A. Cernan
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harrison_H._Schmitt" title="Harrison H. Schmitt">
        Harrison H. Schmitt
       </a>
       when they talked directly to the "youth ambassadors" from the surface of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Matthews was able to see astronaut Neil Armstrong personally at the luxury hotel in Orlando, Florida. He also got quite acquainted with Armstrong's daughter and became lifetime
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pen_pals" title="Pen pals">
        pen pals
       </a>
       with her.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UBC_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-UBC-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The program directors promised each "youth ambassador" a lunar sample from the Apollo 17 mission. In 1973 Matthews was sent the Canadian "goodwill moon rock" encased in a clear Lucite ball about the size of a billiard ball and mounted to a 10-inch by 14-inch wooden board. In the center of the plaque was a small Canadian flag that had flown on the Apollo 17 mission to the Moon and back. On the bottom was a label inscribed:
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         <p>
          <i>
           Presented to the people of CANADA/ From the people of the United States of America/ RICHARD NIXON/ 1973.
          </i>
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UBC_3-2">
           <a href="#cite_note-UBC-3">
            [3]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </p>
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Matthews kept the commemorative Canadian "goodwill moon rock" display in his bedroom. The Apollo 17 display was out in plain view for months in his bedroom. He often showed it off to his space geeks. On September 21, 1973, during a ceremony at
       <a href="/wiki/Rideau_Hall" title="Rideau Hall">
        Rideau Hall
       </a>
       (home of the governor-general
       <a href="/wiki/Roland_Michener" title="Roland Michener">
        Roland Michener
       </a>
       ), Matthews turned over possession of the lunar sample display to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Natural_Sciences" title="National Museum of Natural Sciences">
        National Museum of Natural Sciences
       </a>
       in Ottawa, where it was exhibited for several years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UBC_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-UBC-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CMST-Entrance.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1377" data-file-width="3170" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/CMST-Entrance.jpg/220px-CMST-Entrance.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/CMST-Entrance.jpg/330px-CMST-Entrance.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/CMST-Entrance.jpg/440px-CMST-Entrance.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CMST-Entrance.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Canada Science and Technology Museum
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1978 the museum lost the lunar sample wooden plaque display during a national tour. They told Matthews it was stolen during the tour in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edmonton,_Alberta,_Canada" title="Edmonton, Alberta, Canada">
        Edmonton, Alberta, Canada
       </a>
       . As the years went on. Matthews became a professor at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">
        University of British Columbia
       </a>
       . One day, as he was teaching a class, he
       <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">
        googled
       </a>
       for the commemorative Canadian "goodwill moon rock" display. He came upon a picture taken of it in 2000. Doing more googling, he discovered it was in a museum warehouse in
       <a href="/wiki/Aylmer,_Quebec" title="Aylmer, Quebec">
        Aylmer, Quebec
       </a>
       . Matthews compares it to a huge warehouse scene in "
       <a href="/wiki/Raiders_of_the_Lost_Ark" title="Raiders of the Lost Ark">
        Raiders of the Lost Ark
       </a>
       " where millions of pieces are stored. It was literally lost in the multitude of items stored and only through the accidental chance of Matthews finding a picture of it did its continued existence come to light. The National Museum of Natural Sciences in Ottawa told Matthews back in 1978 that apparently it was stolen, since at that time they themselves had lost it and needed a story to cover that. There wasn't even a 1978 national tour. It was all a cover-up story for Matthews. Eventually the Apollo 17 "goodwill moon rock" display was put back on display at the
       <a href="/wiki/Canada_Science_and_Technology_Museum" title="Canada Science and Technology Museum">
        Canada Science and Technology Museum
       </a>
       in Ottawa.
