      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Northrop Grumman Lunar Lander Challenge
       </b>
       was a competition funded by
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Challenges" title="Centennial Challenges">
        Centennial Challenges
       </a>
       program. The competition offered a series of prizes for teams that launch a
       <a href="/wiki/VTVL" title="VTVL">
        vertical takeoff/vertical landing (VTVL)
       </a>
       rocket that achieved the total
       <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">
        delta-v
       </a>
       needed for a vehicle to move between the surface of the Moon and its orbit. The multi-level competition was conducted by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X_PRIZE_Foundation" title="X PRIZE Foundation">
        X PRIZE Foundation
       </a>
       , with sponsorship from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_Corporation" title="Northrop Grumman Corporation">
        Northrop Grumman Corporation
       </a>
       who ran the ongoing competition. The prize purses were paid by NASA. It was held annually at the X PRIZE Cup, making its debut at the 2006
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wirefly_X_PRIZE_Cup" title="Wirefly X PRIZE Cup">
        Wirefly X PRIZE Cup
       </a>
       in October, 2006,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       until 2009 when the prize purse was awarded to
       <a href="/wiki/Masten_Space_Systems" title="Masten Space Systems">
        Masten Space Systems
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Armadillo_Aerospace" title="Armadillo Aerospace">
        Armadillo Aerospace
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Competition_rules">
        Competition rules
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Lander_Challenge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Competition rules">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The competition is divided into two levels. Both levels require teams to demonstrate control of their vehicle by flying to an altitude of more than 50 meters (160 ft), flying laterally for 100 m (330 ft), and landing on a pad. For level 1, this pad is a simple 10 m (33 ft) diameter circle; for level 2, it is a simulated lunar surface, complete with
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        craters
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Boulder" title="Boulder">
        boulders
       </a>
       . After completing this first flight, the vehicle can then be refueled, and must then fly a second leg back to the original starting point. Each flight must meet a required minimum flight time of 90 seconds for level 1 and 180 seconds for level 2. For each level, the two flights along with any necessary preparation must be accomplished within a short 150-minute time period.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Each Level offered a first- and second-place prize. Level 1 awarded a first place prize purse of $350,000 and a $150,000 purse for second place. The more difficult level 2 awarded first place prize of $1 million and a $500,000 second place prize.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2006">
        2006
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Lander_Challenge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 2006">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       2006 was the first year of the competition. It was announced on May 5, 2006, giving teams only a few months to prepare for the late-October competition. Although four teams officially registered for the competition, only one was able to receive the required permit from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAA" title="FAA">
        FAA
       </a>
       before the event.
       <a href="/wiki/Armadillo_Aerospace" title="Armadillo Aerospace">
        Armadillo Aerospace
       </a>
       arrived at the 2006 event, held at
       <a href="/wiki/Las_Cruces_International_Airport" title="Las Cruces International Airport">
        Las Cruces International Airport
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       , with two matching vehicles, named Pixel and Texel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the end, Armadillo made three attempts to win the prize, each one using Pixel. In all three cases, difficult landings left them short of the mission requirements—on two occasions, rough landings caused damage to the vehicle; on a third, the vehicle failed to land completely on the target pad. Team Armadillo left without any prize money, but still had made history by performing the first successful flight of a private vehicle of this class—as well as the first flight under the FAA's new
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Experimental_Permit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Experimental Permit (page does not exist)">
        Experimental Permit
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2007">
        2007
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Lander_Challenge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2007">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The 2007 Lunar Lander Challenge took place on October 27–28 at the
       <a href="/wiki/Holloman_Air_Force_Base" title="Holloman Air Force Base">
        Holloman Air Force Base
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Micro-Space, one of the teams registered in 2007, had to retreat from the competition after they missed a mandatory meeting.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The only team to compete was again Armadillo Aerospace. Armadillo entered their MOD vehicle for level 1. They attempted six flights, but never completed the full profile. A flight on October 27 ended with the vehicle crashing on the return flight. Their final flight attempt on October 28 caused a fire on the launch pad. Team leader
       <a href="/wiki/John_Carmack" title="John Carmack">
        John Carmack
       </a>
       expressed his disappointment, saying "today is officially a bad day when it comes to our vehicle."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2008">
        2008
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Lander_Challenge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2008">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The 2008 Lunar Lander Challenge took place October 24–25, back at the Las Cruces International Airport. Two teams competed. Because the X PRIZE Cup was canceled for 2008, the Lunar Lander Challenge was held separately, and was open only to members of the press.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was, however, broadcast live by the official event webcast,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SpaceVidcast#2008_Lunar_Lander_Challenge_Coverage" title="SpaceVidcast">
        SpaceVidcast
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XPRIZE08_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-XPRIZE08-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The only teams that flew were Armadillo Aerospace and TrueZer0. Both received waivers from the FAA to fly experimental rockets.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       TrueZer0 attempted level 1, achieved hover, then lost roll control and was aborted and crashed.
      </p>
      <p>
       Armadillo had an unsuccessful first attempt at level 1, and landed early due to inadequate thrust. On their second attempt they completed the first leg, but the second leg was cut short by the FAA closing the flight window. The second leg was held in the afternoon, and they were able to take the Level 1 top prize of $350,000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArmadilloL1Win_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ArmadilloL1Win-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Armadillo made an attempt at the level 2 prize on October 25, but had a fuel valve failure, burned through the engine nozzle, and rolled the vehicle at takeoff. They decided not to make another attempt.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArmadilloL1Win_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ArmadilloL1Win-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <div class="thumb tright">

      <p>
       The method of competing in Lunar Lander Challenge was modified for the 2009 competition season to enable teams to compete from multiple locations, rather than at a single location, as was done in previous seasons. Instead, judges are traveling to a location near the applicants' home base, where prize competition efforts may be attempted over the course of several days during the LLC season of July 20 to October 31.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Lunar Lander Challenge concluded this season with the following flights and results:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa-llc-pr-200911_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa-llc-pr-200911-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xprize-llc-pr-200911_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-xprize-llc-pr-200911-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Armadillo_Aerospace" title="Armadillo Aerospace">
         Armadillo Aerospace
        </a>
        made their attempt for the Level 2 purse from
        <a href="/wiki/Caddo_Mills,_Texas" title="Caddo Mills, Texas">
         Caddo Mills, Texas
        </a>
        , on September 12 and 13, and successfully qualified for the Level 2 prize.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hs14842_14-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-hs14842-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The judges would later rule the accuracy of this flight as 2nd place for Level 2.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20091102b_15-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-nasa20091102b-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Masten Space Systems attempted to win second prize for Level 1 on September 15–16, but aborted after first flight. They successfully flew both legs (Level 1) on October 7 from the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mojave_Air_%26_Space_Port" title="Mojave Air &amp; Space Port">
         Mojave Air &amp; Space Port
        </a>
        . The judges would later rule the accuracy of this flight as second place for Level 1.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20091102b_15-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-nasa20091102b-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=BonNova&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="BonNova (page does not exist)">
         BonNovA
        </a>
        announced it intended to attempt to claim the Level 1 prize in Cantil, California, on Oct 26th-27th,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
        but cancelled the weekend before the scheduled date.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Masten_Space_Systems" title="Masten Space Systems">
         Masten Space Systems
        </a>
        attempted the Level 2 flights on Oct 28-30 from Mojave. After problems with the computer aborting the launch, a fire after one attempt and overnight repairs, the "Xoie" rocket flew both legs and qualified for Level 2. The judges would later rule the accuracy of this flight as 1st place for Level 2, and awarded the
        <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">
         US$
        </a>
        1,000,000 Level Two prize to Masten.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20091102b_15-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-nasa20091102b-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired20091104_18-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-wired20091104-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Unreasonable Rocket (Paul Breed, father &amp; son team) attempted to claim purses in both competition levels from
        <a href="/wiki/Cantil,_California" title="Cantil, California">
         Cantil, California
        </a>
        on Oct 30 to Nov 1, and did make an 84-second pad-to-pad flight on the Level 1 competition, but did not successfully complete either level.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hs14842_14-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-hs14842-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
