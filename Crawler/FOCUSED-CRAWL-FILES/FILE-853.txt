      <p>
       <b>
        Parallax
       </b>
       is a displacement or difference in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_position" title="Apparent position">
        apparent position
       </a>
       of an object viewed along two different
       <a href="/wiki/Sightline" title="Sightline">
        lines of sight
       </a>
       , and is measured by the angle or semi-angle of inclination between those two lines.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-oed-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term is derived from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       παράλλαξις
       <i>
        (parallaxis)
       </i>
       , meaning 'alternation'. Due to
       <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">
        foreshortening
       </a>
       , nearby objects show a larger parallax than farther objects when observed from different positions, so parallax can be used to determine distances.
      </p>
      <p>
       To measure large distances, such as the distance of a planet or a
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , astronomers use the principle of parallax. Here, the term
       <i>
        parallax
       </i>
       is the semi-angle of inclination between two sight-lines to the star, as observed when Earth is on opposite sides of the Sun in its orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These distances form the lowest rung of what is called "the
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">
        cosmic distance ladder
       </a>
       ", the first in a succession of methods by which astronomers determine the distances to celestial objects, serving as a basis for other distance measurements in astronomy forming the higher rungs of the ladder.
      </p>
      <p>
       Parallax also affects optical instruments such as rifle scopes,
       <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">
        binoculars
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">
        microscopes
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Twin-lens_reflex_camera" title="Twin-lens reflex camera">
        twin-lens reflex cameras
       </a>
       that view objects from slightly different angles. Many animals, including humans, have two
       <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">
        eyes
       </a>
       with overlapping
       <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">
        visual fields
       </a>
       that use parallax to gain
       <a href="/wiki/Depth_perception" title="Depth perception">
        depth perception
       </a>
       ; this process is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Stereopsis" title="Stereopsis">
        stereopsis
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_vision" title="Computer vision">
        computer vision
       </a>
       the effect is used for
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_stereo_vision" title="Computer stereo vision">
        computer stereo vision
       </a>
       , and there is a device called a
       <a href="/wiki/Coincidence_rangefinder" title="Coincidence rangefinder">
        parallax rangefinder
       </a>
       that uses it to find range, and in some variations also altitude to a target.
      </p>
      <p>
       A simple everyday example of parallax can be seen in the dashboard of motor vehicles that use a needle-style speedometer gauge. When viewed from directly in front, the speed may show exactly 60; but when viewed from the passenger seat the needle may appear to show a slightly different speed, due to the angle of viewing.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_perception">
        Visual perception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Visual perception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Stereopsis" title="Stereopsis">
        stereopsis
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Depth_perception" title="Depth perception">
        depth perception
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Binocular_vision" title="Binocular vision">
        binocular vision
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Binocular_disparity" title="Binocular disparity">
        Binocular disparity
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       As the eyes of humans and other animals are in different positions on the head, they present different views simultaneously. This is the basis of
       <a href="/wiki/Stereopsis" title="Stereopsis">
        stereopsis
       </a>
       , the process by which the brain exploits the parallax due to the different views from the eye to gain depth perception and estimate distances to objects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Animals also use
       <i>
        motion parallax
       </i>
       , in which the animals (or just the head) move to gain different viewpoints. For example,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pigeon" title="Pigeon">
        pigeons
       </a>
       (whose eyes do not have overlapping fields of view and thus cannot use stereopsis) bob their heads up and down to see depth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The motion parallax is exploited also in
       <a href="/wiki/Wiggle_stereoscopy" title="Wiggle stereoscopy">
        wiggle stereoscopy
       </a>
       , computer graphics which provide depth cues through viewpoint-shifting animation rather than through binocular vision.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">
        Astronomy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Astronomy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Parallax_geo_or_helio_static.PNG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="386" data-file-width="446" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Parallax_geo_or_helio_static.PNG/300px-Parallax_geo_or_helio_static.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/10/Parallax_geo_or_helio_static.PNG 1.5x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Parallax_geo_or_helio_static.PNG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Parallax is an angle subtended by a line on a point. In the upper diagram, the earth in its orbit sweeps the parallax angle subtended on the sun. The lower diagram shows an equal angle swept by the sun in a geostatic model. A similar diagram can be drawn for a star except that the angle of parallax would be minuscule.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Parallax arises due to change in viewpoint occurring due to motion of the observer, of the observed, or of both. What is essential is relative motion. By observing parallax,
       <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">
        measuring
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">
        angles
       </a>
       , and using
       <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">
        geometry
       </a>
       , one can determine
       <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">
        distance
       </a>
       . Astronomers also use the word "parallax" as a synonym for "distance measurement" by other methods: see
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Parallax_(disambiguation)#Astronomy" title="Parallax (disambiguation)">
        parallax (disambiguation)#Astronomy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_parallax">
        Stellar parallax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stellar parallax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_parallax" title="Stellar parallax">
        Stellar parallax
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Stellar parallax created by the relative motion between the Earth and a
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       can be seen, in the Copernican model, as arising from the orbit of the Earth around the Sun: the star only
       <i>
        appears
       </i>
       to move relative to more distant objects in the sky. In a geostatic model, the movement of the star would have to be taken as
       <i>
        real
       </i>
       with the star oscillating across the sky with respect to the background stars.
      </p>
      <p>
       Stellar parallax is most often measured using
       <b>
        annual parallax
       </b>
       , defined as the difference in position of a star as seen from the Earth and Sun, i. e. the angle subtended at a star by the mean radius of the Earth's orbit around the Sun. The
       <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">
        parsec
       </a>
       (3.26
       <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">
        light-years
       </a>
       ) is defined as the distance for which the annual parallax is 1
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">
        arcsecond
       </a>
       . Annual parallax is normally measured by observing the position of a star at different times of the
       <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">
        year
       </a>
       as the Earth moves through its orbit. Measurement of annual parallax was the first reliable way to determine the distances to the closest stars. The first successful measurements of stellar parallax were made by
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel" title="Friedrich Bessel">
        Friedrich Bessel
       </a>
       in 1838 for the star
       <a href="/wiki/61_Cygni" title="61 Cygni">
        61 Cygni
       </a>
       using a
       <a href="/wiki/Heliometer" title="Heliometer">
        heliometer
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZG44_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ZG44-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Stellar parallax remains the standard for calibrating other measurement methods. Accurate calculations of distance based on stellar parallax require a measurement of the distance from the Earth to the Sun, now based on
       <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
        radar
       </a>
       reflection off the surfaces of planets.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The angles involved in these calculations are very small and thus difficult to measure. The nearest star to the Sun (and thus the star with the largest parallax),
       <a href="/wiki/Proxima_Centauri" title="Proxima Centauri">
        Proxima Centauri
       </a>
       , has a parallax of 0.7687 ± 0.0003 arcsec.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj118_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-apj118-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This angle is approximately that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtended" title="Subtended">
        subtended
       </a>
       by an object 2 centimeters in diameter located 5.3 kilometers away.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:14-104-hubble-parallax.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="853" data-file-width="1325" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/14-104-hubble-parallax.png/250px-14-104-hubble-parallax.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/14-104-hubble-parallax.png/375px-14-104-hubble-parallax.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/14-104-hubble-parallax.png/500px-14-104-hubble-parallax.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:14-104-hubble-parallax.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
          Hubble Space Telescope
         </a>
         -
         <a href="/wiki/Parallax#Parallax_in_astronomy" title="Parallax">
          Spatial scanning
         </a>
         precisely measures distances up to 10,000 light-years away (10 April 2014).
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140410_9-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140410-9">
           [9]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The fact that stellar parallax was so small that it was unobservable at the time was used as the main scientific argument against
       <a href="/wiki/Heliocentrism" title="Heliocentrism">
        heliocentrism
       </a>
       during the early modern age. It is clear from
       <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">
        Euclid
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">
        geometry
       </a>
       that the effect would be undetectable if the stars were far enough away, but for various reasons such gigantic distances involved seemed entirely implausible: it was one of
       <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">
        Tycho
       </a>
       's principal objections to
       <a href="/wiki/Copernican_heliocentrism" title="Copernican heliocentrism">
        Copernican heliocentrism
       </a>
       that in order for it to be compatible with the lack of observable stellar parallax, there would have to be an enormous and unlikely void between the orbit of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       (then the most distant known planet) and the eighth
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       (the fixed stars).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1989, the satellite
       <a href="/wiki/Hipparcos" title="Hipparcos">
        Hipparcos
       </a>
       was launched primarily for obtaining improved parallaxes and
       <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">
        proper motions
       </a>
       for over 100,000 nearby stars, increasing the reach of the method tenfold. Even so, Hipparcos is only able to measure parallax angles for stars up to about 1,600
       <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">
        light-years
       </a>
       away, a little more than one percent of the diameter of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_Galaxy" title="Milky Way Galaxy">
        Milky Way Galaxy
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        European Space Agency
       </a>
       's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaia_mission" title="Gaia mission">
        Gaia mission
       </a>
       , launched in December 2013, will be able to measure parallax angles to an accuracy of 10
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microarcsecond" title="Microarcsecond">
        microarcseconds
       </a>
       , thus mapping nearby stars (and potentially planets) up to a distance of tens of thousands of light-years from Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In April 2014, NASA astronomers reported that the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       , by using
       <a href="/wiki/Parallax#Parallax_in_astronomy" title="Parallax">
        spatial scanning
       </a>
       , can now precisely measure distances up to 10,000 light-years away, a ten-fold improvement over earlier measurements.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140410_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140410-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distance_measurement">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

      <p>
       Distance measurement by parallax is a special case of the principle of
       <a href="/wiki/Triangulation" title="Triangulation">
        triangulation
       </a>
       , which states that one can solve for all the sides and angles in a network of triangles if, in addition to all the angles in the network, the length of at least one side has been measured. Thus, the careful measurement of the length of one baseline can fix the scale of an entire triangulation network. In parallax, the triangle is extremely long and narrow, and by measuring both its shortest side (the motion of the observer) and the small top angle (always less than 1
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">
        arcsecond
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZG44_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ZG44-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       leaving the other two close to 90 degrees), the length of the long sides (in practice considered to be equal) can be determined.
      </p>
      <p>
       Assuming the angle is small (see
       <a href="#Derivation">
        derivation
       </a>
       below), the distance to an object (measured in
       <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">
        parsecs
       </a>
       ) is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reciprocal_(mathematics)" title="Reciprocal (mathematics)">

       </a>

       </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diurnal_parallax">
        Diurnal parallax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diurnal parallax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Diurnal parallax
       </i>
       is a parallax that varies with rotation of the Earth or with difference of location on the Earth. The Moon and to a smaller extent the
       <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">
        terrestrial planets
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       seen from different viewing positions on the Earth (at one given moment) can appear differently placed against the background of fixed stars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_parallax">
        Lunar parallax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lunar parallax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Lunar parallax
       </i>
       (often short for
       <i>
        lunar horizontal parallax
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        lunar equatorial horizontal parallax
       </i>
       ), is a special case of (diurnal) parallax: the Moon, being the nearest celestial body, has by far the largest maximum parallax of any celestial body, it can exceed 1 degree.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aa1981_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-aa1981-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The diagram (above) for stellar parallax can illustrate lunar parallax as well, if the diagram is taken to be scaled right down and slightly modified. Instead of 'near star', read 'Moon', and instead of taking the circle at the bottom of the diagram to represent the size of the Earth's orbit around the Sun, take it to be the size of the Earth's globe, and of a circle around the Earth's surface. Then, the lunar (horizontal) parallax amounts to the difference in angular position, relative to the background of distant stars, of the Moon as seen from two different viewing positions on the Earth: one of the viewing positions is the place from which the Moon can be seen directly overhead at a given moment (that is, viewed along the vertical line in the diagram); and the other viewing position is a place from which the Moon can be seen on the horizon at the same moment (that is, viewed along one of the diagonal lines, from an Earth-surface position corresponding roughly to one of the blue dots on the modified diagram).
      </p>
      <p>
       The lunar (horizontal) parallax can alternatively be defined as the angle subtended at the distance of the Moon by the radius of the Earth
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       —equal to angle p in the diagram when scaled-down and modified as mentioned above.
      </p>
      <p>
       The lunar horizontal parallax at any time depends on the linear distance of the Moon from the Earth. The Earth–Moon linear distance varies continuously as the Moon follows its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_moon" title="Orbit of the moon">
        perturbed and approximately elliptical orbit
       </a>
       around the Earth. The range of the variation in linear distance is from about 56 to 63.7 Earth radii, corresponding to horizontal parallax of about a degree of arc, but ranging from about 61.4' to about 54'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aa1981_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-aa1981-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_Almanac" title="Astronomical Almanac">
        Astronomical Almanac
       </a>
       and similar publications tabulate the lunar horizontal parallax and/or the linear distance of the Moon from the Earth on a periodical e.g. daily basis for the convenience of astronomers (and formerly, of navigators), and the study of the way in which this coordinate varies with time forms part of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_theory" title="Lunar theory">
        lunar theory
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:412px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunaparallax.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1913" data-file-width="1582" height="496" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lunaparallax.png/410px-Lunaparallax.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lunaparallax.png/615px-Lunaparallax.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lunaparallax.png/820px-Lunaparallax.png 2x" width="410"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunaparallax.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Diagram of daily lunar parallax
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Parallax can also be used to determine the distance to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       One way to determine the lunar parallax from one location is by using a lunar eclipse. A full shadow of the Earth on the Moon has an apparent radius of curvature equal to the difference between the apparent radii of the Earth and the Sun as seen from the Moon. This radius can be seen to be equal to 0.75 degree, from which (with the solar apparent radius 0.25 degree) we get an Earth apparent radius of 1 degree. This yields for the Earth–Moon distance 60.27 Earth radii or 384,399 kilometres (238,854 mi) This procedure was first used by
       <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samos" title="Aristarchus of Samos">
        Aristarchus of Samos
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gutzwiller_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gutzwiller-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">
        Hipparchus
       </a>
       , and later found its way into the work of
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The diagram at the right shows how daily lunar parallax arises on the geocentric and geostatic planetary model in which the Earth is at the centre of the planetary system and does not rotate. It also illustrates the important point that parallax need not be caused by any motion of the observer, contrary to some definitions of parallax that say it is, but may arise purely from motion of the observed.
      </p>
      <p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_parallax">
        Solar parallax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Solar parallax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       After
       <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">
        Copernicus
       </a>
       proposed his
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliocentric_system" title="Heliocentric system">
        heliocentric system
       </a>
       , with the Earth in revolution around the Sun, it was possible to build a model of the whole Solar System without scale. To ascertain the scale, it is necessary only to measure one distance within the Solar System, e.g., the mean distance from the Earth to the Sun (now called an
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical unit
       </a>
       , or AU). When found by
       <a href="/wiki/Triangulation" title="Triangulation">
        triangulation
       </a>
       , this is referred to as the
       <i>
        solar parallax
       </i>
       , the difference in position of the Sun as seen from the Earth's centre and a point one Earth radius away, i. e., the angle subtended at the Sun by the Earth's mean radius. Knowing the solar parallax and the mean Earth radius allows one to calculate the AU, the first, small step on the long road of establishing the size and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Universe" title="Age of the Universe">
        expansion age
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of the visible Universe.
      </p>
      <p>
       A primitive way to determine the distance to the Sun in terms of the distance to the Moon was already proposed by
       <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samos" title="Aristarchus of Samos">
        Aristarchus of Samos
       </a>
       in his book
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aristarchus_On_the_Sizes_and_Distances" title="Aristarchus On the Sizes and Distances">
         On the Sizes and Distances of the Sun and Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       . He noted that the Sun, Moon, and Earth form a right triangle (with the right angle at the Moon) at the moment of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        first or last quarter moon
       </a>
       . He then estimated that the Moon, Earth, Sun angle was 87°. Using correct
       <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">
        geometry
       </a>
       but inaccurate observational data, Aristarchus concluded that the Sun was slightly less than 20 times farther away than the Moon. The true value of this angle is close to 89° 50', and the Sun is actually about 390 times farther away.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gutzwiller_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gutzwiller-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He pointed out that the Moon and Sun have nearly equal
       <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">
        apparent angular sizes
       </a>
       and therefore their diameters must be in proportion to their distances from Earth. He thus concluded that the Sun was around 20 times larger than the Moon; this conclusion, although incorrect, follows logically from his incorrect data. It does suggest that the Sun is clearly larger than the Earth, which could be taken to support the heliocentric model.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Venus_Transit_%26_Parallax.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="950" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Venus_Transit_%26_Parallax.svg/220px-Venus_Transit_%26_Parallax.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Venus_Transit_%26_Parallax.svg/330px-Venus_Transit_%26_Parallax.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Venus_Transit_%26_Parallax.svg/440px-Venus_Transit_%26_Parallax.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Venus_Transit_%26_Parallax.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Measuring Venus transit times to determine solar parallax
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Although Aristarchus' results were incorrect due to observational errors, they were based on correct geometric principles of parallax, and became the basis for estimates of the size of the Solar System for almost 2000 years, until the
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">
        transit of Venus
       </a>
       was correctly observed in 1761 and 1769.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gutzwiller_17-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gutzwiller-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This method was proposed by
       <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Halley" title="Edmond Halley">
        Edmond Halley
       </a>
       in 1716, although he did not live to see the results. The use of Venus transits was less successful than had been hoped due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Black_drop_effect" title="Black drop effect">
        black drop effect
       </a>
       , but the resulting estimate, 153 million kilometers, is just 2% above the currently accepted value, 149.6 million kilometers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Much later, the Solar System was 'scaled' using the parallax of
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       , some of which, such as
       <a href="/wiki/433_Eros" title="433 Eros">
        Eros
       </a>
       , pass much closer to Earth than Venus. In a favourable opposition, Eros can approach the Earth to within 22 million kilometres.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Both the opposition of 1901 and that of 1930/1931 were used for this purpose, the calculations of the latter determination being completed by
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">
        Astronomer Royal
       </a>
       Sir
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Spencer_Jones" title="Harold Spencer Jones">
        Harold Spencer Jones
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Also
       <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
        radar
       </a>
       reflections, both off Venus (1958) and off asteroids, like
       <a href="/wiki/1566_Icarus" title="1566 Icarus">
        Icarus
       </a>
       , have been used for solar parallax determination. Today, use of
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">
        telemetry
       </a>
       links has solved this old problem. The currently accepted value of solar parallax is 8".794 143.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamical_or_moving-cluster_parallax">
        Dynamical or moving-cluster parallax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dynamical or moving-cluster parallax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moving_cluster_method" title="Moving cluster method">
        Moving cluster method
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The open stellar cluster
       <a href="/wiki/Hyades_(star_cluster)" title="Hyades (star cluster)">
        Hyades
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)" title="Taurus (constellation)">
        Taurus
       </a>
       extends over such a large part of the sky, 20 degrees, that the proper motions as derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">
        astrometry
       </a>
       appear to converge with some precision to a perspective point north of Orion. Combining the observed apparent (angular) proper motion in seconds of arc with the also observed true (absolute) receding motion as witnessed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">
        Doppler
       </a>
       redshift of the stellar spectral lines, allows estimation of the distance to the cluster (151 light-years) and its member stars in much the same way as using annual parallax.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dynamical parallax has sometimes also been used to determine the distance to a supernova, when the optical wave front of the outburst is seen to propagate through the surrounding dust clouds at an apparent angular velocity, while its true propagation velocity is known to be the
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
        speed of light
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       </span>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spatio-temporal_parallax">
        Spatio-temporal parallax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Spatio-temporal parallax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       From enhanced
       <i>
        relativistic positioning systems
       </i>
       ,
       <b>
        spatio-temporal parallax
       </b>
       generalizing the usual notion of parallax in space only has been developed. Then, eventfields in spacetime can be deduced directly without intermediate models of light bending by massive bodies such as the one used in the
       <a href="/wiki/Parameterized_post-Newtonian_formalism" title="Parameterized post-Newtonian formalism">
        PPN formalism
       </a>
       for instance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Metrology">
        <span id="Parallax_error">
        </span>
        Metrology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Metrology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Parallax_pointer_error.PNG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="428" data-file-width="462" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Parallax_pointer_error.PNG/220px-Parallax_pointer_error.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Parallax_pointer_error.PNG/330px-Parallax_pointer_error.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Parallax_pointer_error.PNG/440px-Parallax_pointer_error.PNG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Parallax_pointer_error.PNG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The correct line of sight needs to be used to avoid parallax error.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Measurements made by viewing the position of some marker relative to something to be measured are subject to parallax error if the marker is some distance away from the object of measurement and not viewed from the correct position. For example, if measuring the distance between two ticks on a line with a ruler marked on its top surface, the thickness of the ruler will separate its markings from the ticks. If viewed from a position not exactly perpendicular to the ruler, the apparent position will shift and the reading will be less accurate than the ruler is capable of.
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar error occurs when reading the position of a pointer against a scale in an instrument such as an analog
       <a href="/wiki/Multimeter" title="Multimeter">
        multimeter
       </a>
       . To help the user avoid this problem, the scale is sometimes printed above a narrow strip of
       <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">
        mirror
       </a>
       , and the user's eye is positioned so that the pointer obscures its own reflection, guaranteeing that the user's line of sight is perpendicular to the mirror and therefore to the scale. The same effect alters the speed read on a car's speedometer by a driver in front of it and a passenger off to the side, values read from a
       <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope#Graticule" title="Oscilloscope">
        graticule
       </a>
       not in actual contact with the display on an
       <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope" title="Oscilloscope">
        oscilloscope
       </a>
       , etc.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Photogrammetry">
        Photogrammetry
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Photogrammetry">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Aerial picture pairs, when viewed through a stereo viewer, offer a pronounced stereo effect of landscape and buildings. High buildings appear to 'keel over' in the direction away from the centre of the photograph. Measurements of this parallax are used to deduce the height of the buildings, provided that flying height and baseline distances are known. This is a key component to the process of
       <a href="/wiki/Photogrammetry" title="Photogrammetry">
        photogrammetry
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">
        Photography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Photography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Contax_III_IMG_5349-white.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="4000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Contax_III_IMG_5349-white.JPG/220px-Contax_III_IMG_5349-white.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Contax_III_IMG_5349-white.JPG/330px-Contax_III_IMG_5349-white.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Contax_III_IMG_5349-white.JPG/440px-Contax_III_IMG_5349-white.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Contax_III_IMG_5349-white.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Contax III rangefinder camera with
         <a href="/wiki/Macro_photography" title="Macro photography">
          macro photography
         </a>
         setting. Because the viewfinder is on top of the lens and of the close proximity of the subject, goggles are fitted in front of the rangefinder and a dedicated viewfinder installed to compensate for parallax.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Parallax error can be seen when taking photos with many types of cameras, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Twin-lens_reflex_camera" title="Twin-lens reflex camera">
        twin-lens reflex cameras
       </a>
       and those including
       <a href="/wiki/Viewfinder" title="Viewfinder">
        viewfinders
       </a>
       (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Rangefinder_camera" title="Rangefinder camera">
        rangefinder cameras
       </a>
       ). In such cameras, the eye sees the subject through different optics (the viewfinder, or a second lens) than the one through which the photo is taken. As the viewfinder is often found above the lens of the camera, photos with parallax error are often slightly lower than intended, the classic example being the image of person with his or her head cropped off. This problem is addressed in
       <a href="/wiki/Single-lens_reflex_camera" title="Single-lens reflex camera">
        single-lens reflex cameras
       </a>
       , in which the viewfinder sees through the same lens through which the photo is taken (with the aid of a movable mirror), thus avoiding parallax error.
      </p>
      <p>
       Parallax is also an issue in
       <a href="/wiki/Image_stitching" title="Image stitching">
        image stitching
       </a>
       , such as for panoramas.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sights">
        Sights
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sights">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Parallax affects
       <a href="/wiki/Sight_(device)" title="Sight (device)">
        sights
       </a>
       in many ways. On sights fitted to small arms, bows in archery, etc. the distance between the sighting mechanism and the weapon's bore or axis can introduce significant errors when firing at close range, particularly when firing at small targets. This difference is generally referred to as "
       <i>
        sight height
       </i>
       "
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and is compensated for (when needed) via calculations that also take in other variables such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bullet_drop" title="Bullet drop">
        bullet drop
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Windage" title="Windage">
        windage
       </a>
       , and the distance at which the target is expected to be.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sight height can be used to advantage when "sighting-in" rifles for field use. A typical hunting rifle (.222 with telescopic sights) sighted-in at 75m will be useful from 50m to 200m without further adjustment.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_sights">
        Optical sights
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Optical sights">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Telescopic_sight#Parallax_compensation" title="Telescopic sight">
        Telescopic sight § Parallax compensation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Sight_(device)#Optical_sights" title="Sight (device)">
        optical sights
       </a>
       parallax refers to the apparent movement of the
       <a href="/wiki/Reticle" title="Reticle">
        reticle
       </a>
       in relationship to the target when the user moves his/her head laterally behind the sight (up/down or left/right),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       i.e. it is an error where the reticle does not stay aligned with the sight's own
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_axis" title="Optical axis">
        optical axis
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In optical instruments such as
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescopes
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">
        microscopes
       </a>
       , or in
       <a href="/wiki/Telescopic_sight" title="Telescopic sight">
        telescopic sights
       </a>
       used on small arms and
       <a href="/wiki/Theodolite" title="Theodolite">
        theodolites
       </a>
       , the error occurs when the optics are not precisely focused: the reticle will appear to move with respect to the object focused on if one moves one's head sideways in front of the eyepiece. Some firearm telescopic sights are equipped with a parallax compensation mechanism which basically consists of a movable optical element that enables the optical system to project the picture of objects at varying distances and the reticle crosshairs pictures together in exactly the same optical plane. Telescopic sights may have no parallax compensation because they can perform very acceptably without refinement for parallax with the sight being permanently adjusted for the distance that best suits their intended usage. Typical standard factory parallax adjustment distances for hunting telescopic sights are 100 yd or 100 m to make them suited for hunting shots that rarely exceed 300 yd/m. Some target and military style telescopic sights without parallax compensation may be adjusted to be parallax free at ranges up to 300 yd/m to make them better suited for aiming at longer ranges.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Scopes for
       <a href="/wiki/Rimfire_ammunition" title="Rimfire ammunition">
        rimfires
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Shotgun" title="Shotgun">
        shotguns
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Muzzleloader" title="Muzzleloader">
        muzzleloaders
       </a>
       will have shorter parallax settings, commonly 50 yd/m
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       for rimfire scopes and 100 yd/m
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       for shotguns and muzzleloaders. Scopes for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airgun" title="Airgun">
        airguns
       </a>
       are very often found with adjustable parallax, usually in the form of an adjustable objective, or AO. These may adjust down as far as 3 yards (2.74 m).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Non-magnifying
       <a href="/wiki/Reflector_sight" title="Reflector sight">
        reflector or "reflex" sights
       </a>
       have the ability to be theoretically "parallax free." But since these sights use parallel
       <a href="/wiki/Collimated_light" title="Collimated light">
        collimated light
       </a>
       this is only true when the target is at infinity. At finite distances eye movement perpendicular to the device will cause parallax movement in the reticle image in exact relationship to eye position in the cylindrical column of light created by the collimating optics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_of_Bullseye_Pistol_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia_of_Bullseye_Pistol-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Firearm sights, such as some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_dot_sights" title="Red dot sights">
        red dot sights
       </a>
       , try to correct for this via not focusing the reticle at infinity, but instead at some finite distance, a designed target range where the reticle will show very little movement due to parallax.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_of_Bullseye_Pistol_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia_of_Bullseye_Pistol-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some manufactures market reflector sight models they call "parallax free,"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but this refers to an optical system that compensates for off axis
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_aberration" title="Spherical aberration">
        spherical aberration
       </a>
       , an optical error induced by the spherical mirror used in the sight that can cause the reticle position to diverge off the sight's
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_axis" title="Optical axis">
        optical axis
       </a>
       with change in eye position.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Artillery_gunfire">
        Artillery gunfire
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Artillery gunfire">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because of the positioning of
       <a href="/wiki/Field_artillery" title="Field artillery">
        field
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Naval_artillery" title="Naval artillery">
        naval artillery
       </a>
       guns, each one has a slightly different perspective of the target relative to the location of the
       <a href="/wiki/Fire-control_system" title="Fire-control system">
        fire-control system
       </a>
       itself. Therefore, when aiming its guns at the target, the fire control system must compensate for parallax in order to assure that
       <a href="/wiki/Gunshot" title="Gunshot">
        fire
       </a>
       from each gun converges on the target.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_metaphor">
        As a metaphor
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: As a metaphor">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In a philosophic/geometric sense: an apparent change in the direction of an object, caused by a change in observational position that provides a new line of sight. The apparent displacement, or difference of position, of an object, as seen from two different stations, or points of view. In contemporary writing parallax can also be the same story, or a similar story from approximately the same time line, from one book told from a different perspective in another book. The word and concept feature prominently in
       <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">
        James Joyce
       </a>
       's 1922 novel,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">
         Ulysses
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Orson_Scott_Card" title="Orson Scott Card">
        Orson Scott Card
       </a>
       also used the term when referring to
       <a href="/wiki/Ender%27s_Shadow" title="Ender's Shadow">
        Ender's Shadow
       </a>
       as compared to
       <a href="/wiki/Ender%27s_Game" title="Ender's Game">
        Ender's Game
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The metaphor is invoked by Slovenian philosopher
       <a href="/wiki/Slavoj_%C5%BDi%C5%BEek" title="Slavoj Žižek">
        Slavoj Žižek
       </a>
       in his work
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Parallax_View_(book)" title="The Parallax View (book)">
         The Parallax View
        </a>
       </i>
       , borrowing the concept of "parallax view" from the Japanese philosopher and literary critic
       <a href="/wiki/Kojin_Karatani" title="Kojin Karatani">
        Kojin Karatani
       </a>
       . Žižek notes,
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        The philosophical twist to be added (to parallax), of course, is that the observed distance is not simply subjective, since the same object that exists 'out there' is seen from two different stances, or points of view. It is rather that, as
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hegel" title="Hegel">
         Hegel
        </a>
        would have put it, subject and object are inherently mediated so that an '
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epistemological" title="Epistemological">
         epistemological
        </a>
        ' shift in the subject's point of view always reflects an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontological" title="Ontological">
         ontological
        </a>
        shift in the object itself. Or—to put it in
        <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Lacan" title="Jacques Lacan">
         Lacanese
        </a>
        —the subject's gaze is always-already inscribed into the perceived object itself, in the guise of its 'blind spot,' that which is 'in the object more than object itself', the point from which the object itself returns the gaze. Sure the picture is in my eye, but I am also in the picture.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
         <a href="#cite_note-37">
          [37]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Slavoj Žižek, The Parallax View
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
