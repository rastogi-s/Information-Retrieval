    New Mexico lunar sample displays

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        New Mexico lunar sample displays
       </b>
       are two
       <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_plaque" title="Commemorative plaque">
        commemorative plaques
       </a>
       consisting of small fragments of moon specimen brought back with the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        Apollo 17
       </a>
       lunar missions and given in the 1970s to the people of the state of
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       by United States President
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">
        Richard Nixon
       </a>
       as goodwill gifts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Mexico_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_11">
        Apollo 11
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Mexico_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Apollo 11">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11_lunar_sample_display" title="Apollo 11 lunar sample display">
        Apollo 11 lunar sample display
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_3.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="878" data-file-width="1469" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Moon_3.jpg/220px-Moon_3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Moon_3.jpg/330px-Moon_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Moon_3.jpg/440px-Moon_3.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_3.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Plaques on the California Apollo 11 lunar sample display, similar to the display in New Mexico
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The New Mexico Apollo 11 lunar sample display commemorative podium style plaque consists of four rice-size moon rock particle specimens that were collected by Apollo 11 astronauts
       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
        Buzz Aldrin
       </a>
       in 1969 and a small
       <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_New_Mexico" title="Flag of New Mexico">
        New Mexico state flag
       </a>
       that was taken to the moon and back on Apollo 11.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The four moon rocks weigh about 0.05 grams (0.0018 oz) total and are encased in a clear plastic button the size of a coin which is mounted to a wooden board approximately one foot square on a small
       <a href="/wiki/Podium" title="Podium">
        podium
       </a>
       pedestal display. The small podium plaque display also has mounted on it a small New Mexico state flag that had been to the moon and back that lies directly below the moon rocks. The small podium plaque display was given to the people of the state of New Mexico as a gift by President Richard Nixon. Similar tiny moon rocks were also distributed to the other 49 states.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_17">
        Apollo 17
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Mexico_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apollo 17">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NM_Apollo_17_display.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="706" data-file-width="587" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/NM_Apollo_17_display.jpg/220px-NM_Apollo_17_display.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/NM_Apollo_17_display.jpg/330px-NM_Apollo_17_display.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/NM_Apollo_17_display.jpg/440px-NM_Apollo_17_display.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NM_Apollo_17_display.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <center>
          New Mexico Apollo 17 display
         </center>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17_lunar_sample_display" title="Apollo 17 lunar sample display">
        Apollo 17 lunar sample display
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The New Mexico Apollo 17 lunar sample display commemorative style plaque, measuring 10 by 14 inches (250 mm × 360 mm), consists of one moon rock particle specimen that was cut from
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_basalt_70017" title="Lunar basalt 70017">
        lunar basalt 70017
       </a>
       and a New Mexico state flag. The
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       70017 was collected by Apollo 17 astronaut
       <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">
        Harrison Schmitt
       </a>
       on the moon in 1972. Once lunar basalt 70017 was brought back to earth from the moon, the basalt moon rock was cut up into small fragments of approximately 1 gram (0.035 oz). The specimen was encased in a plastic ball and mounted on the wooden plaque along with the New Mexico state flag which had been taken to the moon and back by the crew of Apollo 17. The plaque was then distributed in 1973 by President Richard Nixon to the state of New Mexico as he did that year to the other 49 states (the same as for the Apollo 11 plaque gifts). This was done as a goodwill gesture to promote peace and harmony.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Mexico_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The New Mexico Apollo 11 lunar sample display was presented by President Richard Nixon to then-Republican Governor
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_F._Cargo" title="David F. Cargo">
        David F. Cargo
       </a>
       . When the new Democratic Governor of New Mexico
       <a href="/wiki/Bruce_King" title="Bruce King">
        Bruce King
       </a>
       came into office on January 1, 1971, there was a clash between him and Cargo over the ownership of the New Mexico Apollo 11 goodwill moon rocks plaque display. Cargo argued that since the plaque was presented to him, it was his to take home when he left office on December 31, 1970. King felt the Apollo 11 commemorative plaque display belonged to the people of the state of New Mexico and should be housed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_New_Mexico" title="Museum of New Mexico">
        Museum of New Mexico
       </a>
       . Cargo argued that since the display with the moon rocks was given to him and not King, it was his right to give it to a museum if he wanted or not give it to them if he didn't want to.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disputed_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-disputed-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kingdisputes_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kingdisputes-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stirs_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-stirs-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The curator of the Museum of New Mexico, Dr. George Ewing, checked the White House records, which showed that the display belonged to the people of New Mexico.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disputed_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-disputed-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Indeed, the label affixed to the New Mexico Apollo 11 goodwill moon rocks display reads:
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         <p>
          Presented to the people of the state of New Mexico.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disputed_3-2">
           <a href="#cite_note-disputed-3">
            [3]
           </a>
          </sup>
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kingdisputes_4-1">
           <a href="#cite_note-Kingdisputes-4">
            [4]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </p>
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Cargo said he had been thinking about giving the New Mexico Apollo 11 lunar sample plaque display with the "moon rocks" to the Museum of New Mexico anyway.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stirs_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-stirs-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cargo was hoping that his astronaunt friend
       <a href="/wiki/James_McDivitt" title="James McDivitt">
        James McDivitt
       </a>
       would be present at the presentation of the New Mexico Apollo 11 plaque display, but McDevitt couldn't make it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ultimately the New Mexico Apollo 11 goodwill moon rocks display was presented to the Museum of New Mexico by Cargo in a formal ceremony attended by 50 people on November 9, 1971.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cargo had invited everyone in New Mexico to the ceremony presentation, except Governor King.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       While other goodwill moon rocks commemorative displays were reported lost or missing by many recipient states,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       both the New Mexico Apollo 11 and Apollo 17 goodwill moon rocks commemorative plaque displays are exhibited in the state. According to moon rocks researcher
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Pearlman" title="Robert Pearlman">
        Robert Pearlman
       </a>
       , the New Mexico Apollo 11 lunar sample display is exhibited at the
       <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_the_Governors" title="Palace of the Governors">
        Palace of the Governors
       </a>
       , a division of the Museum of New Mexico.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The New Mexico Apollo 17 goodwill moon rocks display is at the
       <a href="/wiki/Roswell_Museum_and_Art_Center" title="Roswell Museum and Art Center">
        Roswell Museum and Art Center
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Roswell,_New_Mexico" title="Roswell, New Mexico">
        Roswell, New Mexico
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
