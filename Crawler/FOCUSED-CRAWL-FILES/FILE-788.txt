        Lysithea

       (

       ,
       <span class="nowrap">

          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">

       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Λυσιθέα
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Seth_Barnes_Nicholson" title="Seth Barnes Nicholson">
        Seth Barnes Nicholson
       </a>
       in 1938 at
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory" title="Mount Wilson Observatory">
        Mount Wilson Observatory
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson_1938_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nicholson_1938-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and is named after the mythological
       <a href="/wiki/Lysithea_(mythology)" title="Lysithea (mythology)">
        Lysithea
       </a>
       , daughter of
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">
        Oceanus
       </a>
       and one of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       ' lovers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_2846_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_2846-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lysithea did not receive its present name until 1975; before then, it was simply known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter X
        </span>
       </b>
       . It was sometimes called "
       <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">
        Demeter
       </a>
       "
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaposchkin_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gaposchkin-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       from 1955 to 1975.
      </p>
      <p>
       It belongs to the
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_group" title="Himalia group">
        Himalia group
       </a>
       , five moons orbiting between 11 and 13 Gm from Jupiter at an inclination of about 28.3°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_2000_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson_2000-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its orbital elements are as of January 2000. They are continuously changing due to
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       and planetary perturbations.
      </p>
