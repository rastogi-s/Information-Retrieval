      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Lucky Starr and the Moons of Jupiter
        </b>
       </i>
       is the fifth novel in the
       <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Starr_series" title="Lucky Starr series">
        <i>
         Lucky Starr
        </i>
        series
       </a>
       , six juvenile
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       novels by
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">
        Isaac Asimov
       </a>
       that originally appeared under the pseudonym Paul French. The novel was first published by
       <a href="/wiki/Doubleday_(publisher)" title="Doubleday (publisher)">
        Doubleday &amp; Company
       </a>
       in August 1957. It is the only novel by Asimov set in the Jovian system.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">
        Setting
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucky_Starr_and_the_Moons_of_Jupiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Setting">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Lucky Starr and the Moons of Jupiter
       </i>
       takes place in the
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jovian
       </a>
       system. In the mid-1950s, when the novel was written, Jupiter had twelve known satellites. The first half of the novel takes place on what was then the outermost known satellite, Jupiter IX, discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Seth_Barnes_Nicholson" title="Seth Barnes Nicholson">
        Seth Barnes Nicholson
       </a>
       in 1914. Jupiter IX had been given the unofficial name 'Hades' in 1955, but in the novel Asimov mistakenly refers to it as
       <a href="/wiki/Adrastea_(moon)" title="Adrastea (moon)">
        Adrastea
       </a>
       , which was the unofficial name of Jupiter XII. The confusion doubtless arose from the fact that Jupiter IX was the twelfth farthest known satellite, while Jupiter XII was the ninth farthest known satellite. In 1975, the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       gave Jupiter IX the official name
       <a href="/wiki/Sinope_(moon)" title="Sinope (moon)">
        Sinope
       </a>
       . Asimov describes Jupiter IX as being 89 miles in diameter, but its diameter is now thought to be only 23 miles.
      </p>
      <p>
       Part of the novel is also set on
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       , the innermost of the
       <a href="/wiki/Galilean_moons" title="Galilean moons">
        Galilean moons
       </a>
       . Io is depicted as having a thin atmosphere of
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       , and fields of
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       snow and ice, as well as rivers of liquid ammonia.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">
        Plot summary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucky_Starr_and_the_Moons_of_Jupiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot summary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For six years, Jupiter IX has been the site of a secret project to develop an antigravity, or Agrav,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_drive" title="Space drive">
        space drive
       </a>
       ; but the Council of Science learns that information from the project is released to the enemy Sirians. A month after returning from
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       , protagonists David "Lucky" Starr and John Bigman Jones are sent to Jupiter IX to investigate, bringing a
       <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Starr_and_the_Oceans_of_Venus" title="Lucky Starr and the Oceans of Venus">
        V-frog
       </a>
       to aid the investigation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Upon reaching IX, Starr and Bigman are warned away by the head of the project, Commander Donahue, who argues the men on the project are angry at investigation and may threaten Starr's safety. Starr and Bigman land nevertheless, and are met by a large group of workers led by a man named Red Summers, who insists that Starr take part in a duel in an Agrav corridor against a much larger man called Big Armand. During the duel, Bigman realizes Summers is sabotaging Starr's Agrav harness and forces Summers to stop at gunpoint, whereupon Starr wins the duel and gains Big Armand's friendship.
      </p>
      <p>
       At their quarters, Starr and Bigman are met by their neighbor, a blind man named Harry Norrich, who tells Starr that Summers is a convict on Earth, but has earned responsibility on the project, and is hostile to Starr for fear of having his past crimes revealed; whereas when Norrich's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seeing-eye_dog" title="Seeing-eye dog">
        seeing-eye dog
       </a>
       died, Summers obtained another, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Shepherd_Dog" title="German Shepherd Dog">
        German Shepherd
       </a>
       named Mutt, and has done similar favors for other workers on the project. Starr assures Norrich that he has no intention of getting Summers in trouble for the duel.
      </p>
      <p>
       The next morning someone kills the V-frog while both men are distracted; whereafter Starr and Bigman tell Donahue and James Panner, the chief engineer on the project, that because the V-frog telepathically induced affection in all whom it met, only a
       <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">
        robot
       </a>
       , immune to emotion, could have killed it. It is then suggested that the Sirians are using
       <a href="/wiki/Android_(robot)" title="Android (robot)">
        android
       </a>
       spies throughout the Solar System.
      </p>
      <p>
       Donahue, unconvinced, orders the launch of an Agrav ship, the
       <i>
        Jovian Moon
       </i>
       , to
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       the following evening, and forbids Starr to conduct his investigation until after it returns. The
       <i>
        Jovian Moon
       </i>
       lifts off on schedule, its crew of seventeen including Donahue, Panner, Summers, Norrich, Mutt, Starr, and Bigman; the latter included because Starr believes the spy is on board.
      </p>
      <p>
       On Io, Bigman falls into an
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonial
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">
        river
       </a>
       , and is rescued by Mutt. After lift-off from Io, the Agrav drive fails, leaving the
       <i>
        Jovian Moon
       </i>
       falling towards Jupiter. Starr manages to land the ship on
       <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(moon)" title="Amalthea (moon)">
        Amalthea
       </a>
       , where they find that Red Summers is missing. An investigation reveals that Summers tricked Norrich into reporting him present on the ship while he was still on Io, whereupon Starr realizes that the Sirians had two agents planted in the Agrav project: the still-hidden robot spy, and the Earthman traitor Summers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Panner repairs the Agrav, and the
       <i>
        Jovian Moon
       </i>
       returns to Io, where Starr and Bigman locate Summers. Summers admits to working for the Sirians, but kills himself before revealing the identity of the robot spy. When Norrich helps bury Summers, Starr accuses him of being the robot, and Bigman threatens to shoot him; whereupon Mutt, coming to defend Norrich, is revealed as the robot himself.
      </p>
      <p>
       With the exposure of Mutt, Starr discerns that the Sirian spy ring on Earth must be the people who supplied Summers with Mutt, and who may be giving canid robot spies to others on Earth, and sets out to prevent them.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
        Reception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucky_Starr_and_the_Moons_of_Jupiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_Science_Fiction" title="Galaxy Science Fiction">
        <i>
         Galaxy
        </i>
       </a>
       's Floyd C. Gale reviewed the novel favorably, calling it the best of the Lucky Starr series and saying that Asimov "milks the situation for all its thrills, while painlessly imparting scientific lore and a code of ethics."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gale195809_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gale195809-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Villiers Gerson, writing in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       , declared it to be "well-written adventure".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">
        Themes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucky_Starr_and_the_Moons_of_Jupiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Themes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The introduction of the Sirians in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Starr_and_the_Pirates_of_the_Asteroids" title="Lucky Starr and the Pirates of the Asteroids">
         Lucky Starr and the Pirates of the Asteroids
        </a>
       </i>
       marked the beginning of Asimov's transformation of the Lucky Starr series from the 'masked crimefighter' subgenre to the
       <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">
        espionage
       </a>
       subgenre. For the next two novels in the series, the Sirians served as a distraction from the real solution to the mystery; but in
       <i>
        Rings of Saturn
       </i>
       the Sirians are villains in fact. As with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Starr_and_the_Big_Sun_of_Mercury" title="Lucky Starr and the Big Sun of Mercury">
         Lucky Starr and the Big Sun of Mercury
        </a>
       </i>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Three_Laws_of_Robotics" title="Three Laws of Robotics">
        Three Laws of Robotics
       </a>
       plays a part in the novel, revealing Asimov as the true author, though for the sake of continuity the novel was published under the
       <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">
        pseudonym
       </a>
       'Paul French'.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Lucky Starr series was written over a period of six years, during which Asimov also wrote his first two robot novels and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_End_of_Eternity" title="The End of Eternity">
         The End of Eternity
        </a>
       </i>
       . Consequently, there are small inconsistencies between the various novels. In the first,
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       is mentioned as a settled world of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       , although in later novels Ganymede has no permanent settlement, and has even served as the location of a secret Sirian base. The first man on
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       is named Wingrad in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/David_Starr,_Space_Ranger" title="David Starr, Space Ranger">
         David Starr, Space Ranger
        </a>
       </i>
       , but is named Ching in
       <i>
        Moons of Jupiter
       </i>
       . The name of the state that rules the Solar System also changes from novel to novel. In
       <i>
        Pirates of the Asteroids
       </i>
       , it is the Terrestrial Empire; in
       <i>
        Oceans of Venus
       </i>
       , it is the Solar Confederation; in
       <i>
        Big Sun of Mercury
       </i>
       , it is the Terran Federation; and in
       <i>
        Moons of Jupiter
       </i>
       it is the Solar Federation of Worlds.
      </p>
      <p>
       The book has a number of plot points in common with Asimov's later novel
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robots_of_Dawn" title="Robots of Dawn">
         Robots of Dawn
        </a>
       </i>
       . In both novels, the investigator manages to come up with the solution when half asleep, but forgets it when awake, and considers a psychic probe as a possible way of digging it out. In both cases, the robot responsible for the deed gives itself away by finding an unconscious person despite the odds; Mutt, by infrared vision despite the impossibility of using the sense of smell, and Giskard, due to his abilities but allegedly due to his infrared vision. Telepathy, contradictions between the Three Laws and
       <a href="/wiki/Industrial_espionage" title="Industrial espionage">
        industrial espionage
       </a>
       are all important themes in both books.
      </p>
