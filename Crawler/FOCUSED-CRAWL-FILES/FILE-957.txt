      <p>
       <b>
        Ferdinand
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'f' in 'find'">
            f
           </span>
           <span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">
            ɜːr
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          FUR
         </span>
         -din-and
        </i>
       </a>
       ) is the outermost
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prograde_and_retrograde_motion" title="Prograde and retrograde motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       . It was first seen near Uranus by
       <a href="/wiki/Matthew_J._Holman" title="Matthew J. Holman">
        Matthew J. Holman
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/John_J._Kavelaars" title="John J. Kavelaars">
        John J. Kavelaars
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dan_Milisavljevic" title="Dan Milisavljevic">
        Dan Milisavljevic
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Brett_J._Gladman" title="Brett J. Gladman">
        Brett J. Gladman
       </a>
       on August 13, 2001 and reobserved on September 21, 2001. The object was then lost with no confirmation it was actually orbiting around Uranus. On August 29 and 30, 2003 a team lead by
       <a href="/wiki/Scott_S._Sheppard" title="Scott S. Sheppard">
        Scott S. Sheppard
       </a>
       surveyed the sky around Uranus with the Subaru telescope and detected two unknown objects near Uranus. These two new objects were reoberved by Sheppard et al. with the Gemini telescope on September 20, 2003 and reported to the minor planet center as possible new moons of Uranus. On September 24, 2003, Brian Marsden, at the minor planet center, linked one of the unknown objects reported by Sheppard et al. to the lost object observed by Holman et al. in 2001. Holman was then able to confirm the linkage between the 2001 and 2003 objects on September 30, 2003 by observing its location with the Magellan telescope. The linked 2001 and 2003 object was then given the provisional designation
       <b>
        S/2001 U 2
       </b>
       on October 1, 2003, officially identifying it as a new moon of Uranus. Now Designated
       <b>
        Uranus XXIV
       </b>
       , Ferdinand is named after the son of the King of Naples in
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        William Shakespeare
       </a>
       's play
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Tempest" title="The Tempest">
         The Tempest
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The second new object observed by Sheppard et al. in 2003 was also a new moon of Uranus, now named Margaret.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ferdinand_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TheIrregulars_URANUS_retro.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="400" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/TheIrregulars_URANUS_retro.svg/300px-TheIrregulars_URANUS_retro.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/TheIrregulars_URANUS_retro.svg/450px-TheIrregulars_URANUS_retro.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/TheIrregulars_URANUS_retro.svg/600px-TheIrregulars_URANUS_retro.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TheIrregulars_URANUS_retro.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Retrograde irregular satellites of Uranus.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ferdinand is the most distant known satellite of Uranus. It follows a retrograde, modestly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclined
       </a>
       but highly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentric
       </a>
       orbit. The diagram illustrates the orbital parameters of the retrograde
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellites
       </a>
       of Uranus (in polar co-ordinates) with the eccentricity of the orbits represented by the segments extending from the

        pericentre
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        apocentre
       </a>
