      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Altair
       </b>
       spacecraft, previously known as the
       <b>
        Lunar Surface Access Module
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        LSAM
       </b>
       , was the planned
       <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">
        lander spacecraft
       </a>
       component of
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA's
       </a>
       cancelled
       <a href="/wiki/Constellation_program" title="Constellation program">
        Constellation program
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        Astronauts
       </a>
       would have used the spacecraft for landings on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , which was intended to begin around 2019. The Altair spacecraft was planned to be used both for
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_sortie" title="Lunar sortie">
        lunar sortie
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_outpost_(NASA)" title="Lunar outpost (NASA)">
        lunar outpost
       </a>
       missions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On February 1, 2010, U.S. President
       <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">
        Barack Obama
       </a>
       announced a proposal to cancel the Constellation program (except the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_spacecraft" title="Orion spacecraft">
        Orion spacecraft
       </a>
       ), to be replaced with a re-scoped program, effective with the U.S. 2011 fiscal year budget.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altair_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       On December 13, 2007, NASA's Lunar Surface Access Module was retitled "Altair", after the 12th brightest star in the northern hemisphere's night sky,
       <a href="/wiki/Altair" title="Altair">
        Altair
       </a>
       in the constellation
       <a href="/wiki/Aquila_(constellation)" title="Aquila (constellation)">
        Aquila
       </a>
       . In Latin,
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         <i>
          aquila
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       means "
       <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">
        eagle
       </a>
       ", providing a connection to the first crewed lunar landing,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Eagle
       </i>
       ; the name
       <i>
        Altair
       </i>
       itself is a latinization of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        الطائر
       </span>
       <i>
        <span lang="ar-Latn" xml:lang="ar-Latn">
         <i>
          al-ṭā'ir
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       , meaning "the eagle," "the bird," or "the flyer."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Prior to the announcement of the "Altair" name, reports had suggested other names had been considered by NASA,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but Altair won in a vote by the design team over
       <a href="/wiki/Pegasus" title="Pegasus">
        Pegasus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altair_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       NASA developed only conceptual designs for Altair. No Altair spacecraft were built—plans called for a first landing on the Moon in 2018.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Like the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
        Apollo Lunar Module
       </a>
       (LM), Altair was envisioned as having two
       <a href="/wiki/Multistage_rocket" title="Multistage rocket">
        stages
       </a>
       . The descent stage would have housed the majority of the fuel, power supplies, and breathing oxygen for the crew. The ascent stage would house the astronauts, life-support equipment, and fuel for the ascent-stage motor and steering rockets. Like the Apollo LM, the Altair's crew cabin was based on that of a cylinder. Initially a horizontal cylinder, like that of the LM (despite the "boxy" appearance on the outside), current blueprints and computer simulations show the use of a vertical cylinder.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Unlike its two-man Apollo ancestor, Altair was designed to carry the entire four-person crew to the surface, while the temporarily unoccupied
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_(Constellation_program)" title="Orion (Constellation program)">
        Orion
       </a>
       crew module would have remained in lunar orbit.
      </p>
      <p>
       Altair was intended to be capable of operating away from Earth (in space and on the lunar surface) for up to 210 Earth days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewAltair_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NewAltair-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Altair would also be capable of flying unmanned missions,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewAltair_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NewAltair-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as had been proposed with
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module#LM_Truck" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
        LM Truck
       </a>
       concept during the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Applications_Program" title="Apollo Applications Program">
        Apollo Applications Program
       </a>
       . Mission planners would have been able to choose among three distinct mission modes for Altair:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewAltair_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-NewAltair-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Crewed sortie mode
       </li>
       <li>
        Crewed outpost mode (with no airlock)
       </li>
       <li>
        Uncrewed cargo mode, capable of transporting up to 15 metric tons to the lunar surface
       </li>
      </ol>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Altair_in_rocket_fairing.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1183" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Altair_in_rocket_fairing.jpg/220px-Altair_in_rocket_fairing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Altair_in_rocket_fairing.jpg/330px-Altair_in_rocket_fairing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Altair_in_rocket_fairing.jpg/440px-Altair_in_rocket_fairing.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Altair_in_rocket_fairing.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Artist's rendering of Altair being encased in a shroud
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Altair, like the LM, was planned to have two hatches; one on top for docking and internal transfer between Altair and Orion, and a main hatch for accessing the lunar surface. Unlike the Apollo LM, Altair would have an airlock similar to those on the
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">
        Space Shuttle
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">
        International Space Station
       </a>
       between the cabin and main hatch. The airlock allowed the astronauts to don and doff their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacesuit" title="Spacesuit">
        spacesuits
       </a>
       without tracking
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil#Harmful_effects_of_lunar_dust" title="Lunar soil">
        potentially hazardous moon rocks
       </a>
       into the main cabin and allowed the vehicle to retain its internal pressure.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Unlike the Apollo LM, in which the entire cabin was depressurized during
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">
        extra-vehicular activity
       </a>
       , the airlock would allow a crew member with a malfunctioning spacesuit to quickly return to the Altair spacecraft without having to terminate the entire EVA, and allowed the landing party to complete most of their tasks during their 7-day lunar stay. Also, the airlock would remain as part of the Altair's descent stage, allowing NASA to utilize the airlock as a component of the Lunar Outpost.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because the
       <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">
        Ares V
       </a>
       payload shroud was planned to have a diameter of 33 feet (10 m) and height of 49 feet (15 m) (including landing gear), the landers were designed to retract so as to fit within the Ares V's payload shroud.
      </p>
      <p>
       The spacecraft would also have included an improved miniature camping-style toilet, similar to the unit now used on the ISS and the Russian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">
        Soyuz spacecraft
       </a>
       , a food warmer to eliminate the "cold soup" menu used during Apollo missions, a laser-guided distance measurement system (with radar backup), using data acquired by advanced unmanned lunar orbiting spacecraft, and new "
       <a href="/wiki/Glass_cockpit" title="Glass cockpit">
        glass cockpit
       </a>
       " and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_787" title="Boeing 787">
        Boeing 787
       </a>
       -based computer system identical to that on the Orion spacecraft.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Engines">
        Engines
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altair_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Engines">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Altair_-_cargo_mode.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="349" data-file-width="360" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Altair_-_cargo_mode.jpg/200px-Altair_-_cargo_mode.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Altair_-_cargo_mode.jpg/300px-Altair_-_cargo_mode.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1b/Altair_-_cargo_mode.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Altair_-_cargo_mode.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Cargo variant of Altair
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Altair intended to utilize current
       <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_fuel" title="Cryogenic fuel">
        cryogenic
       </a>
       technologies for the descent stages and
       <a href="/wiki/Hypergolic_propellant" title="Hypergolic propellant">
        hypergolic
       </a>
       technologies for the ascent stage. The Apollo LM, as advanced in both computer and engineering technology in its day, used hypergolic fuels in both of its stages, chemicals that combust on contact with each other, requiring no ignition mechanism and allowing an indefinite storage period. Both the cryogenic and hypergolic systems, like that of the Apollo LM, would be force-fed using high-pressure
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       , eliminating the need for malfunction-prone pumps utilized in most rocket technology.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mission requirements obliged the vehicle to be able to descend from an equatorial or high-inclination lunar orbit to a polar landing site, along with bringing it and the Orion spacecraft into lunar orbit, as the Orion spacecraft's onboard
       <a href="/wiki/Aerojet" title="Aerojet">
        Aerojet
       </a>
       AJ-10 rocket engine and the amount of fuel it carried would have been insufficient to brake the Orion/Altair stack into lunar orbit (also necessary if flown without Orion for cargo-only missions). The new lander would have been powered by a modified
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RL-10" title="RL-10">
        RL-10
       </a>
       engine (currently in use on the upper stage of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_IV_rocket" title="Delta IV rocket">
        Delta IV rocket
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket)" title="Centaur (rocket)">
        Centaur
       </a>
       upper stage of the
       <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">
        Atlas V
       </a>
       rocket), burning
       <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">
        liquid hydrogen
       </a>
       (LH
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">
        liquid oxygen
       </a>
       (LOX) for the descent phase. A single AJ-10 rocket engine, like that on the Orion, was intended to power the ascent stage.
      </p>
      <p>
       Originally, NASA wanted to power the ascent stage using LOX and liquid
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       (LCH
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/RS-18" title="RS-18">
        RS-18
       </a>
       , as future missions to
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       would require the astronauts to live on the planet. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sabatier_process" title="Sabatier process">
        Sabatier Reactor
       </a>
       could be used to convert the carbon dioxide (CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       ) found on Mars into methane, using either found or transported hydrogen, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">
        catalyst
       </a>
       , and a source of heat. Cost overruns and immature LOX/LCH
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       rocket technology forced NASA to stick with cryogenic and hypergolic systems, although later variants of Altair were meant to serve as testbeds for methane rockets and Sabatier reactors after a permanent lunar base was established.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="On-orbit_assembly">
        On-orbit assembly
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altair_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: On-orbit assembly">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because of the spacecraft's size and weight, Altair, and its associated
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_Departure_Stage" title="Earth Departure Stage">
        Earth Departure Stage
       </a>
       , would have been launched into a low-Earth orbit (
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
        LEO
       </a>
       ) using the heavy-lift
       <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">
        Ares V
       </a>
       launch vehicle, followed by a separate launch of an Orion spacecraft lifted by an
       <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">
        Ares I
       </a>
       . After rendezvous and docking with Altair in LEO, the crew would have then configured the Orion/Altair for the journey to the Moon.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Offices_and_development">
        Offices and development
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altair_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Offices and development">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The development of Altair would have been managed by the Constellation Lunar Lander Project Office at
       <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center">
        Johnson Space Center
       </a>
       (JSC). JSC worked directly with Apollo astronauts, various industry suppliers and universities to develop the architecture for Altair. In conjunction with early development a mockup or testbed was to have been developed at JSC to study/develop specialized subsystems and other design considerations.
       <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">
        Northrop Grumman
       </a>
       , which built the Apollo Lunar Module, was contracted to help the project office develop the system concept.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
