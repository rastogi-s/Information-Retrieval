   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lagrangian point
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       "Lagrange Point" redirects here. For the video game, see
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrange_Point_(video_game)" title="Lagrange Point (video game)">

      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_mechanics" title="Celestial mechanics">
        celestial mechanics
       </a>
       , the
       <b>
        Lagrangian points
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">
            ɡ
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">
            ɑː
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">
            dʒ
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
           <span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">
            ən
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ; also
       <b>
        Lagrange points
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        L-points
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        libration points
       </b>
       ) are positions in an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbital
       </a>
       configuration of two large bodies where a small object affected only by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
        gravity
       </a>
       can maintain its position relative to the two large bodies. The Lagrange points mark positions where the combined
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_pull" title="Gravitational pull">
        gravitational pull
       </a>
       of the two large masses provides precisely the
       <a href="/wiki/Centripetal_force" title="Centripetal force">
        centripetal force
       </a>
       required to
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       with them. There are five such points, labeled L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       to L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       , all in the orbital plane of the two large bodies. The first three are on the line connecting the two large bodies; the last two, L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       and L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       , each form an
       <a href="/wiki/Equilateral_triangle" title="Equilateral triangle">
        equilateral triangle
       </a>
       with the two large bodies. The two latter points are stable, which implies that objects can orbit around them in a rotating
       <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">
        coordinate system
       </a>
       tied to the two large bodies.
      </p>
      <p>
       Several planets have
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_(astronomy)" title="Trojan (astronomy)">
        satellites near their L
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
        and L
        <sub>
         5
        </sub>
        points
       </a>
       (trojans) with respect to the Sun, with
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter#Interaction_with_the_Solar_System" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       in particular having more than a million of these.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_satellite" title="Artificial satellite">
        Artificial satellites
       </a>
       have been placed at L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       and L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       with respect to the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , and Earth and the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , for various purposes,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the Lagrangian points have been proposed for a variety of future uses in space exploration.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lagrangian_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The three collinear Lagrange points (L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       , L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , L
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       ) were discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">
        Leonhard Euler
       </a>
       a few years before Lagrange discovered the remaining two.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E327_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-E327-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1772,
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange" title="Joseph-Louis Lagrange">
        Joseph-Louis Lagrange
       </a>
       published an "Essay on the
       <a href="/wiki/Three-body_problem" title="Three-body problem">
        three-body problem
       </a>
       ". In the first chapter he considered the general three-body problem. From that, in the second chapter, he demonstrated two special
       <a href="/wiki/Three-body_problem#Constant-pattern_solutions" title="Three-body problem">
        constant-pattern solutions
       </a>
       , the collinear and the equilateral, for any three masses, with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circular_orbits" title="Circular orbits">
        circular orbits
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gallica.bnf.fr_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gallica.bnf.fr-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lagrange_points">
        Lagrange points
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lagrangian_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lagrange points">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The five Lagrangian points are labeled and defined as follows:
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </b>
       point lies on the line defined by the two large masses
       <i>
        M
       </i>
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       and
       <i>
        M
       </i>
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , and between them. It is the most intuitively understood of the Lagrangian points: the one where the gravitational attraction of
       <i>
        M
       </i>
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       partially cancels
       <i>
        M
       </i>
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       's gravitational attraction.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Explanation
       </dt>
       <dd>
        An object that
        <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
         orbits
        </a>
        the
        <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
         Sun
        </a>
        more closely than
        <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
         Earth
        </a>
        would normally have a shorter orbital period than Earth, but that ignores the effect of Earth's own gravitational pull. If the object is directly between Earth and the Sun, then Earth's gravity counteracts some of the Sun's pull on the object, and therefore increases the orbital period of the object. The closer to Earth the object is, the greater this effect is. At the L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
        point, the orbital period of the object becomes exactly equal to Earth's orbital period. L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
        is about 1.5 million kilometers from Earth.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lagrange_Cornish_5-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lagrange_Cornish-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </b>
       point lies on the line through the two large masses, beyond the smaller of the two. Here, the gravitational forces of the two large masses balance the centrifugal effect on a body at L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       .
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Explanation
       </dt>
       <dd>
        On the opposite side of Earth from the Sun, the orbital period of an object would normally be greater than that of Earth. The extra pull of Earth's gravity decreases the orbital period of the object, and at the L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
        point that orbital period becomes equal to Earth's. Like L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
        , L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
        is about 1.5 million kilometers from Earth.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        L
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
       </b>
       point lies on the line defined by the two large masses, beyond the larger of the two.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Explanation
       </dt>
       <dd>
        L
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
        in the Sun–Earth system exists on the opposite side of the Sun, a little outside Earth's orbit but slightly closer to the Sun than Earth is. (This apparent contradiction is because the Sun is also affected by Earth's gravity, and so orbits around the two bodies'
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barycentric_coordinates_(astronomy)" title="Barycentric coordinates (astronomy)">
         barycenter
        </a>
        , which is, however, well inside the body of the Sun.) At the L
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
        point, the combined pull of Earth and Sun again causes the object to orbit with the same period as Earth.
       </dd>
      </dl>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:L4_diagram.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Gravitational accelerations at L
         <sub>
          4
         </sub>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        L
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        L
        <sub>
         5
        </sub>
       </b>
       points lie at the third corners of the two
       <a href="/wiki/Equilateral_triangle" title="Equilateral triangle">
        equilateral triangles
       </a>
       in the plane of orbit whose common base is the line between the centers of the two masses, such that the point lies behind (L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       ) or ahead (L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       ) of the smaller mass with regard to its orbit around the larger mass.
      </p>
      <p>
       The triangular points (L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       and L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       ) are stable equilibria, provided that the ratio of
       <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;">
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">
         <i>
          M
         </i>
         <sub>
          1
         </sub>
        </span>
        <span class="visualhide">
         /
        </span>
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">
         <i>
          M
         </i>
         <sub>
          2
         </sub>
        </span>
       </span>
       is greater than 24.96.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cornish_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cornish-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is the case for the Sun–Earth system, the Sun–Jupiter system, and, by a
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon#Inclination" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        smaller margin
       </a>
       , the Earth–Moon system. When a body at these points is perturbed, it moves away from the point, but the factor opposite of that which is increased or decreased by the perturbation (either gravity or angular momentum-induced speed) will also increase or decrease, bending the object's path into a stable,
       <a href="/wiki/Kidney_bean" title="Kidney bean">
        kidney bean
       </a>
       -shaped orbit around the point (as seen in the corotating frame of reference).
      </p>
      <p>
       In contrast to L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       and L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       , where
       <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">
        stable equilibrium
       </a>
       exists, the points L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       , L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , and L
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       are positions of
       <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">
        unstable equilibrium
       </a>
       . Any object orbiting at L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       , L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , or L
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       will tend to fall out of orbit; it is therefore rare to find natural objects there, and spacecraft inhabiting these areas must employ
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_station-keeping" title="Orbital station-keeping">
        station keeping
       </a>
       in order to maintain their position.
      </p>

      <p>
       Lagrangian points are the constant-pattern solutions of the restricted
       <a href="/wiki/Three-body_problem" title="Three-body problem">
        three-body problem
       </a>
       . For example, given two massive bodies in orbits around their common
       <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">
        barycenter
       </a>
       , there are five positions in space where a third body, of comparatively negligible
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        mass
       </a>
       , could be placed so as to maintain its position relative to the two massive bodies. As seen in a
       <a href="/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame" title="Rotating reference frame">
        rotating reference frame
       </a>
       that matches the
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">
        angular velocity
       </a>
       of the two co-orbiting bodies, the
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_field" title="Gravitational field">
        gravitational fields
       </a>
       of two massive bodies combined with the minor body's centrifugal force are in balance at the Lagrangian points, allowing the smaller third body to be relatively stationary with respect to the first two.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="L4_and_L5">
        L
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
        and L
        <sub>
         5
        </sub>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lagrangian_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: L4 and L5">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_(astronomy)" title="Trojan (astronomy)">
        Trojan (astronomy)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The reason these points are in balance is that, at L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       and L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       , the distances to the two masses are equal. Accordingly, the gravitational forces from the two massive bodies are in the same ratio as the masses of the two bodies, and so the resultant force acts through the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barycentric_coordinates_(astronomy)" title="Barycentric coordinates (astronomy)">
        barycenter
       </a>
       of the system; additionally, the geometry of the triangle ensures that the
       <a href="/wiki/Parallelogram_law" title="Parallelogram law">
        resultant
       </a>
       acceleration is to the distance from the barycenter in the same
       <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">
        ratio
       </a>
       as for the two massive bodies. The barycenter being both the
       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
        center of mass
       </a>
       and center of rotation of the three-body system, this resultant force is exactly that required to keep the smaller body at the Lagrange point in orbital
       <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_equilibrium" title="Dynamic equilibrium">
        equilibrium
       </a>
       with the other two larger bodies of system. (Indeed, the third body need not have negligible mass.) The general triangular configuration was discovered by Lagrange in work on the
       <a href="/wiki/Three-body_problem" title="Three-body problem">
        three-body problem
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stability">
        Stability
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lagrangian_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Stability">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although the L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       , L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , and L
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       points are nominally unstable, there are (unstable) periodic orbits called
       <a href="/wiki/Halo_orbit" title="Halo orbit">
        "halo" orbits
       </a>
       around these points in a three-body system. A full
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -body
       <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">
        dynamical system
       </a>
       such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       does not contain these periodic orbits, but does contain quasi-periodic (i.e. bounded but not precisely repeating) orbits following
       <a href="/wiki/Lissajous_curve" title="Lissajous curve">
        Lissajous-curve
       </a>
       trajectories. These quasi-periodic
       <a href="/wiki/Lissajous_orbit" title="Lissajous orbit">
        Lissajous orbits
       </a>
       are what most of Lagrangian-point space missions have used until now. Although they are not perfectly stable, a modest effort of
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_station-keeping" title="Orbital station-keeping">
        station keeping
       </a>
       keeps a spacecraft in a desired Lissajous orbit for a long time. Also, for Sun–Earth-L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       missions, it is preferable for the spacecraft to be in a large-amplitude (100,000–200,000 km or 62,000–124,000 mi) Lissajous orbit around L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       than to stay at L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       , because the line between Sun and Earth has increased solar
       <a href="/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation)" title="Interference (wave propagation)">
        interference
       </a>
       on Earth–spacecraft communications. Similarly, a large-amplitude Lissajous orbit around L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       keeps a probe out of Earth's shadow and therefore ensure a better illumination of its solar panels.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spaceflight_applications">
        Spaceflight applications
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lagrangian_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Spaceflight applications">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="Sun.E2.80.93Earth">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sun–Earth">
        Sun–Earth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lagrangian_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sun–Earth">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ACE_at_L1.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="288" data-file-width="576" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/ACE_at_L1.png/300px-ACE_at_L1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/ACE_at_L1.png/450px-ACE_at_L1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/71/ACE_at_L1.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ACE_at_L1.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The satellite
         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ACE_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ACE (satellite) (page does not exist)">
          ACE
         </a>
         in an orbit around L
         <sub>
          1
         </sub>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Sun–Earth L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       is suited for making observations of the Sun–Earth system. Objects here are never shadowed by Earth or the Moon and, if observing Earth, always view the sunlit hemisphere. The first mission of this type was the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Cometary_Explorer" title="International Cometary Explorer">
        International Sun Earth Explorer 3
       </a>
       (ISEE-3) mission used as an interplanetary early warning storm monitor for solar disturbances. Since June 2015,
       <a href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory" title="Deep Space Climate Observatory">
        DSCOVR
       </a>
       has orbited the L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       point. Conversely it is also useful for space-based
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_telescope" title="Solar telescope">
        solar telescopes
       </a>
       , because it provides an uninterrupted view of the Sun and any
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">
        space weather
       </a>
       (including the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejections" title="Coronal mass ejections">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       ) reaches L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       a few hours before Earth. Solar telescopes currently located around L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       include the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Composition_Explorer" title="Advanced Composition Explorer">
        Advanced Composition Explorer
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Sun–Earth L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       is a good spot for space-based observatories. Because an object around L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       will maintain the same relative position with respect to the Sun and Earth, shielding and calibration are much simpler. It is, however, slightly beyond the reach of Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Umbra,_penumbra_and_antumbra" title="Umbra, penumbra and antumbra">
        umbra
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       so solar radiation is not completely blocked at L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       . (Real spacecraft generally orbit around L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , avoiding partial eclipses of the Sun to maintain a constant temperature). From locations near L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , the Sun, Earth and Moon are relatively close together in the sky; this means that a large sunshade with the telescope on the dark-side can allow the telescope to cool passively to around 50 K – this is especially helpful for
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared_astronomy" title="Infrared astronomy">
        infrared astronomy
       </a>
       and observations of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">
        cosmic microwave background
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">
        James Webb Space Telescope
       </a>
       is due to be positioned at L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Sun–Earth L
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       was a popular place to put a "
       <a href="/wiki/Counter-Earth" title="Counter-Earth">
        Counter-Earth
       </a>
       " in
       <a href="/wiki/Pulp_magazine" title="Pulp magazine">
        pulp
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">
        comic books
       </a>
       . Once space-based observation became possible via satellites
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and probes, it was shown to hold no such object. The Sun–Earth L
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       is unstable and could not contain a natural object, large or small, for very long. This is because the gravitational forces of the other planets are stronger than that of Earth (
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       , for example, comes within 0.3
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        AU
       </a>
       of this L
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       every 20 months).
      </p>
      <p>
       A spacecraft orbiting near Sun–Earth L
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       would be able to closely monitor the evolution of active sunspot regions before they rotate into a geoeffective position, so that a 7-day early warning could be issued by the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">
        NOAA
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Weather_Prediction_Center" title="Space Weather Prediction Center">
        Space Weather Prediction Center
       </a>
       . Moreover, a satellite near Sun–Earth L
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       would provide very important observations not only for Earth forecasts, but also for deep space support (Mars predictions and for manned mission to
       <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object#Near-Earth_asteroids" title="Near-Earth object">
        near-Earth asteroids
       </a>
       ). In 2010, spacecraft transfer trajectories to Sun–Earth L
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       were studied and several designs were considered.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transferslibthree_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-transferslibthree-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Missions to Lagrangian points generally orbit the points rather than occupy them directly.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another interesting and useful property of the collinear Lagrangian points and their associated Lissajous orbits is that they serve as "gateways" to control the chaotic trajectories of the
       <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_Transport_Network" title="Interplanetary Transport Network">
        Interplanetary Transport Network
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Earth.E2.80.93Moon">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Earth–Moon">
        Earth–Moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lagrangian_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Earth–Moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Earth–Moon L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       allows comparatively easy access to Lunar and Earth orbits with minimal change in velocity and this has as an advantage to position a half-way manned space station intended to help transport cargo and personnel to the Moon and back.
      </p>
      <p>
       Earth–Moon L
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       would be a good location for a
       <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">
        communications satellite
       </a>
       covering the Moon's far side and would be "an ideal location" for a
       <a href="/wiki/Propellant_depot" title="Propellant depot">
        propellant depot
       </a>
       as part of the proposed depot-based space transportation architecture.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aiaa20100902_p4_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-aiaa20100902_p4-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Sun.E2.80.93Venus">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sun–Venus">
        Sun–Venus
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lagrangian_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sun–Venus">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Scientists at the

      <h3>
       <span id="Spacecraft_at_Sun.E2.80.93Earth_L1">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_at_Sun–Earth_L1">
        Spacecraft at Sun–Earth L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lagrangian_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Spacecraft at Sun–Earth L1">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/International_Cometary_Explorer" title="International Cometary Explorer">
        International Sun Earth Explorer 3
       </a>
       (ISEE-3) began its mission at the Sun–Earth L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       before leaving to intercept a comet in 1982. The Sun–Earth L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       is also the point to which the Reboot ISEE-3 mission was attempting to return the craft as the first phase of a recovery mission (as of September 25, 2014 all efforts have failed and contact was lost).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
       </a>
       (SOHO) is stationed in a halo orbit at L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Composition_Explorer" title="Advanced Composition Explorer">
        Advanced Composition Explorer
       </a>
       (ACE) in a
       <a href="/wiki/Lissajous_orbit" title="Lissajous orbit">
        Lissajous orbit
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/WIND_(spacecraft)" title="WIND (spacecraft)">
        WIND
       </a>
       is also at L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory" title="Deep Space Climate Observatory">
        Deep Space Climate Observatory
       </a>
       (DSCOVR), launched on 11 February 2015, began orbiting L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       on 8 June 2015 to study the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       and its effects on Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       DSCOVR is unofficially known as GORESAT, because it carries a camera always oriented to Earth and capturing full-frame photos of the planet similar to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Marble" title="Blue Marble">
        Blue Marble
       </a>
       . This concept was proposed by then-Vice President of the United States
       <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">
        Al Gore
       </a>
       in 1998
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was a centerpiece in his film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/An_Inconvenient_Truth" title="An Inconvenient Truth">
         An Inconvenient Truth
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/LISA_Pathfinder" title="LISA Pathfinder">
        LISA Pathfinder
       </a>
       (LPF) was launched on 3 December 2015, and arrived at L
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       on 22 January 2016, where, among other experiments, it will test the technology needed by (e)LISA to detect gravitational waves. LISA Pathfinder uses an instrument consisting of two small gold alloy cubes.
      </p>
