      <p>
       <b>
        Styx
       </b>
       is a small
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Pluto" title="Moons of Pluto">
        natural satellite of Pluto
       </a>
       whose discovery was announced on 11 July 2012. It was imaged along with
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and Pluto's other moons by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft in July 2015. A single image was returned.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPSC_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-LPSC-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Styx is the second satellite of
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       by distance and the fifth discovered. It was discovered one year after
       <a href="/wiki/Kerberos_(moon)" title="Kerberos (moon)">
        Kerberos
       </a>
       . Styx is approximately
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="nowrap">
         <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
          7001130000000000000♠
         </span>
         13 × 8
        </span>
        km
       </span>
       across,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPSC_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-LPSC-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and its orbital period is 20.2 days.
      </p>

       Styx was discovered by a team led by astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_R._Showalter" title="Mark R. Showalter">
        Mark R. Showalter
       </a>
       , using fourteen sets of images taken between 26 June and 9 July 2012 by the
       <a href="/wiki/Wide_Field_Camera_3" title="Wide Field Camera 3">
        Wide Field Camera 3
       </a>
       fitted to the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail_2012-07-11_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dailymail_2012-07-11-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUCirc_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUCirc-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The discovery was announced on 11 July 2012. Styx is about half as bright as the dimmest previously known object in the system, Kerberos, and about one hundred thousandth as bright as Pluto.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matson_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matson-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was designated S/2012 (134340) 1,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_2012-07-11_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa_2012-07-11-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and informally referred to as P5.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matson_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matson-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chang_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-chang-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_2012-07-11_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-guardian_2012-07-11-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The survey work leading to the discovery of Styx was in preparation for the mission of the unmanned
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       spaceprobe, which flew by the Pluto system on 14 July 2015. The discovery of another small Plutonian moon heightened concerns that this region of space may harbor more bodies too small to be detected, and that the probe could be damaged by an uncharted body or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">
        ring
       </a>
       as it traversed the system at a speed of over 13 km/s;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanders_2012-07-11_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sanders_2012-07-11-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csmonitor_2012-07-11_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-csmonitor_2012-07-11-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       tiny moons, such as Saturn's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Pallene_(moon)" title="Pallene (moon)">
        Pallene
       </a>
       , tend to be associated with tenuous rings or arcs, because their gravity is unable to hold on to material ejected by meteoroid impacts; such diffuse material represents the chief navigational hazard.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redd_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Redd-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the
       <i>
        New Horizons
       </i>
       probe did not detect any smaller moons or rings, and passed through the Pluto system safely.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Styx_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The unexpectedly complex moon system around Pluto may be the result of a collision between Pluto and another sizable
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       object in the distant past.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_2012-07-11_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-latimes_2012-07-11-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Pluto's moons may have
       <a href="/wiki/Collisional_family" title="Collisional family">
        coalesced from the debris
       </a>
       from such an event, similar to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        early giant impact
       </a>
       thought to have created the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . The orbital resonances may have acted as "ruts" to gather material from the smashup.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matson_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matson-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">
        Physical properties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Styx_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical properties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Styx was originally estimated to have a diameter of between 10 and 25 kilometers (6 and 16 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hubblesite_2012-07-11_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hubblesite_2012-07-11-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_2012-07-11_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BBC_2012-07-11-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These figures were inferred from the
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent magnitude
       </a>
       of Styx and by using an estimated
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       of 0.35 and 0.04 for the lower and upper bounds, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanders_2012-07-11_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sanders_2012-07-11-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After measurements made by
       <i>
        New Horizons
       </i>
       , it turns out that Styx (albeit not surprisingly) is very irregularly shaped, measuring 5 by 7 kilometers (3.1 mi × 4.3 mi). It is thought to have formed from the debris lofted by a collision, which would have led to losses of the more
       <a href="/wiki/Volatiles" title="Volatiles">
        volatile ices
       </a>
       , such as those of
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
        nitrogen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">
        methane
       </a>
       , in the composition of the impactors. This process is expected to have created a body consisting mainly of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        water ice
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APOD_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-APOD-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Styx_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pluto_moon_P5_discovery_with_moons%27_orbits.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1578" data-file-width="1546" height="306" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Pluto_moon_P5_discovery_with_moons%27_orbits.jpg/300px-Pluto_moon_P5_discovery_with_moons%27_orbits.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Pluto_moon_P5_discovery_with_moons%27_orbits.jpg/450px-Pluto_moon_P5_discovery_with_moons%27_orbits.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Pluto_moon_P5_discovery_with_moons%27_orbits.jpg/600px-Pluto_moon_P5_discovery_with_moons%27_orbits.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pluto_moon_P5_discovery_with_moons%27_orbits.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
          Hubble Space Telescope
         </a>
         discovery image of Styx (circled), with the outer moons' orbits depicted. Relative to the other bodies, Pluto and Charon are shown greatly reduced in brightness.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Styx orbits the Pluto–Charon
       <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">
        barycenter
       </a>
       at a distance of 42,656 km,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShowalterHamilton2015_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ShowalterHamilton2015-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       putting it between the orbits of Charon and
       <a href="/wiki/Nix_(moon)" title="Nix (moon)">
        Nix
       </a>
       . All of
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Pluto" title="Moons of Pluto">
        Pluto's moons
       </a>
       appear to travel in orbits that are very nearly circular and coplanar, described by Styx's discoverer Mark Showalter as "neatly nested ... a bit like
       <a href="/wiki/Matryoshka_doll" title="Matryoshka doll">
        Russian dolls
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_2012-07-11_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-latimes_2012-07-11-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is in an 11:6
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(moon)" title="Hydra (moon)">
        Hydra
       </a>
       , and an 11:9 resonance with Nix (the ratios represent numbers of orbits completed per unit time; the period ratios are the inverses).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShowalterHamilton2015_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ShowalterHamilton2015-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Witze2015_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Witze2015-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a result of this "Laplace-like" 3-body resonance, it has conjunctions with Nix and Hydra in a 2:5 ratio.
      </p>
      <p>
       Its orbital period of 20.16155 days
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShowalterHamilton2015_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-ShowalterHamilton2015-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is about 5.0% from a 1:3 mean-motion resonance with the Charon–Pluto orbital period of 6.387 days. With the other moons Nix,
       <a href="/wiki/Kerberos_(moon)" title="Kerberos (moon)">
        Kerberos
       </a>
       and Hydra, it forms part of an unusual 1:3:4:5:6 (period ratio) sequence of
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance#Coincidental_'near'_ratios_of_mean_motion" title="Orbital resonance">
        near resonances
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matson_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matson-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In contrast to its orbit, Styx's rotation is very chaotic; like the other small Plutonian moons, Styx's rotation is not tidally locked, and it varies rapidly over short timescales (with a rate of about 3.239 days at the time of the
       <i>
        New Horizons
       </i>
       flyby).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">
        Naming
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Styx_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Naming">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Upon discovery, Styx received the
       <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet_designation" title="Minor planet designation">
        minor planet designation
       </a>
       S/2012 (134340) 1 because it was the first satellite (S) discovered orbiting minor planet (134340) in 2012. It is known informally as "P5", meaning the fifth Plutonian moon to be discovered.
      </p>
      <p>
       The convention for naming Plutonian moons is to use names associated with the god
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pluto_(god)" title="Pluto (god)">
        Pluto
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_mythology" title="Classical mythology">
        classical mythology
       </a>
       . To decide on names for P4 and P5,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark_Showalter" title="Mark Showalter">
        Mark Showalter
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/SETI_Institute" title="SETI Institute">
        SETI Institute
       </a>
       , on behalf of the discovery team, conducted a non-binding internet poll in 2013, in which the general public was invited to vote for their favorite names. The public could choose from a selection of Greek mythological names related to the god Pluto, or could propose their own names.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After the initial announcement,
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shatner" title="William Shatner">
        William Shatner
       </a>
       , the actor who plays
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_James_T._Kirk" title="Captain James T. Kirk">
        Captain James T. Kirk
       </a>
       in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">
         Star Trek
        </a>
       </i>
       franchise, proposed the names Vulcan and Romulus, ostensibly referring to the fire god
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulcan_(god)" title="Vulcan (god)">
        Vulcan
       </a>
       (a nephew of Pluto), and to
       <a href="/wiki/Romulus" title="Romulus">
        Romulus
       </a>
       the founder of Rome, but also alluding to the fictional planets of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulcan_(Star_Trek_planet)" title="Vulcan (Star Trek planet)">
        Vulcan
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romulans#Homeworld" title="Romulans">
        Romulus
       </a>
       in the
       <i>
        Star Trek
       </i>
       universe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marcia_Dunn_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marcia_Dunn-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nature-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 'Romulus' suggestion was discounted, as there is already
       <a href="/wiki/Romulus_(moon)" title="Romulus (moon)">
        an asteroid moon of that name
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but Vulcan won the poll after Shatner tweeted about it, with
       <a href="/wiki/Cerberus" title="Cerberus">
        Cerberus
       </a>
       (the dog that guards Pluto's underworld) coming second and
       <a href="/wiki/Styx" title="Styx">
        Styx
       </a>
       (the goddess of the river of the same name in the underworld) coming third. The winning names were submitted to the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_23-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nature-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However,
       <i>
        Vulcan
       </i>
       was unacceptable to the IAU because it was not the name of an underworld figure and had already been used for a
       <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(hypothetical_planet)" title="Vulcan (hypothetical planet)">
        hypothetical planet
       </a>
       inside the orbit of Mercury, as well as having given its name to the
       <a href="/wiki/Vulcanoid" title="Vulcanoid">
        vulcanoids
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marcia_Dunn_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marcia_Dunn-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 2 July 2013, the IAU announced that it had formally approved the names
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Styx" title="Styx">
         Styx
        </a>
       </i>
       for P5 and
       <i>
        Kerberos
       </i>
       for P4.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
