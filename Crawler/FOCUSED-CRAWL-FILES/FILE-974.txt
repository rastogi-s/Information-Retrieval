      <p>
       <b>
        Romulus
       </b>
       is the outer and larger moon of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main-belt" title="Main-belt">
        main-belt
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/87_Sylvia" title="87 Sylvia">
        87 Sylvia
       </a>
       , not to be confused with the directly Sun-orbiting asteroid
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10386_Romulus" title="10386 Romulus">
        10386 Romulus
       </a>
       . It follows an almost-circular close-to-equatorial orbit around the parent asteroid. In this respect it is similar to the other moon
       <a href="/wiki/Remus_(moon)" title="Remus (moon)">
        Remus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Romulus was discovered in February 2001 from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_telescope" title="Keck telescope">
        Keck II telescope
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">
        Michael E. Brown
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Margot" title="Jean-Luc Margot">
        Jean-Luc Margot
       </a>
       . Its full designation is
       <b>
        (87) Sylvia I Romulus
       </b>
       ; before receiving its name, it was known as
       <b>
        S/2001 (87) 1
       </b>
       . The moon is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Romulus_and_Remus" title="Romulus and Remus">
        Romulus
       </a>
       , the mythological founder of
       <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       , one of the twins of
       <a href="/wiki/Rhea_Silvia" title="Rhea Silvia">
        Rhea Silvia
       </a>
       raised by a wolf.
      </p>
      <p>
       87 Sylvia has a low density, which indicates that it is probably a
       <a href="/wiki/Rubble_pile" title="Rubble pile">
        rubble pile
       </a>
       formed when debris from a collision between its parent body and another asteroid re-accreted gravitationally. Therefore, it is likely that both Romulus and
       <a href="/wiki/Remus_(moon)" title="Remus (moon)">
        Remus
       </a>
       , the second of Sylvia's moons, are smaller rubble piles which accreted in orbit around the main body from debris of the same collision. In this case their albedo and density are expected to be similar to Sylvia's.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis05_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchis05-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Romulus' orbit is expected to be quite stable − it lies far inside Sylvia's
       <a href="/wiki/Hill_sphere" title="Hill sphere">
        Hill sphere
       </a>
       (about 1/50 of Sylvia's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hill_radius" title="Hill radius">
        Hill radius
       </a>
       ), but also far outside the
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous orbit
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis05_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchis05-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From Romulus' surface, Sylvia takes up an angular region 16°×10° across, while Remus's apparent size varies between 0.62° and 0.19° (for comparison, Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       has an apparent size of about 0.5°).
      </p>
