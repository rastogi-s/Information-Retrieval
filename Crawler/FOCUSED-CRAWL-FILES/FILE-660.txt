   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Great Moon Hoax
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       The "
       <b>
        Great Moon Hoax
       </b>
       " refers to a series of six articles that were published in
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sun_(New_York)" title="The Sun (New York)">
         The Sun
        </a>
       </i>
       , a New York newspaper, beginning on August 25, 1835, about the supposed discovery of life and even civilization on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . The discoveries were falsely attributed to Sir
       <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">
        John Herschel
       </a>
       , one of the best-known contemporary
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">
        astronomers
       </a>
       of that time.
      </p>
      <p>
       The story was advertised on August 21, 1835, as an upcoming feature allegedly reprinted from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Courant" title="Edinburgh Courant">
         The Edinburgh Courant
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first in a series of six was published four days later on August 25.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Article">
        Article
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Moon_Hoax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Article">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1836_the-great-moon-hoax-new-inhabitants-of-the-moon.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1423" data-file-width="1153" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/1836_the-great-moon-hoax-new-inhabitants-of-the-moon.png/220px-1836_the-great-moon-hoax-new-inhabitants-of-the-moon.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/1836_the-great-moon-hoax-new-inhabitants-of-the-moon.png/330px-1836_the-great-moon-hoax-new-inhabitants-of-the-moon.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/1836_the-great-moon-hoax-new-inhabitants-of-the-moon.png/440px-1836_the-great-moon-hoax-new-inhabitants-of-the-moon.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1836_the-great-moon-hoax-new-inhabitants-of-the-moon.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Portrait of a man-bat ("
         <i>
          Vespertilio-homo
         </i>
         "), from an edition of the Moon series published in
         <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">
          Naples
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The headline read:
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         <center>
          GREAT ASTRONOMICAL DISCOVERIES
         </center>
         <center>
          LATELY MADE
         </center>
         <center>
          BY SIR JOHN HERSCHEL, L.L.D. F.R.S. &amp;c.
         </center>
         <center>
          <b>
           At the
           <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">
            Cape of Good Hope
           </a>
          </b>
         </center>
         <center>
          [From Supplement to the
          <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">
           Edinburgh
          </a>
          Journal of Science]
         </center>
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       The articles described fantastic animals on the Moon, including
       <a href="/wiki/Bison" title="Bison">
        bison
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goats" title="Goats">
        goats
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Unicorn" title="Unicorn">
        unicorns
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bipedal" title="Bipedal">
        bipedal
       </a>
       tail-less
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beavers" title="Beavers">
        beavers
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">
        bat
       </a>
       -like winged humanoids ("
       <i>
        Vespertilio-homo
       </i>
       ") who built temples. There were trees, oceans and beaches. These discoveries were supposedly made with "an immense
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       of an entirely new principle."
      </p>
      <p>
       The author of the narrative was ostensibly Dr. Andrew Grant, the travelling companion and
       <a href="/wiki/Amanuensis" title="Amanuensis">
        amanuensis
       </a>
       of Sir John Herschel, but Grant was fictitious.
      </p>
      <p>
       Eventually, the authors announced that the observations had been terminated by the destruction of the telescope, by means of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       causing the lens to act as a "
       <a href="/wiki/Burning_glass" title="Burning glass">
        burning glass
       </a>
       ," setting fire to the observatory.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AsimovGunn1975_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AsimovGunn1975-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Authorship">
        Authorship
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Moon_Hoax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Authorship">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <div style="width:52px">

      <p>
       Authorship of the article has been attributed to Richard Adams Locke,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deseret_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Deseret-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a reporter who, in August 1835, was working for
       <i>
        The Sun
       </i>
       . Locke publicly admitted to being the author in 1840, in a letter to the weekly paper
       <i>
        New World
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Still, rumours persisted that others were involved. Two other men have been noted in connection with the
       <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">
        hoax
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Nicollet" title="Joseph Nicollet">
        Jean-Nicolas Nicollet
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deseret_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Deseret-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a French astronomer travelling in America at the time (though he was in Mississippi, not New York, when the Moon-hoax issues appeared), and
       <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Gaylord_Clark" title="Lewis Gaylord Clark">
        Lewis Gaylord Clark
       </a>
       , editor of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Knickerbocker" title="The Knickerbocker">
         The Knickerbocker
        </a>
       </i>
       , a literary magazine. However, there is no good evidence to indicate that anyone but Locke was the author of the hoax.
      </p>
      <p>
       Assuming that Richard A. Locke was the author, his intentions were probably, first, to create a sensational story which would increase sales of
       <i>
        The Sun
       </i>
       , and, second, to ridicule some of the more extravagant astronomical theories that had recently been published. For instance, in 1824,
       <a href="/wiki/Franz_von_Gruithuisen" title="Franz von Gruithuisen">
        Franz von Paula Gruithuisen
       </a>
       , professor of Astronomy at
       <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Maximilian_University_of_Munich" title="Ludwig Maximilian University of Munich">
        Munich University
       </a>
       , had published a paper titled "Discovery of Many Distinct Traces of Lunar Inhabitants, Especially of One of Their Colossal Buildings." Gruithuisen claimed to have observed various shades of color on the lunar surface, which he correlated with climate and vegetation zones. He also observed lines and geometrical shapes, which he felt indicated the existence of walls, roads, fortifications, and cities.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, a more direct object of Locke's satire was Rev.
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dick_(scientist)" title="Thomas Dick (scientist)">
        Thomas Dick
       </a>
       , who was known as "The Christian Philosopher" after the title of his first book.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Dick had computed that the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       contained 21,891,974,404,480 (21.9 trillion) inhabitants. In fact, the Moon alone, by his count, would contain 4,200,000,000 inhabitants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His writings were enormously popular in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       , his fans including intellectual
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/luminaries" title="wikt:luminaries">
        luminaries
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">
        Ralph Waldo Emerson
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_and_effect">
        Reaction and effect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Moon_Hoax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reaction and effect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to legend,
       <i>
        The Sun'
       </i>
       s circulation increased dramatically because of the hoax and remained permanently greater than before, thereby establishing
       <i>
        The Sun
       </i>
       as a successful paper. However, the degree to which the hoax increased the paper's circulation has certainly been exaggerated in popular accounts of the event. It was not discovered to be a hoax for several weeks after its publication and, even then, the newspaper did not issue a retraction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Herschel was initially amused by the hoax, noting that his own real observations could never be as exciting. He became annoyed later when he had to answer questions from people who believed the hoax was serious.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">
        Edgar Allan Poe
       </a>
       claimed the story was a plagiarism of his earlier work
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Unparalleled_Adventure_of_One_Hans_Pfaall" title="The Unparalleled Adventure of One Hans Pfaall">
         The Unparalleled Adventure of One Hans Pfaall
        </a>
       </i>
       . His editor at the time was Richard Adams Locke. He later published "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Balloon-Hoax" title="The Balloon-Hoax">
        The Balloon-Hoax
       </a>
       " in the same newspaper.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Poe had published his own Moon hoax in late June 1835, two months before the similar Locke Moon hoax, in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Southern_Literary_Messenger" title="Southern Literary Messenger">
         Southern Literary Messenger
        </a>
       </i>
       entitled "Hans Phaall – A Tale," later republished as "The Unparalleled Adventure of One Hans Pfaall." The story was reprinted in the
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_Transcript&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="New York Transcript (page does not exist)">
         New York Transcript
        </a>
       </i>
       on September 2–5, 1835, under the headline "Lunar Discoveries, Extraordinary Aerial Voyage by Baron Hans Pfaall." Poe described a voyage to the Moon in a hot-air balloon, in which Pfaall lives for five years on the Moon with lunarians and sends back a lunarian to earth. The Poe Moon hoax was less successful because of the satiric and comical tone of the account. Locke was able to upstage Poe and to steal his thunder. In 1846, Poe would write a biographical sketch of Locke as part of his series "The Literati of New York City" which appeared in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Godey%27s_Lady%27s_Book" title="Godey's Lady's Book">
         Godey's Lady's Book
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The hoax, as well as Poe's "Hans Pfaall", are mentioned by characters in
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
        Jules Verne
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon" title="From the Earth to the Moon">
         From the Earth to the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Nate DiMeo's historical podcast
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Memory_Palace" title="The Memory Palace">
         The Memory Palace
        </a>
       </i>
       dedicated an episode to the Great Moon Hoax entitled "The Moon in the Sun."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Episode_24:_The_Moon_in_the_Sun_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Episode_24:_The_Moon_in_the_Sun-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
