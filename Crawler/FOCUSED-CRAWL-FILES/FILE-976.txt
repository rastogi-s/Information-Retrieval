      <p>
       <b>
        (22) Kalliope I Linus
       </b>
       is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_moon" title="Asteroid moon">
        asteroid moon
       </a>
       that orbits the large
       <a href="/wiki/M-type_asteroid" title="M-type asteroid">
        M-type asteroid
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/22_Kalliope" title="22 Kalliope">
        22 Kalliope
       </a>
       . It was discovered on August 29, 2001, by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomers" title="Astronomers">
        astronomers
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Margot" title="Jean-Luc Margot">
        Jean-Luc Margot
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">
        Michael E. Brown
       </a>
       with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_telescope" title="Keck telescope">
        Keck telescope
       </a>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">
        Hawaii
       </a>
       . Another team also detected the moon with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canada-France-Hawaii_Telescope" title="Canada-France-Hawaii Telescope">
        Canada-France-Hawaii Telescope
       </a>
       on September 2, 2001. Both telescopes are on
       <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea" title="Mauna Kea">
        Mauna Kea
       </a>
       . It received the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provisional_designation" title="Provisional designation">
        provisional designation
       </a>
       S/2001 (22) 1,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC7703_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC7703-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       until it was named. The naming proposal appeared in the discovery paper
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Margot2003_2-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Margot2003-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was approved by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IAU" title="IAU">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       in July 2003.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8177_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC8177-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although the naming proposal referred to the mythological
       <a href="/wiki/Linus_(mythology)" title="Linus (mythology)">
        Linus
       </a>
       , son of the muse
       <a href="/wiki/Calliope" title="Calliope">
        Calliope
       </a>
       and the inventor of melody and rhythm, the name was also meant to honor
       <a href="/wiki/Linus_Torvalds" title="Linus Torvalds">
        Linus Torvalds
       </a>
       , inventor of the
       <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">
        Linux
       </a>
       operating system, and
       <a href="/wiki/Linus_van_Pelt" title="Linus van Pelt">
        Linus van Pelt
       </a>
       , a character in the
       <a href="/wiki/Peanuts" title="Peanuts">
        Peanuts
       </a>
       comic strip.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       With an estimated
       <span class="nowrap">
        28 ± 2 km
       </span>
       (17 ± 1 mi) diameter,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Descamps2008_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Descamps2008-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Linus is very large compared to most asteroid moons, and would be a sizable asteroid by itself. The only known larger moons in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_belt" title="Main belt">
        main belt
       </a>
       are the smaller components of the double asteroids
       <a href="/wiki/617_Patroclus" title="617 Patroclus">
        617 Patroclus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/90_Antiope" title="90 Antiope">
        90 Antiope
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It has been estimated that Linus' orbit
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        precesses
       </a>
       at quite a rapid rate, making one cycle in several years. This is attributed primarily to the non-spherical shape of Kalliope.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Margot2003_2-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Margot2003-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis03_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchis03-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Linus's brightness has varied appreciably between observations, which may indicate that its shape is elongated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis03_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchis03-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Linus may have formed out of
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">
        impact
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ejecta" title="Ejecta">
        ejecta
       </a>
       from a
       <a href="/wiki/Collision" title="Collision">
        collision
       </a>
       with Kalliope, or a fragment captured after disruption of a parent asteroid (a proto-Kalliope).
      </p>
