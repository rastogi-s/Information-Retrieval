      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Goodnight Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       is an American
       <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_literature" title="Children's literature">
        children's
       </a>
       novel written by
       <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Wise_Brown" title="Margaret Wise Brown">
        Margaret Wise Brown
       </a>
       and illustrated by
       <a href="/wiki/Clement_Hurd" title="Clement Hurd">
        Clement Hurd
       </a>
       . It was published on September 3,
       <a href="/wiki/1947_in_literature" title="1947 in literature">
        1947
       </a>
       , and is a highly acclaimed example of a
       <a href="/wiki/Bedtime_story" title="Bedtime story">
        bedtime story
       </a>
       . It features a bunny saying "good night" to everything around: "Goodnight room. Goodnight
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        moon
       </a>
       . Goodnight cow jumping over the moon. Goodnight light, and the red balloon ...".
      </p>
      <p>
       This book is the second in Brown and Hurd's "classic series", which also includes
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Runaway_Bunny" title="The Runaway Bunny">
         The Runaway Bunny
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/My_World_(book)" title="My World (book)">
         My World
        </a>
       </i>
       . The three books have been published together as a collection titled
       <i>
        Over the Moon
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">
        Synopsis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodnight_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synopsis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Goodnight Moon
       </i>
       is classic children's literature in North America. The text is a rhyming poem, describing an
       <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">
        anthropomorphic
       </a>
       bunny's bedtime ritual of saying "good night" to various inanimate and living objects in the bunny's bedroom: a red balloon, the bunny's dollhouse, two kittens, etc.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Video_adaptation">
        Video adaptation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodnight_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Video adaptation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1999,
       <i>
        Goodnight Moon
       </i>
       was adapted into a 26-minute animated
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HBO_Family" title="HBO Family">
        HBO Family
       </a>
       video, which was released on
       <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">
        VHS
       </a>
       a year later, and
       <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">
        DVD
       </a>
       in 2005, in the United States. The video features an animated short of
       <i>
        Goodnight Moon
       </i>
       , narrated by
       <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sarandon" title="Susan Sarandon">
        Susan Sarandon
       </a>
       , along with 6 other animated segments of children's bedtime stories and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lullabies" title="Lullabies">
        lullabies
       </a>
       with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live-action" title="Live-action">
        live-action
       </a>
       clips of children offering wise reflections on a series of sleepytime topics in between, a reprise of
       <i>
        Goodnight Moon
       </i>
       at the end, and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Everly_Brothers" title="Everly Brothers">
        Everly Brothers
       </a>
       ' "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All_I_Have_To_Do_Is_Dream" title="All I Have To Do Is Dream">
        All I Have To Do Is Dream
       </a>
       " playing over the
       <a href="/wiki/Closing_credits" title="Closing credits">
        closing credits
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       Illustrator

       <a href="/wiki/Edith_Thacher_Hurd" title="Edith Thacher Hurd">
        Edith Thacher Hurd
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Goodnight Moon
       </i>
       slowly became a bestseller. Annual sales grew from about 1,500 copies in 1953 to 20,000 in 1970; by 1990, the total number of copies sold was more than 4 million.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Currently, the book sells about 800,000 copies annually
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adcock_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Adcock-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and in 2017 has sold an estimated 48 million copies cumulatively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Goodnight Moon
       </i>
       has been translated into French, Spanish,
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian Portuguese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
        Swedish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">
        Korean
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Hmong_language" title="Hmong language">
        Hmong
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Brown, who died in 1952, bequeathed the royalties to the book to (among many others) Albert Clarke, who was the nine-year-old son of a neighbor when Brown died. In 2000, reporter
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joshua_Harris_Prager" title="Joshua Harris Prager">
        Joshua Prager
       </a>
       detailed in the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">
         Wall Street Journal
        </a>
       </i>
       the troubled life of Mr. Clarke who has squandered the millions of dollars the book has earned him and who believes that Brown was his mother, a claim others dismiss.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2005, publisher
       <a href="/wiki/HarperCollins" title="HarperCollins">
        HarperCollins
       </a>
       digitally altered the photograph of illustrator Hurd, which had been on the book for at least twenty years, to remove a cigarette. Its editor-in-chief for children's books, Kate Jackson, said, "It is potentially a harmful message to very young [children]." HarperCollins had the reluctant permission of Hurd's son,
       <a href="/wiki/Thacher_Hurd" title="Thacher Hurd">
        Thacher Hurd
       </a>
       , but the younger Hurd said the photo of Hurd with his arm and fingers extended, holding nothing, "looks slightly absurd to me".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       HarperCollins has said it will likely replace the picture with a different, unaltered photo of Hurd in future editions. In response, a satirical article demanded the removal of other potentially dangerous objects in the book, such as the fireplace and balloon (a choking hazard for young children).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Allusions_and_references">
        Allusions and references
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodnight_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Allusions and references">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Goodnight Moon
       </i>
       contains a number of references to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Runaway_Bunny" title="The Runaway Bunny">
         The Runaway Bunny
        </a>
       </i>
       . For example, the painting hanging over the fireplace of "The Cow Jumping Over the Moon" first appeared in
       <i>
        The Runaway Bunny
       </i>
       . However, when reprinted in
       <i>
        Goodnight Moon
       </i>
       , the udder "for caution's sake was reduced to an anatomical blur" to avoid the controversy that E.B. White's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Little" title="Stuart Little">
         Stuart Little
        </a>
       </i>
       had undergone when published in 1945.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The other painting in the room, which is never explicitly mentioned in the text, portrays a bunny fly-fishing for another bunny, using a carrot as bait. This picture is also a reference to
       <i>
        The Runaway Bunny
       </i>
       . The top shelf of the bookshelf holds an open copy of
       <i>
        The Runaway Bunny
       </i>
       , and there is a copy of
       <i>
        Goodnight Moon
       </i>
       on the nightstand.
      </p>
      <p>
       The telephone is mentioned early in the book, but is absent from the litany of "Good night ..." salutations. The primacy of the reference to the telephone indicates that the bunny is in his mother's room and his mother's bed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_significance_and_reception">
        Literary significance and reception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodnight_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Literary significance and reception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Based on a 2007 on-line poll, the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Education_Association" title="National Education Association">
        National Education Association
       </a>
       named the book one of its "Teachers' Top 100 Books for Children".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEA2007_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NEA2007-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2012 it was ranked number four among the "Top 100 Picture Books" in a survey published by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/School_Library_Journal" title="School Library Journal">
         School Library Journal
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SLJPicture2012_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SLJPicture2012-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Author
       <a href="/wiki/Susan_Cooper" title="Susan Cooper">
        Susan Cooper
       </a>
       writes that the book is possibly the only "realistic story" to gain the universal affection of a fairy-tale, although she also noted that it is actually a "deceptively simple ritual" rather than a story.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
