      <p>
       A
       <b>
        sample-return mission
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       mission with the goal of collecting and returning with tangible samples from an
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/extraterrestrial#Adjective" title="wiktionary:extraterrestrial">
        extraterrestrial
       </a>
       location to
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       for analysis. Sample-return missions may bring back merely atoms and molecules or a deposit of complex compounds such as loose material ("soil") and rocks. These samples may be obtained in a number of ways, including a collector array used for capturing particles of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       or cometary debris, soil and rock excavation, mining, and any other possible way for retrieving samples in the environment.
      </p>
      <p>
       Up to the present, humanity has collected samples of six identified Solar System bodies, as well as samples of the solar wind. These samples were acquired through three methods: The collection of samples of Earth itself, the collection of meteorites that have fallen on Earth, and the collection of samples through sample-return missions. Samples of
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_rock" title="Moon rock">
        Moon rock
       </a>
       from Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       were collected both from
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_meteorite" title="Lunar meteorite">
        meteorites
       </a>
       and through unmanned and manned sample-return missions. The comet
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_2" title="Wild 2">
        Wild 2
       </a>
       and the asteroid
       <a href="/wiki/25143_Itokawa" title="25143 Itokawa">
        25143 Itokawa
       </a>
       were visited by unmanned spacecraft, which returned samples to Earth. Furthermore, samples for three identified Solar System bodies were only collected by means other than sample-return missions: samples from Earth itself, samples from
       <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">
        Vesta
       </a>
       in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/HED_meteorite" title="HED meteorite">
        HED meteorites
       </a>
       , and samples from
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Martian_meteorite" title="Martian meteorite">
        Martian meteorites
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_use">
        Scientific use
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scientific use">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bilanga_meteorite,_4.3g_slice.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3200" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg/220px-Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg/330px-Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg/440px-Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bilanga_meteorite,_4.3g_slice.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A meteorite from Vesta that fell on Africa
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Samples available on Earth can be analyzed in laboratories, so we can further our understanding and knowledge as part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Discovery_and_exploration_of_the_Solar_System" title="Discovery and exploration of the Solar System">
        discovery and exploration of the Solar System
       </a>
       . Until now many important scientific discoveries about the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       were made remotely with telescopes, and some Solar System bodies were visited by orbiting or even landing spacecraft with instruments capable of
       <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">
        remote sensing
       </a>
       or sample analysis. While such an investigation of the Solar System is technically easier than a sample-return mission, the scientific tools available here on Earth to study such samples are far more advanced and diverse than those that can go on spacecraft. Analysis of samples on Earth allows to follow up any findings with different tools, including tools that have yet to be developed; in contrast, a spacecraft can carry only a limited set of analytic tools, and these have to be chosen and built long before launch.
      </p>
      <p>
       Samples analyzed on Earth can be matched against findings of
       <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">
        remote sensing
       </a>
       , for more insight into the
       <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">
        processes that formed the Solar System
       </a>
       . This was done, for example, with findings by the
       <a href="/wiki/Dawn_(spacecraft)" title="Dawn (spacecraft)">
        Dawn spacecraft
       </a>
       , which visited the asteroid Vesta from 2011 to 2012 for imaging, and samples from
       <a href="/wiki/HED_meteorite" title="HED meteorite">
        HED meteorites
       </a>
       (collected on Earth until then), which were compared to data gathered by Dawn.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These meteorites could then be identified as material ejected from the large impact crater
       <a href="/wiki/Rheasilvia" title="Rheasilvia">
        Rheasilvia
       </a>
       on Vesta. This allowed deducing the composition of crust,
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       and core of Vesta. Similarly some
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_spectral_types" title="Asteroid spectral types">
        differences in composition of asteroids
       </a>
       (and, to a lesser extent, different compositions of
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comets
       </a>
       ) can be discerned by imaging alone. However, for a more precise inventory of the material on these different bodies, more samples will be collected and returned in the future, to match their compositions with the data gathered through telescopes and
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">
        astronomical spectroscopy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       One further focus of such investigation – besides the basic composition and
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_geology" title="Historical geology">
        geologic history
       </a>
       of the various Solar System bodies – is the presence of the
       <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">
        building blocks of life
       </a>
       on comets, asteroids, Mars or the moons of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">
        gas giants
       </a>
       . Several sample-return missions to asteroids and comets are currently in the works. More samples from asteroids and comets will help determine whether life formed in space and was carried to Earth in the form of meteorites. Another question under investigation is whether
       <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">
        extraterrestrial life
       </a>
       formed on other Solar System bodies like Mars or on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_satellite_habitability" title="Natural satellite habitability">
        the moons of the gas giants
       </a>
       , and whether life might even exist there today. The result of NASA's last "Decadal Survey" was to prioritize a Mars sample-return mission, as Mars has a special importance: it is comparatively "nearby", might have harbored life in the past, and might even be able to sustain life today. Jupiter's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       is another important focus in the search for life in the Solar System. However, due to the distance and other constraints, Europa might not be the target of a sample-return mission in the foreseeable future.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_protection">
        Planetary protection
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Planetary protection">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_protection" title="Planetary protection">
        Planetary protection
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Planetary protection aims to prevent biological contamination of both the target
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">
        celestial body
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_(planet)" title="Earth (planet)">
        Earth
       </a>
       - in the case of sample-return missions. No sample has yet been returned with alien life in it. A sample-return from Mars or other location with potential to host life, is a
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_protection" title="Planetary protection">
        category V mission under COSPAR
       </a>
       which directs to containment of any unsterilized sample returned to Earth. This is because it is unknown the effects of such hypothetical life would be on humans or on the
       <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">
        biosphere
       </a>
       of Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lederberg_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lederberg-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For this reason,
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">
        Carl Sagan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Lederberg" title="Joshua Lederberg">
        Joshua Lederberg
       </a>
       argued in the 1970s that we should do sample-return missions classified as category V missions with extreme caution, and later studies by the NRC and ESF agreed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lederberg_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lederberg-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc2009_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nrc2009-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iMars_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-iMars-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esf2010_PP_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-esf2010_PP-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAS_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NAS-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sample-return_missions">
        Sample-return missions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sample-return missions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_11_2004_scan.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2373" data-file-width="2349" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Apollo_11_2004_scan.jpg/250px-Apollo_11_2004_scan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Apollo_11_2004_scan.jpg/375px-Apollo_11_2004_scan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Apollo_11_2004_scan.jpg/500px-Apollo_11_2004_scan.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_11_2004_scan.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Apollo 11 was the first mission to return extraterrestrial samples
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="First_missions">
        First missions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First missions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The manned US
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       mission in July 1969 achieved the first successful sample return from another Solar System body. It returned approximately 22 kilograms (49 lb) of
       <b>
        Lunar
       </b>
       surface material. This was followed by 34 kilograms (75 lb) of material from
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        Apollo 12
       </a>
       and further 326 kilograms (719 lb) material from four more missions from the manned
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Perhaps one of the most significant advances in sample-return missions occurred in 1970 when the robotic Soviet mission known as
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_16" title="Luna 16">
        Luna 16
       </a>
       , successfully returned 101 grams (3.6 oz) of lunar soil. Likewise,
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_20" title="Luna 20">
        Luna 20
       </a>
       returned 55 grams (1.9 oz) in 1974, and
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_24" title="Luna 24">
        Luna 24
       </a>
       returned 170 grams (6.0 oz) in 1976. Although they recovered far less than the Apollo missions, they did this fully automatically. Beside these three successes, other attempts under
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">
        Luna programme
       </a>
       failed. The first two missions intended to outstrip Apollo 11 and were undertaken shortly before them in June and July 1969:
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_E-8-5_No._402" title="Luna E-8-5 No. 402">
        Luna E-8-5 No. 402
       </a>
       failed at start, and
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_15" title="Luna 15">
        Luna 15
       </a>
       crashed on the Moon. Later, other sample-return missions failed:
       <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_300" title="Kosmos 300">
        Kosmos 300
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_305" title="Kosmos 305">
        Kosmos 305
       </a>
       in 1969,
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_E-8-5_No._405" title="Luna E-8-5 No. 405">
        Luna E-8-5 No. 405
       </a>
       in 1970,
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_E-8-5M_No._412" title="Luna E-8-5M No. 412">
        Luna E-8-5M No. 412
       </a>
       in 1975 at unsuccessful launches;
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_18" title="Luna 18">
        Luna 18
       </a>
       in 1971,
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_23" title="Luna 23">
        Luna 23
       </a>
       in 1974 at unsuccessful landings on Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EA-E8_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EA-E8-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1970, the Soviet Union planned for a 1975 first
       <b>
        Martian
       </b>
       sample-return mission in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_5NM" title="Mars 5NM">
        Mars 5NM
       </a>
       project. This mission was planned to use an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N1_rocket" title="N1 rocket">
        N1 rocket
       </a>
       , but as this rocket never flew successfully, the mission evolved into the
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_5M" title="Mars 5M">
        Mars 5M
       </a>
       project, which would use a double launch with the smaller
       <a href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket_family)" title="Proton (rocket family)">
        Proton
       </a>
       rocket and an assembly at a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salyut" title="Salyut">
        Salyut
       </a>
       space station. This
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_5M" title="Mars 5M">
        Mars 5M
       </a>
       mission was planned for 1979, but got canceled in 1977 due to technical problems and complexity; all hardware was ordered destroyed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="New_missions_after_a_20-year_hiatus">
        New missions after a 20-year hiatus
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New missions after a 20-year hiatus">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-disputed" role="presentation">
       <tr>

      <p>
       NASA has a long-term stratospheric flight program to collect cosmic dust.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 1984
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LDEF" title="LDEF">
        LDEF
       </a>
       mission returned
       <a href="/wiki/Carbonaceous_chondrite" title="Carbonaceous chondrite">
        chondritic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Meteoric_iron" title="Meteoric iron">
        iron-nickel
       </a>
       impactors,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as did the 1992
       <a href="/wiki/European_Retrievable_Carrier" title="European Retrievable Carrier">
        EURECA
       </a>
       mission.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Earth-Orbital Debris Collection (ODC)
       </i>
       experiment was deployed on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mir_space_station" title="Mir space station">
        Mir space station
       </a>
       for 18 months during 1996–1997 and used
       <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">
        aerogel
       </a>
       to capture
       <b>
        particles from low Earth orbit
       </b>
       , consisting of interplanetary dust and man-made particles. Far from being "the last sample-return mission... in... twenty years", ODC was a portable version of an LDEF collector, decreasing collection time significantly, and effective area by orders of magnitude.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="850" data-file-width="1200" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg/250px-GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg/375px-GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg/500px-GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         An artist's rendering of
         <i>
          Genesis
         </i>
         while collecting solar wind
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The next mission to return extraterrestrial samples was known as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(spacecraft)" title="Genesis (spacecraft)">
         Genesis
        </a>
       </i>
       – it was able to return
       <b>
        solar wind
       </b>
       samples to Earth from beyond Earth orbit. Unfortunately, the
       <i>
        Genesis
       </i>
       capsule failed to open its parachute while re-entering the Earth's atmosphere and crash-landed in the Utah desert in 2004. There were fears of severe contamination or even total mission loss, but scientists have managed to save many of the samples, which were the first to be collected from beyond lunar orbit.
       <i>
        Genesis
       </i>
       used a collector array made of wafers of ultra-pure
       <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">
        silicon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">
        gold
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">
        sapphire
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">
        diamond
       </a>
       . Each different wafer was used to collect a different part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1656" data-file-width="1664" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg/250px-Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg/375px-Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg/500px-Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Sample-return capsule from the Stardust mission
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Genesis was followed by
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)" title="Stardust (spacecraft)">
         Stardust
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft, which returned
       <b>
        comet
       </b>
       samples to Earth on January 15, 2006. It safely passed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_Wild_2" title="Comet Wild 2">
        Comet Wild 2
       </a>
       and collected dust samples from the comet's
       <a href="/wiki/Coma_(cometary)" title="Coma (cometary)">
        coma
       </a>
       while imaging the comet's nucleus.
       <i>
        Stardust
       </i>
       used a collector array made of low-density
       <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">
        aerogel
       </a>
       (99% of which is empty space), which has about 1/1000 of the
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       of glass. This permits the ability to collect the cometary particles without damaging them due to high impact velocities. Particle collisions with even slightly porous solid collectors would result in destruction of those particles and damage to the collection apparatus. During cruise, the second side of the array collected at least seven
       <b>
        interstellar dust particles
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In June 2010 the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency">
        Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency
       </a>
       (JAXA)
       <a href="/wiki/Hayabusa" title="Hayabusa">
        Hayabusa
       </a>
       probe returned
       <b>
        asteroid
       </b>
       samples to Earth after a rendezvous with (and a landing on)
       <a href="/wiki/S-type_asteroid" title="S-type asteroid">
        S-type asteroid
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/25143_Itokawa" title="25143 Itokawa">
        25143 Itokawa
       </a>
       . In November 2010, scientists at the agency confirmed that, despite failure of the sampling device, the probe retrieved micrograms of dust from the asteroid, the first ever brought back to Earth in pristine condition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Russian
       <a href="/wiki/Fobos-Grunt" title="Fobos-Grunt">
        Fobos-Grunt
       </a>
       was a failed sample-return mission that was supposed to return samples from
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
         Phobos
        </a>
       </b>
       , one of the moons of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       . It was launched on November 8, 2011. However, the probe failed to leave Earth orbit and crashed after several weeks into the southern Pacific Ocean.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2250" data-file-width="2700" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg/250px-OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg/375px-OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg/500px-OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Illustration of OSIRIS-REx collecting a sample from asteroid
         <span class="nowrap">

           <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Planned_missions">
        Planned missions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Planned missions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) launched the improved
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hayabusa_2" title="Hayabusa 2">
        Hayabusa 2
       </a>
       space probe on December 3, 2014 and plans to return
       <b>
        asteroid
       </b>
       samples by 2020. Target for the mission is the
       <a href="/wiki/C-type_asteroid" title="C-type asteroid">
        C-type asteroid
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/162173_Ryugu" title="162173 Ryugu">
        162173 Ryugu
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/OSIRIS-REx" title="OSIRIS-REx">
        OSIRIS-REx
       </a>
       mission was launched in September 2016 on a mission to return samples from the asteroid
       <a href="/wiki/101955_Bennu" title="101955 Bennu">
        101955 Bennu
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The samples are expected to enable scientists to learn more about the time before the birth of the Solar System, initial stages of planet formation, and the source of organic compounds that led to the formation of life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSIRIS-REx_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-OSIRIS-REx-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program" title="Chinese Lunar Exploration Program">
        China is planning
       </a>
       to conduct a
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_5" title="Chang'e 5">
        Chang'e 5
       </a>
       <b>
        lunar
       </b>
       sample return around 2017. If successful, it would make the first
       <b>
        lunar
       </b>
       sample return in over 40 years.
      </p>

      <p>
       There were plans to launch a
       <b>
        Mars Sample Return
       </b>
       (MSR) mission in 2004, but following the twin-failures of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Climate_Orbiter" title="Mars Climate Orbiter">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Polar_Lander" title="Mars Polar Lander">
        Mars Polar Lander
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Mars_sample_return_mission" title="Mars sample return mission">
        <b>
         Martian
        </b>
        sample-return mission
       </a>
       , but has yet to secure the budget to successfully design, build, launch, and land a probe that would do just that. There have been mission proposals in the past, but most have not made it far beyond the drawing boards. The mission remained on NASA's roadmap for planetary science as of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2013_Planetary_Science_Decadal_Survey" title="2013 Planetary Science Decadal Survey">
        2013 Planetary Science Decadal Survey
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A Mars sample-return mission in
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Joint_Initiative" title="Mars Exploration Joint Initiative">
        collaboration with Europe
       </a>
       (as part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Aurora_programme" title="Aurora programme">
        Aurora programme
       </a>
       ) was proposed launch around 2018. Due to budget cuts at NASA, the future of this mission is uncertain.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_of_sample_return">
        Methods of sample return
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Methods of sample return">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Genesis_Collector_Array.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="890" data-file-width="1295" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Genesis_Collector_Array.jpg/200px-Genesis_Collector_Array.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Genesis_Collector_Array.jpg/300px-Genesis_Collector_Array.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Genesis_Collector_Array.jpg/400px-Genesis_Collector_Array.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Genesis_Collector_Array.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A Genesis collector array consisting of a grid of ultra-pure wafers of silicon, gold, sapphire, and diamond
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Sample-return methods include, but are not restricted to the following:
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Collector_array">
        Collector array
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Collector array">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A collector array may be used to collect millions or billions of atoms, molecules, and fine particulates by using a number of wafers made of different elements. The molecular structure of these wafers allows the collection of various sizes of particles. Collector arrays, such as those flown on
       <i>
        Genesis
       </i>
       , are ultra-pure in order to ensure maximal collection efficiency, durability, and analytical distinguishability.
      </p>
      <p>
       Collector arrays are useful for collecting tiny, fast-moving atoms such as those expelled by the Sun through solar wind, but can also be used for collection of larger particles such as those found in the
       <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">
        coma
       </a>
       of a comet. The NASA spacecraft known as
       <a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)" title="Stardust (spacecraft)">
        Stardust
       </a>
       implemented this technique. However, due to the high speeds and size of the particles that make up the coma and the area nearby, a dense solid-state collector array was not viable. As a result, another means for collecting samples had to be designed as to preserve the safety of the spacecraft and the samples themselves.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aerogel">
        Aerogel
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aerogel">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">
        Aerogel
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="690" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg/200px-Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg/300px-Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg/400px-Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A particle captured in aerogel
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">
        Aerogel
       </a>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">
        silicon
       </a>
       -based porous solid with a sponge-like structure, 99.8% of whose volume is empty space. Aerogel has about 1/1000 of the
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       of glass. An aerogel was used in the
       <a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)" title="Stardust (spacecraft)">
        <i>
         Stardust
        </i>
        spacecraft
       </a>
       because the dust particles the spacecraft was to collect would have an impact speed of about 6 km/s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A collision with a dense solid at that speed could alter their
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_composition" title="Chemical composition">
        chemical composition
       </a>
       or perhaps vaporize them completely.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the aerogel is mostly transparent, and the particles leave a carrot-shaped path once they penetrate the surface, scientists can easily find and retrieve them. Since its pores are on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">
        nanometer
       </a>
       scale, particles, even ones smaller than a grain of sand, do not merely pass through the aerogel completely. Instead, they slow to a stop and then are embedded within it.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Stardust
       </i>
       spacecraft has a
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tennis-racket&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tennis-racket (page does not exist)">
        tennis-racket
       </a>
       -shaped collector with aerogel fitted to it. The collector is retracted into its capsule for safe storage and delivery back to Earth. Aerogel is quite strong and easily survives both launching and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer-space" title="Outer-space">
        outer-space
       </a>
       environments.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Excavation_and_rocket_return">
        Excavation and rocket return
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Excavation and rocket return">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some of the most risky and difficult types of sample-return missions are those that require landing on an extraterrestrial body such as an asteroid, moon, or planet. It takes a great deal of time, money, and technical ability in order to even initiate such plans. It is a difficult feat that requires that everything from launch to landing to retrieval and launch back to Earth be planned out with high precision and accuracy.
      </p>
      <p>
       This type of sample return, although having the most risks, is the most rewarding for planetary science. Furthermore, such missions carry a great deal of public outreach potential, which is an important attribute for
       <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">
        space exploration
       </a>
       when it comes to publicity.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       is considering launching an international sample-return mission of this type to Mars around the year 2018, depending on its budget. Previous attempts to launch this type of sample-return mission have been scrubbed due to technical difficulty, budget constraints, and other factors such as recent mission failures (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Climate_Orbiter" title="Mars Climate Orbiter">
        Mars Climate Orbiter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Polar_Lander" title="Mars Polar Lander">
        Mars Polar Lander
       </a>
       ). The only successful robotic sample-return missions of this type have been the Soviet
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">
        Luna landers
       </a>
       .
      </p>
