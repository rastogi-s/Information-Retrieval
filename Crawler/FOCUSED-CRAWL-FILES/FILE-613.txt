    Lunar outpost (NASA)
   </h1>

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        lunar outpost
       </b>
       was an element of the
       <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">
        George W. Bush
       </a>
       era
       <a href="/wiki/Vision_for_Space_Exploration" title="Vision for Space Exploration">
        Vision for Space Exploration
       </a>
       , which has been replaced with President
       <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">
        Barack Obama
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Space_policy_of_the_Barack_Obama_administration" title="Space policy of the Barack Obama administration">
        space policy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExpStrat_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ExpStrat-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The outpost would have been an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_base" title="Lunar base">
        inhabited facility on the surface of the Moon
       </a>
       . At the time it was proposed,
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       was to construct the outpost over the five years between 2019 and 2024. The
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">
        United States Congress
       </a>
       directed that the U.S. portion, "shall be designated the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neil_A._Armstrong" title="Neil A. Armstrong">
        Neil A. Armstrong
       </a>
       Lunar Outpost".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On December 4, 2006, NASA announced the conclusion of its Global Exploration Strategy and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Architecture_(NASA)" title="Lunar Architecture (NASA)">
        Lunar Architecture
       </a>
       Study.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Lunar Architecture Study's purpose was to "define a series of lunar missions constituting NASA's Lunar campaign to fulfill the Lunar Exploration elements" of the Vision for Space Exploration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExpStrat_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ExpStrat-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       What resulted was a basic plan for a lunar outpost near one of the poles of the Moon, which would permanently house astronauts in six-month shifts. These studies were made before the discovery of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        water ice
       </a>
       (5.6 ± 2.9% by mass) in a polar crater,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colaprete2010_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Colaprete2010-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which may substantially affect plans.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reference_architecture">
        Reference architecture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_outpost_(NASA)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reference architecture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A reference architecture was established for this outpost, based on a location on the rim of the
       <a href="/wiki/Shackleton_(crater)" title="Shackleton (crater)">
        Shackleton crater
       </a>
       , located in the immense
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pole-Aitken_basin" title="South Pole-Aitken basin">
        South Pole-Aitken basin
       </a>
       , near the Moon's south pole. At a presentation on December 4, 2006,
       <a href="/wiki/Doug_Cooke" title="Doug Cooke">
        Doug Cooke
       </a>
       (Deputy Associate Administrator, NASA Exploration Systems Mission Directorate) described an area "that is... sunlit... 75 to 80 percent of the time, and it is adjacent to a permanently dark region in which there are potentially volatiles that we can extract and use.... This sunlit area is about the size of the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Mall" title="National Mall">
        Washington Mall
       </a>
       ."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (approximately 1.25 km²). The Indian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exploration_of_the_moon" title="Exploration of the moon">
        Chandrayaan
       </a>
       orbiter helped in the determination of the precise location of the outpost.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other locations considered for possible lunar outposts included the rim of
       <a href="/wiki/Peary_(crater)" title="Peary (crater)">
        Peary crater
       </a>
       near the lunar north pole and the Malapert Mountain region on the rim of
       <a href="/wiki/Malapert_(crater)" title="Malapert (crater)">
        Malapert crater
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The outpost design included:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Habitation modules
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">
         Solar power
        </a>
        units
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_rover" title="Lunar rover">
         Unpressurized rovers
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-Situ_Resource_Utilization" title="In-Situ Resource Utilization">
         In-Situ Resource Utilization
        </a>
        (ISRU) unit
       </li>
       <li>
        Surface mobility carrier
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The outpost would have been supplied by a mixed crew and cargo
       <a href="/wiki/Altair_(spacecraft)" title="Altair (spacecraft)">
        Altair
       </a>
       lander, capable of bringing four astronauts and a payload of six tons to the Moon's surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       As planned, an incremental buildup would begin with four-person crews making several seven-day visits to the moon until their power supplies, rovers and living quarters were operational. The first mission would begin by 2020. This would be followed by 180-day missions to prepare for journeys to
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_outpost_(NASA)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The concept of establishing a
       <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_Moon" title="Colonization of the Moon">
        long-term human presence on the Moon
       </a>
       can be traced back to the late 1950s. The
       <a href="/wiki/Lunex_Project" title="Lunex Project">
        Lunex Project
       </a>
       , conceptualized in 1958, was a US Air Force plan to construct an underground
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Base" title="Air Force Base">
        Air Force Base
       </a>
       on the Moon. On June 8, 1959, the US Army's
       <a href="/wiki/Army_Ballistic_Missile_Agency" title="Army Ballistic Missile Agency">
        Ballistic Missile Agency
       </a>
       (ABMA) organized a task force called
       <a href="/wiki/Project_Horizon" title="Project Horizon">
        Project Horizon
       </a>
       to assess the feasibility of constructing a
       <a href="/wiki/Military_base" title="Military base">
        military base
       </a>
       on the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Project Horizon proposed using a series of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn_(rocket_family)" title="Saturn (rocket family)">
        Saturn
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaturnPlan_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SaturnPlan-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       launches to pre-construct an outpost while in Earth orbit, with the intention of subsequently delivering and landing the completed assembly on the Moon. Additional
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn_(rocket_family)" title="Saturn (rocket family)">
        Saturn
       </a>
       launches each month would then ship supplies to the inhabitants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaturnPlan_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-SaturnPlan-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

         <div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">
          Heavy, pressurized lunar rover for long duration treks across the moon's surface. The rover contains all facilities and supplies to house approx 4 crew for up to 2 weeks. A crew airlock permits crew to exit and enter the rover and may double as a docking port to the lunar base. A smaller sample airlock permits the crew, using remote manipulators mounted on the rover front to select, pick-up and retrieve samples without exiting the rover. Wheel design is based on one of the more favorable flex wheels developed during Apollo. Cupola on top is important for viewing the terrain at a much greater distance and along 360 degrees of the horizon, and is based on the ISS cupola design. Concept: NASA (1990)
         </div>
        </div>
        <div style="clear:left">
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h2>
       <span id="Other_countries.27_plans">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries'_plans">
        Other countries' plans
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_outpost_(NASA)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other countries' plans">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency (
       <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">
        JAXA
       </a>
       ) plans a manned lunar landing around 2020 that would lead to a manned lunar base by 2030; however, there is no budget yet for this project.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       China National Space Administration (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNSA" title="CNSA">
        CNSA
       </a>
       ) has commenced the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chang%27e_program" title="Chang'e program">
        Chang'e program
       </a>
       for exploring the Moon to investigate the prospect of lunar mining, specifically for mining
       <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">
        isotope
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Helium-3" title="Helium-3">
        helium-3
       </a>
       for use as an energy source on Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luan_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Luan-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       CNSA director
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Luan_Enjie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Luan Enjie (page does not exist)">
        Luan Enjie
       </a>
       has stated, humans must learn to leave
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and "set up self-sufficient extraterrestrial homeland."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luan_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Luan-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       China launched the
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_1" title="Chang'e 1">
        Chang'e 1
       </a>
       robotic lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">
        orbiter
       </a>
       on October 10, 2007.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federal_Space_Agency" title="Russian Federal Space Agency">
        Russian Federal Space Agency
       </a>
       (Roscosmos) has planned a fully robotic lunar base called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunny_Poligon" title="Lunny Poligon">
        Lunny Poligon
       </a>
       . The project is planned for 2020, with an expected completion date of 2037.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Justification">
        Justification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_outpost_(NASA)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Justification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the words of former
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       Administrator,
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Griffin" title="Michael D. Griffin">
        Michael D. Griffin
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         The goal isn't just scientific exploration.... It's also about extending the range of human habitat out from Earth into the solar system as we go forward in time.... In the long run a single-planet species will not survive.... If we humans want to survive for hundreds of thousands or millions of years, we must ultimately populate other planets.
         <p>
          Now, today the technology is such that this is barely conceivable. We're in the infancy of it.... I'm talking about that one day, I don't know when that day is, but there will be more human beings who live off the Earth than on it. We may well have people living on the moon. We may have people living on the moons of Jupiter and other planets. We may have people making habitats on asteroids... I know that humans will colonize the solar system and one day go beyond.
         </p>
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       proposes six "Lunar exploration themes" to answer the question, "Why should we return to the Moon?"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        <b>
         Human Civilization:
        </b>
        Extend human presence to the Moon to enable
        <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_Moon" title="Colonization of the Moon">
         eventual settlement
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Scientific Knowledge:
        </b>
        Pursue scientific activities that address fundamental questions about the history of Earth, the solar system and the universe; and therefore, about our place in them.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Exploration Preparation:
        </b>
        Test technologies, systems, flight operations and exploration techniques to reduce the risks and increase the productivity of future missions to Mars and beyond.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Global Partnerships:
        </b>
        Provide a challenging, shared and peaceful activity that unites nations in pursuit of common objectives.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Economic Expansion:
        </b>
        Expand Earth's economic sphere, and conduct lunar activities with benefits to life on the home planet.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Public Engagement:
        </b>
        Use a lively space exploration program to engage the public, encourage students and help develop the high-technology workforce that will be required to address the challenges of tomorrow.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       According to retired
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       Office of Inspector General Senior Special Agent Joseph Richard Gutheinz, Jr., if "NASA succeeds, we may very well see the first permanent manned presence on the moon in 2024."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Gutheinz, who teaches risk management, once investigated the
       <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">
        Mir Space Station
       </a>
       fire and collision and frequently speaks out against unnecessary risks in space, although he considers settling the moon to be an acceptable risk.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
        Criticism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_outpost_(NASA)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criticism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Criticisms come from groups that want the manned exploration money diverted to Mars, from those who prefer unmanned exploration, and from those who simply want the money spent elsewhere. The criticisms listed here mostly predate the discovery of significant amounts of polar water ice.
       <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Foust" title="Jeff Foust">
        Jeff Foust
       </a>
       , writing for
       <i>
        The Space Review
       </i>
       , called the six themes that NASA released too "broad" and the explanations supporting them "shallow." He also argues that a Moon base is a poor use of resources, since "science can be done for far less money by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_versus_robotic_spaceflight" title="Human versus robotic spaceflight">
        robotic missions
       </a>
       —which also don’t put human lives at risk."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        Los Angeles Times
       </i>
       seconded that in an editorial, saying "Manned moon flight may appeal to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_boomer" title="Baby boomer">
        baby boomers
       </a>
       , but it makes little scientific sense for most space missions these days. Robots can now perform, or be developed to perform, most of the tasks people would do at a moon station."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Columnist
       <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Easterbrook" title="Gregg Easterbrook">
        Gregg Easterbrook
       </a>
       , who has reported on the space program for decades, has criticized the plans as a poor use of resources. He writes that
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Although, of course, the base could yield a great discovery, its scientific value is likely to be small while its price is extremely high. Worse, moon-base nonsense may for decades divert NASA resources from the agency's legitimate missions, draining funding from real needs in order to construct human history's silliest
        <a href="/wiki/White_elephant" title="White elephant">
         white elephant
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       According to Easterbrook, the billions of dollars that a lunar colony might cost should instead be devoted to exploring the solar system with
       <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">
        space probes
       </a>
       ;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_observatory" title="Space observatory">
        space observatories
       </a>
       ; and protecting the Earth from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-Earth_asteroid" title="Near-Earth asteroid">
        near-Earth asteroids
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
        Buzz Aldrin
       </a>
       , the second of twelve men to have walked on the Moon, disagrees with NASA's current goals and priorities, including their plans for a lunar outpost. While not necessarily opposed to sending people back to the Moon, Aldrin argues that NASA should concentrate on a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_mission_to_Mars" title="Manned mission to Mars">
        crewed mission to Mars
       </a>
       and leave further lunar exploration and the establishment of a base there to a consortium of other countries under U.S. leadership.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aldrin_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-aldrin-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In a July 2009 editorial in the
       <i>
        Washington Post
       </i>
       , he said that NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Vision_for_Space_Exploration" title="Vision for Space Exploration">
        Vision for Space Exploration
       </a>
       "is not visionary; nor will it ultimately be successful in restoring American space leadership. Like its Apollo predecessor, this plan will prove to be a dead end littered with broken spacecraft, broken dreams and broken policies." He continued by saying that
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        the lunar surface... is a poor location for homesteading. The moon is a lifeless, barren world, its stark desolation matched by its hostility to all living things. And replaying the glory days of Apollo will not advance the cause of American space leadership or inspire the support and enthusiasm of the public and the next generation of space explorers.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aldrin2_18-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-aldrin2-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2008_concepts_study">
        2008 concepts study
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_outpost_(NASA)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2008 concepts study">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       On June 6, 2008 NASA announced a set of six research opportunities and requested proposals for research funding in response to the announcement.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The overall budget for research conducted as part of this "Lunar Surface Systems Concepts Study" is anticipated to be $2 million. Proposals will be selected and contracts awarded in August 2008 by the NASA Constellation
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Surface_Systems_Project_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lunar Surface Systems Project Office (page does not exist)">
        Lunar Surface Systems Project Office
       </a>
       (LSSPO).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="2010.2F2011_surface_system_concept_review">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2010/2011_surface_system_concept_review">
        2010/2011 surface system concept review
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_outpost_(NASA)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2010/2011 surface system concept review">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The LSSPO was established at the
       <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center">
        Johnson Space Center
       </a>
       in August 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The LSSPO was studying lunar surface systems such as "habitation systems",
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISRU" title="ISRU">
        ISRU
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rover_(space_exploration)" title="Rover (space exploration)">
        rovers
       </a>
       , power production and storage, systems to meet science and exploration objectives and safety systems. The LSSPO was expected to conduct a surface system concept review in the 2010 or 2011 timeframe.
      </p>
