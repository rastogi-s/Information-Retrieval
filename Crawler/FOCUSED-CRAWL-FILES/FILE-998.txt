   </h1>

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       An
       <b>
        exomoon
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        extrasolar moon
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       that orbits an
       <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">
        exoplanet
       </a>
       or other non-stellar
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object#Categories_by_location" title="Astronomical object">
        extrasolar body
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WRD-20150127_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-WRD-20150127-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is
       <a href="/wiki/Inference" title="Inference">
        inferred
       </a>
       from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical" title="Empirical">
        empirical
       </a>
       study of natural satellites in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       that they are likely to be common elements of
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">
        planetary systems
       </a>
       . The majority of detected exoplanets are
       <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">
        giant planets
       </a>
       . In the Solar System, the giant planets have large collections of natural satellites (see
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">
        Moons of Jupiter
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Saturn" title="Moons of Saturn">
        Moons of Saturn
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">
        Moons of Uranus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Neptune" title="Moons of Neptune">
        Moons of Neptune
       </a>
       ). Therefore, it is reasonable to assume that exomoons are equally common.
      </p>
      <p>
       Though exomoons are difficult to detect and confirm using current techniques,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kipping09_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kipping09-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       observations from missions such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(spacecraft)" title="Kepler (spacecraft)">
         Kepler
        </a>
       </i>
       have observed a number of candidates, including some that may be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_satellite_habitability" title="Natural satellite habitability">
        habitats for extraterrestrial life
       </a>
       and one that may be a
       <a href="/wiki/Rogue_planet" title="Rogue planet">
        rogue planet
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WRD-20150127_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-WRD-20150127-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_of_satellites_around_brown_dwarfs">
        Definition of satellites around brown dwarfs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition of satellites around brown dwarfs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although traditional usage implies
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moons
       </a>
       orbit a
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       , the discovery of planet-sized satellites around
       <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">
        brown dwarfs
       </a>
       blurs the distinction between planets and moons, due to the low mass of such failed
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       . To resolve this confusion, the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       declared, "Objects with
       <a href="/wiki/True_mass" title="True mass">
        true masses
       </a>
       below the limiting mass for
       <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_fusion" title="Thermonuclear fusion">
        thermonuclear fusion
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">
        deuterium
       </a>
       , that orbit stars or stellar remnants, are planets."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Characteristics of any extrasolar satellite are likely to vary, as do the Solar System's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Solar_System_objects_in_hydrostatic_equilibrium#Round_satellites" title="List of Solar System objects in hydrostatic equilibrium">
        moons
       </a>
       . For extrasolar giant planets orbiting within their stellar
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">
        habitable zone
       </a>
       , there is a prospect a
       <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">
        terrestrial-planet
       </a>
       -sized satellite may be capable of supporting life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canup06_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Canup06-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_inclination">
        Orbital inclination
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbital inclination">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For impact-generated moons of
       <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">
        terrestrial planets
       </a>
       not too far from their star, with a large planet–moon distance, it is expected that the orbital planes of moons will tend to be aligned with the planet's orbit around the star due to tides from the star, but if the planet–moon distance is small it may be inclined. For
       <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">
        gas giants
       </a>
       , the orbits of moons will tend to be aligned with the giant planet's equator because these formed in circumplanetary disks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epj_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-epj-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_moons_around_planets_close_to_their_stars">
        Lack of moons around planets close to their stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lack of moons around planets close to their stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Planets close to their stars on circular orbits will tend to despin and become
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidally_locked" title="Tidally locked">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       . As the planet's rotation slows down the radius of a
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous orbit
       </a>
       of the planet moves outwards from the planet. For planets tidally locked to their stars, the distance from the planet at which the moon will be in a synchronous orbit around the planet is outside the
       <a href="/wiki/Hill_sphere" title="Hill sphere">
        Hill sphere
       </a>
       of the planet. The Hill sphere of the planet is the region where its gravity dominates that of the star so it can hold on to its moons. Moons inside the synchronous orbit radius of a planet will spiral into the planet. Therefore, if the synchronous orbit is outside the Hill sphere, then all moons will spiral into the planet. If the synchronous orbit is not
       <a href="/wiki/Three-body_problem" title="Three-body problem">
        three-body stable
       </a>
       then moons outside this radius will escape orbit before they reach the synchronous orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epj_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-epj-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_detection_methods">
        Proposed detection methods
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proposed detection methods">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Blue_Moon.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="724" data-file-width="1280" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/The_Blue_Moon.png/220px-The_Blue_Moon.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/The_Blue_Moon.png/330px-The_Blue_Moon.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/The_Blue_Moon.png/440px-The_Blue_Moon.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Blue_Moon.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Artist's impression of a hypothetical Earth-like moon around a Saturn-like exoplanet
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The existence of exomoons around many
       <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">
        exoplanets
       </a>
       is theorized.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canup06_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Canup06-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite the great successes of planet hunters with
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_spectroscopy" title="Doppler spectroscopy">
        Doppler spectroscopy
       </a>
       of the host star,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       exomoons cannot be found with this technique. This is because the resultant shifted stellar spectra due to the presence of a planet plus additional satellites would behave identically to a single point-mass moving in orbit of the host star. In recognition of this, there have been several other methods proposed for detecting exomoons, including:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methods_of_detecting_extrasolar_planets#Direct_imaging" title="Methods of detecting extrasolar planets">
         Direct imaging
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microlensing" title="Microlensing">
         Microlensing
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methods_of_detecting_extrasolar_planets#Pulsar_timing" title="Methods of detecting extrasolar planets">
         Pulsar timing
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="#Transit_timing_effects">
         Transit timing effects
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transit_method" title="Transit method">
         Transit method
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_imaging">
        Direct imaging
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Direct imaging">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Direct imaging of an exoplanet is extremely challenging due to the large difference in brightness between the star and exoplanet as well as the small size and irradiance of the planet. These problems are greater for exomoons in most cases. However, it has been theorized that tidally heated exomoons could shine as brightly as some exoplanets.
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        Tidal forces
       </a>
       can heat up an exomoon because energy is dissipated by differential forces on it.
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       , a tidally heated moon orbiting
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       , has volcanoes powered by tidal forces. If a tidally heated exomoon is sufficiently tidally heated and is distant enough from its star for the moon's light not to be drowned out, it would be possible for future telescopes (such as the
       <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">
        James Webb Space Telescope
       </a>
       ) to image it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Doppler_spectroscopy_of_host_planet">
        Doppler spectroscopy of host planet
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Doppler spectroscopy of host planet">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The spectra of exoplanets have been successfully partially retrieved for several cases, including
       <a href="/wiki/HD_189733_b" title="HD 189733 b">
        HD 189733 b
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/HD_209458_b" title="HD 209458 b">
        HD 209458 b
       </a>
       . The quality of the retrieved spectra is significantly more affected by noise than the stellar spectrum. As a result, the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_resolution" title="Spectral resolution">
        spectral resolution
       </a>
       , and number of retrieved spectral features, is much lower than the level required to perform doppler spectroscopy of the exoplanet.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_of_radio_wave_emissions_from_the_magnetosphere_of_host_planet">
        Detection of radio wave emissions from the magnetosphere of host planet
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Detection of radio wave emissions from the magnetosphere of host planet">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       During its orbit,
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       ’s ionosphere interacts with
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       ’s
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       , to create a frictional current that causes radio wave emissions. These are called “Io-controlled decametric emissions” and the researchers believe finding similar emissions near known exoplanets could be key to predicting where other moons exist.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Microlensing">
        Microlensing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Microlensing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2002, Cheongho Han &amp; Wonyong Han proposed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microlensing" title="Microlensing">
        microlensing
       </a>
       be used to detect exomoons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The authors found detecting satellite signals in lensing light curves will be very difficult because the signals are seriously smeared out by the severe finite-source effect even for events involved with source stars with small angular radii.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pulsar_timing">
        Pulsar timing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pulsar timing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2008, Lewis,
       <a href="/wiki/Penny_Sackett" title="Penny Sackett">
        Sackett
       </a>
       , and Mardling
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Monash_University" title="Monash University">
        Monash University
       </a>
       , Australia, proposed using
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methods_of_detecting_extrasolar_planets#Pulsar_timing" title="Methods of detecting extrasolar planets">
        pulsar timing
       </a>
       to detect the moons of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulsar_planets" title="Pulsar planets">
        pulsar planets
       </a>
       . The authors applied their method to the case of
       <a href="/wiki/PSR_B1620-26_b" title="PSR B1620-26 b">
        PSR B1620-26 b
       </a>
       and found that a stable moon orbiting this planet could be detected, if the moon had a separation of about one fiftieth of that of the orbit of the planet around the pulsar, and a mass ratio to the planet of 5% or larger.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transit_timing_effects">
        Transit timing effects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transit timing effects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2009,
       <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">
        University College London
       </a>
       -based astronomer David Kipping published a paper
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kipping09_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-kipping09-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       outlining how by combining multiple observations of variations in the time of mid-transit (TTV, caused by the planet leading or trailing the planet–moon system's
       <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">
        barycenter
       </a>
       when the pair are oriented roughly perpendicular to the line of sight) with variations of the transit duration (TDV, caused by the planet moving along the direction path of transit relative to the planet–moon system's barycenter when the moon–planet axis lies roughly along the line of sight) a unique exomoon signature is produced. Furthermore, the work demonstrated how both the mass of the exomoon and its orbital distance from the planet could be determined using the two effects.
      </p>
      <p>
       In a later study, Kipping concluded that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">
        habitable zone
       </a>
       exomoons could be detected by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler_Mission" title="Kepler Mission">
        <i>
         Kepler Space Telescope
        </i>
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       using the TTV and TDV effects.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transit_method">
        Transit method
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transit method">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When an exoplanet passes in front of the host star, a small dip in the light received from the star may be observed. This effect, also known as occultation, is proportional to the square of the planet's radius. If a planet and a moon passed in front of a host star, both objects should produce a dip in the observed light.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A planet–moon eclipse may also occur
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       during the transit, but such events have an inherently low probability.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_sampling_effects">
        Orbital sampling effects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Orbital sampling effects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       If a glass bottle is held up to the light it is easier to see through the middle of the glass than it is near the edges. Similarly a sequence of samples of a moon's position will be more bunched up at the edges of the moon's orbit of a planet than in the middle. If a moon orbits a planet that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transit_method" title="Transit method">
        transits
       </a>
       its star then the moon will also transit the star and this bunching up at the edges may be detectable in the transit light curves if a sufficient number of measurements are made. The larger the star the greater the number of measurements are needed to create observable bunching. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler_spacecraft" title="Kepler spacecraft">
        Kepler spacecraft
       </a>
       data may contain enough data to detect moons around red dwarfs using orbital sampling effects but won't have enough data for Sun-like stars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20150130_25-0">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitability">
        Habitability
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exomoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Habitability">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_satellite_habitability" title="Natural satellite habitability">
        Natural satellite habitability
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Habitability of exomoons has been considered in at least two studies published in peer-reviewed journals. René Heller &amp; Rory Barnes
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       considered stellar and planetary illumination on moons as well as the effect of eclipses on their orbit-averaged surface illumination. They also considered tidal heating as a threat for their habitability. In Sect. 4 in their paper, they introduce a new concept to define the habitable orbits of moons. Referring to the concept of the circumstellar habitable zone for planets, they define an inner border for a moon to be habitable around a certain planet and call it the circumplanetary "habitable edge". Moons closer to their planet than the habitable edge are uninhabitable. In a second study, René Heller
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       then included the effect of eclipses into this concept as well as constraints from a satellite's orbital stability. He found that, depending on a moon's orbital eccentricity, there is a minimum mass for stars to host habitable moons at around 0.2 solar masses.
      </p>
      <p>
       Taking as an example the smaller
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       , at less than 1% the mass of the Earth, Lehmer et al. found if it were to end up near to Earth orbit it would only be able to hold onto its atmosphere for a few million years. However, for any larger,
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       -sized moons venturing into its solar system’s habitable zone, an atmosphere and surface water could be retained pretty much indefinitely. Models for moon formation suggest the formation of even more massive moons than Ganymede is common around many of the super-Jovian exoplanets.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
