      <p>
       <b>
        Amalthea
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/əl/: 'le' in 'bottle'">
            əl
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/θ/: 'th' in 'thigh'">
            θ
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         am-əl-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          THEE
         </span>
         -ə
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Ἀμάλθεια
       </span>
       ) is the third
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moon
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       in order of distance from the planet. It was discovered on 9 September 1892, by
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Emerson_Barnard" title="Edward Emerson Barnard">
        Edward Emerson Barnard
       </a>
       and named after
       <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(mythology)" title="Amalthea (mythology)">
        Amalthea
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">
        nymph
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarnard1892_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarnard1892-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter V
        </span>
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Amalthea is in a close orbit around Jupiter and is within the outer edge of the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter#Amalthea_gossamer_ring" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        Amalthea Gossamer Ring
       </a>
       , which is formed from dust ejected from its surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From its surface, Jupiter would appear 46.5
       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">
        degrees
       </a>
       in diameter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stub2_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-stub2-10">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Amalthea is the largest of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        inner satellites of Jupiter
       </a>
       . Irregularly shaped and reddish in color, it is thought to consist of porous water ice with unknown amounts of other materials. Its surface features include large craters and ridges.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Amalthea was photographed in 1979 by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">
         Voyager 1 and 2
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft, and later, in more detail, by the
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">
        <i>
         Galileo
        </i>
        orbiter
       </a>
       in the 1990s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalthea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalthea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amalthea_Voyager-1.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="254" data-file-width="244" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Amalthea_Voyager-1.png/170px-Amalthea_Voyager-1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8f/Amalthea_Voyager-1.png 1.5x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amalthea_Voyager-1.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          Voyager 1
         </i>
         color image of Amalthea (1979)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Amalthea was discovered on 9 September 1892, by
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Emerson_Barnard" title="Edward Emerson Barnard">
        Edward Emerson Barnard
       </a>
       using the
       <a href="/wiki/James_Lick_telescope" title="James Lick telescope">
        36 inch (91 cm) refractor telescope
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">
        Lick Observatory
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarnard1892_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarnard1892-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Observatory1894_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Observatory1894-11">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was the last planetary satellite to be discovered by direct visual observation (as opposed to photographically) and was the first new satellite of Jupiter since
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
        Galileo Galilei
       </a>
       's discovery of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galilean_satellites" title="Galilean satellites">
        Galilean satellites
       </a>
       in 1610.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bakich_2000_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bakich_2000-12">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalthea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Amalthea is named after the
       <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">
        nymph
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(mythology)" title="Amalthea (mythology)">
        Amalthea
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       , who nursed the infant
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       (the Greek equivalent of Jupiter) with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goat%27s_milk" title="Goat's milk">
        goat's milk
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazetteer_names_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gazetteer_names-13">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_numeral" title="Roman numeral">
        Roman numeral
       </a>
       designation is
       <span class="nowrap">
        Jupiter V
       </span>
       . The name "Amalthea" was not formally adopted by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       until 1976,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blunck_2010_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blunck_2010-14">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU_1975_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAU_1975-15">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although it had been in informal use for many decades. The name was initially suggested by
       <a href="/wiki/Camille_Flammarion" title="Camille Flammarion">
        Camille Flammarion
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFlammarion1893_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFlammarion1893-16">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Before 1976, Amalthea was most commonly known simply as
       <span class="nowrap">
        Jupiter V
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimonelli1983_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimonelli1983-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalthea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Amalthea orbits
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       at a distance of 181 000 km (2.54 Jupiter radii). The orbit of Amalthea has an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of 0.003 and an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       of 0.37° relative to the equator of Jupiter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECooper_Murray_et_al.2006_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECooper_Murray_et_al.2006-2">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Such appreciably nonzero values of inclination and eccentricity, though still small, are unusual for an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellite" title="Inner satellite">
        inner satellite
       </a>
       and can be explained by the influence of the innermost
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galilean_satellites" title="Galilean satellites">
        Galilean satellite
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       : in the past Amalthea has passed through several
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        mean-motion resonances
       </a>
       with Io that have excited its inclination and eccentricity (in a mean-motion resonance the ratio of orbital periods of two bodies is a rational number like
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Amalthea's orbit lies near the outer edge of the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter#Amalthea_gossamer_ring" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        Amalthea Gossamer Ring
       </a>
       , which is composed of the dust ejected from the satellite.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Showalter_et_al.1999_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Showalter_et_al.1999-17">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalthea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The surface of Amalthea is very
       <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">
        red
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The reddish color may be due to
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
        sulfur
       </a>
       originating from
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       or some other non-ice material.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Bright patches of less red tint appear on the major slopes of Amalthea, but the nature of this color is currently unknown.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The surface of Amalthea is slightly brighter than surfaces of other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        inner satellites of Jupiter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimonelli_Rossier_et_al.2000_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimonelli_Rossier_et_al.2000-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is also a substantial asymmetry between leading and trailing
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        hemispheres
       </a>
       : the leading hemisphere is 1.3 times brighter than the trailing one. The asymmetry is probably caused by the higher velocity and frequency of
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impacts
       </a>
       on the leading hemisphere, which excavate a bright material—presumably ice—from the interior of the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimonelli_Rossier_et_al.2000_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimonelli_Rossier_et_al.2000-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">

      <p>
       Amalthea is irregularly shaped, with the best
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">
        ellipsoidal
       </a>
       approximation being
       <span class="nowrap">
        250 × 146 × 128 km
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From this, Amalthea's surface area is likely between 88,000 and 170,000 square kilometers, or somewhere near 130,000. Like all other inner
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">
        moons of Jupiter
       </a>
       it is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidally_locked" title="Tidally locked">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       with the planet, the long axis pointing towards Jupiter at all times.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its surface is heavily scarred by
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        craters
       </a>
       , some of which are extremely large relative to the size of the moon:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan_crater" title="Pan crater">
        Pan
       </a>
       , the largest crater, measures 100 km across and is at least 8 km deep.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another crater,
       <a href="/wiki/Gaea_(crater)" title="Gaea (crater)">
        Gaea
       </a>
       , measures 80 km across and is likely twice as deep as Pan.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Amalthea has several prominent bright spots, two of which are named. They are
       <a href="/wiki/Lyctos_Facula" title="Lyctos Facula">
        Lyctos Facula
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ida_Facula" title="Ida Facula">
        Ida Facula
       </a>
       , with width reaching up to 25 km. They are located on the edge of ridges.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Amalthea's irregular shape and large size led in the past to a conclusion that it is a fairly strong, rigid body,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_9-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       where it was argued that a body composed of ices or other weak materials would have been pulled into a more
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        spherical
       </a>
       shape by its own gravity. However, on 5 November 2002, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_spacecraft" title="Galileo spacecraft">
        <i>
         Galileo
        </i>
        orbiter
       </a>
       made a targeted flyby that came within 160 km of Amalthea and the deflection of its orbit was used to compute the moon's mass (its volume had been calculated previously—to within 10% or so—from a careful analysis of all extant images).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the end, Amalthea's density was found to be as low as
       <span class="nowrap">
        0.86 g/cm
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESwiss_Cheese_Moon_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwiss_Cheese_Moon-18">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       so it must be either a relatively icy body or very porous "
       <a href="/wiki/Rubble_pile" title="Rubble pile">
        rubble pile
       </a>
       " or, more likely, something in between. Recent measurements of infrared spectra from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subaru_(telescope)" title="Subaru (telescope)">
        Subaru telescope
       </a>
       suggest that the moon indeed contains
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrous" title="Hydrous">
        hydrous
       </a>
       minerals (or organic materials), indicating that it cannot have formed in its current position, since the hot primordial Jupiter would have melted it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETakato_Bus_et_al.2004_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETakato_Bus_et_al.2004-19">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is therefore likely to have formed farther from the planet or to be a captured
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       body.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       No images were taken during this flyby (
       <i>
        Galileo
       </i>
       <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">
        '
       </span>
       s cameras had been deactivated due to radiation damage in January 2002), and the resolution of other available images is generally low.
      </p>
      <p>
       Amalthea radiates slightly more heat than it receives from the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , which is probably due to the influence of Jovian heat flux (&lt;9
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        kelvin
       </a>
       ), sunlight reflected from the planet (&lt;5 K), and charged particle bombardment (&lt;2 K).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimonelli1983_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimonelli1983-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is a trait shared with
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       , although for different reasons.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id="Relationship_with_Jupiter.27s_rings">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Jupiter's_rings">
        Relationship with Jupiter's rings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalthea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship with Jupiter's rings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal force
       </a>
       from Jupiter and Amalthea's low density and irregular shape, the
       <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">
        escape velocity
       </a>
       at its surface points closest to and furthest from
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       is no more than 1 m/s and dust can easily escape from it after, e.g. micrometeorite impacts; this dust forms the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter#Amalthea_gossamer_ring" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        Amalthea Gossamer Ring
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_9-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During its flyby of Amalthea, the
       <i>
        Galileo
       </i>
       orbiter's
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)#The_Galileo_star_scanner" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">
        star scanner
       </a>
       detected nine flashes that appear to be small
       <a href="/wiki/Moonlet" title="Moonlet">
        moonlets
       </a>
       near the orbit of Amalthea. Because they were sighted only from one location, their true distances could not be measured. These moonlets may be anywhere in size from gravel to stadium-sized. Their origins are unknown, but they may be gravitationally captured into current orbit or they may be ejecta from
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">
        meteor impacts
       </a>
       on Amalthea. On the next and final orbit (just an hour before destruction),
       <i>
        Galileo
       </i>
       detected one more such moonlet. However, this time Amalthea was on the other side of the planet, so it is probable that the particles form a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">
        ring
       </a>
       around the planet near Amalthea's orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fieseler_2004_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fieseler_2004-21">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL_2003_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-JPL_2003-22">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU_2003_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAU_2003-23">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakdawalla_2013_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lakdawalla_2013-24">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Views_to_and_from_Amalthea">
        Views to and from Amalthea
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalthea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Views to and from Amalthea">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_skies" title="Extraterrestrial skies">
        Extraterrestrial skies
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       From Jupiter's surface—or rather, from just above its cloudtops—Amalthea would appear very bright, shining with a
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        magnitude
       </a>
       of −4.7,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stub2_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-stub2-10">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
       similar to that of
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       from Earth. At only 8
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcminutes" title="Arcminutes">
        arcminutes
       </a>
       across,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sizecalc_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sizecalc-25">
         [c]
        </a>
       </sup>
       its disc would be barely discernible. Amalthea's orbital period is only slightly longer than its parent planet's day (about 20% in this case), which means it would cross Jupiter's sky very slowly. The time between moonrise and moonset would be over 29 hours.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stub2_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-stub2-10">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter_from_Amalthea.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="549" data-file-width="792" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Jupiter_from_Amalthea.jpg/220px-Jupiter_from_Amalthea.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Jupiter_from_Amalthea.jpg/330px-Jupiter_from_Amalthea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Jupiter_from_Amalthea.jpg/440px-Jupiter_from_Amalthea.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter_from_Amalthea.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Simulated view of Jupiter from Amalthea.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Willy_Ley" title="Willy Ley">
        Willy Ley
       </a>
       suggested Amalthea as a base for observing Jupiter, because of its nearness to the planet, almost synchronous orbit, and small size making a landing easy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196807_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ley196807-26">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From the surface of Amalthea, Jupiter would look enormous: 46
       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">
        degrees
       </a>
       across,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sizecalc_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-sizecalc-25">
         [c]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it would appear roughly 92 times larger than the
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       . Because Amalthea is in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchronous_rotation" title="Synchronous rotation">
        synchronous rotation
       </a>
       , Jupiter would not appear to move, and would not be visible from one side of Amalthea. The Sun would disappear behind Jupiter's bulk for an hour and a half each revolution, and Amalthea's short rotation period gives it just under six hours of
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/daylight" title="wiktionary:daylight">
        daylight
       </a>
       . Though Jupiter would appear 900 times brighter than the full moon, its light would be spread over an area some 8500 times greater and it would not look as bright per surface unit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stub2_10-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-stub2-10">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>

        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amalthea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       During 1979, the unmanned
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       space probes obtained the first images of Amalthea to resolve its surface features.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_3-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They also measured the visible and infrared
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        spectra
       </a>
       and surface temperature.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimonelli1983_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimonelli1983-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_spacecraft" title="Galileo spacecraft">
        <i>
         Galileo
        </i>
        orbiter
       </a>
       completed the imaging of Amalthea's surface.
       <i>
        Galileo
       </i>
       made its final satellite fly-by at a distance of approximately 244 km (152 mi) from Amalthea's center (at a height of about 160–170 km) on 5 November 2002, permitting the moon's mass to be accurately determined, while changing
       <i>
        Galileo's
       </i>
       trajectory so that it would plunge into Jupiter in September 2003 at the end of its mission.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2006, Amalthea's orbit was refined with measurements from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
