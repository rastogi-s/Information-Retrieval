      <p>
       <b>
        Nix
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Pluto" title="Moons of Pluto">
        natural satellite of Pluto
       </a>
       . It was discovered along with
       <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(moon)" title="Hydra (moon)">
        Hydra
       </a>
       (Pluto's outermost satellite) in June 2005. It was imaged along with
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and its other moons by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft as it flew by the Pluto system in July 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UniverseToday_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UniverseToday-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Of the four small Plutonian moons the best pictures are of Nix, with resolutions as high as 330 meters per pixel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nix_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pluto_system_2005_discovery_images.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Pluto_system_2005_discovery_images.jpg/250px-Pluto_system_2005_discovery_images.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Pluto_system_2005_discovery_images.jpg/375px-Pluto_system_2005_discovery_images.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Pluto_system_2005_discovery_images.jpg/500px-Pluto_system_2005_discovery_images.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pluto_system_2005_discovery_images.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Discovery images of Nix (and Hydra)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Nix was found by the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       Pluto Companion Search Team, composed of Hal A. Weaver,
       <a href="/wiki/Alan_Stern" title="Alan Stern">
        Alan Stern
       </a>
       , Max J. Mutchler, Andrew J. Steffl,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marc_W._Buie" title="Marc W. Buie">
        Marc W. Buie
       </a>
       , William J. Merline, John R. Spencer, Eliot F. Young, and Leslie A. Young. The discovery images were taken on 15 May 2005 and 18 May 2005. Nix and Hydra were independently discovered by Max J. Mutchler on 15 June 2005 and by Andrew J. Steffl on 15 August 2005. The discoveries were announced on 31 October 2005, after confirmation by
       <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">
        precoveries
       </a>
       from 2002. They were
       <a href="/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy" title="Provisional designation in astronomy">
        provisionally designated
       </a>
       <b>
        S/2005 P 1
       </b>
       (Hydra) and
       <b>
        S/2005 P 2
       </b>
       (Nix) and casually referred to as "P1" and "P2", respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8723_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC8723-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8625_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC8625-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nix_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The formal name "Nix", from
       <a href="/wiki/Nyx" title="Nyx">
        the Greek goddess of darkness and night
       </a>
       and mother of
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(mythology)" title="Charon (mythology)">
        Charon
       </a>
       (ferryman of
       <a href="/wiki/Hades" title="Hades">
        Hades
       </a>
       ), was announced on 21 June 2006 on
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       Circular 8723,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8723_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC8723-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       where the designation
       <b>
        Pluto II
       </b>
       is also given. The initials N and H, for Nix and Hydra, come from "
       <i>
        New Horizons
       </i>
       ". The original proposal was to use the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Greek" title="Classical Greek">
        classical
       </a>
       spelling
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyx_(mythology)" title="Nyx (mythology)">
        Nyx
       </a>
       , but to avoid confusion with the
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/3908_Nyx" title="3908 Nyx">
        3908 Nyx
       </a>
       , the spelling was changed to Nix. Jürgen Blunck explains it as the "Spanish translation" of the Greek name.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nix_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Nix follows a circular orbit in the same plane as
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scimag000_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-scimag000-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is in a 3:2
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(moon)" title="Hydra (moon)">
        Hydra
       </a>
       , and a 9:11 resonance with
       <a href="/wiki/Styx_(moon)" title="Styx (moon)">
        Styx
       </a>
       (the ratios represent numbers of orbits completed per unit time; the period ratios are the inverses).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShowalterHamilton2015_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ShowalterHamilton2015-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Witze2015_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Witze2015-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a result of this "Laplace-like" 3-body resonance, it has conjunctions with Styx and Hydra in a 2:3 ratio.
      </p>
      <p>
       Its orbital period of 24.9 days is also close to a 1:4 orbital resonance with Charon, but the timing discrepancy is 2.8%; there is no active resonance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShowalterHamilton2015_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ShowalterHamilton2015-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_2006_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buie_2006-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A hypothesis explaining such a near-resonance is that it originated before the outward migration of Charon following the formation of all five known moons, and is maintained by the periodic local fluctuation of 9% in the Pluto–Charon gravitational field strength.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation">
        Rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nix_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As with Hydra and perhaps the other small Plutonian moons, Nix
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tumbling_(rigid_body)" title="Tumbling (rigid body)">
        tumbles
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">
        chaotically
       </a>
       ; the moon's axial tilt and day length vary greatly over short timescales.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbcnews_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nbcnews-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the time of the
       <i>
        New Horizons
       </i>
       flyby, Nix was rotating with a period of 43.9 hours retrograde to Pluto's equator with an inclination of 132 degrees — in other words it was rotating "backwards" in relation to its orbit around Pluto.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPS15_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DPS15-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The rotation period has actually increased by 10% since Nix was discovered.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPS15_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-DPS15-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nix_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Nix has been measured to be 54 by 41 by 36 kilometers (33.6 mi × 25.5 mi × 22.4 mi) in diameter, indicating a very elongated shape, and a very high
       <a href="/wiki/Geometric_albedo" title="Geometric albedo">
        geometric albedo
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scimag000_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-scimag000-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the discovery image, Nix is 6,300 times fainter than Pluto.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brightness_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-brightness-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Early research appeared to show that Nix was reddish like
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and unlike the other moons,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_2006_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buie_2006-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but more-recent reports have been that it is grey like the remaining satellites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern06b_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stern06b-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        New Horizons
       </i>
       spotted a large region with a distinctive red tint surrounding a large crater, which may explain these conflicting results.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa.gov_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa.gov-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Detailed images of the moon have revealed at least 6 impact craters on it, the largest of which is about 15 km across.
      </p>

       <i>
        New Horizons
       </i>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Measurement_20-0">
