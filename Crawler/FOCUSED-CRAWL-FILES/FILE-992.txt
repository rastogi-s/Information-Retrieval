      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=617_Patroclus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Patroclus
       </i>
       orbits in Jupiter's trailing
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrangian point
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         5
        </sub>
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnstonsarchive_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-johnstonsarchive-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in an area called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trojan_camp" title="Trojan camp">
        Trojan camp
       </a>
       after one of the sides in the legendary
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">
        Trojan War
       </a>
       (the other node, at the
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
        point
       </a>
       , is called the "Greek camp").
      </p>
      <p>
       It orbits the Sun at a distance of 4.5–5.9
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        AU
       </a>
       once every 11 years and 11 months (4,353 days). Its orbit has an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of 0.14 and an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       of 22
       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">
        °
       </a>
       with respect to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_1-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-jpldata-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The asteroid's
       <a href="/wiki/Observation_arc" title="Observation arc">
        observation arc
       </a>
       begins at the discovering Heidelberg Observatory in November 1906, about 3 weeks after its official discovery observation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-Patroclus_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPC-Patroclus-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Binary_system">
        Binary system
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=617_Patroclus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Binary system">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 2001, it was discovered that Patroclus is a
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_asteroid" title="Binary asteroid">
        binary system
       </a>
       , made up of two components with a
       <a href="/wiki/Minor-planet_moon" title="Minor-planet moon">
        minor-planet moon
       </a>
       of roughly similar size.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnstonsarchive_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-johnstonsarchive-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merline_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Merline-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU-list_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAU-list-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is one of
       <a href="/wiki/Minor-planet_moon#Jupiter_trojans" title="Minor-planet moon">
        18 binary Trojan asteroids
       </a>
       known to exist. In 2006, accurate measurements of the orbit from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_telescopes" title="Keck telescopes">
        Keck
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Laser_guide_star" title="Laser guide star">
        Laser guide star
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">
        adaptive optics
       </a>
       system were reported.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis2006_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchis2006-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was estimated
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanders_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sanders-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that the two components orbit around their
       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
        center of mass
       </a>
       in 4.283±0.004 d at a distance of 680±20 km in a roughly circular orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnstonsarchive_5-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-johnstonsarchive-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Combining these observations with thermal measurements taken in 2000, the sizes of the components of the system were estimated. The slightly larger component, which measures 141 km in diameter, retains the name Patroclus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnstonsarchive_5-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-johnstonsarchive-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The smaller component, measuring 112 km,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is now named Menoetius,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnstonsarchive_5-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-johnstonsarchive-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       after the legendary Patroclus's father. It was previously known by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provisional_designation" title="Provisional designation">
        provisional designation
       </a>
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic; white-space: nowrap; font-size: 110%">
        S/2001 (617) 1
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=617_Patroclus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
        Composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=617_Patroclus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Recent evidence suggests that the objects are icy like
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comets
       </a>
       , rather than rocky like most
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       . In the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tholen_classification" title="Tholen classification">
        Tholen classification
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Patroclus
       </i>
       is a dark
       <a href="/wiki/P-type_asteroid" title="P-type asteroid">
        P-type asteroid
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lcdb_17-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-lcdb-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because the density of the components (0.8 g/cm³) is less than water and about one third that of rock, it was suggested that the Patroclus system, previously thought to be a pair of rocky
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       , is more similar to a
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comet
       </a>
       in composition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis2006_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchis2006-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is suspected that many Jupiter trojans are in fact small
       <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">
        planetesimals
       </a>
       captured in the Lagrange point of the Jupiter–Sun system during the
       <a href="/wiki/Nice_model" title="Nice model">
        migration
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">
        giant planets
       </a>
       3.9 billion years ago. This scenario was proposed by A. Morbidelli and colleagues in a series of articles published in May 2005 in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">
         Nature
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morbidelli2005_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morbidelli2005-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lightcurves">
        Lightcurves
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=617_Patroclus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lightcurves">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Since 1989, several rotational
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lightcurve" title="Lightcurve">
        lightcurves
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        Patroclus
       </i>
       have been obtained from photometric observations. Analysis of the best rated lightcurves gave a
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation_period" title="Rotation period">
        rotation period
       </a>
       between 102.8 and 103.5 hours with a brightness amplitude of less than 0.1
       <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy)" title="Magnitude (astronomy)">
        magnitude
       </a>
       (
       <small>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LCDB_quality_code" title="LCDB quality code">
         U=2/3/
        </a>
       </small>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis-2006a_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchis-2006a-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geneva-obs_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-geneva-obs-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mueller-2010_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mueller-2010-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oey-2012c_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Oey-2012c-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A low brightness variation typically indicates that a body has a nearly spheroidal shape. Its long rotation period makes it a
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_slow_rotators_(minor_planets)" title="List of slow rotators (minor planets)">
        slow rotator
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diameter_and_albedo">
        Diameter and albedo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=617_Patroclus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diameter and albedo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       According to the surveys carried out by the Infrared Astronomical Satellite
       <a href="/wiki/IRAS" title="IRAS">
        IRAS
       </a>
       , the Japanese
       <a href="/wiki/Akari_(satellite)" title="Akari (satellite)">
        Akari
       </a>
       satellite, and NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Wide-field_Infrared_Survey_Explorer" title="Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer">
        Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer
       </a>
       with its subsequent
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NEOWISE" title="NEOWISE">
        NEOWISE
       </a>
       mission,
       <i>
        Patroclus
       </i>
       measures between 140.36 and 140.92 kilometers in diameter and its surface has an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_albedo" title="Astronomical albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       of 0.047.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WISE_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-WISE-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav-2012_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav-2012-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AKARI_8-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-AKARI-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIMPS_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-SIMPS-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        Collaborative Asteroid Lightcurve Link
       </i>
       adopts the results obtained by IRAS, that is, an albedo of 0.0471 and a diameter of 140.92 kilometers based on an
       <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">
        absolute magnitude
       </a>
       of 8.19.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lcdb_17-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-lcdb-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=617_Patroclus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Patroclus is a proposed target for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lucy_(spacecraft)" title="Lucy (spacecraft)">
         Lucy
        </a>
       </i>
       , a mission to several asteroids, mostly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter_trojans" title="Jupiter trojans">
        Jupiter trojans
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lucy-announcement_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lucy-announcement-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mission's targets with their flyby dates are:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levison-2017_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Levison-2017-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/52246_Donaldjohanson" title="52246 Donaldjohanson">
         52246 Donaldjohanson
        </a>
        — 20 April 2025: 4 km diameter C-type asteroid in the inner main-belt, member of ~130Myr old Erigone family;
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/3548_Eurybates" title="3548 Eurybates">
         3548 Eurybates
        </a>
        — 12 August 2027: 64 km diameter C-type Jupiter Trojan in the Greek camp at
        <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
         L
         <sub>
          4
         </sub>
        </a>
        , largest member of the only confirmed disruptive collisional family in the Trojans;
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/15094_Polymele" title="15094 Polymele">
         15094 Polymele
        </a>
        — 15 September 2027: 21 km diameter P-type Trojan at L
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
        , likely collisional fragment;
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/11351_Leucus" title="11351 Leucus">

        </a>

        <a href="/wiki/List_of_slow_rotators_(minor_planets)" title="List of slow rotators (minor planets)">

        </a>
        Trojan at L
        <sub>

        </sub>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/21900_Orus" title="21900 Orus">

        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic; white-space: nowrap; font-size: 110%">

        </span>
        — 2 March 2033: P-type
        <a href="/wiki/Binary_asteroid" title="Binary asteroid">
         binary
        </a>
        Trojan. The primary, Patroclus, has a mean diameter of 113 km and its companion, Menoetius, has a diameter of 104 km. The pair orbit at a separation of 680 km. The binary resides in the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trojan_camp" title="Trojan camp">

        <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">

         <sub>
          5
         </sub>
        </a>
        .

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">
        Naming
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=617_Patroclus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Naming">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This
       <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">
        minor planet
       </a>
       was named after the Greek hero
       <a href="/wiki/Patroclus" title="Patroclus">
        Patroclus
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       . Friend of
       <a href="/wiki/Achilles" title="Achilles">
        Achilles
       </a>
       , he was killed by
       <a href="/wiki/Hector" title="Hector">
        Hector
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">
        Trojan War
       </a>
       <i>
        (see also
        <a href="/wiki/Meanings_of_minor_planet_names:_1%E2%80%931000#588" title="Meanings of minor planet names: 1–1000">
         (588)
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Meanings_of_minor_planet_names:_1%E2%80%931000#624" title="Meanings of minor planet names: 1–1000">
         (624)
        </a>
        )
       </i>
       . The minor planet's name was proposed by Austrian astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Palisa" title="Johann Palisa">
        Johann Palisa
       </a>
       . The official naming citation was mentioned in
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Names_of_the_Minor_Planets" title="The Names of the Minor Planets">
         The Names of the Minor Planets
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Herget" title="Paul Herget">
        Paul Herget
       </a>
       in 1955 (
       <small>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herget%27s_discovery_circumstances" title="Herget's discovery circumstances">
         H 65
        </a>
       </small>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-springer_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-springer-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Patroclus
       </i>
       is the only object in the Trojan camp to be named after a Greek rather than a Trojan character. The naming conventions for the Jupiter trojans were not adopted until after Patroclus was named (similarly, the asteroid
       <a href="/wiki/624_Hektor" title="624 Hektor">
        Hektor
       </a>
       is the only Trojan character to appear in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_camp" title="Greek camp">
        Greek camp
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
