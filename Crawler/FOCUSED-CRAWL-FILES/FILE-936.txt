      <p>
       <b>
        Ymir
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪər/: 'ear' in 'near'">
            ɪər
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          IM
         </span>
         -eer
        </i>
       </a>
       ), or
       <b>
        Saturn XIX
       </b>
       , is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_and_direct_motion" title="Retrograde and direct motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Irregular_moon" title="Irregular moon">
        irregular moon
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Brett_J._Gladman" title="Brett J. Gladman">
        Brett J. Gladman
       </a>
       , et al. in 2000, and given the temporary designation
       <b>
        S/2000 S 1
       </b>
       . It was named in August 2003, from
       <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">
        Norse mythology
       </a>
       , where
       <a href="/wiki/Ymir" title="Ymir">
        Ymir
       </a>
       is the ancestor of all the
       <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunn" title="Jötunn">
        Jotuns
       </a>
       or frost giants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naming_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-naming-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Of the moons that take more than 3 Earth years to orbit Saturn, Ymir is the largest, at about 18 kilometres (11 miles) in diameter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sheppard_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-sheppard-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It takes 3.6 Earth years to complete an orbit around Saturn. During this time, hypothetical Ymir visitors would experience ~2650 sunsets.
      </p>
