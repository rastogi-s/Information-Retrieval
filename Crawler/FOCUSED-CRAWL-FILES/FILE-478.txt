      <p>
       <b>
        Equine recurrent uveitis
       </b>
       (ERU), also known as
       <b>
        moon blindness
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        recurrent iridocyclitis
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        periodic ophthalmia
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilger_2016_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gilger_2016-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is an acute, nongranulomatous
       <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">
        inflammation
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Uvea" title="Uvea">
        uveal tract
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equine_vision" title="Equine vision">
        eye
       </a>
       , occurring commonly in
       <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">
        horses
       </a>
       of all breeds, worldwide. The causative factor is not known, but several pathogeneses have been suggested. It is the most common cause of blindness in horses. In some breeds, a genetic factor may be involved.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aetiology">
        Aetiology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equine_recurrent_uveitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Aetiology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Several aetiologies are suggested, and any combination of these may be present in any given case.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_deficiency" title="Vitamin deficiency">
         Vitamin deficiency
        </a>
        (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_a" title="Vitamin a">
         A
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_b" title="Vitamin b">
         B
        </a>
        or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_c" title="Vitamin c">
         C
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viral_infection" title="Viral infection">
         Viral infection
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial_infection" title="Bacterial infection">
         Bacterial infection
        </a>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           <a href="/wiki/Leptospira" title="Leptospira">
            Leptospira
           </a>
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           <a href="/wiki/Streptococcus" title="Streptococcus">
            Streptococcus
           </a>
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           <a href="/wiki/Brucella" title="Brucella">
            Brucella
           </a>
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">
         Parasitic infection
        </a>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strongyle_(worm)" title="Strongyle (worm)">
           Strongyle
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Onchocerca" title="Onchocerca">
           <i>
            Onchocerca cervicalis
           </i>
          </a>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autoimmune" title="Autoimmune">
         Autoimmune
        </a>
        disease
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The disease has been suggested to be primarily autoimmune in nature, being a delayed
       <a href="/wiki/Hypersensitivity" title="Hypersensitivity">
        hypersensitivity
       </a>
       reaction to any of the above agents.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_signs">
        Clinical signs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equine_recurrent_uveitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Clinical signs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the acute stage of the disease, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catarrhal" title="Catarrhal">
        catarrhal
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Conjunctivitis" title="Conjunctivitis">
        conjunctivitis
       </a>
       is present, with signs of ocular pain, usually
       <a href="/wiki/Blepharospasm" title="Blepharospasm">
        blepharospasm
       </a>
       , increased
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacrimation" title="Lacrimation">
        lacrimation
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Photophobia" title="Photophobia">
        photophobia
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Miosis" title="Miosis">
        Miosis
       </a>
       is also usually present. After a few days, this will progress to a
       <a href="/wiki/Keratitis" title="Keratitis">
        keratitis
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iridocyclitis" title="Iridocyclitis">
        iridocyclitis
       </a>
       . Other ocular problems may also occur, including conjunctival and
       <a href="/wiki/Cornea" title="Cornea">
        corneal
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oedema" title="Oedema">
        oedema
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueous_flare" title="Aqueous flare">
        aqueous flare
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       After an acute flare-up, no clinical signs of disease may be seen for a prolonged period, which can vary from a few hours to a few years. With frequent acute incidents, though, additional clinical signs may be seen, including anterior and posterior
       <a href="/wiki/Synechia_(eye)" title="Synechia (eye)">
        synechiae
       </a>
       , poor
       <a href="/wiki/Pupil" title="Pupil">
        pupillary
       </a>
       responses,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cataracts" title="Cataracts">
        cataracts
       </a>
       , and a cloudy appearance to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitreous_humour" title="Vitreous humour">
        vitreous humour
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">
        Diagnosis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equine_recurrent_uveitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The cornerstone of diagnosis is an accurate history, and a good clinical examination of the eye, to eliminate traumatic uveitis. Ultrasonography is a useful tool, as it can detect a thickened
       <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">
        iris
       </a>
       , but only in the hands of an expert.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">
        Treatment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equine_recurrent_uveitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treatment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       During an acute flare-up, therapy is targeted at reducing the inflammation present, and dilating the pupil.
       <a href="/wiki/Mydriasis" title="Mydriasis">
        Mydriasis
       </a>
       is important, as pupillary constriction is the primary reason for pain. Anti-inflammatory therapy is usually given both systemically, often in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Flunixin" title="Flunixin">
        flunixin meglumine
       </a>
       , and topically, as
       <a href="/wiki/Prednisolone_acetate" title="Prednisolone acetate">
        prednisolone acetate
       </a>
       . The mydriatic of choice is
       <a href="/wiki/Atropine" title="Atropine">
        atropine
       </a>
       . In the periods between acute attacks, no therapy has been shown to be beneficial.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prognosis">
        Prognosis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equine_recurrent_uveitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prognosis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Horses that suffer from this disease can never be considered cured, although they can be managed by careful use of the therapy described above, and fast detection of new flare-ups. If the disease is not properly treated, it will eventually lead to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">
        blindness
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">
        Epidemiology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equine_recurrent_uveitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       ERU occurs in horses worldwide, but is more common in North America than in Europe, Australia or South Africa.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maggs_2013_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Maggs_2013-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Males and females are equally affected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maggs_2013_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Maggs_2013-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Genetics">
        Genetics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equine_recurrent_uveitis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Genetics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Appaloosa" title="Appaloosa">
        Appaloosa
       </a>
       has a higher risk of developing ERU than other breeds; this predisposition has a genetic basis. Appaloosas which develop ERU are more likely than other breeds to have ERU in both eyes, and more likely to become blind in one or both eyes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilger_2016_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gilger_2016-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="References">
        References
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
