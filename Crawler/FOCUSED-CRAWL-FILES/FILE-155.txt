      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       , an
       <b>
        irregular moon
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        irregular satellite
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        irregular natural satellite
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       following a distant,
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclined
       </a>
       , and often
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentric
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde orbit
       </a>
       . They have been captured by their parent planet, unlike
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regular_satellite" title="Regular satellite">
        regular satellites
       </a>
       , which formed in orbit around them.
      </p>
      <p>
       Counting
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       , 115 irregular satellites have been discovered, orbiting all four of the
       <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">
        giant planets
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       ). The largest of each planet are
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_(moon)" title="Himalia (moon)">
        Himalia
       </a>
       of Jupiter,
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)" title="Phoebe (moon)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       of Saturn,
       <a href="/wiki/Sycorax_(moon)" title="Sycorax (moon)">
        Sycorax
       </a>
       of Uranus, and
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       of Neptune. It is currently thought that the irregular satellites were captured from
       <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">
        heliocentric orbits
       </a>
       near their current locations, shortly after the formation of their parent planet. An alternative theory, that they originated further out in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       , is not supported by current observations.
      </p>

      <p>
       There is no widely accepted precise definition of an irregular satellite. Informally, satellites are considered irregular if they are far enough from the planet that the
       <a href="/wiki/Precession#Precession_of_planetary_orbits" title="Precession">
        precession
       </a>
       of their
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy)" title="Orbital plane (astronomy)">
        orbital plane
       </a>
       is primarily controlled by the Sun.
      </p>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheppard2006_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sheppard2006-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The radius of the Hill sphere is given in the adjacent table.
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Current_distribution">
        Current distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irregular_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Current distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TheIrregulars.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="400" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/TheIrregulars.svg/300px-TheIrregulars.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/TheIrregulars.svg/450px-TheIrregulars.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/TheIrregulars.svg/600px-TheIrregulars.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TheIrregulars.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Irregular satellites of Jupiter (red), Saturn (yellow), Uranus (green) and Neptune (blue) (excluding Triton). The horizontal axis shows their distance from the planet (
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">
          semi-major axis
         </a>
         ) expressed as a fraction of the planet’s
         <a href="/wiki/Hill_sphere" title="Hill sphere">
          Hill sphere
         </a>
         's radius. The vertical axis shows their
         <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
          orbital inclination
         </a>
         . Points or circles represent their relative sizes.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The orbits of the known irregular satellites are extremely diverse, but there are certain patterns.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_and_direct_motion" title="Retrograde and direct motion">
        Retrograde orbits
       </a>
       are far more common (83%) than prograde orbits. No satellites are known with orbital inclinations higher than 55° (or smaller than 130° for retrograde satellites). In addition, some groupings can be identified, in which one large satellite shares a similar orbit with a few smaller ones.
      </p>
      <p>
       Given their distance from the planet, the orbits of the outer satellites are highly perturbed by the Sun and their orbital elements change widely over short intervals. The semi-major axis of
       <a href="/wiki/Pasiphae_(moon)" title="Pasiphae (moon)">
        Pasiphae
       </a>
       , for example, changes as much as 1.5 Gm in two years (single orbit), the inclination around 10°, and the eccentricity as much as 0.4 in 24 years (twice Jupiter’s orbit period).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carruba2000_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carruba2000-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Consequently,
       <i>
        mean
       </i>
       orbital elements (averaged over time) are used to identify the groupings rather than
       <a href="/wiki/Osculating_orbit" title="Osculating orbit">
        osculating elements
       </a>
       at the given date. (Similarly, the
       <a href="/wiki/Proper_orbital_elements" title="Proper orbital elements">
        proper orbital elements
       </a>
       are used to determine the
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_family" title="Asteroid family">
        families of asteroids
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irregular_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)#Capture" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton (moon) § Capture
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Irregular satellites have been captured from heliocentric orbits. (Indeed, it appears that the irregular moons of the giant planets, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trojan_asteroid" title="Trojan asteroid">
        Jovian
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neptune_Trojan" title="Neptune Trojan">
        Neptunian trojans
       </a>
       , and grey
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       objects have a similar origin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nep_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nep-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) For this to occur, at least one of three things needs to have happened:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        energy dissipation (e.g. in interaction with the primordial gas cloud)
       </li>
       <li>
        a substantial (40%) extension of the planet's
        <a href="/wiki/Hill_sphere" title="Hill sphere">
         Hill sphere
        </a>
        in a brief period of time (thousands of years)
       </li>
       <li>
        a transfer of energy in a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three-body_interaction" title="Three-body interaction">
         three-body interaction
        </a>
        . This could involve:
        <ul>
         <li>
          a collision (or close encounter) of an incoming body and a satellite, resulting in the incoming body losing energy and being captured.
         </li>
         <li>
          a close encounter between an incoming binary object and the planet (or possibly an existing moon), resulting in one component of the binary being captured. Such a route has been suggested as most likely for
          <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
           Triton
          </a>
          .
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agnor06_4-0">
           <a href="#cite_note-Agnor06-4">
            [4]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       After the capture, some of the satellites could break up leading to
       <a href="#Families_with_a_common_origin">
        groupings
       </a>
       of smaller moons following similar orbits.
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        Resonances
       </a>
       could further modify the orbits making these groupings less recognizable.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Long-term_stability">
        Long-term stability
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irregular_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Long-term stability">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phoebe_cassini.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1184" data-file-width="813" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Phoebe_cassini.jpg/150px-Phoebe_cassini.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Phoebe_cassini.jpg/225px-Phoebe_cassini.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Phoebe_cassini.jpg/300px-Phoebe_cassini.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phoebe_cassini.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)" title="Phoebe (moon)">
          Phoebe
         </a>
         , Saturn's largest irregular satellite
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The current orbits of the irregular moons are stable, in spite of substantial perturbations near the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apocenter" title="Apocenter">
        apocenter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nesvorny2003_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nesvorny2003-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The cause of this stability in a number of irregulars is the fact that they orbit with a
       <a href="/wiki/Secular_resonance" title="Secular resonance">
        secular
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Kozai_mechanism" title="Kozai mechanism">
        Kozai resonance
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2004_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Burns2004-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition, simulations indicate the following conclusions:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Orbits with inclinations between 50° and 130° are very unstable: their eccentricity increases quickly resulting in the satellite being lost
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carruba2000_2-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Carruba2000-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Retrograde orbits are more stable than prograde (stable retrograde orbits can be found further from the planet)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Increasing eccentricity results in smaller pericenters and large apocenters. The satellites enter the zone of the regular (larger) moons and are lost or ejected via collision and close encounters. Alternatively, the increasing perturbations by the Sun at the growing apocenters push them beyond the Hill sphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       Retrograde satellites can be found further from the planet than prograde ones. Detailed numerical integrations have shown this asymmetry. The limits are a complicated function of the inclination and eccentricity, but in general, prograde orbits with semi-major axes up to 0.47 r
       <sub>
        H
       </sub>
       (Hill sphere radius) can be stable, whereas for retrograde orbits stability can extend out to 0.67 r
       <sub>
        H
       </sub>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The boundary for the semimajor axis is surprisingly sharp for the prograde satellites. A satellite on a prograde, circular orbit (inclination=0°) placed at 0.5 r
       <sub>
        H
       </sub>
       would leave Jupiter in as little as forty years. The effect can be explained by so-called
       <i>
        evection resonance
       </i>
       . The apocenter of the satellite, where the planet’s grip on the moon is at its weakest, gets locked in resonance with the position of the Sun. The effects of the perturbation accumulate at each passage pushing the satellite even further outwards.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nesvorny2003_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nesvorny2003-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The asymmetry between the prograde and retrograde satellites can be explained very intuitively by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_acceleration" title="Coriolis acceleration">
        Coriolis acceleration
       </a>
       in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotating_frame" title="Rotating frame">
        frame rotating
       </a>
       with the planet. For the prograde satellites the acceleration points outward and for the retrograde it points inward, stabilising the satellite.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HamBurns91_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HamBurns91-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        <td>
         <b>
          <a href="/wiki/A-type_asteroid" title="A-type asteroid">
           A
          </a>
         </b>
         <sub>
          13–35%
         </sub>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         high
        </td>
        <td>
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          <a href="/wiki/E-type_asteroid" title="E-type asteroid">
           E
          </a>
         </b>
         <sub>
          25–60%
         </sub>
        </td>
        <td>
        </td>
       </tr>

      <p>
       Each planet's system displays slightly different characteristics. Jupiter's irregulars are grey to slightly red, consistent with
       <a href="/wiki/C-type_asteroid" title="C-type asteroid">
        C
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/P-type_asteroid" title="P-type asteroid">
        P
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/D-type_asteroid" title="D-type asteroid">
        D-type asteroids
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2003_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2003-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some groups of satellites are observed to display similar colours (see later sections). Saturn's irregulars are slightly redder than those of Jupiter.
      </p>
      <p>
       The large Uranian irregular satellites (
       <a href="/wiki/Sycorax_(moon)" title="Sycorax (moon)">
        Sycorax
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Caliban_(moon)" title="Caliban (moon)">
        Caliban
       </a>
       ) are light red, whereas the smaller
       <a href="/wiki/Prospero_(moon)" title="Prospero (moon)">
        Prospero
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Setebos_(moon)" title="Setebos (moon)">
        Setebos
       </a>
       are grey, as are the Neptunian satellites
       <a href="/wiki/Nereid_(moon)" title="Nereid (moon)">
        Nereid
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Halimede_(moon)" title="Halimede (moon)">
        Halimede
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GravHolmanFraser2004_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GravHolmanFraser2004-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spectra">
        Spectra
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irregular_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spectra">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       With the current resolution, the visible and near-infrared spectra of most satellites appear featureless. So far, water ice has been inferred on Phoebe and Nereid and features attributed to aqueous alteration were found on Himalia.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation">
        Rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irregular_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Regular satellites are usually
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       (that is, their orbit is
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous
       </a>
       with their rotation so that they only show one face toward their parent planet). In contrast, tidal forces on the irregular satellites are negligible given their distance from the planet, and rotation periods in the range of only ten hours have been measured for the biggest moons
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_(moon)" title="Himalia (moon)">
        Himalia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)" title="Phoebe (moon)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nereid_(moon)" title="Nereid (moon)">
        Nereid
       </a>
       (to compare with their orbital periods of hundreds of days). Such rotation rates are in the same range that is typical for
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div style="clear:both;">
      </div>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Families_with_a_common_origin">
        Families with a common origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irregular_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Families with a common origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some irregular satellites appear to orbit in 'groups', in which several satellites share similar orbits. The leading theory is that these objects constitute
       <a href="/wiki/Collisional_family" title="Collisional family">
        collisional families
       </a>
       , parts of a larger body that broke up.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamic_groupings">
        Dynamic groupings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irregular_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Dynamic groupings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Simple collision models can be used to estimate the possible dispersion of the orbital parameters given a velocity impulse
       <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">
        Δ
        <i>
         v
        </i>
       </a>
       . Applying these models to the known orbital parameters makes it possible to estimate the Δ
       <i>
        v
       </i>
       necessary to create the observed dispersion. A Δ
       <i>
        v
       </i>
       of tens of meters per seconds (5–50 m/s) could result from a break-up. Dynamical groupings of irregular satellites can be identified using these criteria and the likelihood of the common origin from a break-up evaluated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nesvorny2004_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nesvorny2004-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When the dispersion of the orbits is too wide (i.e. it would require Δ
       <i>
        v
       </i>
       in the order of hundreds of m/s)
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        either more than one collision must be assumed, i.e. the cluster should be further subdivided into groups
       </li>
       <li>
        or significant post-collision changes, for example resulting from resonances, must be postulated.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Colour_groupings">
        Colour groupings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irregular_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Colour groupings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When the colours and spectra of the satellites are known, the homogeneity of these data for all the members of a given grouping is a substantial argument for a common origin. However, lack of precision in the available data often makes it difficult to draw statistically significant conclusions. In addition, the observed colours are not necessarily representative of the bulk composition of the satellite.
      </p>

      <p>
       According to current knowledge, the number of irregular satellites orbiting Uranus and Neptune is smaller than that of Jupiter and Saturn. However, it is thought that this is simply a result of observational difficulties due to the greater distance of Uranus and Neptune. The table at right shows the minimum
       <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">
        radius
       </a>
       (r
       <sub>
        min
       </sub>
       ) of satellites that can be detected with current technology, assuming an
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       of 0.04; thus, there are almost certainly small Uranian and Neptunian moons that cannot yet be seen.
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to the smaller numbers, statistically significant conclusions about the groupings are difficult. A single origin for the retrograde irregulars of Uranus seems unlikely given a dispersion of the orbital parameters that would require high impulse (Δ
       <i>
        v
       </i>
       ≈ 300 km), implying a large diameter of the impactor (395 km), which is incompatible in turn with the size distribution of the fragments. Instead, the existence of two groupings has been speculated:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2003_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2003-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Caliban_(moon)" title="Caliban (moon)">
         Caliban
        </a>
        group
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Sycorax_(moon)" title="Sycorax (moon)">
         Sycorax
        </a>
        group
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These two groups are distinct (with 3σ confidence) in their distance from Uranus and in their eccentricity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardUranus2005_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardUranus2005-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, these groupings are not directly supported by the observed colours: Caliban and Sycorax appear light red, whereas the smaller moons are grey.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GravHolmanFraser2004_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-GravHolmanFraser2004-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For Neptune, a possible common origin of
       <a href="/wiki/Psamathe_(moon)" title="Psamathe (moon)">
        Psamathe
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Neso_(moon)" title="Neso (moon)">
        Neso
       </a>
       has been noted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardJewittKleyna2006_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardJewittKleyna2006-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Given the similar (grey) colours, it was also suggested that
       <a href="/wiki/Halimede_(moon)" title="Halimede (moon)">
        Halimede
       </a>
       could be a fragment of Nereid.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GravHolmanFraser2004_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-GravHolmanFraser2004-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The two satellites have had a very high probability (41%) of collision over the age of the solar system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HolmanKavelaarsGrav2004_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HolmanKavelaarsGrav2004-16">
         [16]

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irregular_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Himalia.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="735" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Himalia.png/220px-Himalia.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Himalia.png/330px-Himalia.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Himalia.png/440px-Himalia.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Himalia.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Distant
         <i>
          Cassini
         </i>
         image of Himalia
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       To date, the only irregular satellites to have been visited by a spacecraft are
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)" title="Phoebe (moon)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       , the largest of Neptune's and Saturn's irregulars respectively. Triton was imaged by
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
        Voyager 2
       </a>
       in 1989 and Phoebe by the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini_probe" title="Cassini probe">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       probe in 2004.
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       also captured a distant, low-resolution image of Jupiter's
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_(moon)" title="Himalia (moon)">
        Himalia
       </a>
       in 2000. There are no spacecraft planned to visit any irregular satellites in the future.
      </p>
