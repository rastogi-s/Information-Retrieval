      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Nicaraguan lunar sample displays
       </b>
       are two
       <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_plaque" title="Commemorative plaque">
        commemorative plaques
       </a>
       consisting of small fragments of moon specimen brought back with the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        Apollo 17
       </a>
       moon missions and given in the 1970s to the people of
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">
        Nicaragua
       </a>
       by United States President
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">
        Richard Nixon
       </a>
       as goodwill gifts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_11">
        Apollo 11
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaragua_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Apollo 11">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11_lunar_sample_display" title="Apollo 11 lunar sample display">
        Apollo 11 lunar sample display
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Nicaragua Apollo 11 lunar sample display commemorative podium style plaque consists of four rice-size "moon rock" particle specimens collected by Apollo 11 astronauts
       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
        Buzz Aldrin
       </a>
       in 1969 and a small
       <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Nicaragua" title="Flag of Nicaragua">
        Nicaraguan flag
       </a>
       that was taken to the moon and back on Apollo 11.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 4 "moon rocks" weigh about 0.05 grams total and are entirely enveloped in a clear plastic ball the size of a coin which is mounted to an approximately one foot square wooden display board on a small
       <a href="/wiki/Podium" title="Podium">
        podium
       </a>
       pedestal. The small sample display plaque also has mounted, directly below the "goodwill moon rocks", a small
       <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Nicaragua" title="Flag of Nicaragua">
        Nicaraguan flag
       </a>
       that went to the moon and back on Apollo 11. The lunar sample display plaque was given to the people of Nicaragua as a gift by President Richard Nixon. Similar lunar sample displays were also given to all the states and territories of the United States and all the countries of the world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_17">
        Apollo 17
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaragua_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apollo 17">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17_lunar_sample_display" title="Apollo 17 lunar sample display">
        Apollo 17 lunar sample display
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_17_Goodwill_msg1.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="231" data-file-width="688" height="74" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Apollo_17_Goodwill_msg1.jpg/220px-Apollo_17_Goodwill_msg1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Apollo_17_Goodwill_msg1.jpg/330px-Apollo_17_Goodwill_msg1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Apollo_17_Goodwill_msg1.jpg/440px-Apollo_17_Goodwill_msg1.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_17_Goodwill_msg1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <center>
          Message on Apollo 17 plaque
         </center>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Nicaragua Apollo 17 lunar sample display commemorative style plaque, 10 by 14 inches in size, consists of one "moon rock" particle specimen cut from
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_basalt_70017" title="Lunar basalt 70017">
        lunar basalt 70017
       </a>
       and a Nicaraguan flag. The
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       70017 was collected by Apollo 17 astronaut
       <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">
        Harrison Schmitt
       </a>
       on the moon in 1972. Once brought to earth from the moon, lunar basalt 70017 was cut into small fragments of approximately 1 gram. The specimen was enveloped in a plastic ball and mounted on the wooden plaque along with the Nicaraguan flag which had been taken to the moon and back by the crew of Apollo 17. In 1973, President Nixon gave the plaque to the country of Nicaragua. He also gave similar Apollo 17 lunar sample plaques to the same recipients that had previously received the Apollo 11 gift plaques, the 134 countries of the world and all the states and provinces of the United States. The gifts were a goodwill gesture to promote peace and harmony.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaragua_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Nicaragua Apollo 11 lunar plaque display with its moon rocks were originally presented as a gift to the people of Nicaragua by way of then-Nicaraguan dictator
       <a href="/wiki/Anastasio_Somoza_Debayle" title="Anastasio Somoza Debayle">
        Anastasio Somoza Debayle
       </a>
       by President Nixon in 1970. The plaque display with the Apollo 11 "moon rocks" was then stolen by a Costa Rican mercenary soldier who opposed the
       <a href="/wiki/Sandinista_National_Liberation_Front" title="Sandinista National Liberation Front">
        Sandinista
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Junta_of_National_Reconstruction" title="Junta of National Reconstruction">
        Junta of National Reconstruction
       </a>
       government in Nicaragua. The plaque was then traded to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist">
        Baptist
       </a>
       missionary Harry Coates, who sold it to
       <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Valley" title="Las Vegas Valley">
        Las Vegas
       </a>
       casino magnate
       <a href="/wiki/Bob_Stupak" title="Bob Stupak">
        Bob Stupak
       </a>
       by 1987 for $10,000 and 200,000 shares in Stupak's casino. Stupak displayed the plaque at his Moon Rock Café before he decided to hide it away in a
       <a href="/wiki/Safe_deposit_box" title="Safe deposit box">
        safe deposit box
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mogul_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-mogul-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Attorney Richard Wright, designated attorney for Stupak's estate, sent the Nicaragua Apollo 11 "goodwill moon rocks" plaque display to
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       officials in Houston in May 2012, two and a half years after Stupak's death.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mogul_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-mogul-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Renee Juhans, NASA inspector general executive officer, told reporters in May 2012 that NASA was "taking steps to authenticate" the display it received from attorney Wright. If found to be the authentic Nicaragua Apollo 11 "goodwill moon rocks" commemorative podium plaque display, it would be returned to the government of Nicaragua by NASA.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mogul_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-mogul-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Apollo 11 Moon Rock was returned to the people of Nicaragua in November 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
