     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        co-orbital configuration
       </b>
       is a configuration of two or more
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">
        astronomical objects
       </a>
       (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moons
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       ) orbiting at the same, or very similar, distance from their primary, i.e. they are in a
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        1:1 mean-motion resonance
       </a>
       . (or 1:−1 if orbiting in
       <a href="/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion" title="Retrograde and prograde motion">
        opposite directions
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are several classes of co-orbital objects, depending on their point of
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       . The most common and best-known class is the
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_(astronomy)" title="Trojan (astronomy)">
        trojan
       </a>
       , which librates around one of the two stable
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrangian points
       </a>
       (Trojan points), L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       and L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       , 60° ahead of and behind the larger body respectively. Another class is the
       <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit" title="Horseshoe orbit">
        horseshoe orbit
       </a>
       , in which objects librate around 180° from the larger body. Objects librating around 0° are called
       <a href="/wiki/Quasi-satellite" title="Quasi-satellite">
        quasi-satellites
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coorbplanet_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-coorbplanet-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An
       <b>
        exchange orbit
       </b>
       occurs when two co-orbital objects are of similar masses and thus exert a non-negligible influence on each other. The objects can exchange
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">
        semi-major axes
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricities
       </a>
       when they approach each other.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trojans">
        Trojans
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trojans">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lagrange_very_massive.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="500" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Lagrange_very_massive.svg/250px-Lagrange_very_massive.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Lagrange_very_massive.svg/375px-Lagrange_very_massive.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Lagrange_very_massive.svg/500px-Lagrange_very_massive.svg.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lagrange_very_massive.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Trojan points are the points labelled
         <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
          L
          <sub>
           4
          </sub>
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
          L
          <sub>
           5
          </sub>
         </a>
         , highlighted in red, on the orbital path of the secondary object (blue), around the primary object (yellow).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_(astronomy)" title="Trojan (astronomy)">
        Trojan (astronomy)

      <p>
       Trojan objects orbit 60° ahead of (L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       ) or behind (L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       ) a more massive object, both in orbit around an even more massive central object. The best known example are the asteroids that orbit ahead of or behind
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       around the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       . Trojan objects do not orbit exactly at one of either
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrangian points
       </a>
       , but do remain relatively close to it, appearing to slowly orbit it. In technical terms, they librate around
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

             <mo>
              ,
             </mo>
             <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">

               <!-- Δ -->
              </mi>
             </mrow>
             <mi>
              ϖ
              <!-- ϖ -->

       </span>
       = (±60°, ±60°). The point around which they librate is the same, irrespective of their mass or orbital eccentricity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coorbplanet_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-coorbplanet-2">

      </p>

       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are several thousand known trojan minor planets orbiting the Sun. Most of these orbit near Jupiter's Lagrangian points, the traditional
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_trojan" title="Jupiter trojan">
        Jupiter trojans
       </a>
       . As of 2015
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       there are also 13
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune_trojan" title="Neptune trojan">
        Neptune trojans
       </a>
       , 7
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_trojan" title="Mars trojan">
        Mars trojans
       </a>
       , 1
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus_trojan" title="Uranus trojan">
        Uranus trojan
       </a>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2011_QF99" title="2011 QF99">
         2011 QF
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           99
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       ) and 1
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_trojan" title="Earth trojan">
        Earth trojan
       </a>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2010_TK7" title="2010 TK7">
         2010 TK
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           7
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       ) known to exist.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trojan_moons">
        Trojan moons
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trojan moons">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Saturnian system contains two sets of trojan moons. Both
       <a href="/wiki/Tethys_(moon)" title="Tethys (moon)">
        Tethys
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dione_(moon)" title="Dione (moon)">
        Dione
       </a>
       have two trojan moons,
       <a href="/wiki/Telesto_(moon)" title="Telesto (moon)">
        Telesto
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Calypso_(moon)" title="Calypso (moon)">
        Calypso
       </a>
       in Tethys's L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       and L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       respectively, and
       <a href="/wiki/Helene_(moon)" title="Helene (moon)">
        Helene
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Polydeuces_(moon)" title="Polydeuces (moon)">
        Polydeuces
       </a>
       in Dione's L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       and L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       respectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       Polydeuces is noticeable for its wide
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       : it wanders as far as ±30° from its Lagrangian point and ±2% from its mean orbital radius, along a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tadpole_orbit" title="Tadpole orbit">
        tadpole orbit
       </a>
       in 790 days (288 times its orbital period around Saturn, the same as Dione's).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trojan_planets">
        Trojan planets
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Trojan planets">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A pair of co-orbital
       <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">
        exoplanets
       </a>
       was proposed to be orbiting the star
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler-223" title="Kepler-223">
        Kepler-223
       </a>
       , but this was later retracted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The possibility of a trojan planet to
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler-91b" title="Kepler-91b">
        Kepler-91b
       </a>
       was studied but the conclusion was that the transit-signal was a false-positive.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One possibility for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">
        habitable zone
       </a>
       is a
       <b>
        trojan planet
       </b>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">
        giant planet
       </a>
       close to its
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Formation_of_the_Earth.E2.80.93Moon_system">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_the_Earth–Moon_system">
        Formation of the Earth–Moon system
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Formation of the Earth–Moon system">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       According to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        giant impact hypothesis
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       formed after a collision between two co-orbital objects—Theia, thought to have had about 10% of the mass of Earth (about as massive as
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       ), and the proto-Earth—whose orbits were perturbed by other planets, bringing Theia out of its trojan position and causing the collision.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Horseshoe_orbits">
        Horseshoe orbits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Horseshoe orbits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="697" data-file-width="1029" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png/300px-Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png/450px-Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png/600px-Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame" title="Rotating reference frame">
          Rotating-frame
         </a>
         depiction of the
         <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit" title="Horseshoe orbit">
          horseshoe
         </a>
         exchange orbits of Janus and Epimetheus
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit" title="Horseshoe orbit">
        Horseshoe orbit
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Objects in a horseshoe orbit librate around 180° from the primary. Their orbits encompass both equilateral Lagrangian points, i.e. L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       and L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coorbplanet_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-coorbplanet-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Co-orbital_moons">
        Co-orbital moons
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Co-orbital moons">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Epimetheus_(moon)#Orbit" title="Epimetheus (moon)">
        Epimetheus (moon) § Orbit
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturnian
       </a>
       moons
       <a href="/wiki/Janus_(moon)" title="Janus (moon)">
        Janus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Epimetheus_(moon)" title="Epimetheus (moon)">
        Epimetheus
       </a>
       share their orbits, the difference in semi-major axes being less than either's mean diameter. This means the moon with the smaller semi-major axis will slowly catch up with the other. As it does this, the moons gravitationally tug at each other, increasing the semi-major axis of the moon that has caught up and decreasing that of the other. This reverses their relative positions proportionally to their masses and causes this process to begin anew with the moons' roles reversed. In other words, they effectively swap orbits, ultimately oscillating both about their mass-weighted mean orbit.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_co-orbital_asteroids">
        Earth co-orbital asteroids
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Earth co-orbital asteroids">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A small number of asteroids have been found which are co-orbital with Earth. The first of these to be discovered, asteroid
       <a href="/wiki/3753_Cruithne" title="3753 Cruithne">
        3753 Cruithne
       </a>
       , orbits the Sun with a period slightly less than one Earth year, resulting in an orbit that (from the point of view of Earth) appears as a bean-shaped orbit centered on a position ahead of the position of Earth. This orbit slowly moves further ahead of Earth's orbital position. When Cruithne's orbit moves to a position where it trails Earth's position, rather than leading it, the gravitational effect of Earth increases the orbital period, and hence the orbit then begins to lag, returning to the original location. The full cycle from leading to trailing Earth takes 770 years, leading to a horseshoe-shaped movement with respect to Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       More resonant
       <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">
        near-Earth objects
       </a>
       (NEOs) have since been discovered. These include
       <a href="/wiki/54509_YORP" title="54509 YORP">
        54509 YORP
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/(85770)_1998_UP1" title="(85770) 1998 UP1">
         (85770) 1998 UP
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           1
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2002_AA29" title="2002 AA29">
         2002 AA
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           29
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010_SO16" title="2010 SO16">
         2010 SO
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           16
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2009_BD" title="2009 BD">
         2009 BD
        </a>
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2015_SO2" title="2015 SO2">
         2015 SO
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           2
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       which exist in resonant orbits similar to Cruithne's.
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2010_TK7" title="2010 TK7">
         2010 TK
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           7
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       is the first and so far only identified
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_trojan" title="Earth trojan">
        Earth trojan
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Quasi-satellite">
        Quasi-satellite
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Quasi-satellite">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Quasi-satellite" title="Quasi-satellite">
        Quasi-satellite
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Quasi-satellites are co-orbital objects that librate around 0° from the primary. Low-eccentricity quasi-satellite orbits are highly unstable, but for moderate to high eccentricities such orbits can be stable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coorbplanet_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-coorbplanet-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From a co-rotating perspective the quasi-satellite appears to orbit the primary like a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde satellite
       </a>
       , although at distances so large that it is not gravitationally bound to it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coorbplanet_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-coorbplanet-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Two examples of
       <a href="/wiki/Quasi-satellite" title="Quasi-satellite">
        quasi-satellites
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       are
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2014_OL339" title="2014 OL339">
         2014 OL
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           339
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dynamics_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dynamics-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/(469219)_2016_HO3" title="(469219) 2016 HO3">
         (469219) 2016 HO
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           3
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20160615_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20160615-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2016HO3_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-2016HO3-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exchange_orbits">
        Exchange orbits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
