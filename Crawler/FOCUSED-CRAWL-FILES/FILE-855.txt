      <p>
       <b>
        50000 Quaoar
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="'w' in 'wind'">
            w
           </span>
           <span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">
            ɑː
           </span>
           <span title="'w' in 'wind'">
            w
           </span>
           <span title="/ɑːr/: 'ar' in 'far'">
            ɑːr
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dict-def_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-dict-def-6">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "Kwawar"), provisional designation
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic; white-space: nowrap; font-size: 110%">
        2002 LM
        <sub>
         60
        </sub>
       </span>
       , is a non-resonant
       <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">
        trans-Neptunian object
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper belt object">
        cubewano
       </a>
       ) and possibly a
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       , located in the outermost region of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       . It measures approximately 1110 kilometers in diameter or half the size of
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       . The object was discovered by American astronomers
       <a href="/wiki/Chad_Trujillo" title="Chad Trujillo">
        Chad Trujillo
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">
        Michael Brown
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Palomar_Observatory" title="Palomar Observatory">
        Palomar Observatory
       </a>
       on 6 June 2002.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-object_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPC-object-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Signs of water ice have been found, which suggests that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryovolcanism" title="Cryovolcanism">
        cryovolcanism
       </a>
       may be occurring. A small amount of
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       is present on its surface, which can only be retained by the largest Kuiper belt objects.
      </p>
      <p>
       In February 2007, Weywot, a 80-kilometer sized synchronous
       <a href="/wiki/Minor-planet_moon" title="Minor-planet moon">
        minor-planet moon
       </a>
       in orbit of Quaoar was discovered by Michael Brown. Both objects were named after mythological figures from the Native American
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tongva_people" title="Tongva people">
        Tongva people
       </a>
       in Southern California.
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaoar_(mythology)" title="Quaoar (mythology)">
         Quaoar
        </a>
       </i>
       is the Tongva creator deity and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weywot_(mythology)" title="Weywot (mythology)">
         Weywot
        </a>
       </i>
       is his son.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-springer_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-springer-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=50000_Quaoar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Quaoar was officially discovered on June 6, 2002, by astronomers
       <a href="/wiki/Chad_Trujillo" title="Chad Trujillo">
        Chad Trujillo
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">
        Michael Brown
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">
        California Institute of Technology
       </a>
       , from images acquired at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_Oschin_Telescope" title="Samuel Oschin Telescope">
        Samuel Oschin Telescope
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Palomar_Observatory" title="Palomar Observatory">
        Palomar Observatory
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-object_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPC-object-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The actual discovering image was taken on June 4, 2002, 05:41:40
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">
        UT
       </a>
       and analyzed on June 5, 2002, 10:48:08
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Daylight_Time" title="Pacific Daylight Time">
        PDT
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ-Quaoar_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FAQ-Quaoar-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The discovery of this
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        magnitude
       </a>
       18.5 object, at the time located in the
       <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">
        constellation
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ophiuchus" title="Ophiuchus">
        Ophiuchus
       </a>
       , was announced on October 7, 2002, at a meeting of the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Astronomical_Society" title="American Astronomical Society">
        American Astronomical Society
       </a>
       . The earliest
       <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">
        prediscovery image
       </a>
       proved to be a May 25, 1954, plate from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palomar_observatory_sky_survey" title="Palomar observatory sky survey">
        Palomar observatory sky survey
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-jpldata-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=50000_Quaoar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Quaoar is named for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaoar_(mythology)" title="Quaoar (mythology)">
        Tongva creator god
       </a>
       , following
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       (IAU)
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nomenclature" title="Planetary nomenclature">
        naming conventions
       </a>
       for non-resonant
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt_object" title="Kuiper belt object">
        Kuiper belt objects
       </a>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tongva_people" title="Tongva people">
        Tongva
       </a>
       are the native people of the area around
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">
        Los Angeles
       </a>
       , where the discovery of Quaoar was made. Brown et al. had picked the name with the more intuitive spelling
       <i>
        Kwawar
       </i>
       , but the preferred spelling among the Tongva was
       <i>
        Qua-o-ar
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Prior to IAU approval of the name, Quaoar went by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provisional_designation" title="Provisional designation">
        provisional designation
       </a>
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         2002 LM
         <sub>
          60
         </sub>
        </span>
       </b>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_planet_number" title="Minor planet number">
        minor planet number
       </a>
       50000 was not coincidence, but chosen to commemorate a particularly large object found in the search for a Pluto-sized object in the Kuiper belt, parallel to the similarly numbered
       <a href="/wiki/20000_Varuna" title="20000 Varuna">
        20000 Varuna
       </a>
       . However, subsequent even-larger discoveries such as 136199 Eris were simply numbered according to the order in which their orbits were confirmed.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Size">
        Size
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=50000_Quaoar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Size">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 2004, Quaoar was estimated to have a diameter of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7006126000000000000♠
        </span>
        1260
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        190 km
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2004_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2004-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       subsequently revised downward, which at the time of discovery in 2002 made it the largest object found in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       since the discovery of
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       . Quaoar was later supplanted by
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/90377_Sedna" title="90377 Sedna">
        Sedna
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Makemake" title="Makemake">
        Makemake
       </a>
       , though Sedna was later found to be somewhat smaller than Quaoar. Quaoar is about as massive as (if somewhat smaller than) Pluto's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       , which is approximately ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        2
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </span>
       times as massive as
       <a href="/wiki/90482_Orcus" title="90482 Orcus">
        Orcus
       </a>
       . Quaoar is roughly one twelfth the diameter of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , one third the diameter of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , and half the size of Pluto.
      </p>

          <a href="#cite_note-occ2-9">
           [8]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Quaoar was the first
       <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">
        trans-Neptunian object
       </a>
       to be measured
       <i>
        directly
       </i>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       (HST) images, using a new, sophisticated method (see
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.gps.caltech.edu/~mbrown" rel="nofollow">
        Brown’s pages
       </a>
       for a non-technical description and his paper
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2004_17-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2004-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       for details). Given its distance Quaoar is on the limit of the HST resolution (40
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">
        milliarcseconds
       </a>
       ) and its image is consequently "smeared" on a few adjacent pixels. By comparing carefully this image with the images of stars in the background and using a sophisticated model of HST optics (
       <a href="/wiki/Point_spread_function" title="Point spread function">
        point spread function
       </a>
       (PSF)), Brown and Trujillo were able to find the best-fit disk size that would give a similar blurred image. This method was recently applied by the same authors to measure the size of Eris.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quaoar_hubble.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="140" data-file-width="200" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Quaoar_hubble.jpg/180px-Quaoar_hubble.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/Quaoar_hubble.jpg 1.5x" width="180"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quaoar_hubble.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Hubble photo used to measure size of Quaoar
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The uncorrected 2004 HST estimates only marginally agree with the 2007
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       measurements by the
       <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">
        Spitzer Space Telescope
       </a>
       (SST) that suggest a higher
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       (0.19) and consequently a smaller diameter (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005844400000000000♠
        </span>
        844.4
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;">
         <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">
          +206.7
          <br/>
          −189.6
         </span>
        </span>
        km
       </span>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StansberryGrundy_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-StansberryGrundy-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During the 2004 HST observations, little was known about the surface properties of Kuiper belt objects, but we now know that the surface of Quaoar is in many ways similar to those of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">
        icy satellites of Uranus
       </a>
       and Neptune.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Adopting a Uranian-satellite
       <a href="/wiki/Limb_darkening" title="Limb darkening">
        limb darkening
       </a>
       profile suggests that the 2004 HST size estimate for Quaoar was approximately 40% too large, and that a more proper estimate would be about 900 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_12-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Using a weighted average of the SST and corrected HST estimates, Quaoar, as of 2010, can be estimated at about
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005890000000000000♠
        </span>
        890
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        70 km
       </span>
       in diameter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_12-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 4 May 2011 Quaoar occulted a 16th-magnitude star, which gave 1170 km as the longest chord and suggested an elongated shape.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-occ_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-occ-19">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       New measurement from
       <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory" title="Herschel Space Observatory">
        Herschel Space Observatory
       </a>
       with revised data from SST suggested that Quaoar has a diameter of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7006107000000000000♠
        </span>
        1070
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        38 km
       </span>
       and its satellite, Weywot, of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7004810000000000000♠
        </span>
        81
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        11 km
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_10-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=50000_Quaoar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Because Quaoar is a
       <a href="/wiki/Minor-planet_moon" title="Minor-planet moon">
        binary
       </a>
       object, the mass of the system can be calculated from the orbit of the secondary. Quaoar's estimated density of around 2.2 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       and estimated size of 1,100 km suggests that it is a
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       . Mike Brown estimates that rocky bodies around 900 km in diameter relax into hydrostatic equilibrium, and that icy bodies relax into hydrostatic equilibrium somewhere between 200 and 400 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown400km_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown400km-20">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       With an estimated mass greater than 1.6
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         21
        </span>
       </sup>
       kg, Quaoar has the mass and diameter "usually" required for being in hydrostatic equilibrium according to the
       <a href="/wiki/IAU_definition_of_planet#Draft_proposal" title="IAU definition of planet">
        2006 IAU draft definition of a planet
       </a>
       (5
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         20
        </span>
       </sup>
       kg, 800 km),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU0601_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAU0601-21">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Brown states that Quaoar "must be" a
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown-dplist_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown-dplist-22">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Light-curve-amplitude analysis shows only small deviations, suggesting that Quaoar is indeed a spheroid with small albedo spots and hence a dwarf planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSDwarfs_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSDwarfs-23">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hit-and-run_collision">
        Hit-and-run collision
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=50000_Quaoar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hit-and-run collision">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Planetary scientist
       <a href="/wiki/Erik_Ian_Asphaug" title="Erik Ian Asphaug">
        Erik Asphaug
       </a>
       has suggested that Quaoar may have collided with a much larger body, stripping the lower-density mantle from Quaoar, and leaving behind the denser core. He envisions that Quaoar was originally covered by a mantle of ice that made it 300 to 500 kilometers bigger than it is today, and that it collided with another Kuiper-belt body about twice its size—an object roughly the diameter of Pluto (or even approaching the size of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scientamer_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-scientamer-24">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       possibly Pluto itself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on-the-fringe_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-on-the-fringe-25">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This model was made assuming Quaoar actually had a density of 4.2 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       , but more-recent estimates have given it a more Pluto-like density of only 2 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       , with no more need for the collision theory.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=50000_Quaoar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Quaoar orbits at about 43.3 astronomical units (6.48
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        9
       </sup>
       km; 4.02
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        9
       </sup>
       mi) from the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       with an orbital period of 284.5 years. Its orbit is nearly circular and
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        moderately inclined
       </a>
       at approximately 8°, typical for the population of small
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper-belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper-belt object">
        classical Kuiper-belt objects
       </a>
       (KBO) but exceptional among the large KBOs.
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Makemake" title="Makemake">
        Makemake
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/90482_Orcus" title="90482 Orcus">
        Orcus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/20000_Varuna" title="20000 Varuna">
        Varuna
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/120347_Salacia" title="120347 Salacia">
        Salacia
       </a>
       are all on highly inclined, more
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentric
       </a>
       orbits.
      </p>
      <p>
       Quaoar is the largest body that is classified as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubewano" title="Cubewano">
        cubewano
       </a>
       by both the
       <a href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center" title="Minor Planet Center">
        Minor Planet Center
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K08O05_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-K08O05-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Deep_Ecliptic_Survey" title="Deep Ecliptic Survey">
        Deep Ecliptic Survey
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buie-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The polar view compares the near-circular orbit of Quaoar to the highly eccentric (e=0.25) orbit of
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       (Quaoar’s orbit in blue, Pluto’s in red,
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       in grey). The circles illustrate the positions in April 2006, relative sizes, and colours. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">
        perihelia
       </a>
       (q),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">
        aphelia
       </a>
       (Q) and the dates of passage are also marked.
      </p>
      <p>
       At 43 AU and a near-circular orbit, Quaoar is not significantly perturbed by Neptune,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buie-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       unlike Pluto, which is in 2:3
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       with Neptune. The ecliptic view illustrates the relative inclinations of the orbits of Quaoar and Pluto. Note that Pluto's aphelion is beyond (and below) Quaoar's orbit, so that Pluto is closer to the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       than Quaoar at some times of its orbit, and farther at others.
      </p>
      <p>
       As of 2008, Quaoar was only 14 AU
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-27">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       from Pluto, which made it the closest large body to the Pluto–Charon system. By Kuiper belt standards this is very close.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics

      <p>
       Quaoar's
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       could be as low as 0.1, which would still be much higher than the lower estimate of 0.04 for
       <a href="/wiki/20000_Varuna" title="20000 Varuna">
        Varuna
       </a>
       . This may indicate that fresh ice has disappeared from Quaoar's surface. The surface is moderately red, meaning that Quaoar is relatively more reflective in the red and near-infrared than in the blue.
       <a href="/wiki/20000_Varuna" title="20000 Varuna">
        20000 Varuna
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/28978_Ixion" title="28978 Ixion">
        28978 Ixion
       </a>
       are also moderately red in the spectral class. Larger KBOs are often much brighter because they are covered in more fresh ice and have a higher albedo, and thus they present a neutral colour (see
       <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object#Physical_characteristics" title="Trans-Neptunian object">
        colour comparison
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       A 2006 model of internal heating via
       <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">
        radioactive decay
       </a>
       suggested that, unlike Orcus, Quaoar may not be capable of sustaining an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_ocean" title="Internal ocean">
        internal ocean
       </a>
       of liquid water at the mantle–core boundary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussman2006_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussman2006-28">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cryovolcanism">
        Cryovolcanism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=50000_Quaoar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cryovolcanism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2004, scientists were surprised to find signs of
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">
        crystalline
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        ice
       </a>
       on Quaoar, indicating that the temperature rose to at least −160 °C (110 K or −260 °F) sometime in the last ten million years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewitt2004_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jewitt2004-29">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Speculation began as to what could have caused Quaoar to heat up from its natural temperature of −220 °C (55 K or −360 °F). Some have theorized that a barrage of mini-
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">
        meteors
       </a>
       may have raised the temperature, but the most discussed theory speculates that
       <a href="/wiki/Cryovolcano" title="Cryovolcano">
        cryovolcanism
       </a>
       may be occurring, spurred by the decay of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">
        radioactive
       </a>
       elements within Quaoar's core.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JewittPage_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-JewittPage-30">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since then (2006), crystalline water ice was also found on
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       , but present in larger quantities and thought to be responsible for the very high albedo of that object (0.7).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trujillo_2006_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Trujillo_2006-31">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       More precise (2007) observations of Quaoar's near
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_spectrum" title="Infrared spectrum">
        infrared spectrum
       </a>
       indicate the presence of small (5%) quantity of (solid)
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">
        ethane
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quaoar-ShallerBrown2007_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Quaoar-ShallerBrown2007-32">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Given its
       <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">
        boiling point
       </a>
       (112 K), methane is a volatile ice at average Quaoar surface temperatures, unlike water ice or ethane (boiling point 185 K). Both models and observations suggest that only a few larger bodies (
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Makemake" title="Makemake">
        Makemake
       </a>
       ) can retain the volatile ices whereas the dominant population of small
       <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">
        TNOs
       </a>
       lost them. Quaoar, with only small amounts of methane, appears to be in an intermediary category.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quaoar-ShallerBrown2007_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Quaoar-ShallerBrown2007-32">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2019, when the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       mission visits the small Kuiper-belt object
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/(486958)_2014_MU69" title="(486958) 2014 MU69">
         (486958) 2014 MU
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           69
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       after having visited Pluto in 2015, knowledge of the surfaces of KBOs should improve.
      </p>

       Quaoar has one known moon, Weywot (full designation
       <i>
        (50000) Quaoar I Weywot
       </i>
       ). Its discovery by
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">
        Michael E. Brown
       </a>
       was reported in IAUC 8812 on 22 February 2007, based on imagery taken on 14 February 2006.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8812_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC8812-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston50000_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-johnston50000-34">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The satellite was found at 0.35
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">
        arcsec
       </a>
       from Quaoar with an
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent magnitude
       </a>
       difference of 5.6.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EKO51_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EKO51-35">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Two possible orbits have been determined from the observations: the first is a
       <a href="/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion" title="Retrograde and prograde motion">
        prograde orbit
       </a>
       with an inclination of 14°, the second a
       <a href="/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion" title="Retrograde and prograde motion">
        retrograde orbit
       </a>
       with an inclination of 30° (150°); the other parameters are very similar between the two orbits. It orbits at a distance of 14,500 km from the primary and has an eccentricity of about 0.14, it completes one orbit in about 12.5 days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_12-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From the surface of Quaoar and at a point where Weywot is at
       <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">
        zenith
       </a>
       it would have an
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        angular diameter
       </a>
       of 15.9
       <a href="/wiki/Minute_and_second_of_arc" title="Minute and second of arc">
        arcminutes
       </a>
       at apoapsis and 21.4 arcminutes at periapsis, in comparison the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       's size varies between 29.4 and 33.5 arcminutes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonsize_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonsize-36">
         [c]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its apparent magnitude at full
       <a href="/wiki/Phase_angle_(astronomy)" title="Phase angle (astronomy)">
        phase
       </a>
       would be about -3, comparable to
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       at maximum brightness.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonshine_33-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonshine-33">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Assuming an equal
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo#Astronomical_albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       and density to the primary, the apparent magnitude suggests that the moon has a diameter of about 74 km (​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         12
        </sub>
       </span>
       of Quaoar).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_12-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Weywot is estimated to only have ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         2000
        </sub>
       </span>
       the mass of Quaoar.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_12-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name_2">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=50000_Quaoar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Upon discovery, Weywot was issued a provisional designation,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <b>
         S/2006 (50000) 1
        </b>
       </span>
       . Brown left the choice of a name up to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tongva_people" title="Tongva people">
        Tongva
       </a>
       (whose creator god Quaoar had been named after), who chose the sky god
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weywot_(mythology)" title="Weywot (mythology)">
        Weywot
       </a>
       , son of Quaoar.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The name was made official in MPC #67220 published on October 4, 2009.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=50000_Quaoar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quaoar_animation_dark_crsub_circle.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2250" data-file-width="2250" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Quaoar_animation_dark_crsub_circle.gif/220px-Quaoar_animation_dark_crsub_circle.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Quaoar_animation_dark_crsub_circle.gif/330px-Quaoar_animation_dark_crsub_circle.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Quaoar_animation_dark_crsub_circle.gif/440px-Quaoar_animation_dark_crsub_circle.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quaoar_animation_dark_crsub_circle.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
          New Horizons
         </a>
         image sequence of Quaoar at a distance of about 14
         <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
          AUs
         </a>
         , with 12.8 magnitude galaxy
         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=IC_1048&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="IC 1048 (page does not exist)">
          IC 1048
         </a>
         and dimmer
         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=UGC_9485&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="UGC 9485 (page does not exist)">
          UGC 9485
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       It was calculated that a flyby mission to Quaoar could take 13.57 years using a Jupiter gravity assist, based on launch dates of 25 December 2016, 22 November 2027, 22 December 2028, 22 January 2030 or 20 December 2040. Quaoar would be 41 to 43 AU from the Sun when the spacecraft arrives.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGranaghan_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-McGranaghan-39">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In July 2016
       <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
        New Horizons
       </a>
       spacecraft took a sequence of four images of Quaoar from a distance of about 14 AU.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Horizons_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-New_Horizons-40">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
