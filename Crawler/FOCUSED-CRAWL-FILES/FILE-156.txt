   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Naming of moons
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        naming of
        <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
         moons
        </a>
       </b>
       has been the responsibility of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       's committee for Planetary System Nomenclature since 1973. That committee is known today as the
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nomenclature" title="Planetary nomenclature">
        Working Group for Planetary System Nomenclature
       </a>
       (WGPSN).
      </p>
      <p>
       Prior to its formation, the names of satellites have had varying histories. The choice of names is often determined by a satellite's discoverer; however, historically some satellites were not given names for many years after their discovery; for instance,
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">
        Titan
       </a>
       was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">
        Huygens
       </a>
       in 1655, but was not named until 1847, almost two centuries later.
      </p>
      <p>
       Before the IAU assumed responsibility for astronomical nomenclature, only twenty-five satellites had been given names that were in wide use and are still used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since then, names have been given to 129 additional satellites: 46 satellites of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       , 43 of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       , 22 of
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       , 11 of
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       , 5 of
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       , 1 of
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       , and 2 of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haumea_(dwarf_planet)" title="Haumea (dwarf planet)">
        Haumea
       </a>
       . The number will continue to rise as current satellite discoveries are documented and new satellites are discovered.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the IAU General Assembly in July 2004,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iau.org_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-iau.org-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the WGPSN suggested it may become advisable to not name small satellites, as
       <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
        CCD
       </a>
       technology makes it possible to discover satellites as small as 1 km in diameter. Until 2015, however, names had been applied to all moons discovered, regardless of size. Since then nine small Jovian satellites (Jupiter LI, LII, and LIV through LX) have received permanent Roman-numeral designations but not names.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_of_moons_by_solar_system_object">
        Naming of moons by solar system object
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_of_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Naming of moons by solar system object">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Earth">
        Earth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_of_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Earth">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Every human language has its own word for the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , and these words are the ones normally used in astronomical contexts. However, a number of fanciful or mythological names for the Moon have been used in the context of astronomy (an even larger number of lunar epithets have been used in non-astronomical contexts). In the 17th century, the Moon was sometimes referred to as
       <i>
        Proserpina
       </i>
       . More recently, especially in science-fictional contexts, the Moon has been called by the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       name
       <i>
        Luna
       </i>
       , presumably on the analogy of the Latin names of the planets, or by association with the adjectival form
       <i>
        lunar
       </i>
       . In technical terminology, the word-stems
       <i>
        seleno-
       </i>
       (from
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       <i>
        selēnē
       </i>
       "moon") and
       <i>
        cynthi-
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        Cynthia
       </i>
       , an epithet of the goddess
       <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">
        Artemis
       </a>
       ) are sometimes used to refer to the Moon, as in
       <i>
        selenography, selenology,
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        pericynthion.
       </i>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mars">
        Mars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_of_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Mars" title="Moons of Mars">
        Moons of Mars
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The moons of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       ) were named by
       <a href="/wiki/Asaph_Hall" title="Asaph Hall">
        Asaph Hall
       </a>
       in 1878, soon after he discovered them. They are named after the sons of the god
       <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">
        Ares
       </a>
       (the Greek equivalent of the Roman god
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">
        Mars
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_of_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Jupiter">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">
        Moons of Jupiter
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Galilean moons of Jupiter (
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">
        Callisto
       </a>
       ) were named by
       <a href="/wiki/Simon_Marius" title="Simon Marius">
        Simon Marius
       </a>
       soon after their discovery in 1610. However, by the late 19th century these names had fallen out of favor, and for a long time it was most common to refer to them in the astronomical literature simply as "Jupiter I", "Jupiter II", etc., or as "the first satellite of Jupiter", "Jupiter's second satellite", etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       By the first decade of the 20th century, the names Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto had once again recovered popularity, but the later-discovered moons, numbered, usually in Roman numerals V (5) through XII (12), remained unnamed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nicholson-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By a popular though unofficial convention, Jupiter V, discovered in 1892, was given the name
       <i>
        Amalthea
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnard_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barnard-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       first used by the French astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Camille_Flammarion" title="Camille Flammarion">
        Camille Flammarion
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellites
       </a>
       (discovered 1904 to 1951) were, in the overwhelming majority of astronomical literature, simply left nameless. No names were proposed until
       <a href="/wiki/Brian_G._Marsden" title="Brian G. Marsden">
        Brian G. Marsden
       </a>
       suggested a nomenclature for these satellites in 1955.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marsden_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marsden-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although the 1955 names met with immediate acceptance in some quarters (e.g. in science fiction
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Starr_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Starr-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and popular science articles
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rollcall_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rollcall-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), they were still rarely if ever met in astronomical literature until the 1970s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaposchkin_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gaposchkin-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Two other proposals for naming the satellites were made between 1955 and 1975, both by Soviet astronomers, E. I. Nesterovich (in 1962) and Yu. A. Karpenko (in 1973).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Owen_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Owen-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karpenko_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Karpenko-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These met no particularly enthusiastic reception.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1975, following
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Kowal" title="Charles Kowal">
        Charles Kowal
       </a>
       's discovery of the satellite Jupiter XIII in 1974 the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       Task Group for Outer Solar System Nomenclature granted names to satellites V-XIII, and provided for a formal naming process for future satellites to be discovered. Under the new process, Jupiter V continued as
       <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(moon)" title="Amalthea (moon)">
        Amalthea
       </a>
       , Jupiter XIII was named
       <a href="/wiki/Leda_(moon)" title="Leda (moon)">
        Leda
       </a>
       in accordance with a suggestion of Kowal's, and all previous proposals for the seven satellites VI-XII were abandoned in favor of new names, in accordance with a scheme suggested by the German philologist
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%BCrgen_Blunck&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jürgen Blunck (page does not exist)">
        Jürgen Blunck
       </a>
       where
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_motion" title="Direct motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       moons received names ending in 'a' and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       moons received names ending in 'e'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The new names met considerable protest from some quarters. Kowal, despite suggesting a name for Jupiter XIII, was of the opinion that Jupiter's irregular satellites should not be named at all.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kowal_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kowal-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">
        Carl Sagan
       </a>
       noted that the names chosen were extraordinarily obscure (a fact that
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tobias_Owen,_Sr.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tobias Owen, Sr. (page does not exist)">
        Tobias Owen
       </a>
       , chair of the Task Group, admitted was intentional in a response to Sagan
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Owen_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Owen-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) and suggested his own names in 1976;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sagan-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       these preserved some of the names from the 1955 proposal. Karpenko had noted the same in his 1981 book "The Names of the Starry Sky", along with stating that the names chosen for retrograde moons, and therefore the "e" ending, were not always the ones for which it was the more common one.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        Jupiter

       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       ). Blunck's scheme for the outer moons was retained, with the addition that names ending in 'o' could also be used for prograde moons. At the IAU General Assembly in July 2004,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iau.org_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-iau.org-2">

        </a>
       </sup>

       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Euporie_(moon)" title="Euporie (moon)">
        Euporie
       </a>
       ) on were named for daughters of Zeus, until Jupiter LIII (
       <a href="/wiki/Dia_(moon)" title="Dia (moon)">
        Dia
       </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_of_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Saturn">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Saturn" title="Moons of Saturn">
        Moons of Saturn
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1847 the seven then known moons of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       were named by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">
        John Herschel
       </a>
       . Herschel named Saturn's two innermost moons (Mimas and Enceladus) after the mythological Greek
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigantes" title="Gigantes">
        Giants
       </a>
       , and the outer five after the
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">
        Titans
       </a>
       (Titan, Iapetus) and Titanesses (Tethys, Dione, Rhea) of the same mythology. Until then,
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">
        Titan
       </a>
       was known as the "Huygenian (or Huyghenian) satellite of Saturn" and the other moons had Roman numeral designations in order of their distance from Saturn. Subsequent discoverers of Saturnian moons followed Herschel's scheme:
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(moon)" title="Hyperion (moon)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
       was discovered soon after in 1848, and the ninth moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)" title="Phoebe (moon)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       , was named by its discoverer in 1899 soon after its discovery; they were named for a Titan and a Titaness respectively. The name of
       <a href="/wiki/Janus_(moon)" title="Janus (moon)">
        Janus
       </a>
       was suggested by its discoverer,
       <a href="/wiki/Audouin_Dollfus" title="Audouin Dollfus">
        Audouin Dollfus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Current IAU practice for newly discovered inner moons is to continue with Herschel's system, naming them after Titans or their descendants. However, the increasing number of moons that were being discovered in the 21st century caused the IAU to draw up a new scheme for the outer moons. At the IAU General Assembly in July 2004,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iau.org_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-iau.org-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the WGPSN allowed satellites of Saturn to have names of giants and monsters in mythologies other than the Greco-Roman. Since the outer moons fall naturally into three groups, one group is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">
        Norse
       </a>
       giants, one after
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">
        Gallic
       </a>
       giants, and one after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inuit_mythology" title="Inuit mythology">
        Inuit
       </a>
       giants. The only moon that fails to fit this scheme is the Greek-named Phoebe, which is in the Norse group.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_of_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uranus">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">
        Moons of Uranus
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Roman numbering scheme of
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       ' moons was in a state of flux for a considerable time. Sir William Herschel thought he had discovered up to six moons and maybe even a ring. For nearly fifty years, Herschel's instrument was the only one the moons had been seen with.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the 1840s, better instruments and a more favourable position of Uranus in the sky led to sporadic indications of satellites additional to Titania and Oberon. Publications hesitated between William Herschel's designations (where Titania and Oberon are Uranus II and IV) and William Lassell's (where they are sometimes I and II).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       With the confirmation of Ariel and Umbriel, Lassell numbered the moons I through IV from Uranus outward, and this finally stuck.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first two Uranian moons, discovered in 1787, did not receive names until 1852, a year after two more moons had been discovered. The responsibility for naming was taken by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">
        John Herschel
       </a>
       , son of the discoverer of Uranus. Herschel, instead of assigning names from
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       , named the moons after magical spirits in
       <a href="/wiki/English_literature" title="English literature">
        English literature
       </a>
       : the fairies
       <a href="/wiki/Oberon" title="Oberon">
        Oberon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Titania" title="Titania">
        Titania
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        William Shakespeare
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night's Dream">
         A Midsummer Night's Dream
        </a>
       </i>
       , and the sylphs Ariel and Umbriel from
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">
        Alexander Pope
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Rape_of_the_Lock" title="The Rape of the Lock">
         The Rape of the Lock
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(The_Tempest)" title="Ariel (The Tempest)">
        Ariel
       </a>
       is also a sprite in Shakespeare's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Tempest" title="The Tempest">
         The Tempest
        </a>
       </i>
       ). The reasoning was presumably that Uranus, as god of the sky and air, would be attended by spirits of the air.
      </p>
      <p>
       Subsequent names, rather than continuing the "airy spirits" theme (only
       <a href="/wiki/Puck_(moon)" title="Puck (moon)">
        Puck
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mab_(moon)" title="Mab (moon)">
        Mab
       </a>
       continuing the trend), have focused on Herschel's source material. In 1949, the fifth moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Miranda_(moon)" title="Miranda (moon)">
        Miranda
       </a>
       , was named by its discoverer,
       <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Kuiper" title="Gerard Kuiper">
        Gerard Kuiper
       </a>
       , after a thoroughly mortal character in Shakespeare's
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Tempest_(play)" title="The Tempest (play)">
         The Tempest
        </a>
       </i>
       . Current IAU practice is to name moons after characters from Shakespeare's plays and
       <i>
        The Rape of the Lock
       </i>
       (although at present only Ariel, Umbriel, and Belinda have names drawn from the latter poem, all the rest being from Shakespeare). At first, the outermost moons were all named after characters from one play,
       <i>
        The Tempest
       </i>
       ; but with
       <a href="/wiki/Margaret_(moon)" title="Margaret (moon)">
        Margaret
       </a>
       being named from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Much_Ado_About_Nothing" title="Much Ado About Nothing">
         Much Ado About Nothing
        </a>
       </i>
       that trend has ended.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_of_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Neptune">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Neptune" title="Moons of Neptune">
        Moons of Neptune
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The one known moon (at the time) of
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       was not named for many decades. Although the name
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       was suggested in 1880 by
       <a href="/wiki/Camille_Flammarion" title="Camille Flammarion">
        Camille Flammarion
       </a>
       , it did not come into general use until the mid 20th-century, and for many years was considered "unofficial". In the astronomical literature it was simply referred to as "the satellite of Neptune". Later, the second known moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Nereid_(moon)" title="Nereid (moon)">
        Nereid
       </a>
       , was named by its discoverer in 1949,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerard_P._Kuiper" title="Gerard P. Kuiper">
        Gerard P. Kuiper
       </a>
       , soon after its discovery.
      </p>
      <p>
       Current IAU practice for newly discovered Neptunian moons is to accord with these first two choices by naming them after Greek sea deities.
      </p>

      <p>
       The name of

       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">

      </p>
      <p>
       The other four moons are named
       <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(moon)" title="Hydra (moon)">
        Hydra
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nix_(moon)" title="Nix (moon)">
        Nix
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kerberos_(moon)" title="Kerberos (moon)">
        Kerberos
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Styx_(moon)" title="Styx (moon)">
        Styx
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Charon, Hydra, Nix, and Kerberos are all characters in Greek mythology, with ties to Hades (the Greek equivalent of Pluto).
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(mythology)" title="Charon (mythology)">
        Charon
       </a>
       ferries the dead across the River Acheron,
       <a href="/wiki/Lernaean_Hydra" title="Lernaean Hydra">
        Hydra
       </a>
       guards the waters of the underworld, and Nix (a respelling of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyx_(mythology)" title="Nyx (mythology)">
        Nyx
       </a>
       ), mother of Charon, is the goddess of darkness and the night.
       <a href="/wiki/Cerberus" title="Cerberus">
        Kerberos
       </a>
       is a giant three-headed dog who guards the entrance to the underworld. The fifth moon is named for the river
       <a href="/wiki/Styx" title="Styx">
        Styx
       </a>
       that forms the boundary between the worlds of the living and the dead.
      </p>

        Eris
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Dysnomia_(moon)" title="Dysnomia (moon)">
        Dysnomia (moon)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The name of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/136199_Eris" title="136199 Eris">
        Eris's
       </a>
       moon
       <a href="/wiki/Dysnomia_(moon)" title="Dysnomia (moon)">
        Dysnomia
       </a>
       was suggested by its discoverer
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">
        Michael E. Brown
       </a>
       , who also suggested the name of the dwarf planet. The name has two meanings: in mythology
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dysnomia_(mythology)" title="Dysnomia (mythology)">
        Dysnomia
       </a>
       (lawlessness) is the daughter of
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(mythology)" title="Eris (mythology)">
        Eris
       </a>
       (chaos). However, the name is also an intentional reference to the actor
       <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Lawless" title="Lucy Lawless">
        Lucy Lawless
       </a>
       who plays the character
       <a href="/wiki/Xena:_Warrior_Princess" title="Xena: Warrior Princess">
        Xena
       </a>
       . The background for this is that during the long period when Eris had no formal name, the name 'Xena' – originally Brown's nickname for his discovery – spread and became popular. When the name 'Eris' was chosen, Brown suggested Dysnomia (which until then had been referred to as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gabrielle_(Xena)" title="Gabrielle (Xena)">
        Gabrielle
       </a>
       ) as a reference to this.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hence, Dysnomia is the only moon which could be said to be named after an actor. The names Eris and Dysnomia were accepted by the IAU on 14 September 2006.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_numeral_designations">
        <span id="Roman_numeral_designation">
        </span>
        Roman numeral designations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_of_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Roman numeral designations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Roman numbering system for satellites arose with the very first discovery of natural satellites other than Earth's Moon:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">
        Galileo
       </a>
       referred to the
       <a href="/wiki/Galilean_moons" title="Galilean moons">
        Galilean moons
       </a>
       as
       <b>
        I
       </b>
       through
       <b>
        IV
       </b>
       (counting from Jupiter outward), refusing to adopt the names proposed by his rival
       <a href="/wiki/Simon_Marius" title="Simon Marius">
        Simon Marius
       </a>
       . Similar numbering schemes naturally arose with the discovery of multiple moons around Saturn, Uranus, and Mars. The numbers initially designated the moons in orbital sequence, and were re-numbered after each new discovery; for instance, before the discovery of Mimas and Enceladus in 1789, Tethys was Saturn I, Dione Saturn II, etc.,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but after the new moons were discovered, Mimas became Saturn I, Enceladus Saturn II, Tethys Saturn III and Dione Saturn IV.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the middle of the 19th century, however, the numeration became fixed, and later discoveries failed to conform with the orbital sequence scheme.
       <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(moon)" title="Amalthea (moon)">
        Amalthea
       </a>
       , discovered in 1892, was labelled "Jupiter V" although it orbits more closely to Jupiter than does
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       (Jupiter I). The unstated convention then became, at the close of the 19th century, that the numbers more or less reflected the order of discovery, except for prior historical exceptions (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_discovery_of_Solar_System_planets_and_their_natural_satellites" title="Timeline of discovery of Solar System planets and their natural satellites">
        Timeline of discovery of Solar System planets and their natural satellites
       </a>
       ); though if a large number of satellites were discovered in a short span of time, the group could be numbered in orbital sequence, or according to other principles than strictly by order of discovery. The convention has been extended to natural satellites of minor planets, such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/(87)_Sylvia_I_Romulus" title="(87) Sylvia I Romulus">
        (87) Sylvia I Romulus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Roman numerals are usually not assigned to satellites until they are named, so many satellites that have been discovered but only have provisional designations do not usually have Roman numerals assigned to them. (An exception is Saturn's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Helene_(moon)" title="Helene (moon)">
        Helene
       </a>
       , which received the Roman numeral XII in 1982, but was not named until 1988.) Since the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       began assigning names to all satellites in 1975, the use of Roman numeral designations has diminished, and some are very rarely used;
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       are rarely referred to as Mars I and Mars II, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       is never referred to as "Earth I". However, some of the more recently discovered outer moons of Jupiter have not been named even after their orbital elements were known well enough to receive Roman numerals, and as such the only possible nomenclature for them is their Roman numeral designations, such as "Jupiter LIX".
      </p>
      <p>
       The thirteen named satellites of Saturn from
       <a href="/wiki/Aegir_(moon)" title="Aegir (moon)">
        Aegir
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Surtur_(moon)" title="Surtur (moon)">
        Surtur
       </a>
       were named in
       <a href="/wiki/Alphabetical_order" title="Alphabetical order">
        alphabetical order
       </a>
       corresponding to their Roman numerals.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Provisional_designations">
        Provisional designations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_of_moons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Provisional designations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When satellites are first discovered, they are given provisional designations such as "
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_LII" title="Jupiter LII">
        S/2010 J 2
       </a>
       " (the 2nd new satellite of Jupiter discovered in 2010) or "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S/2003_S_1" title="S/2003 S 1">
        S/2003 S 1
       </a>
       " (the 1st new satellite of Saturn discovered in 2003). The initial "S/" stands for "satellite", and distinguishes from such prefixes as "D/", "C/", and "P/", used for
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comets
       </a>
       . The designation "R/" is used for planetary rings. These designations are sometimes written like "S/2003 S1", dropping the second space. The letter following the category and year identifies the planet (
       <b>
        J
       </b>
       upiter,
       <b>
        S
       </b>
       aturn,
       <b>
        U
       </b>
       ranus,
       <b>
        N
       </b>
       eptune; although no occurrence of the other planets is expected, Mars and Mercury are disambiguated through the use of
       <b>
        H
       </b>
       ermes for the latter).
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       was designated by
       <b>
        P
       </b>
       prior to its
       <a href="/wiki/IAU_definition_of_planet" title="IAU definition of planet">
        recategorization
       </a>
       as a
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       . When the object is found around a minor planet, the identifier used is the latter's number in parentheses. Thus,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dactyl_(asteroid)" title="Dactyl (asteroid)">
        Dactyl
       </a>
       , the moon of
       <a href="/wiki/243_Ida" title="243 Ida">
        243 Ida
       </a>
       , was at first designated "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S/1993_(243)_1" title="S/1993 (243) 1">
        S/1993 (243) 1
       </a>
       ". Once confirmed and named, it became
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/(243)_Ida_I_Dactyl" title="(243) Ida I Dactyl">
         (243) Ida I Dactyl
        </a>
       </b>
       . Similarly, the fourth satellite of Pluto,
       <a href="/wiki/Kerberos_(moon)" title="Kerberos (moon)">
        Kerberos
       </a>
       , discovered after Pluto was categorized as a dwarf planet and assigned a minor planet number, was designated
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S/2011_(134340)_1" title="S/2011 (134340) 1">
        S/2011 (134340) 1
       </a>
       rather than S/2011 P 1,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       though the New Horizon team, who disagreed with the dwarf planet classification, used the latter.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         H
        </b>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
         Mercury
        </a>
        (Hermes)
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         V
        </b>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
         Venus
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         E
        </b>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
         Earth
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         M
        </b>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
         Mars
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         J
        </b>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
         Jupiter
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         S
        </b>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
         Saturn
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         U
        </b>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
         Uranus
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         N
        </b>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
         Neptune
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <small>
        Note: The assignation of "H" for Mercury is specified by the
        <a class="external text" href="http://planetarynames.wr.usgs.gov/specifics.html" rel="nofollow">
         USGS Gazetteer of Planetary Nomenclature
        </a>
        ; since they usually follow IAU guidelines closely, this is very likely the IAU convention, but confirmation is needed.
       </small>
      </p>
      <p>
       After a few months or years, when a newly discovered satellite's existence has been confirmed and its orbit computed, a permanent name is chosen, which replaces the "S/" provisional designation. However, in the past, some satellites remained unnamed for surprisingly long periods after their discovery.
      </p>
