      <p>
       <b>
        Paaliaq
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">
            ɑː
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
           <span title="/./: syllable break">
            .
           </span>
           <span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">
            ɑː
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          PAH
         </span>
         -lee-ahk
        </i>
       </a>
       ) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Brett_J._Gladman" title="Brett J. Gladman">
        Brett J. Gladman
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/John_J._Kavelaars" title="John J. Kavelaars">
        John J. Kavelaars
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean-Marc_Petit" title="Jean-Marc Petit">
        Jean-Marc Petit
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hans_Scholl_(astronomer)" title="Hans Scholl (astronomer)">
        Hans Scholl
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Matthew_J._Holman" title="Matthew J. Holman">
        Matthew J. Holman
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Brian_G._Marsden" title="Brian G. Marsden">
        Brian G. Marsden
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_D._Nicholson" title="Philip D. Nicholson">
        Philip D. Nicholson
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_A._Burns" title="Joseph A. Burns">
        Joseph A. Burns
       </a>
       in early October 2000,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and given the temporary designation
       <b>
        S/2000 S 2
       </b>
       . It was named in August 2003 after a fictional shaman in the book
       <i>
        The Curse of the Shaman,
       </i>
       written by Michael Kusugak, who supplied Kavelaars with the names of giants from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inuit_mythology" title="Inuit mythology">
        Inuit mythology
       </a>
       that were used for other Saturnian moons.
      </p>
      <p>
       Paaliaq is thought to be about 22 kilometres in diameter, and orbits Saturn at an average distance of 15.2 million km in 687 days. It is a member of the
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn%27s_Inuit_group_of_satellites" title="Saturn's Inuit group of satellites">
        Inuit group
       </a>
       of irregular satellites. It also has a proximity with 9 other moons reaching up to ten miles from each.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is light red in color, and in the
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       the Paaliapian (Paaliaqan) spectrum is very similar to the Inuit-group satellites
       <a href="/wiki/Kiviuq_(moon)" title="Kiviuq (moon)">
        Kiviuq
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siarnaq_(moon)" title="Siarnaq (moon)">
        Siarnaq
       </a>
       , supporting the thesis of a possible common origin of the Inuit group in the break-up of a larger body.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2006_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2006-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gladman2001_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gladman2001-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
