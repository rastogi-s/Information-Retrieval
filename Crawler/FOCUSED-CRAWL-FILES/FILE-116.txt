      <p>
       The
       <b>
        splitting of the moon
       </b>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       :
       <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        انشقاق القمر
       </span>
       ‎) was a claimed miracle attributed to the Islamic prophet
       <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">
        Muhammad
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is derived from the
       <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">
        Quranic
       </a>
       verses
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D54%3Averse%3D1-2" rel="nofollow">
        54:1-2
       </a>
       , and mentioned by
       <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">
        Muslim
       </a>
       traditions such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Asbab_al-nuzul" title="Asbab al-nuzul">
        Asbab al-nuzul
       </a>
       (context of revelation). Most Muslim commentators interpret the event as a literal split in the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        moon
       </a>
       , while some others identify it as an event that will happen at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yawm_al-Qiyamah" title="Yawm al-Qiyamah">
        judgment day
       </a>
       or an optical illusion. The splitting of the moon was never reported in any other country.
      </p>
      <p>
       Early traditions supporting a literal interpretation are transmitted on the authority of companions of Muhammad such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Abbas" title="Ibn Abbas">
        Ibn Abbas
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anas_bin_Malik" title="Anas bin Malik">
        Anas bin Malik
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abdullah_bin_Masud" title="Abdullah bin Masud">
        Abdullah bin Masud
       </a>
       and others.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kathir_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kathir-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to the Indian Muslim scholar
       <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_Yusuf_Ali" title="Abdullah Yusuf Ali">
        Abdullah Yusuf Ali
       </a>
       , the moon will split again when the day of judgment approaches. He says that the verses may also have an
       <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">
        allegorical
       </a>
       meaning, i.e. the matter has become clear as the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mourison_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mourison-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Qur'anic verses
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D54%3Averse%3D1-2" rel="nofollow">
        54:1-2
       </a>
       was part of the debate between medieval Muslim theologians and Muslim philosophers over the issue of the inviolability of heavenly bodies. In 2010 a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_Lunar_Science_Institute" title="NASA Lunar Science Institute">
        NASA Lunar Science Institute
       </a>
       (NLSI) staff scientist named Brad Bailey said, "No current scientific evidence reports that the Moon was split into two (or more) parts and then reassembled at any point in the past."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The narrative was used by some later Muslims to convince others of the prophethood of Muhammad.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annemarie_Schimmel_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Annemarie_Schimmel-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has also inspired many Muslim poets, especially in India.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoI-Muhammad_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EoI-Muhammad-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>

      <p>
       Verses
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D54%3Averse%3D1-2" rel="nofollow">
        54:1-2
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">
        Quran
       </a>
       reads:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p style="direction: rtl; unicode-bidi: embed;">
        <span style="font-size:125%;line-height:150%">
         <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
          اقْتَرَبَتِ السَّاعَةُ وَانشَقَّ الْقَمَرُ وَإِن يَرَوْا آيَةً يُعْرِضُوا وَيَقُولُوا سِحْرٌ مُّسْتَمِرٌّ
         </span>
        </span>
       </p>
       <i>
        The Hour (of Judgment) is nigh, and the moon is cleft asunder. But if they see a Sign, they turn away, and say, "This is (but) transient magic.
       </i>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Early traditions and stories explain this verse as a miracle performed by Muhammad, following requests of some members of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quraysh_(tribe)" title="Quraysh (tribe)">
        Quraysh
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allameh_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allameh-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Most Muslim commentators accept the authenticity of those traditions
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="Do they accept the miraculous split as factual (and a citation is needed), or that there are genuinely early traditions and stories? (May 2014)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . The following verse 54:2, "But if they see a Sign, they turn away, and say, 'This is (but) transient magic'" is taken in the support of this view.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allameh_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allameh-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The classical commentator
       <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Kathir" title="Ibn Kathir">
        Ibn Kathir
       </a>
       provides a list of the early traditions mentioning the incident: A tradition transmitted on the authority of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anas_bin_Malik" title="Anas bin Malik">
        Anas bin Malik
       </a>
       states that Muhammad split the moon after the pagan Meccans asked for a miracle. Another tradition from Malik transmitted through other chains of narrations, mentions that the
       <a href="/wiki/Jabal_al-Nour" title="Jabal al-Nour">
        mount Nur
       </a>
       was visible between the two parts of the moon (Mount Nur is located in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hijaz" title="Hijaz">
        Hijaz
       </a>
       . Muslims believe that Muhammad received his first revelations from God in a cave on this mountain,
       <a href="/wiki/Hira" title="Hira">
        Cave Hira'
       </a>
       ). A tradition narrated on the authority of
       <a href="/wiki/Jubayr_ibn_Mut%27im" title="Jubayr ibn Mut'im">
        Jubayr ibn Mut'im
       </a>
       with a single chain of transmission says that the two parts of the moon stood on two mountains. This tradition further states that the Meccan responded by saying "Muhammad has taken us by his magic... If he was able to take us by magic, he will not be able to do so with all people." Traditions transmitted on the authority of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Abbas" title="Ibn Abbas">
        Ibn Abbas
       </a>
       briefly mention the incident and do not provide much details.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kathir_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-kathir-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Traditions transmitted on the authority of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abdullah_bin_Masud" title="Abdullah bin Masud">
        Abdullah bin Masud
       </a>
       describe the incident as follows:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kathir_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-kathir-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        We were along with God's Messenger at Mina, that moon was split up into two. One of its parts was behind the mountain and the other one was on this side of the mountain. God's Messenger said to us: Bear witness to this
        <a class="external text" href="http://cmje.usc.edu/religious-texts/hadith/muslim/039-smt.php#039.6725" rel="nofollow">
         039:6725
        </a>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The narrative was used by some later Muslims to convince others of the prophethood of Muhammad.
       <a href="/wiki/Annemarie_Schimmel" title="Annemarie Schimmel">
        Annemarie Schimmel
       </a>
       for example quotes the following from Muslim scholar
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qadi_Iyad" title="Qadi Iyad">
        Qadi Iyad
       </a>
       who worked in the 12th century:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annemarie_Schimmel_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Annemarie_Schimmel-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        It has not been said of any people on the earth that the moon was observed that night such that it could be stated that it was
        <i>
         not
        </i>
        split. Even if this had been reported from many different places, so that one would have to exclude the possibility that all agreed upon a lie, yet, we would not accept this as proof to the contrary, for the moon is not seen in the same way by different people... An eclipse is visible in one country but not in the other one; in one place it is total, in the other one only partial.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_perspectives">
        Other perspectives
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Zamakhshari" title="Al-Zamakhshari">
        Al-Zamakhshari
       </a>
       , a famous commentator of the Qur'an, acknowledged the splitting of the moon as one of Muhammad's miracles. But he also suggested that the splitting might take place only on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_view_of_the_Last_Judgment" title="Islamic view of the Last Judgment">
        day of judgment
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mourison_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mourison-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Muslim scholar
       <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_Yusuf_Ali" title="Abdullah Yusuf Ali">
        Yusuf Ali
       </a>
       provides three different interpretations of the verse. He holds that perhaps all three are applicable to the verse: Moon once appeared cleft asunder at the time of Muhammad in order to convince the unbelievers. It will split again when the day of judgment approaches (here the prophetic past tense is taken to indicate the future). Yusuf Ali connects this incident with the disruption of the solar system mentioned in
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D75%3Averse%3D8-9" rel="nofollow">
        75:8-9
       </a>
       . Lastly, he says that the verses can be metaphorical, meaning that the matter has become clear as the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yusuf_Ali_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Yusuf_Ali-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some dissenting commentators who do not accept the miracle narration believe that the verse only refers to the splitting of the moon at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_view_of_the_Last_Judgment" title="Islamic view of the Last Judgment">
        day of judgment
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allameh_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allameh-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Likewise,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M._A._S._Abdel_Haleem" title="M. A. S. Abdel Haleem">
        M. A. S. Abdel Haleem
       </a>
       writes:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        The Arabic uses the past tense, as if that Day were already here, to help the reader/listener imagine how it will be. Some traditional commentators hold the view that this describes an actual event at the time of the Prophet, but it clearly refers to the end of the world.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Some Muslim scholars postulate and believe that a (now known) Astronomical event must have happened at that time, which made it appear to the observers as if the moon had been split in two, because the phenomenon was seen at least in India as well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One of the possible lunar events could be a large asteroid hitting the moon, and the plume and debris from the strike blocking enough lunar view to make it appear as if the Moon had split in two. A second possibility could be a celestial body passing between Earth and the Moon and blocking some part of lunar surface for short time. Moreover, in the light of the verse that was revealed at that time, the word "Saa'at" also means spiritual revolution, so the event also symbolized the end of the Pagan Arab culture and religions which used Moon as their symbol or worshipped moon gods.
      </p>
      <p>
       Western historians such as A.J. Wensinck and Denis Gril, reject the historicity of the miracle arguing that the Qur'an itself denies miracles, in their traditional sense, in connection with Muhammad.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoI-Muhammad_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-EoI-Muhammad-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoQ_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EoQ-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Debate_over_the_inviolability_of_heavenly_bodies">
        Debate over the inviolability of heavenly bodies
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Quran
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D54%3Averse%3D1-2" rel="nofollow">
        54:1-2
       </a>
       was part of the debate between medieval Muslim theologians and Muslims philosophers over the issue of the inviolability of heavenly bodies. The philosophers held that nature was composed of four fundamental elements: earth, air, fire, and water. These philosophers however held that the composition of heavenly bodies were different. This belief was based on the observation that the motion of heavenly bodies, unlike that of terrestrial bodies, was circular and without any beginnings or ends. This appearance of eternity in the heavenly bodies, led the philosophers to conclude that the heavens were inviolable. Theologians on the other hand proposed their own conception of the terrestrial matter: the nature was composed of uniform atoms that were re-created at every instant by God (the latter idea was added to defend God's omnipotence against the encroachment of the independent secondary causes). According to this conception, the heavenly bodies were essentially the same as the terrestrial bodies, and thus could be pierced.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mourison_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mourison-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In order to deal with implication of the traditional understanding of the Quranic verse
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A2002.02.0006%3Asura%3D54%3Averse%3D1-2" rel="nofollow">
        54:1-2
       </a>
       , some philosophers argued that the verse should be interpreted metaphorically (e.g. the verse could have referred to a partial lunar eclipse in which then earth obscured part of the moon).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mourison_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mourison-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This tradition has inspired many Muslim poets, especially in India.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoI-Muhammad_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-EoI-Muhammad-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In poetical language Muhammad is sometimes equated with the sun or the morning light. As such, part of a poem from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sana%27i" title="Sana'i">
        Sana'i
       </a>
       , a renowned early twelfth century
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">
        Persian
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sufi" title="Sufi">
        Sufi
       </a>
       poet, reads: "the sun should split the moon in two".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annemarie_Schimmel_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Annemarie_Schimmel-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Rumi" title="Rumi">
        Jalal ad-Din Rumi
       </a>
       , a renowned Persian-writing poet and mystic, in one of his poems conveys the idea that to be split by the Muhammad's finger is the greatest bliss the lowly moon can hope for and a devoted believer splits the moon with Muhammad's finger.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annemarie_Schimmel_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Annemarie_Schimmel-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Elaborating on this idea,
       <a href="/wiki/Jami" title="Jami">
        Abd ar-Rahman Jami
       </a>
       , one of the classical poets and mystics of Persia, plays with the shapes and numerical values of
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic_alphabet" title="Arabic alphabet">
        Arabic letters
       </a>
       in a complicated way: The full moon, Jami says, resembles the Arabic letter for a circular m with the numerical value 40. When Muhammad split the moon, it became like two crescent-shaped n's (the Arabic letter for "n") whose numerical value is 50 each. This would mean that, thanks for the miracle, the value of moon had increased.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annemarie_Schimmel_6-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Annemarie_Schimmel-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In another place
       <a href="/wiki/Rumi" title="Rumi">
        Rumi
       </a>
       , according to Schimmel, alludes to two miracles attributed to Muhammad in tradition, i.e. the splitting of the moon (which shows the futility of man's scientific approach to nature), and the other that Muhammad was illiterate.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annemarie_Schimmel_6-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Annemarie_Schimmel-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
