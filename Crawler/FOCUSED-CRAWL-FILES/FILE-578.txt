      <p>
       <b>
        Lunar Polar Hydrogen Mapper
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        LunaH-Map
       </b>
       , is one of 13
       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">
        CubeSats
       </a>
       planned to be launched with
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Mission_1" title="Exploration Mission 1">
        Exploration Mission 1
       </a>
       in 2019. Along with
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_IceCube" title="Lunar IceCube">
        Lunar IceCube
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Flashlight" title="Lunar Flashlight">
        Lunar Flashlight
       </a>
       , LunaH-Map will help investigate the possible presence of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_water" title="Lunar water">
        water-ice on the Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harbaugh_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harbaugh-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Arizona_State_University" title="Arizona State University">
        Arizona State University
       </a>
       began development of LunaH-Map after being awarded a contract by
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       in early 2015. The development team consists of about 20 professionals and students led by Craig Hardgrove, the Principal Investigator.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Objective">
        Objective
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Polar_Hydrogen_Mapper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Objective">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       LunaH-Map's primary objective is to map the abundance of hydrogen down to one meter beneath the surface of the lunar south pole. It will be inserted into a
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_orbit" title="Polar orbit">
        polar orbit
       </a>
       around the Moon, with its periapsis located near the lunar south pole, initially passing above
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shackleton_Crater" title="Shackleton Crater">
        Shackleton Crater
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harbaugh_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harbaugh-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       LunaH-Map will provide a high resolution map of the abundance and distribution of hydrogen rich compounds, like water, in this region of the Moon and expand on the less accurate maps made by previous missions. This information may then be used to improve scientific understanding of how water is created and spread throughout the Solar System or used by future manned missions for life support and fuel production.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreier_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dreier-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       LunaH-Map, along with other long distance CubeSat missions like
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Cube_One" title="Mars Cube One">
        Mars Cube One
       </a>
       , will demonstrate vital technologies for including CubeSats in other interplanetary missions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Polar_Hydrogen_Mapper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       LunaH-Map was conceived in a discussion between Craig Hardgrove and future LunaH-Map chief engineer, Igor Lazbin, about issues with the spatial resolution of various
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron_detection" title="Neutron detection">
        neutron detectors
       </a>
       in use around Mars. Instruments like
       <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_Albedo_of_Neutrons" title="Dynamic Albedo of Neutrons">
        Dynamic Albedo of Neutrons
       </a>
       on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curiosity_rover" title="Curiosity rover">
        Curiosity rover
       </a>
       can only make measurements of about 3 m (9.8 ft) in radius from between the rear wheels of the rover, while on orbit neutron detectors, like the High Energy Neutron Detector on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_Odyssey" title="Mars Odyssey">
        Mars Odyssey
       </a>
       probe, can only provide large, inaccurate maps over hundreds of square kilometers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreier_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dreier-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Similar issues are present in current maps of hydrogen distributions on the Moon, so Hardgrove designed LunaH-Map to orbit closer to the lunar south pole than previous crafts to improve the resolution of these maps.
      </p>
      <p>
       By April 2015, Hardgrove had assembled a team composed of members of various government, academic and private institutions and drafted a proposal to NASA. In early 2015 LunaH-Map was one of two CubeSats chosen by NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Science_Mission_Directorate" title="Science Mission Directorate">
        Science Mission Directorate
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreier_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dreier-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware">
        Hardware
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Polar_Hydrogen_Mapper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hardware">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because of the scope of this mission, several unique challenges need to be addressed in implementing hardware. Typical
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
        low Earth orbit
       </a>
       (LEO) CubeSats can use "off-the-shelf" hardware, or parts available commercially for other uses, but because LunaH-Map is intended to run longer and travel further than most LEO CubSat missions, commercial parts cannot be expected to perform reliably for the mission duration unmodified. Also, unlike most conventional CubeSats, LunaH-Map will need to navigate to its desired orbit after leaving the
       <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">
        launch vehicle
       </a>
       , so it will need to be equipped with its own propulsion system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The primary science instrument will be a
       <a href="/wiki/Scintillation_counter" title="Scintillation counter">
        scintillation neutron detector
       </a>
       composed of elpasolite (Cs
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       YLiCl
       <sub>
        6
       </sub>
       :Ce or CLYC). This material is a
       <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">
        scintillator
       </a>
       , which measurably glows when it interacts with
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron_temperature#Thermal" title="Neutron temperature">
        thermal
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron_temperature#Epithermal" title="Neutron temperature">
        epithermal
       </a>
       neutrons. LunaH-Map's neutron detector will consist of an array of sixteen 2.5×2.5×2 cm CLYC scintillators.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
