       </b>
       is the largest
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       . It is the sixth largest Jovian satellite overall in size, and only the four
       <a href="/wiki/Galilean_moons" title="Galilean moons">
        Galilean moons
       </a>
       of Jupiter have greater mass. It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dillon_Perrine" title="Charles Dillon Perrine">
        Charles Dillon Perrine
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">
        Lick Observatory
       </a>
       on 3 December 1904 and is named after the
       <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">
        nymph
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_(mythology)" title="Himalia (mythology)">
        Himalia
       </a>
       , who bore three sons of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       (the Greek equivalent of Jupiter). It is one of the largest planetary moons in the Solar System not imaged in detail, and the largest not including the moons of Neptune and several trans-Neptunian objects, particularly
       <a href="/wiki/Dysnomia_(moon)" title="Dysnomia (moon)">
        Dysnomia
       </a>
       , the moon of
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Discovery-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Himalia_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Himalia was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dillon_Perrine" title="Charles Dillon Perrine">
        Charles Dillon Perrine
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">
        Lick Observatory
       </a>
       on 3 December 1904.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Discovery-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Himalia is Jupiter's most easily observed small satellite; though
       <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(moon)" title="Amalthea (moon)">
        Amalthea
       </a>
       is brighter, its proximity to the planet's brilliant disk makes it a far more difficult object to view.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Himalia_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Himalia is named after the
       <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">
        nymph
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_(mythology)" title="Himalia (mythology)">
        Himalia
       </a>
       , who bore three sons of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       (the Greek equivalent of Jupiter). The moon did not receive its present name until 1975;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_2846_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_2846-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       before then, it was simply known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter VI
        </span>
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Jupiter Satellite VI
       </b>
       , although calls for a full name appeared shortly after its and
       <a href="/wiki/Elara_(moon)" title="Elara (moon)">
        Elara
       </a>
       's discovery; A.C.D. Crommelin wrote in 1905:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Unfortunately the numeration of Jupiter's satellites is now in precisely the same confusion as that of Saturn's system was before the numbers were abandoned and names substituted. A similar course would seem to be advisable here; the designation V for the inner satellite [Amalthea] was tolerated for a time, as it was considered to be in a class by itself; but it has now got companions, so that this subterfuge disappears. The substitution of names for numerals is certainly more poetic.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crommelin_11-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Crommelin-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The moon was sometimes called
       <b>
        Hestia
       </b>
       , after the
       <a href="/wiki/Hestia" title="Hestia">
        Greek goddess
       </a>
       , from 1955 to 1975.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaposchkin_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gaposchkin-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Himalia_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       At a distance of about 11.5 million km from Jupiter, Himalia takes about 251 Earth days to complete one orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is the largest member of the
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_group" title="Himalia group">
        group that bears its name
       </a>
       , the moons orbiting between 11.4 and 13 million kilometres from Jupiter at an inclination of about 27.5°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewitt2004_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jewitt2004-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The orbital elements are as of January 2000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson2000_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson2000-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are continuously changing due to
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       and planetary perturbations.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

         </div>
         Himalia's rotational
         <a href="/wiki/Light_curve" title="Light curve">

         </a>
         from Earth-based observations taken between August and October 2010.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pilcher2012_7-1">
          <a href="#cite_note-Pilcher2012-7">
           [7]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Himalia's
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation_period" title="Rotation period">
        rotational period
       </a>
       is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7004252000000000000♠
        </span>
        7 h
       </span>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001460000000000000♠
        </span>
        46 m
       </span>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001550000000000000♠
        </span>
        55
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        2 s
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pilcher2012_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pilcher2012-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Himalia appears neutral in color (grey), like the other members of its group, with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colour_index" title="Colour index">
        colour indices
       </a>
       B−V=0.62, V−R=0.4, similar to a
       <a href="/wiki/C-type_asteroid" title="C-type asteroid">
        C-type asteroid
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RettigWalshConsolmagno2001_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RettigWalshConsolmagno2001-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Measurements by
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       confirm a featureless
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       , with a slight absorption at
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6994300000000000000♠
        </span>
        3 μm
       </span>
       , which could indicate the presence of water.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chamberlain_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chamberlain-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mass">
        Mass
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2005, Emelyanov estimated Himalia to have a mass of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7018419000000000000♠
        </span>
        4.19
        <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         18
        </sup>
        <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">
         kg
        </a>
       </span>
       (GM=0.28), based on a
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        perturbation
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Elara_(moon)" title="Elara (moon)">
        Elara
       </a>
       on July 15, 1949.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emelyanov2005_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Emelyanov2005-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       JPL's Solar System dynamics web site assumes that Himalia has a mass of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7018670000000000000♠
        </span>
        6.7
        <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         18
        </sup>
        kg
       </span>
       (GM=0.45) with a radius of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7004850000000000000♠
        </span>
        85 km
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jplssd_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-jplssd-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Himalia's density will depend on whether it has an average radius of about
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7004670000000000000♠
        </span>
        67 km
       </span>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Geometric_mean" title="Geometric mean">
        geometric mean
       </a>
       from
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emelyanov2005_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Emelyanov2005-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or a radius closer to
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7004850000000000000♠
        </span>
        85 km
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jplssd_4-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-jplssd-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Himalia_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:77px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Himalia_from_New_Horizons.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="72" data-file-width="75" height="72" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dc/Himalia_from_New_Horizons.jpg" width="75"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Himalia_from_New_Horizons.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Image of Himalia taken by the
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
           New Horizons
          </a>
         </i>
         spacecraft in 2006. The moon covers only a few pixels.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In November 2000, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft, en route to
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       , made a number of images of Himalia, including photos from a distance of 4.4 million km. Himalia covers only a few pixels, but seems to be an elongated object with axes
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7002150000000000000♠
        </span>
        150
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        20
       </span>
       and
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005120000000000000♠
        </span>
        120
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        20 km
       </span>
       , close to the Earth-based estimations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porco2003_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Porco2003-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In February and March 2007, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft en route to Pluto made a series of images of Himalia, culminating in photos from a distance of 8 million km. Again, Himalia appears only a few pixels across.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Himalia_ring.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="298" data-file-width="300" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Himalia_ring.jpg/100px-Himalia_ring.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Himalia_ring.jpg/150px-Himalia_ring.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Himalia_ring.jpg/200px-Himalia_ring.jpg 2x" width="100"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Himalia_ring.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          New Horizons
         </i>
         image of possible Himalia ring
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The small moon
       <a href="/wiki/Dia_(moon)" title="Dia (moon)">
        Dia
       </a>
       , 4 kilometres in diameter, had gone missing since its discovery in 2000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL-SSD_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-JPL-SSD-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One theory was that it had crashed into the much larger moon Himalia, 170 kilometres in diameter, creating a faint ring. This possible ring appears as a faint streak near Himalia in images from NASA's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       mission to
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       . This suggests that Jupiter sometimes gains and loses small moons through collisions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HimaliaRing_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HimaliaRing-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the recovery of Dia in 2010 and 2011
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEC2012-R22_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPEC2012-R22-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       disproves the link between Dia and the Himalia ring, although it is still possible that a different moon may have been involved.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
