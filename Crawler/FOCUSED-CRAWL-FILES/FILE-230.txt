      <p>
       <b>
        Historically significant lunar eclipses
       </b>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        eclipses of the Moon
       </a>
       that are mentioned in historical accounts in connection with a significant event. Lunar eclipses are somewhat rare events, although not as rare as solar eclipses, because unlike solar eclipses they can be viewed from anywhere on the dark side of the Earth. Throughout history lunar eclipses have been held to be responsible for lost battles, and have helped make possible extraordinary escapes.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="29_January_1137_BC">
        29 January 1137 BC
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Historically_significant_lunar_eclipses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 29 January 1137 BC">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_12th-century_BCE_lunar_eclipses" title="List of 12th-century BCE lunar eclipses">
        List of 12th-century BCE lunar eclipses
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The first mention of a lunar eclipse was found in the
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_people" title="Chinese people">
        Chinese
       </a>
       book Zhou-Shu, a book of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhou_Dynasty" title="Zhou Dynasty">
        Zhou Dynasty
       </a>
       . The book was discovered in 280 AD, in a
       <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">
        tomb
       </a>
       of a king or noblemen. The eclipse mentioned in this book took place many centuries before that time. Professor S.M. Russell believes that the eclipse described in the book may refer to the event that happened on 29 January 1137 BC (-1136).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Observatory_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_Observatory-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-History-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="9_October_425_BC">
        9 October 425 BC
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Historically_significant_lunar_eclipses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 9 October 425 BC">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_5th-century_BCE_lunar_eclipses" title="List of 5th-century BCE lunar eclipses">
        List of 5th-century BCE lunar eclipses
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       When eclipses were not well understood, they were sometimes associated with unnatural forces.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Witch" title="Witch">
        Witches
       </a>
       from the Greek region of
       <a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">
        Thessaly
       </a>
       claimed the ability to extinguish the moon's light and draw it down from the sky. In his famous comedy
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Clouds" title="The Clouds">
         The Clouds
        </a>
       </i>
       (419 BC),
       <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">
        Aristophanes
       </a>
       describes the eclipse that took place two years prior to that.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         the moon deserted her course and the sun at once veiled his beam threatening, no longer to give you light, if Cleon became general...
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_clouds_5-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-The_clouds-5">
           [5]
          </a>
         </sup>
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
          <a href="#cite_note-6">
           [6]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="28_August_413_BC">
        28 August 413 BC
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Historically_significant_lunar_eclipses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 28 August 413 BC">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_5th-century_BCE_lunar_eclipses" title="List of 5th-century BCE lunar eclipses">
        List of 5th-century BCE lunar eclipses
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       This eclipse happened during
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_Expedition#Second_Battle_of_Syracuse" title="Sicilian Expedition">
        Second Battle of Syracuse
       </a>
       . Just as the Athenians were preparing to sail home, there was a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Nicias" title="Nicias">
        Nicias
       </a>
       , described by
       <a href="/wiki/Thucydides" title="Thucydides">
        Thucydides
       </a>
       as a particularly superstitious man, asked the priests what he should do. The priests suggested the Athenians wait for another 27 days, and Nicias agreed. The Syracusans took advantage of this, and 76 of their ships attacked 86 Athenian ships in the harbor. The Athenians were defeated and
       <a href="/wiki/Eurymedon_(strategos)" title="Eurymedon (strategos)">
        Eurymedon
       </a>
       was killed. Many of the ships were pushed up on to the shore, where
       <a href="/wiki/Gylippus" title="Gylippus">
        Gylippus
       </a>
       was waiting. He killed some of the crews and captured 18 beached ships, but a force of Athenians and Etruscans forced Gylippus back.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">
        Plutarch
       </a>
       described this eclipse and the superstitious response:
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         And when all were in readiness, and none of the enemy had observed them, not expecting such a thing, the moon was eclipsed in the night, to the great fright of Nicias and others, who, for want of experience, or out of superstition, felt alarm at such appearances.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plutarch_7-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-Plutarch-7">
           [7]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1_March_1504">
        1 March 1504
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Historically_significant_lunar_eclipses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1 March 1504">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_16th-century_lunar_eclipses" title="List of 16th-century lunar eclipses">
        List of 16th-century lunar eclipses
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/March_1504_lunar_eclipse" title="March 1504 lunar eclipse">
        March 1504 lunar eclipse
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1302" data-file-width="1602" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg/220px-Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg/330px-Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg/440px-Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Illustration of Columbus using a lunar eclipse to impress the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people" title="Indigenous people">
          Indigenous people
         </a>
         of Jamaica.
         <br/>
         From
         <i>
          Astronomie Populaire
         </i>
         1879, p 231 fig. 86
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       On 30 June 1503,
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">
        Christopher Columbus
       </a>
       beached his two last
       <a href="/wiki/Caravel" title="Caravel">
        caravels
       </a>
       and was stranded in
       <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">
        Jamaica
       </a>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people" title="Indigenous people">
        indigenous people
       </a>
       of the island welcomed Columbus and his crew and fed them, but Columbus' sailors cheated and stole from the natives. After six months, the natives halted the food supply.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbus_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Columbus-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Columbus had on board an
       <a href="/wiki/Almanac" title="Almanac">
        almanac
       </a>
       authored by
       <a href="/wiki/Regiomontanus" title="Regiomontanus">
        Regiomontanus
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical" title="Astronomical">
        astronomical
       </a>
       tables covering the years 1475–1506; upon consulting the book, he noticed the date and the time of an upcoming lunar eclipse. He was able to use this information to his advantage. He requested a meeting for that day with the
       <a href="/wiki/Cacique" title="Cacique">
        Cacique
       </a>
       , the leader, and told him that his
       <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">
        god
       </a>
       was angry with the local people's treatment of Columbus and his men. Columbus said his god would provide a clear sign of his displeasure by making the rising full Moon appear "inflamed with wrath".
      </p>
      <p>
       The lunar eclipse and the red moon appeared on schedule, and the indigenous people were impressed and frightened. The son of Columbus, Ferdinand, wrote that the people:
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         with great howling and lamentation came running from every direction to the ships laden with provisions, praying to the Admiral to intercede with his god on their behalf...
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Columbus timed the eclipse with his
       <a href="/wiki/Hourglass" title="Hourglass">
        hourglass
       </a>
       , and shortly before the totality ended after 48 minutes, he told the frightened indigenous people that they were going to be forgiven.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbus_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Columbus-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When the moon started to reappear from the shadow of the Earth, he told them that his god had pardoned them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-space-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fictionalized_variations">
        Fictionalized variations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Historically_significant_lunar_eclipses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fictionalized variations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1885
       <a href="/wiki/H._Rider_Haggard" title="H. Rider Haggard">
        H. Rider Haggard
       </a>
       used an altered version of the real story of the rescue of Columbus in his novel,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/King_Solomon%27s_Mines" title="King Solomon's Mines">
         King Solomon's Mines
        </a>
       </i>
       . In that novel,
       <a href="/wiki/Allan_Quatermain" title="Allan Quatermain">
        Allan Quatermain
       </a>
       and his fellow Englishmen use their foreknowledge of a solar eclipse to claim that they will black out the sun as proof of their powers, and save captive girls from an unjust death sentence.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1889
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">
        Mark Twain
       </a>
       used a similar plot device in his novel,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_Connecticut_Yankee_in_King_Arthur%27s_Court" title="A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court">
         A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court
        </a>
       </i>
       . In that novel, Hank Morgan, a 19th-century resident of
       <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">
        Hartford, Connecticut
       </a>
       , after a blow to the head, awakens to find himself inexplicably transported back in time to
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Anglo-Saxon_England" title="History of Anglo-Saxon England">
        early medieval England
       </a>
       at the time of the legendary
       <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">
        King Arthur
       </a>
       . When Morgan is about to be burned at the stake, he pretends to conjure a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       that he knew was about to happen; this prediction saves Morgan's life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-space-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another novel that used a solar-eclipse scene modeled after Columbus' lunar eclipse was
       <a href="/wiki/Boles%C5%82aw_Prus" title="Bolesław Prus">
        Bolesław Prus
       </a>
       ' historical novel,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh_(novel)#Inspirations" title="Pharaoh (novel)">
         Pharaoh
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar plot also features in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="The Adventures of Tintin">
         The Adventures of Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       comic
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_the_Sun" title="Prisoners of the Sun">
         Prisoners of the Sun
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="15_July_1916">
        15 July 1916
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Historically_significant_lunar_eclipses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 15 July 1916">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_20th-century_lunar_eclipses" title="List of 20th-century lunar eclipses">
        List of 20th-century lunar eclipses
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_eclipse_path_July_15_1916_1.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="492" data-file-width="777" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Lunar_eclipse_path_July_15_1916_1.jpg/200px-Lunar_eclipse_path_July_15_1916_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Lunar_eclipse_path_July_15_1916_1.jpg/300px-Lunar_eclipse_path_July_15_1916_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Lunar_eclipse_path_July_15_1916_1.jpg/400px-Lunar_eclipse_path_July_15_1916_1.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_eclipse_path_July_15_1916_1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The path of the
         <a href="/wiki/July_1916_lunar_eclipse" title="July 1916 lunar eclipse">
          lunar eclipse on 15 July 1916
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ross_Sea_party" title="Ross Sea party">
        Ross Sea party
       </a>
       was a component of
       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Shackleton" title="Ernest Shackleton">
        Sir Ernest Shackleton
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Trans-Antarctic_Expedition" title="Imperial Trans-Antarctic Expedition">
        Imperial Trans-Antarctic Expedition
       </a>
       of 1914–17. Five men were stranded not far away from
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Evans" title="Cape Evans">
        Cape Evans
       </a>
       . There was
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">
        sea ice
       </a>
       between them and the relative safety of the hut on Cape Evans. On 8 May two of the men,
       <a href="/wiki/Aeneas_Mackintosh" title="Aeneas Mackintosh">
        Aeneas Mackintosh
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hayward" title="Victor Hayward">
        Victor Hayward
       </a>
       , decided to make an attempt to reach the hut. Soon after they set out a
       <a href="/wiki/Blizzard" title="Blizzard">
        blizzard
       </a>
       hit. When the weather cleared up, the remaining men tried to look for them, but realized that the ice was far too thin to cross, and that their friends had been lost. Now they knew that they should wait for a thicker ice and for the full moon to attempt the crossing. Having the full moon was essential, because during
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">
        polar night
       </a>
       the Moon is the only source of natural light other than the extremely dim light of the stars.
      </p>
      <p>
       The weather did not cooperate during the full moon of June, but on 15 July, everything seemed to be just right: calm weather, thick ice, clear skies and a full Moon. The men started their journey in the morning. When the Moon rose, however, the men were surprised to find it was about to be eclipsed.
       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Wild" title="Ernest Wild">
        Ernest Wild
       </a>
       wrote later:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        "I thought we were going to be left in darkness but a very little bit of the rim remained to light us..."
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Although the eclipse continued for few hours, the men were fortunate because it was only a partial eclipse. They reached Cape Evans later on the same day.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polar_castaways_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Polar_castaways-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
