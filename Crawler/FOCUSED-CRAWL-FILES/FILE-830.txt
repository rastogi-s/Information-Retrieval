      <p>
       <b>
        Galilaei
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_craters" title="Lunar craters">
        lunar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       located in the western
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Oceanus Procellarum
       </a>
       . Some distance to the southeast is the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Reiner_(crater)" title="Reiner (crater)">
        Reiner
       </a>
       , while to the south-southwest is
       <a href="/wiki/Cavalerius_(crater)" title="Cavalerius (crater)">
        Cavalerius
       </a>
       . Northeast of the crater is a meandering
       <a href="/wiki/Rille" title="Rille">
        rille
       </a>
       named the Rima Galilaei. To the southeast is the unusual
       <a href="/wiki/Reiner_Gamma" title="Reiner Gamma">
        Reiner Gamma
       </a>
       formation, a swirling arrangement of light-hued
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_system" title="Ray system">
        ray-like
       </a>
       material.
      </p>
      <p>
       Galilaei is relatively undistinguished, with a sharp-edged rim that has a higher
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       than the surrounding maria. The inner walls slope down to a ring of debris on the outer edges of the interior floor. There is a small central rise near the midpoint.
      </p>
      <p>
       About 40 kilometers to the south is the landing site of the
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_9" title="Luna 9">
        Luna 9
       </a>
       robotic probe, the first such vehicle to make a controlled landing on the lunar surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite being the first person to publish astronomical observations of the Moon with a
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
        Galileo Galilei
       </a>
       is honored only with this unremarkable formation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Initially, the name Galilaeus had been applied by
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Riccioli" title="Giovanni Battista Riccioli">
        Giovanni Battista Riccioli
       </a>
       , an
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italian
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">
        Jesuit
       </a>
       who produced one of the first detailed maps of the Moon in 1651, to a large and bright nearby albedo feature (now known as
       <a href="/wiki/Reiner_Gamma" title="Reiner Gamma">
        Reiner Gamma
       </a>
       ). The name was transferred to its present location by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Heinrich_M%C3%A4dler" title="Johann Heinrich Mädler">
        Johann Heinrich Mädler
       </a>
       in his influential
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mappa_Selenographica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mappa Selenographica (page does not exist)">
        Mappa Selenographica
       </a>
       , published in collaboration with
       <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Beer" title="Wilhelm Beer">
        Wilhelm Beer
       </a>
       in four parts between 1834 and 1836. Mädler's motive for this change was the fact that his lunar map did not name albedo features, forcing him to transfer Galileo's name to an insignificant nearby crater.
      </p>
