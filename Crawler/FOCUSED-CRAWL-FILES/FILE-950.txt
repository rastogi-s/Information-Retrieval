      <p>
       <b>
        Sycorax
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">
            ər
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          SIK
         </span>
         -or-aks
        </i>
       </a>
       ) is the largest
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       . Sycorax was discovered on 6 September 1997 by
       <a href="/wiki/Brett_J._Gladman" title="Brett J. Gladman">
        Brett J. Gladman
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_D._Nicholson" title="Philip D. Nicholson">
        Philip D. Nicholson
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_A._Burns" title="Joseph A. Burns">
        Joseph A. Burns
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/John_J._Kavelaars" title="John J. Kavelaars">
        John J. Kavelaars
       </a>
       using the 200-inch
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hale_telescope" title="Hale telescope">
        Hale telescope
       </a>
       , together with
       <a href="/wiki/Caliban_(moon)" title="Caliban (moon)">
        Caliban
       </a>
       , and given the temporary designation
       <b>
        S/1997 U 2
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGladman_Nicholson_et_al.1998_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGladman_Nicholson_et_al.1998-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <b>
        Uranus XVII
       </b>
       , it was named after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sycorax_(Shakespeare)" title="Sycorax (Shakespeare)">

        Caliban
       </a>
       's mother in
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        William Shakespeare
       </a>
       's play
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Tempest_(play)" title="The Tempest (play)">
         The Tempest
        </a>
       </i>
       .

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sycorax_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Sycorax follows a distant orbit, more than 20 times further from Uranus than the furthest regular moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(moon)" title="Oberon (moon)">
        Oberon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGladman_Nicholson_et_al.1998_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGladman_Nicholson_et_al.1998-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its orbit is retrograde, moderately
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclined
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentric
       </a>
       . The orbital parameters suggest that it may belong, together with
       <a href="/wiki/Setebos_(moon)" title="Setebos (moon)">
        Setebos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Prospero_(moon)" title="Prospero (moon)">
        Prospero
       </a>
       , to the same dynamic cluster, suggesting common origin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGravHolmanFraser2004_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGravHolmanFraser2004-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The diagram illustrates the orbital parameters of the retrograde
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellites
       </a>
       of Uranus (in polar co-ordinates) with the eccentricity of the orbits represented by the segments extending from the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        pericentre
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        apocentre
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sycorax_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The diameter of Sycorax is estimated at 165 km based on the thermal emission data from
       <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">
        Spitzer
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory" title="Herschel Space Observatory">
        Herschel
       </a>
       Space telescopes
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool9_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool9-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       making it the largest irregular satellite of Uranus, comparable in size with
       <a href="/wiki/Puck_(moon)" title="Puck (moon)">
        Puck
       </a>
       and with
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_(moon)" title="Himalia (moon)">
        Himalia
       </a>
       , the biggest irregular satellite of Jupiter.
      </p>
      <p>
       The satellite appears
       <i>
        light-red
       </i>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">
        visible spectrum
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Color_index" title="Color index">
        colour indices
       </a>
       <span class="nowrap">
        B–V = 0.87
       </span>
       <span class="nowrap">
        V–R = 0.44
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERettigWalshConsolmagno2001_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERettigWalshConsolmagno2001-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <span class="nowrap">
        B–V = 0.78 ± 0.02
       </span>
       <span class="nowrap">
        V–R = 0.62 ± 0.01
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGravHolmanFraser2004_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGravHolmanFraser2004-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <span class="nowrap">
        B–V = 0.839 ± 0.014
       </span>
       <span class="nowrap">
        V–R = 0.531 ± 0.005
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maris_2007_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Maris_2007-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), redder than Himalia but still less red than most
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       objects. However, in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_infrared" title="Near infrared">
        near infrared
       </a>
       , the spectrum turns blue between 0.8 and 1.25 μm and finally becomes neutral at the longer wavelengths.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Romon_2001_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Romon_2001-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The rotation period of Sycorax is estimated at about 3.6 hours. Rotation causes periodical variations of the visible magnitude with the amplitude of 0.07.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maris_2007_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Maris_2007-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sycorax_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       It is hypothesized that Sycorax is a captured object; it did not form in the accretion disk which existed around Uranus just after its formation. No exact capture mechanism is known; but capturing a moon requires the
       <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">
        dissipation
       </a>
       of energy. Possible capture processes include gas drag in the
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">
        protoplanetary disk
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/N-body_problem" title="N-body problem">
        many-body interactions
       </a>
       and capture during the fast growth of Uranus's mass (so called
       <i>
        pull-down
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheppardJewittKleyna2005_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheppardJewittKleyna2005-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maris_2007_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Maris_2007-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
