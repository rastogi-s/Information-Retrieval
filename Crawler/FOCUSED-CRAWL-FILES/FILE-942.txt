      <p>
       <b>
        Mab
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'b' in 'buy'">
            b
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          MAB
         </span>
        </i>
       </a>
       ), or
       <b>
        Uranus XXVI
       </b>
       (26), is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellite" title="Inner satellite">
        inner satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_R._Showalter" title="Mark R. Showalter">
        Mark R. Showalter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jack_J._Lissauer" title="Jack J. Lissauer">
        Jack J. Lissauer
       </a>
       in 2003 using the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_8209_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_8209-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was named after
       <a href="/wiki/Queen_Mab" title="Queen Mab">
        Queen Mab
       </a>
       , a fairy queen from English folklore who is mentioned in
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        William Shakespeare
       </a>
       's play
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">
         Romeo and Juliet
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gazetteer_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gazetteer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because the moon is small and dark, it was not seen in the heavily scrutinized images taken by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       during its Uranus flyby in 1986. However, it is brighter than another moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Perdita_(moon)" title="Perdita (moon)">
        Perdita
       </a>
       , which was discovered from Voyager's photos in 1997. This led scientists to re-examine the old photos again, and the satellite was finally found in the images.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter_Lissauer_2006_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Showalter_Lissauer_2006-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The size of Mab is not known exactly. If it is as dark as
       <a href="/wiki/Puck_(moon)" title="Puck (moon)">
        Puck
       </a>
       , it is about 24 km in diameter. On the other hand, if it is brightly coloured like the neighbouring moon
       <a href="/wiki/Miranda_(moon)" title="Miranda (moon)">
        Miranda
       </a>
       , it would be even smaller than
       <a href="/wiki/Cupid_(moon)" title="Cupid (moon)">
        Cupid
       </a>
       and comparable to the smallest outer satellites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter_Lissauer_2006_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Showalter_Lissauer_2006-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Mab is heavily
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        perturbed
       </a>
       . The actual source for perturbation is still unclear, but is presumed to be one or more of the nearby orbiting moons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter_Lissauer_2006_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Showalter_Lissauer_2006-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Mab orbits at the same distance from Uranus as the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Uranus" title="Rings of Uranus">
        μ
       </a>
       ring (formerly known as R/2003 U 1), a recently discovered dusty
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">
        ring
       </a>
       . The moon is nearly the optimal size for dust production, since larger moons can recollect the escaping dust and smaller moons have too small surface areas for supplying the ring via ring particle or
       <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">
        meteoroid
       </a>
       collisions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Layton_2005_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Layton_2005-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       No rings associated with Perdita and Cupid have been found, probably because
       <a href="/wiki/Belinda_(moon)" title="Belinda (moon)">
        Belinda
       </a>
       limits the lifetimes of dust they generate.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter_Lissauer_2006_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Showalter_Lissauer_2006-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Following its discovery, Mab was given the temporary designation
       <b>
        S/2003 U 1
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_8209_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_8209-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The moon is also designated
       <b>
        Uranus XXVI
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gazetteer_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gazetteer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
