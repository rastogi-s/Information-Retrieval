      <p>
       <b>
        Moonshine
       </b>
       was originally a slang term for high-
       <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_proof" title="Alcohol proof">
        proof
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distilled_spirit" title="Distilled spirit">
        distilled spirits
       </a>
       usually produced
       <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_law" title="Alcohol law">
        illicitly
       </a>
       , without government authorization.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In recent years, however, moonshine has been legalized in various countries and has become a commercial product.
      </p>
      <p>
       Legal in the United States since 2010, moonshine is defined as "clear, unaged whiskey",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       typically made with
       <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">
        corn
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Mashing" title="Mashing">
        mash
       </a>
       as its main ingredient.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-okstatencyclo_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-okstatencyclo-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Liquor-control laws in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       always applied to moonshine, with efforts accelerated during the total ban on alcohol production mandated under the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/18th_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="18th Amendment to the United States Constitution">
        18th Amendment of the Constitution
       </a>
       . Since its repeal, and moonshine's recent legalization, they focus on evasion of revenue taxation on spiritous or intoxicating liquors. Applicable laws are enforced by the
       <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">
        Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Justice" title="US Department of Justice">
        US Department of Justice
       </a>
       , once known colloquially as "revenooers."
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
        Terminology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonshine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Moonshine is known by many nicknames, including
       <b>
        white liquor
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        white lightning
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        mountain dew
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        choop
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        hooch
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        homebrew
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        white whiskey
       </b>
       , and
       <b>
        mash liquor
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonshine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonshine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The word "moonshine" is believed to be derived from the term "
       <a href="/wiki/Moonrakers" title="Moonrakers">
        moonrakers
       </a>
       " used for early English smugglers and the clandestine nature of the operations of the illegal
       <a href="/wiki/Appalachia" title="Appalachia">
        Appalachian
       </a>
       distillers who produced and distributed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whiskey" title="Whiskey">
        whiskey
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moonshine_apparatus_Estes-Winn_Antique_Car_Museum.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="3072" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Moonshine_apparatus_Estes-Winn_Antique_Car_Museum.JPG/200px-Moonshine_apparatus_Estes-Winn_Antique_Car_Museum.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Moonshine_apparatus_Estes-Winn_Antique_Car_Museum.JPG/300px-Moonshine_apparatus_Estes-Winn_Antique_Car_Museum.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Moonshine_apparatus_Estes-Winn_Antique_Car_Museum.JPG/400px-Moonshine_apparatus_Estes-Winn_Antique_Car_Museum.JPG 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moonshine_apparatus_Estes-Winn_Antique_Car_Museum.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A historical moonshine distilling-apparatus in a museum.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Process">
        Process
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonshine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Process">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When illegal in the United States, moonshine distillation was done at night to avoid discovery.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moonshine was especially important to the Appalachian area. This white whiskey most likely entered the Appalachian region in the late 18th century to early 1800s.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_American" title="Scotch-Irish American">
        Scots-Irish
       </a>
       immigrants from the province of
       <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">
        Ulster
       </a>
       in the north of Ireland brought their recipe for
       <i>
        uisce beatha
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">
        Gaelic
       </a>
       for "water of life". The settlers made their whiskey without aging it, and this is the same recipe that became traditional in the Appalachian area.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the early 20th century, moonshine became a key source of income for many Appalachian residents like
       <a href="/wiki/Popcorn_Sutton" title="Popcorn Sutton">
        Marvin "Popcorn" Sutton
       </a>
       , since the limited road network made it difficult and expensive to transport corn crops. As a study of farmers in Cocke County, Tennessee, observes: "One could transport much more value in corn if it was first converted to whiskey. One horse could haul ten times more value on its back in whiskey than in corn."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeineSchafft201298–99_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeineSchafft201298–99-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moonshiners in
       <a href="/wiki/Harlan_County,_Kentucky" title="Harlan County, Kentucky">
        Harlan County, Kentucky
       </a>
       , like Maggie Bailey, made the whiskey to sell in order to provide for their families.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Others, like
       <a href="/wiki/Amos_Owens" title="Amos Owens">
        Amos Owens
       </a>
       , from
       <a href="/wiki/Rutherford_County,_North_Carolina" title="Rutherford County, North Carolina">
        Rutherford County, North Carolina
       </a>
       and "Popcorn" Sutton from Maggie Valley, North Carolina sold moonshine to nearby areas.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pot_Still_Diagram.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="707" data-file-width="736" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Pot_Still_Diagram.jpg/200px-Pot_Still_Diagram.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Pot_Still_Diagram.jpg/300px-Pot_Still_Diagram.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Pot_Still_Diagram.jpg/400px-Pot_Still_Diagram.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pot_Still_Diagram.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A diagram showing the metal pot and the condensation coil apparatus.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Marvin "Popcorn" Sutton's life was covered in a documentary on the Discovery Channel called "Moonshiners". The legendary bootlegger once said that the malt (a combination of corn, barley, rye) is what makes the basic moonshine recipe
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       work. In modern usage, the term "moonshine" ordinarily implies that the liquor is produced illegally; however, the term has also been used on the labels of some legal products as a way of marketing them as providing a similar drinking experience as found with illegal liquor.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">
        Safety
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonshine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Safety">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Poorly produced moonshine can be contaminated, mainly from materials used in the construction of the
       <a href="/wiki/Still" title="Still">
        still
       </a>
       . Stills employing
       <a href="/wiki/Radiator_(engine_cooling)" title="Radiator (engine cooling)">
        automotive radiators
       </a>
       as condensers are particularly dangerous; in some cases,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycol" title="Glycol">
        glycol
       </a>
       , products from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antifreeze_(coolant)" title="Antifreeze (coolant)">
        antifreeze
       </a>
       , can appear as well. Radiators used as condensers also may contain
       <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">
        lead
       </a>
       at the connections to the plumbing. These methods often resulted in blindness or
       <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">
        lead poisoning
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       for those consuming tainted liquor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeineSchafft201297_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeineSchafft201297-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This was an issue during Prohibition when many died from ingesting unhealthy substances. Consumption of lead-tainted moonshine is an important risk factor for saturnine
       <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">
        gout
       </a>
       , which is a very painful but treatable medical condition that damages the kidneys and joints.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although
       <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">
        methanol
       </a>
       is not produced in toxic amounts by fermentation of sugars from grain starches,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       contamination is still possible by unscrupulous distillers using cheap methanol to increase the apparent strength of the product. Moonshine can be made both more palatable and less damaging by discarding the "foreshot"—the first few ounces of
       <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">
        alcohol
       </a>
       that drip from the condenser. Because methanol vaporizes at a lower temperature than ethanol it is commonly believed that the foreshot contains most of the methanol, if any, from the mash. Research shows that this is not the case and that methanol will be present until the very end of the distillation run. But the foreshot does typically contain small amounts of other undesirable compounds such as
       <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">
        acetone
       </a>
       and various
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldehydes" title="Aldehydes">
        aldehydes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Alcohol concentrations at higher strengths (the
       <a href="/wiki/Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals" title="Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals">
        GHS
       </a>
       identifies concentrations above 24% ABV as dangerous
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) are flammable and therefore dangerous to handle. This is especially true during the distilling process when vaporized alcohol may accumulate in the air to dangerous concentrations if adequate ventilation has not been provided.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tests">
        Tests
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonshine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tests">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A quick estimate of the alcoholic strength, or proof, of the distillate (the ratio of alcohol to water) is often achieved by shaking a clear container of the distillate. Large bubbles with a short duration indicate a higher alcohol content, while smaller bubbles that disappear more slowly indicate lower alcohol content.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A common folk test for the quality of moonshine was to pour a small quantity of it into a spoon and set it on fire. The theory was that a safe distillate burns with a blue flame, but a tainted distillate burns with a yellow flame. Practitioners of this simple test also held that if a radiator coil had been used as a condenser, then there would be
       <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">
        lead
       </a>
       in the distillate, which would give a reddish flame. This led to the
       <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic" title="Mnemonic">
        mnemonic
       </a>
       , "Lead burns red and makes you dead." or "Red means dead."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although the flame test will show the presence of lead and
       <a href="/wiki/Fusel_alcohol" title="Fusel alcohol">
        fusel oils
       </a>
       , it will not reveal the presence of methanol (also poisonous), which burns with an invisible flame.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A more reliable method of determinating the alcoholic strength is to use scientific testing equipment, such as an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholmeter" title="Alcoholmeter">
        alcoholmeter
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrometer" title="Hydrometer">
        hydrometer
       </a>
       . A hydrometer is used during and after the fermentation process to determine the potential alcohol percent of the moonshine, whereas an alcoholmeter is used after the product has been distilled to determine the volume percent or proof.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
