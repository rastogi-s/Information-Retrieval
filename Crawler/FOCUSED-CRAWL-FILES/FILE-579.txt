      <p>
       <b>
        Lunar IceCube
       </b>
       is a planned NASA
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanosatellite" title="Nanosatellite">
        nanosatellite
       </a>
       mission to prospect, locate, and estimate size and composition of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_water" title="Lunar water">
        water ice deposits on the Moon
       </a>
       for future exploitation by robots or humans. It will potentially fly as a secondary payload mission on the first flight of the
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">
        Space Launch System
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Mission_1" title="Exploration Mission 1">
        Exploration Mission 1
       </a>
       (EM-1) scheduled to launch in 2019.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2019-slip_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-2019-slip-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSU_Selected_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MSU_Selected-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_IceCube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The lunar mission was designed by
       <a href="/wiki/Morehead_State_University" title="Morehead State University">
        Morehead State University
       </a>
       and its partners, the
       <a href="/wiki/Busek" title="Busek">
        Busek Company
       </a>
       , NASA Goddard Spaceflight Center, and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Catholic_University_of_America" title="The Catholic University of America">
        The Catholic University of America
       </a>
       (CUA).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-August2015_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-August2015-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was selected in April 2015 by NASA's NextSTEP program ('Next Space Technologies for Exploration Partnerships') and awarded a contract worth up to $7.9 million for further development.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunters_IceCube_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gunters_IceCube-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSU_Selected_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-MSU_Selected-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Lunar IceCube spacecraft will have a 6U
       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">
        CubeSat
       </a>
       format, and a mass of about ≈14 kg (31 lb). It is one of eleven
       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">
        CubeSats
       </a>
       planned to be carried on board the maiden flight of the SLS
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EM-1" title="EM-1">
        EM-1 mission
       </a>
       as secondary payloads in cis-lunar space, scheduled for 2019.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2019-slip_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2019-slip-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It will be deployed during lunar trajectory and will use an innovative electric RF
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_engine" title="Ion engine">
        ion engine
       </a>
       to achieve lunar capture and the science orbit to allow the team to make systematic measurements of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_water" title="Lunar water">
        lunar water
       </a>
       features from an orbit about 100 km (62 mi) above the lunar surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSU_Selected_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-MSU_Selected-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Principal Investigator is Ben Malphrus, Director of the Space Science Center at Morehead State University.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_IceCube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Clementine_(spacecraft)" title="Clementine (spacecraft)">
        Clementine
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Crater_Observation_and_Sensing_Satellite" title="Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite">
        Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite
       </a>
       (LCROSS), the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       (LRO) and India's
       <a href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-1" title="Chandrayaan-1">
        Chandrayaan-1
       </a>
       lunar orbiters and other missions, confirmed both water (H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O) and hydroxyl (—OH
       <sup>
        −
       </sup>
       ) deposits at high latitudes on the lunar surface, indicating the presence of trace amounts of
       <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">
        adsorbed
       </a>
       or bound water are present,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_Flash2015_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_Flash2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RPMWall_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RPMWall-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but their instruments weren't optimized for fully or systematically characterizing the elements in the infrared wavelength bands ideal for detecting water.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-August2015_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-August2015-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These missions suggest that there might be enough ice water at polar regions to be used by future landed missions,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_Flash2015_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_Flash2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RPMWall_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-RPMWall-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but the distribution is difficult to reconcile with thermal maps.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lunar prospecting missions are intended to pave the way toward incorporating use of space resources into mission architectures. NASA's planning for eventual
       <a href="/wiki/Human_mission_to_Mars" title="Human mission to Mars">
        human missions to Mars
       </a>
       depends on tapping the local natural resources to make oxygen and propellant for launching the return ship back to Earth, and a lunar precursor mission is a convenient location to test such
       <a href="/wiki/In_situ_resource_utilization" title="In situ resource utilization">
        in situ resource utilization
       </a>
       (ISRU) technology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_2015_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_2015-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Goals">
        Goals
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_IceCube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Goals">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The science goals are to investigate the distribution of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_water" title="Lunar water">
        water
       </a>
       and other volatiles, as a function of time of day, latitude, and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil" title="Lunar soil">
        lunar soil
       </a>
       composition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunters_IceCube_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gunters_IceCube-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSU_Selected_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-MSU_Selected-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Payload">
        Payload
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_IceCube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Payload">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BIT-3_Iodine_60W_with_BHC-50E.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2489" data-file-width="4865" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/BIT-3_Iodine_60W_with_BHC-50E.jpg/220px-BIT-3_Iodine_60W_with_BHC-50E.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/BIT-3_Iodine_60W_with_BHC-50E.jpg/330px-BIT-3_Iodine_60W_with_BHC-50E.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/BIT-3_Iodine_60W_with_BHC-50E.jpg/440px-BIT-3_Iodine_60W_with_BHC-50E.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BIT-3_Iodine_60W_with_BHC-50E.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Busek's iodine BIT-3 in operation
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Lunar IceCube will include a version of the Broadband InfraRed Compact High Resolution Exploration Spectrometer (BIRCHES) instrument, developed by NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">
        GSFC
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-August2015_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-August2015-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       BIRCHES is a compact version of the volatile-seeking
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
        spectrometer
       </a>
       instrument onbord the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       Pluto flyby mission.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSU_Selected_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-MSU_Selected-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Propulsion">
        Propulsion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_IceCube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Propulsion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The tiny
       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">
        CubeSat
       </a>
       spacecraft will make use of a miniature electric
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">
        RF
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_engine" title="Ion engine">
        ion engine
       </a>
       system based on
       <a href="/wiki/Busek" title="Busek">
        Busek
       </a>
       's 3 centimeter RF ion thruster, also known as BIT-3.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSU_Selected_2-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-MSU_Selected-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Busek_BIT-3_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Busek_BIT-3-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It utilizes a solid
       <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">
        iodine
       </a>
       propellant and an inductively-coupled plasma system that produces 1.1 mN thrust and 2800 sec specific impulse from approximately 50W total input power.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Busek_BIT-3_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Busek_BIT-3-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It will also use this engine for capture into lunar orbit, and orbit adjustments.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSU_Selected_2-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-MSU_Selected-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is estimated the spacecraft will take about 3 months to reach the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-August2015_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-August2015-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_software">
        Flight software
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_IceCube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flight software">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The flight software is being developed in
       <a href="/wiki/SPARK_(programming_language)" title="SPARK (programming language)">
        SPARK/Ada
       </a>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/Vermont_Technical_College" title="Vermont Technical College">
        Vermont Technical College
       </a>
       CubeSat Lab
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CubeSat_Lab_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CubeSat_Lab-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/SPARK_(programming_language)" title="SPARK (programming language)">
        SPARK/Ada
       </a>
       has the lowest error rate of any computer language, important for the reliability and success of this complicated spacecraft. It is used in commercial and military aircraft, air traffic control and high speed trains. This is the second spacecraft using
       <a href="/wiki/SPARK_(programming_language)" title="SPARK (programming language)">
        SPARK/Ada
       </a>
       , the first being the Basic Low Earth Orbit CubeSat
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BasicLEO_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BasicLEO-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       also by the Vermont Tech CubeSat Lab, the only fully successful university CubeSat out of 12 on the NASA ELaNa IV Air Force ORS-3 launch
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ElanaIV_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ElanaIV-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
