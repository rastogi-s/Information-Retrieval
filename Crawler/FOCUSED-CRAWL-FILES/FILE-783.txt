      <p>
       <b>
        Metis
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Μήτις
       </span>
       ), also known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter XVI
        </span>
       </b>
       , is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter%27s_inner_moons" title="Jupiter's inner moons">
        innermost
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_of_Jupiter" title="Moon of Jupiter">
        moon of Jupiter
       </a>
       . It was discovered in 1979 in images taken by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       , and was named in 1983 after the first wife of Zeus,
       <a href="/wiki/Metis_(mythology)" title="Metis (mythology)">
        Metis
       </a>
       . Additional observations made between early 1996 and September 2003 by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_spacecraft" title="Galileo spacecraft">
        <i>
         Galileo
        </i>
        spacecraft
       </a>
       allowed its surface to be imaged.
      </p>
      <p>
       Metis is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidally_locked" title="Tidally locked">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       , and its shape is strongly asymmetrical, with one of the diameters being almost twice as large as the smallest one. It is also one of the two moons known to orbit Jupiter in less than the length of Jupiter's day, the other being
       <a href="/wiki/Adrastea_(moon)" title="Adrastea (moon)">
        Adrastea
       </a>
       . It orbits within the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter#Main_ring" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        main ring of Jupiter
       </a>
       , and is thought to be a major contributor of material to the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        rings
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_observations">
        Discovery and observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metis_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Metis was discovered in 1979 by
       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_P._Synnott" title="Stephen P. Synnott">
        Stephen P. Synnott
       </a>
       in images taken by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       probe and was
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provisional_designation" title="Provisional designation">
        provisionally designated
       </a>
       as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/1979 J 3
        </span>
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3507_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3507-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESynnott1981_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESynnott1981-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1983 it was officially named after the mythological
       <a href="/wiki/Metis_(mythology)" title="Metis (mythology)">
        Metis
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">
        Titaness
       </a>
       who was the first wife of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek equivalent
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The photographs taken by
       <i>
        Voyager 1
       </i>
       showed Metis only as a dot, and hence knowledge about Metis was very limited until the arrival of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_spacecraft" title="Galileo spacecraft">
        <i>
         Galileo
        </i>
        spacecraft
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        Galileo
       </i>
       imaged almost all of the surface of Metis and put constraints on its composition by 1998.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the
       <a href="/wiki/Juno_(spacecraft)" title="Juno (spacecraft)">
        Juno
       </a>
       orbiter, which arrived at Jupiter in 2016, has a camera called
       <a href="/wiki/JunoCam" title="JunoCam">
        JunoCam
       </a>
       , it is almost entirely focused on observations of Jupiter itself. However, if all goes well it should be able to capture some limited images of the innermost moons Metis and
       <a href="/wiki/Adrastea_(moon)" title="Adrastea (moon)">
        Adrastea
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metis_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Metis has an irregular shape and measures 60×40×34 km across, which makes it the second smallest of the four
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        inner satellites of Jupiter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Therefore, a very rough estimate of its surface area could be placed between 5,800 and 11,600 square kilometers (approx. 8,700). The bulk composition and mass of Metis are not known, but assuming that its mean density is like that of
       <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(moon)" title="Amalthea (moon)">
        Amalthea
       </a>
       (~0.86 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       its mass can be estimated as ~3.6×10
       <sup>
        16
       </sup>
       kg. This density would imply that it is composed of water ice with a
       <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">
        porosity
       </a>
       of 10–15%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The surface of Metis is heavily cratered, dark, and appears to be reddish in color. There is a substantial asymmetry between leading and trailing
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        hemispheres
       </a>
       : the leading hemisphere is 1.3 times brighter than the trailing one. The asymmetry is probably caused by the higher velocity and frequency of impacts on the leading hemisphere, which excavate a bright material (presumably ice) from its interior.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimonelli_Rossier_et_al.2000_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimonelli_Rossier_et_al.2000-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit_and_rotation">
        Orbit and rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metis_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbit and rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Metis is the innermost of Jupiter's four small
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        inner moons
       </a>
       . It orbits Jupiter at a distance of ~128,000 km (1.79 Jupiter radii) within Jupiter's
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter#Main_ring" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        main ring
       </a>
       . Metis's orbit has very small
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       (~0.0002) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       (~ 0.06°) relative to the equator of Jupiter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEvans_Porco_et_al.2002_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEvans_Porco_et_al.2002-2">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal locking
       </a>
       , Metis rotates synchronously with its orbital period, with its longest axis aligned towards Jupiter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_3-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Metis lies inside Jupiter's
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous orbit
       </a>
       radius (as does
       <a href="/wiki/Adrastea_(moon)" title="Adrastea (moon)">
        Adrastea
       </a>
       ), and as a result,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_deceleration" title="Tidal deceleration">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       slowly cause its orbit to decay. If its density is similar to Amalthea's, Metis's orbit lies within the fluid
       <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">
        Roche limit
       </a>
       ; however, because it has not broken up, it must lie outside its rigid Roche limit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_3-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Metis is the fastest-moving of Jupiter's moons.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       It orbits Jupiter at 31.5 km/s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calculated_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-calculated-1">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Relationship_with_Jupiter.27s_rings">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Jupiter's_rings">
        Relationship with Jupiter's rings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metis_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relationship with Jupiter's rings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Metis's orbit lies ~1000 km within the main ring of Jupiter. It orbits within a ~500 km wide "gap" or "notch" in the ring.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_3-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOckert-Bell_Burns_et_al.1999_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOckert-Bell_Burns_et_al.1999-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The gap is clearly somehow related to the moon but the origin of this connection has not been established. Metis supplies a significant part of the main ring's dust.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Showalter_et_al.1999_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Showalter_et_al.1999-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This material appears to consist primarily of material that is ejected from the surfaces of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        Jupiter's four small inner satellites
       </a>
       by meteorite impacts. It is easy for the impact ejecta to be lost from the satellites into space because the satellites' surfaces lie fairly close to the edge of their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roche_sphere" title="Roche sphere">
        Roche spheres
       </a>
       due to their low density.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_3-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
