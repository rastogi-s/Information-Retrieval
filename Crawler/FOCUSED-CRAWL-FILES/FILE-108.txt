      <p>
       <b>
        Copernicus
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_craters" title="Lunar craters">
        lunar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       named after the astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">
        Nicolaus Copernicus
       </a>
       , located in eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Oceanus Procellarum
       </a>
       . It typifies craters that formed during the
       <a href="/wiki/Copernican_period" title="Copernican period">
        Copernican period
       </a>
       in that it has a prominent
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_system" title="Ray system">
        ray system
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Copernicus_(lunar_crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Location_of_lunar_crater_copernicus.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="150" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7b/Location_of_lunar_crater_copernicus.jpg" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Location_of_lunar_crater_copernicus.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Location of Copernicus
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Copernicus is visible using
       <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">
        binoculars
       </a>
       , and is located slightly northwest of the center of the Moon's Earth-facing hemisphere. South of the crater is the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Insularum" title="Mare Insularum">
        Mare Insularum
       </a>
       , and to the south-south west is the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Reinhold_(crater)" title="Reinhold (crater)">
        Reinhold
       </a>
       . North of Copernicus are the
       <a href="/wiki/Montes_Carpatus" title="Montes Carpatus">
        Montes Carpatus
       </a>
       , which lie at the south edge of
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Imbrium" title="Mare Imbrium">
        Mare Imbrium
       </a>
       . West of Copernicus is a group of dispersed lunar hills. Due to its relative youth, the crater has remained in a relatively pristine shape since it formed.
      </p>
      <p>
       The circular rim has a discernible hexagonal form, with a
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/terrace" title="wiktionary:terrace">
        terraced
       </a>
       inner wall and a 30 km wide, sloping
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rampart" title="wikt:rampart">
        rampart
       </a>
       that descends nearly a kilometer to the surrounding
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        mare
       </a>
       . There are three distinct terraces visible, and arc-shaped
       <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">
        landslides
       </a>
       due to slumping of the inner wall as the crater debris subsided.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most likely due to its recent formation, the crater floor has not been flooded by
       <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
        lava
       </a>
       . The terrain along the bottom is hilly in the southern half while the north is relatively smooth. The central peaks consist of three isolated mountainous rises climbing as high as 1.2 km above the floor. These peaks are separated from each other by valleys, and they form a rough line along an east-west axis. Infrared observations of these peaks during the 1980s determined that they were primarily composed of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">
        mafic
       </a>
       form of
       <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">
        olivine
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Copernicus.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="627" data-file-width="463" height="406" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Copernicus.gif/300px-Copernicus.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Copernicus.gif/450px-Copernicus.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/Copernicus.gif 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Copernicus.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         "Picture of the Century" – oblique view of the interior of Copernicus from
         <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_2" title="Lunar Orbiter 2">
          Lunar Orbiter 2
         </a>
         , which orbited the moon from 1966 to 1967.
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
           NASA
          </a>
          photo.
         </i>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Based on high-resolution images from
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_5" title="Lunar Orbiter 5">
        Lunar Orbiter 5
       </a>
       , Terry W. Offield of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Geological_Survey" title="U.S. Geological Survey">
        U.S. Geological Survey
       </a>
       described the crater as having,
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         ...a hummocky crater rim, numerous large slump blocks on the crater wall, and a complex of central peaks. Sets of parallel
         <a href="/wiki/Fracture" title="Fracture">
          fractures
         </a>
         , aligned with the lunar structure grid, formed after the crater wall took its present form, but before the smoothest floor materials were emplaced. The smooth floor materials show a swirling pattern of cracks like those seen on terrestrial
         <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
          lava
         </a>
         flows. These materials are associated with numerous hills that have summit craters and are probably small
         <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
          volcanoes
         </a>
         . Several low places on the rim and wall are partly filled by what appears to be ponded volcanic material, or possibly fluidized impact debris.
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_system" title="Ray system">
        crater rays
       </a>
       spread as far as 800 kilometers across the surrounding
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_maria" title="Lunar maria">
        mare
       </a>
       , overlying rays from the craters
       <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_(crater)" title="Aristarchus (crater)">
        Aristarchus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(lunar_crater)" title="Kepler (lunar crater)">
        Kepler
       </a>
       . The rays are less distinct than the long, linear rays extending from
       <a href="/wiki/Tycho_(lunar_crater)" title="Tycho (lunar crater)">
        Tycho
       </a>
       , instead forming a nebulous pattern with plumy markings. In multiple locations the rays lie at glancing angles, instead of forming a true radial dispersal. An extensive pattern of smaller secondary craters can also be observed surrounding Copernicus, a detail that was depicted in a map by
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Domenico_Cassini" title="Giovanni Domenico Cassini">
        Giovanni Cassini
       </a>
       in 1680. Some of these secondary craters form sinuous chains in the ejecta.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1966 the crater was photographed from an oblique angle by
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_2" title="Lunar Orbiter 2">
        Lunar Orbiter 2
       </a>
       as one of 12 "housekeeping" pictures that were taken to advance the roll of film between possible astronaut landing sites being surveyed. At the time this detailed image of the lunar surface was termed by NASA scientist Martin Swetnick and subsequently quoted by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
         Time
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine as "one of the great pictures of the century."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        Apollo 12
       </a>
       mission landed south of Copernicus on mare basalts of
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Oceanus Procellarum
       </a>
       that were believed to have been in the path of one of the crater's rays, and scientists hoped
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">
        cosmic ray
       </a>
       exposure ages of soil samples would help constrain the crater age. The results were inconclusive, but not inconsistent with the estimated 800 million year age of crater formation. Copernicus itself was a possible landing site for the canceled
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_20" title="Apollo 20">
        Apollo 20
       </a>
       mission.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Names">
        Names
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Copernicus_(lunar_crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:424px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Space_Telescope_Science_Institute_-_Crater_Copernicus_(pd).jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="754" data-file-width="1125" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Space_Telescope_Science_Institute_-_Crater_Copernicus_%28pd%29.jpg/422px-Space_Telescope_Science_Institute_-_Crater_Copernicus_%28pd%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Space_Telescope_Science_Institute_-_Crater_Copernicus_%28pd%29.jpg/633px-Space_Telescope_Science_Institute_-_Crater_Copernicus_%28pd%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Space_Telescope_Science_Institute_-_Crater_Copernicus_%28pd%29.jpg/844px-Space_Telescope_Science_Institute_-_Crater_Copernicus_%28pd%29.jpg 2x" width="422"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Space_Telescope_Science_Institute_-_Crater_Copernicus_(pd).jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Hubble views of Copernicus crater (
         <i>
          inset
         </i>
         ), showing its location on the near side
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Copernicus is named after the astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">
        Nicolaus Copernicus
       </a>
       . Like many of the craters on the Moon's near side, it was given its name by
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Riccioli" title="Giovanni Battista Riccioli">
        Giovanni Riccioli
       </a>
       , whose 1651 nomenclature system has become standardized.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Riccioli awarded Copernicus a prominent crater despite the fact that, as an
       <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">
        Italian
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">
        Jesuit
       </a>
       , he conformed with church doctrine in publicly opposing Copernicus's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliocentric" title="Heliocentric">
        heliocentric
       </a>
       system. Riccioli justified the name by noting that he had symbolically thrown all the heliocentrist astronomers into the
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Ocean of Storms
       </a>
       . However, astronomical historian Ewan Whitaker suspects that the prominence of Copernicus crater is a sign that Riccioli secretly supported the heliocentric system and was ensuring that Nicolaus Copernicus would receive a worthy legacy for future generations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Earlier lunar cartographers had given the feature different names.
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Gassendi" title="Pierre Gassendi">
        Pierre Gassendi
       </a>
       named it Carthusia after the
       <a href="/wiki/Chartreuse_Mountains" title="Chartreuse Mountains">
        Chartreuse Mountains
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_van_Langren" title="Michael van Langren">
        Michael van Langren
       </a>
       's 1645 map calls it "Phillipi IV" after
       <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">
        Philip IV of Spain
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       And
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Hevelius" title="Johannes Hevelius">
        Johannes Hevelius
       </a>
       named it 'Etna M.' after
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Etna" title="Mount Etna">
        Mount Etna
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Later the crater was nicknamed "the Monarch of the Moon" by lunar cartographer
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gwyn_Elger" title="Thomas Gwyn Elger">
        Thomas Gwyn Elger
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Copernicus H, a typical "dark-halo" crater, was a target of observation by
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_5" title="Lunar Orbiter 5">
        Lunar Orbiter 5
       </a>
       in 1967. Dark-halo craters were once believed to be volcanic in origin rather than the result of impacts. The Orbiter image showed that the crater had blocks of ejecta like other craters of similar size, indicating an impact origin. The halo results from excavation of darker material (
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        mare
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       ) at depth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
