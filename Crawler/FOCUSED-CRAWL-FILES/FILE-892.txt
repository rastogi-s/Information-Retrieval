    Shepherd moon
   </h1>

         <b>
          Operation of a shepherd moon
         </b>
         – particles are located in front or behind the Moon in its orbit, so these are either accelerated in the direction of the moon and thrown to the outside, or they are slowed down on their path and pulled inwards.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        shepherd moon
       </b>
       (also herder moon, watcher moon) is a small
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       that clears a gap in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">
        planetary-ring
       </a>
       material or keeps particles within a ring contained. The name is a result of the fact they limit the "herd" of the ring particles as a shepherd.
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to their
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravitational
       </a>
       effect, they pick up particles and deflect them from their original orbits through
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       . This causes gaps in the ring system, such as the particularly striking
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini_Division" title="Cassini Division">
        Cassini Division
       </a>
       , as well as other characteristic bands, or strange "twisted" deformation of rings.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Jupiter">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Several of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       's small innermost moons, namely
       <a href="/wiki/Metis_(moon)" title="Metis (moon)">
        Metis
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Adrastea_(moon)" title="Adrastea (moon)">
        Adrastea
       </a>
       , are within Jupiter's
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        ring system
       </a>
       and are also within Jupiter's
       <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">
        Roche limit
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is possible that these rings are composed of material that is being pulled off these two bodies by Jupiter's
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       , possibly facilitated by impacts of ring material on their surfaces.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Saturn">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The complex
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn" title="Rings of Saturn">
        ring system of
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       has several such satellites. These include
       <a href="/wiki/Prometheus_(moon)" title="Prometheus (moon)">
        Prometheus
       </a>
       (F ring),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Daphnis_(moon)" title="Daphnis (moon)">
        Daphnis
       </a>
       (Keeler Gap),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Pan_(moon)" title="Pan (moon)">
        Pan
       </a>
       (Encke Gap),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Janus_(moon)" title="Janus (moon)">
        Janus
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Epimetheus_(moon)" title="Epimetheus (moon)">
        Epimetheus
       </a>
       (both A ring).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uranus">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       also has shepherd moons on its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epsilon_ring" title="Epsilon ring">
        ε ring
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cordelia_(moon)" title="Cordelia (moon)">
        Cordelia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ophelia_(moon)" title="Ophelia (moon)">
        Ophelia
       </a>
       . They are interior and exterior shepherds, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Both moons are well within Uranus's synchronous orbit radius, and their orbits are therefore slowly decaying due to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_deceleration" title="Tidal deceleration">
        tidal deceleration
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Neptune">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Neptune's rings are very unusual in that they first appeared to be composed of incomplete arcs in Earth-based observations, but
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
        Voyager 2
       </a>
       's images showed them to be complete rings with bright clumps.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is thought
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaloHanninen1998_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SaloHanninen1998-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that the gravitational influence of the shepherd moon
       <a href="/wiki/Galatea_(moon)" title="Galatea (moon)">
        Galatea
       </a>
       and possibly other as-yet undiscovered shepherd moons are responsible for this clumpiness.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_planets">
        Minor planets
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherd_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Minor planets">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Rings around some
       <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(minor_planet)" title="Centaur (minor planet)">
        centaurs
       </a>
       have been identified.
       <a href="/wiki/10199_Chariklo" title="10199 Chariklo">
        Chariklo's
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Chariklo" title="Rings of Chariklo">
        rings
       </a>
       are remarkably well-defined and are suspected to either be very young or kept in place by a shepherd moon similar in mass to the rings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiron_(minor_planet)" title="Chiron (minor planet)">
        Chiron
       </a>
       is also thought to have rings similar in form to those of Chariklo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
