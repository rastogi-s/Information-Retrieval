      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Dark Side of the Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       is a French
       <a href="/wiki/Mockumentary" title="Mockumentary">
        mockumentary
       </a>
       by director
       <a href="/wiki/William_Karel" title="William Karel">
        William Karel
       </a>
       which originally aired on
       <a href="/wiki/Arte" title="Arte">
        Arte
       </a>
       in 2002 with the title
       <i>
        <b>
         Opération Lune
        </b>
       </i>
       . The basic premise for the film is the theory that the television footage from the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       Moon landing was
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing_conspiracy_theories" title="Moon landing conspiracy theories">
        faked and recorded in a studio
       </a>
       by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">
        CIA
       </a>
       with help from director
       <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">
        Stanley Kubrick
       </a>
       . It features some surprising guest appearances, most notably by
       <a href="/wiki/Donald_Rumsfeld" title="Donald Rumsfeld">
        Donald Rumsfeld
       </a>
       , Dr.
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">
        Henry Kissinger
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Haig" title="Alexander Haig">
        Alexander Haig
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernon_Walters" title="Vernon Walters">
        Vernon Walters
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
        Buzz Aldrin
       </a>
       and Stanley Kubrick's widow,
       <a href="/wiki/Christiane_Kubrick" title="Christiane Kubrick">
        Christiane Kubrick
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
        Plot
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The first part tells in an apparently neutral way the inception of the
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's lunar program, emphasizing the issues related to its funding and the necessary public support to the program. NASA regards Stanley Kubrick's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey" title="2001: A Space Odyssey">
         2001: A Space Odyssey
        </a>
       </i>
       as the prototype of the show that the space program needs to be in order to gain this support, leading them to design the spacesuits and vessels in a "Hollywood" fashion and even to hire 700 Hollywood technicians, making all of Hollywood stop working on other projects. But the outcome of
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       is disappointing: although the landing is successful,
       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>
       makes a fool of himself and not a single shot of the moonwalk is usable.
      </p>
      <p>
       The incapability of shooting images on the moon had been anticipated by
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">
        Richard Nixon
       </a>
       and his staff who decided to fake the pictures on the moon, using the set of
       <i>
        2001
       </i>
       that was still available in
       <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
        London
       </a>
       . Kubrick had refused, then accepted and finally directed the fake footage himself, appalled by the lack of skills of the CIA crew. The
       <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">
        KGB
       </a>
       soon realized that "the whole thing was a hoax", that Apollo 11 had indeed landed but it was physically impossible to make pictures in the lunar environment. Besides, they had found a photo showing a portrait of Kubrick lying on the false lunar soil in the studio.
      </p>
      <p>
       After the success of the fake footage, Nixon gets scared that the truth might be discovered, and in a drunken state asks CIA Colonel
       <a href="/wiki/North_by_Northwest" title="North by Northwest">
        George Kaplan
       </a>
       to dispose of the whole film crew. The next morning he tries to cancel the order, but it is too late: meanwhile Kaplan has gone mad, sent his killers and disappeared. The death squad goes to
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
        Vietnam
       </a>
       where the film crew has sought refuge, but is immediately caught by the villagers: despite a perfect accent and disguise, their commanding officer was
       <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">
        black
       </a>
       . Nixon reacts by sending 150,000 men and a half of the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Sixth_Fleet" title="United States Sixth Fleet">
        6th Fleet
       </a>
       to find and kill the four members of the crew. They fail, and the CIA takes over and assassinates all but one of them, who takes shelter in a
       <a href="/wiki/Yeshiva" title="Yeshiva">
        yeshiva
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">
        Brooklyn
       </a>
       where he dies ten years later. Only Kubrick is spared.
      </p>
      <p>
       Five years after Apollo 11, Kubrick calls NASA to borrow the top-secret wide-aperture
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_AG" title="Carl Zeiss AG">
        Zeiss
       </a>
       lens he needs to make
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Barry_Lyndon" title="Barry Lyndon">
         Barry Lyndon
        </a>
       </i>
       . As a result, Nixon's
       <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">
        successor
       </a>
       decides to get rid of him as the last witness of the conspiracy. Informed of the threat to his life, Kubrick locks himself up in his home and never leaves it until his death.
      </p>
      <p>
       As a conclusion, General
       <a href="/wiki/Vernon_A._Walters" title="Vernon A. Walters">
        Vernon Walters
       </a>
       accepts to reveal the secret of Kubrick's demise, but unexpectedly dies the next evening: he has accepted to break the CIA rule of silence and anonymity.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is finally revealed that this is a mockumentary as the end credits roll over a montage of
       <a href="/wiki/Blooper" title="Blooper">
        blooper
       </a>
       reels, with the main participants laughing over their lines or over their inability to remember them.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
        Production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
        Development
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       William Karel had just completed
       <i>
        Hollywood
       </i>
       , a film based on lying, when he and the documentary unit of Arte had the idea of making a mockumentary, to play with the serious tonality of Arte but also for pleasure, and to make a funny film based on the idea that one must not believe everything that one is told, that witnesses can lie, archives can be tricked, and that any subject can be twisted by misleading subtitling or dubbing. They looked for a topic that would be universal and historical but not sensitive like a war. Hence the choice of the Moon landing, which had for more than 30 years been a topic of debate regarding the reality of these images. They found it was a fairly funny topic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itv_arte_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-itv_arte-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Karel took care to avoid any conspiracist tonality. In particular, at no point is it said that Armstrong did not walk on the moon. The script just hypothesizes that the US might have wanted a contingency plan in case the first steps could not be filmed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itv_arte_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-itv_arte-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The arguments regarding the impossibility to operate a camera on the moon were found in websites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cerimes_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cerimes-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Since it was impossible to know at what point of the film the spectators would start having doubts, parodic bloopers were added in the end credits to make the hoax obvious, in case someone would believe it until the end.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itv_arte_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-itv_arte-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Elements">
        Elements
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Elements">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The film uses four main types of elements:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itv_arte_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-itv_arte-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cerimes_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-cerimes-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Karel interviewed Kubrick's widow,
        <a href="/wiki/Christiane_Kubrick" title="Christiane Kubrick">
         Christiane Kubrick
        </a>
        , and his brother-in-law,
        <a href="/wiki/Jan_Harlan" title="Jan Harlan">
         Jan Harlan
        </a>
        , pretending he was making a documentary on Kubrick, his film
        <i>
         2001: A Space Odyssey
        </i>
        , the Moon or NASA, and asking intentionally vague questions. Karel also interviewed
        <a href="/wiki/Vernon_A._Walters" title="Vernon A. Walters">
         Vernon Walters
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
         Buzz Aldrin
        </a>
        , his wife and some NASA personnel, under the same pretext of making a bona fide documentary.
       </li>
       <li>
        He used footage of staff of President Richard Nixon, including
        <a href="/wiki/Donald_Rumsfeld" title="Donald Rumsfeld">
         Rumsfeld
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">
         Kissinger
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Haig" title="Alexander Haig">
         Haig
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Eagleburger" title="Lawrence Eagleburger">
         Lawrence Eagleburger
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Director_of_the_Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Director of the Central Intelligence Agency">
         CIA director
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Richard_Helms" title="Richard Helms">
         Richard Helms
        </a>
        , recycled from his earlier documentary
        <i>
         Les hommes de la Maison Blanche
        </i>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-les_hommes_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-les_hommes-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and edited in order to twist their words.
       </li>
       <li>
        Other "witnesses" are played by actors to make the connections between the sentences pronounced by "real witnesses" and make a credible story. Among many giveaways (mainly in the second half) that the entire film is a hoax in jest, these "staging" witnesses are named after characters in Kubrick and Hitchcock films, for instance an astronaut named "David Bowman" (
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(film)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (film)">
          2001: A Space Odyssey (film)
         </a>
        </i>
        ), a film producer named "Jack Torrance" (
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Shining_(film)" title="The Shining (film)">
          The Shining
         </a>
        </i>
        ) or Nixon's secretary called "Eve Kendall" (
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/North_by_Northwest" title="North by Northwest">
          North by Northwest
         </a>
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Other elements appearing in the second half are "borrowed" from various documentaries that are mentioned in the end credits. These are the only parts of the film in which deceptive subtitling is used:
        <i>
         Australie, la route de Tanami
        </i>
        (Arnaud Mansir, Hervé Rébillon, 1999),
        <i>
         L'archipel aux savants
        </i>
        (Laurence Graffin, 1997),
        <i>
         Philippine : la vallée des rizières éternelles
        </i>
        (Patrick Boitet, Jacques Massart, 1994),
        <i>
         Cambodge : Païlin, le refuge des criminels
        </i>
        (Hubert Dubois, 1999),
        <i>
         Chine : union furtive
        </i>
        (Fang Wui Wang, 2000),
        <i>
         Laos : les montagnards de l'opium
        </i>
        (Eric Pierrot, 1997).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Special_effects">
        Special effects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Special effects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The photo showing a portrait of Kubrick on the lunar soil is the only image in which a
       <a href="/wiki/Special_effect" title="Special effect">
        special effect
       </a>
       was used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cerimes_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-cerimes-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Giveaways">
        Giveaways
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Giveaways">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In addition to the increasingly incredible claims made as the film progresses, several factual errors of note are introduced by the narrator:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">
         John F. Kennedy
        </a>
        ’s "
        <a href="/wiki/We_choose_to_go_to_the_Moon" title="We choose to go to the Moon">
         We choose to go to the moon
        </a>
        " speech was in 1962,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennedy_speech_4-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-kennedy_speech-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        not 1961 as was claimed.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Luna_9" title="Luna 9">
         Luna 9
        </a>
        landed on the moon in February 1966,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-luna9_5-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-luna9-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        but the narrator states it was in January.
       </li>
       <li>
        The narrator states that Apollo 11 was launched on 17 July 1969, when in fact it was launched the day before on July 16.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apollo11_overview_6-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-apollo11_overview-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">
         Korolev
        </a>
        died following surgery to remove a polyp from his intestines,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-korolev_bio_7-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-korolev_bio-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        not from a tonsillectomy as is claimed.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">
         Lyndon Johnson
        </a>
        is said to have been the
        <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Texas" title="Governor of Texas">
         Governor of Texas
        </a>
        – an office he never held.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-texas_governors_8-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-texas_governors-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Likewise, Richard Nixon is erroneously stated as having once been the
        <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_California" title="Governor of California">
         Governor of California
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-california_governors_9-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-california_governors-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The narrator implies the
        <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">
         Cape
        </a>
        was selected in part due to the
        <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">
         George Bush
        </a>
        family influence in
        <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">
         Florida
        </a>
        , yet no Bush had any connection with Florida until 1980 when
        <a href="/wiki/Jeb_Bush" title="Jeb Bush">
         Jeb Bush
        </a>
        moved his family there.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeb_bush_10-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-jeb_bush-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The Cape, however, had become the new missile test facility by 1950.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cape_history_11-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-cape_history-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The premiere of
        <i>
         2001: A Space Odyssey
        </i>
        was in April 1968
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2001_premiere_12-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-2001_premiere-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and Richard Nixon was inaugurated as president in January 1969, so the latter could not possibly take advantage of the filming of
        <i>
         2001
        </i>
        as the narrator says.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Farouk_El-Baz" title="Farouk El-Baz">
         Farouk El-Baz
        </a>
        is presented as NASA technical director, a position he never held.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-el_baz_13-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-el_baz-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Most of the fictitious witnesses are named after characters from movies directed by Stanley Kubrick or
       <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">
        Alfred Hitchcock
       </a>
       . "W.A. Koenigsberg" is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Woody_Allen" title="Woody Allen">
        Woody Allen
       </a>
       , who was born "Allan Koenigsberg". They are listed in the credits along with the names of the actors portraying them.
      </p>
      <p>
       The film crew member who takes shelter in a yeshiva in Brooklyn is said to be an "acidic" Jew. In the original French version the giveaway is more obvious: it is said that "he didn't work any more, he checked in with the
       <a href="/wiki/Hasidic_Judaism" title="Hasidic Judaism">
        Hasidic
       </a>
       ", ("Il ne travaillait plus, il pointait aux Hassidiques", which does not make sense in French but is an obvious pun between a branch of Judaism and
       <a href="/wiki/ASSEDIC" title="ASSEDIC">
        ASSEDIC
       </a>
       which was the French employment office at the time the film was made).
      </p>
      <p>
       The soundtrack also includes the song "The American Dream" from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wag_the_Dog" title="Wag the Dog">
         Wag the Dog
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Barry_Levinson" title="Barry Levinson">
        Barry Levinson
       </a>
       , a fiction feature about a secretly government-commissioned Hollywood production of a fake war. At one point, footage featuring the military boarding a plane is underscored by the "right, left, right, left" of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunnery_Sergeant" title="Gunnery Sergeant">
        Gunnery Sergeant
       </a>
       Hartman (
       <a href="/wiki/R._Lee_Ermey" title="R. Lee Ermey">
        R. Lee Ermey
       </a>
       ) from Kubrick's own film,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Full_Metal_Jacket" title="Full Metal Jacket">
         Full Metal Jacket
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The most obvious giveaway appears in the closing credits with
       <a href="/wiki/Blooper" title="Blooper">
        blooper
       </a>
       reels showing the characters, real and fictitious alike, laughing over their lines or over their inability to remember them. But there is a final trick here, a "hoax within a hoax": the real characters are shown joking, giving the false impression that they are voluntarily part of the hoax.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itv_arte_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-itv_arte-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In fact at least one of these "jokes" - Kissinger saying that he would do it all over again - appears in
       <i>
        Les hommes de la Maison Blanche
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-les_hommes_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-les_hommes-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       where he turns out to be actually talking quite seriously about the war in Vietnam.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fictitious_witnesses">
        Fictitious witnesses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The names of the fictitious winesses and of the actors impersonating them appear in the end credits. Moreover, Arte (co-producer and original broadcaster of the film) accompanied the airing in France by a web site featuring a quiz
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arte_jeu_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-arte_jeu-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in which the player was led to guess in which films the characters having those names originally appeared. The following list reproduces the correct answers of the quiz.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Jack_Torrance" title="Jack Torrance">
         Jack Torrance
        </a>
        is a fictional character in Kubrick’s
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Shining_(film)" title="The Shining (film)">
          The Shining
         </a>
        </i>
        , not a Hollywood Producer, and is played by David Winger.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Bowman_(Space_Odyssey)" title="David Bowman (Space Odyssey)">
         David Bowman
        </a>
        is a fictional character in Kubrick’s
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(film)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (film)">
          2001: A Space Odyssey (film)
         </a>
        </i>
        , not a real astronaut, and is played by Tad Brown.
       </li>
       <li>
        Maria Vargas (lead character in
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Barefoot_Contessa" title="The Barefoot Contessa">
          The Barefoot Contessa
         </a>
        </i>
        ) is played by Jacquelyn Toman and is not Buzz Aldrin’s sister.
       </li>
       <li>
        Eve Kendall is a fictional character in Alfred Hitchcock’s
        <i>
         North by Northwest
        </i>
        and was not Nixon’s secretary (that woman's name was Rose Marie Woods), and is played by Barbara Rogers.
       </li>
       <li>
        Dimitri Muffley is a play on the names from Kubrick’s
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Dr._Strangelove" title="Dr. Strangelove">
          Dr. Strangelove
         </a>
        </i>
        (Soviet Premier Dimitri Kisov and American President Merkin Muffley) and is not a “former KGB agent.” He is played by Bernard Kirschoff.
       </li>
       <li>
        Ambrose Chapel is the name of a place in Hitchcock’s
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Knew_Too_Much_(1956_film)" title="The Man Who Knew Too Much (1956 film)">
          The Man Who Knew Too Much
         </a>
        </i>
        , not an “ex-CIA agent”, and is played by John Rogers.
       </li>
       <li>
        George Kaplan (mentioned by narrator) is a fictional character within a fictional character in the Hitchcock film,
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_By_Northwest" title="North By Northwest">
          North By Northwest
         </a>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        W. A. Koenigsberg ("W. A." stands for "
        <a href="/wiki/Woody_Allen" title="Woody Allen">
         Woody Allen
        </a>
        ", as Koenigsberg is Woody Allen’s true name) is played by Binem Oreg.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
        Reception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Arte aired the film for the first time on 16 October 2002, and a second time on 1 April 2004, followed by a debate and accompanied by a web site
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       including an interview of William Karel
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itv_arte_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-itv_arte-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and a quiz demonstrating some of the giveaways including the fake witnesses and out-of-context quotes from interviews.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arte_jeu_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-arte_jeu-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the press screening before the first airing, some people who had missed the beginning left the screening room infuriated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cerimes_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-cerimes-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Australian broadcaster
       <a href="/wiki/Special_Broadcasting_Service" title="Special Broadcasting Service">
        SBS television
       </a>
       aired the film on 1 April 2003 as an
       <a href="/wiki/April_Fools%27_Day" title="April Fools' Day">
        April fools' joke
       </a>
       , on 17 November 2008 as part of Kubrick week, and again on 27 July 2009. About ten television channels worldwide aired the film on 1 April 2004, and
       <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">
        Channel 4
       </a>
       aired it in September the same year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cerimes_2-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-cerimes-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The producer proposed the film to the
       <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
        BBC
       </a>
       , who liked it but rejected it because they perceived it as being part of an "anti-American campaign" in French documentaries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itv_arte_1-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-itv_arte-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_and_moon_landing_conspiracy_theories">
        <i>
         Dark Side of the Moon
        </i>
        and moon landing conspiracy theories
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Dark Side of the Moon and moon landing conspiracy theories">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When the film was shown to a group of sociology students studying conspiracy theories, many mistakenly believed that this was a sincere and serious film.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Furthermore, moon-landing hoax advocate Wayne Green cited the film as evidence for his views, apparently believing the out of context footage of Nixon staff was really about a moon landing hoax, as discussed on Jay Windley's "clavius.org" site defending the reality of the moon landings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Extracts ranging from a few minutes
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to the whole film stripped of the credits and key sequences
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       have been posted on YouTube by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_moon_landing_hoax_accusations" title="Apollo moon landing hoax accusations">
        conspiracy theorists
       </a>
       as "proof" that, either the images on the moon are fake, or the moon landing itself never happened. Karel himself received e-mails congratulating him for "exposing the moon landing hoax", which amused him.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cerimes_2-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-cerimes-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">
        Influences
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Influences">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       William Karel indicated that he found inspiration in
       <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">
        Orson Welles
       </a>
       ' radio broadcast
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_War_of_the_Worlds_(radio_drama)" title="The War of the Worlds (radio drama)">
         The War of the Worlds
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Capricorn_One" title="Capricorn One">
         Capricorn One
        </a>
       </i>
       , and the docudramas by
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Watkins" title="Peter Watkins">
        Peter Watkins
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cerimes_2-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-cerimes-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He refers to
       <i>
        Dark side of the moon
       </i>
       as a "documenteur", a French
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portmanteau_word" title="Portmanteau word">
        portmanteau word
       </a>
       meaning "liar documentary" (from
       <i>
        documentaire
       </i>
       "documentary" +
       <i>
        menteur
       </i>
       "liar"), after the title of a film made by
       <a href="/wiki/Agn%C3%A8s_Varda" title="Agnès Varda">
        Agnès Varda
       </a>
       in 1980-1981.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itv_arte_1-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-itv_arte-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other influences are the staging of well-known historical events for the camera, such as the raising of the flags at Iwo-Jima and at the Reichstag, the American landing in Somalia which was remade a couple of times for the cameras, examplifying the influence of cinema on news coverage.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itv_arte_1-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-itv_arte-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="The_Zeiss_1:0.7_.2F_50_mm_lens_story">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Zeiss_1:0.7_/_50_mm_lens_story">
        The Zeiss 1:0.7 / 50 mm lens story
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The Zeiss 1:0.7 / 50 mm lens story">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The starting point of the film is the alleged loan of a unique, secret lens by NASA to Kubrick. The truly rare, but not unique and definitely not secret,
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_Planar_50mm_f/0.7" title="Carl Zeiss Planar 50mm f/0.7">
        Carl Zeiss Planar 50mm f/0.7
       </a>
       lens was designed by
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_AG" title="Carl Zeiss AG">
        Zeiss
       </a>
       in 1966,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       on special request for NASA for the Apollo program,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:12-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and a batch of ten were made. The cinematographer of
       <i>
        Barry Lyndon
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/John_Alcott" title="John Alcott">
        John Alcott
       </a>
       , said that Kubrick bought three left over lenses from this batch,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and had them extensively modified to make them usable on his 35 mm movie camera.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_24-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:12-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Six other lenses of the batch had been sold to NASA, and the tenth one is at the German Movie Museum in Frankfurt.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is no evidence that NASA ever lent one of their own lenses to Kubrick.
      </p>
