      <p>
       <b>
        Actaea
       </b>
       , officially
       <b>
        (120347)
       </b>
       <b>
        Salacia I Actaea
       </b>
       , is the single known
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper belt object">
        classical Kuiper belt object
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/120347_Salacia" title="120347 Salacia">
        120347 Salacia
       </a>
       . Its diameter is estimated 300 km, which is ⅓ of the diameter of Salacia; thus, Salacia and Actaea are viewed by some to be a
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_asteroid" title="Binary asteroid">
        binary object
       </a>
       . Given that the following size estimates are correct, Actaea is about the sixth-biggest known moon of a trans-Neptunian object, after
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       (1212 km),
       <a href="/wiki/Dysnomia_(moon)" title="Dysnomia (moon)">
        Dysnomia
       </a>
       (685 km),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanth_(moon)" title="Vanth (moon)">
        Vanth
       </a>
       (378 km),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ilmar%C3%AB_(moon)" title="Ilmarë (moon)">
        Ilmarë
       </a>
       (361 km) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiiaka_(moon)" title="Hiiaka (moon)">
        Hiiaka
       </a>
       (320 km).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_name">
        Discovery and name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actaea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       It was discovered on 21 July 2006 by Keith S. Noll,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harold_Levison" title="Harold Levison">
        Harold Levison
       </a>
       , Denise Stephens and Will Grundy with the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8751_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC8751-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 18 February 2011, it was officially named Actaea after the
       <a href="/wiki/Nereid" title="Nereid">
        nereid
       </a>
       Aktaia.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actaea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Actaea orbits its primary every
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005474664320000000♠
        </span>
        5.493
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         80
        </span>
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.000
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         16
        </span>
        d
       </span>
       at a distance of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7006561900000000000♠
        </span>
        5619
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        87 km
       </span>
       and with an eccentricity of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6997840000000000000♠
        </span>
        0.0084
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.0076
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The ratio of its semi-major axis to its primary's Hill radius is 0.0023, the tightest trans-Neptunian binary with a known orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stansberry-result_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stansberry-result-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actaea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Actaea is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7000237200000000000♠
        </span>
        2.372
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.060
       </span>
       magnitudes fainter than Salacia,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       implying a diameter ratio of 2.98 for equal albedos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stansberry-result_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stansberry-result-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hence, assuming equal albedos, it has a diameter of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005303000000000000♠
        </span>
        303
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        35 km
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Actaea has the same color as Salacia (V−I =
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6999890000000000000♠
        </span>
        0.89
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.02
       </span>
       and
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6999870000000000000♠
        </span>
        0.87
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.01
       </span>
       , respectively), supporting the assumption of equal albedos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stansberry-result_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stansberry-result-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has been calculated that the Salacia system should have undergone enough
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        tidal
       </a>
       evolution to circularize their orbits, which is consistent with the low measured eccentricity, but that the primary need not have been tidally locked.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stansberry-result_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stansberry-result-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The low density calculated for the system (1.16 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ) implies that both Salacia and Actaea consist chiefly of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        water ice
       </a>
       . Salacia and Actaea will next occult each other in 2067.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stansberry-result_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stansberry-result-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mass of the system is 4.66 ± 0.22  × 10
       <sup>
        20
       </sup>
       kg, with about 4% of this being in Actaea.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stansberry-result_3-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stansberry-result-3">
         [3]
        </a>
