   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    <i>
     The New Moon
    </i>
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         The New Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       is the name of an
       <a href="/wiki/Operetta" title="Operetta">
        operetta
       </a>
       with music by
       <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Romberg" title="Sigmund Romberg">
        Sigmund Romberg
       </a>
       and book and lyrics by
       <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Hammerstein_II" title="Oscar Hammerstein II">
        Oscar Hammerstein II
       </a>
       , Frank Mandel, and Laurence Schwab. The show was the third and last in a string of
       <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">
        Broadway
       </a>
       hits for Romberg (after
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Student_Prince" title="The Student Prince">
         The Student Prince
        </a>
       </i>
       (1924) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Desert_Song" title="The Desert Song">
         The Desert Song
        </a>
       </i>
       (1926)) written in the style of Viennese operetta. It spawned a number of revivals and two
       <a href="#Film_versions">
        film versions
       </a>
       , and it is still played by light opera companies. The piece turned out to be "Broadway's last hit operetta",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encores!_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Encores!-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as World War II and the
       <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre#The_Golden_Age_(1940s_to_1960s)" title="Musical theatre">
        Golden Age of musicals
       </a>
       approached.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Performance_history">
        Performance history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Performance history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        The New Moon
       </i>
       debuted in Philadelphia on Christmas Eve, 1927. The tryout was a failure, and the show was extensively revised before another tryout in
       <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">
        Cleveland
       </a>
       in August 1928 and then moving to New York.
       <a href="/wiki/Al_Goodman" title="Al Goodman">
        Al Goodman
       </a>
       conducted in both Philadelphia and New York.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The operetta opened on Broadway at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Theatre_(Broadway)" title="Imperial Theatre (Broadway)">
        Imperial Theatre
       </a>
       on September 19,
       <a href="/wiki/1928_in_music#Musical_theater" title="1928 in music">
        1928
       </a>
       , ran for 519 performances, and closed at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casino_Theatre_(New_York,_New_York)" title="Casino Theatre (New York, New York)">
        Casino Theatre
       </a>
       on December 14, 1929. The production used set designs by
       <a href="/wiki/Donald_Oenslager" title="Donald Oenslager">
        Donald Oenslager
       </a>
       . The work was produced in London's
       <a href="/wiki/West_End_theatre" title="West End theatre">
        West End
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Theatre_Royal,_Drury_Lane" title="Theatre Royal, Drury Lane">
        Drury Lane Theatre
       </a>
       in 1929.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although the piece then had international productions and stock revivals into the 1950s,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suskin_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Suskin-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it then disappeared for a few decades. One commentator wrote, "What has kept
       <i>
        The New Moon
       </i>
       from being as familiar as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Naughty_Marietta_(operetta)" title="Naughty Marietta (operetta)">
         Naughty Marietta
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Student_Prince" title="The Student Prince">
         The Student Prince
        </a>
       </i>
       is perhaps its chronological place at the end of operetta's reign over the musical stage.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The operetta was restaged faithfully in 1986 by the
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Opera" title="New York City Opera">
        New York City Opera
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was telecast by
       <a href="/wiki/Public_Broadcast_Service" title="Public Broadcast Service">
        PBS
       </a>
       in 1989. The
       <a href="/wiki/Light_Opera_of_Manhattan" title="Light Opera of Manhattan">
        Light Opera of Manhattan
       </a>
       staged the work several times in the 1980s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Encores!" title="Encores!">
        City Center Encores!
       </a>
       presented a semi-staged revival at
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Center" title="New York City Center">
        New York City Center
       </a>
       in March 2003. The Encores production was presented during the run-up to the
       <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">
        Iraq War
       </a>
       and part of the audience responded with loud applause and cheers to the line "One can be loyal to one's country and yet forswear its leader".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encores!_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Encores!-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suskin_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Suskin-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">
        Synopsis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Synopsis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Robert is a young French aristocrat whose
       <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
        revolutionist
       </a>
       inclinations force him to flee his country. Under an assumed name, he sells himself as a bond-servant to planter and ship-owner Monsieur Beaunoir and his family in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">
        New Orleans
       </a>
       in 1792. Because the Paris police are looking everywhere for him, Robert cannot tell Beaunoir or Beaunoir's beautiful daughter Marianne, with whom he has fallen in love, that he is of noble blood. Eventually he is tracked down by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vicomte" title="Vicomte">
        Vicomte
       </a>
       Ribaud, the detective villain, and put aboard a ship, the
       <i>
        New Moon
       </i>
       , so that he can be returned to France. Robert thinks he has been betrayed by Marianne, who has gained her father's consent to travel on the same ship, pretending that she is in love with the ship's captain, Duval. A
       <a href="/wiki/Mutiny" title="Mutiny">
        mutiny
       </a>
       occurs, and Robert and the bond-servants come into power. Everyone goes ashore on the Isle of Pines, and a new republic is founded.
      </p>
      <p>
       The republic flourishes under Robert's guidance, but Marianne, her pride hurt, at first refuses to marry Robert. French ships arrive, apparently to reclaim the island. Vicomte Ribaud expects them to conquer the island for the King of France. But the French Commander reveals that there has been a
       <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
        revolution
       </a>
       in France, and that all aristocrats must die unless they renounce their titles. Ribaud, a
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">
        Royalist
       </a>
       , heads for execution, but republican Robert renounces his title. All ends happily for him and Marianne.
      </p>

        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Film versions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/New_Moon_(1940_film)" title="New Moon (1940 film)">
        New Moon (1940 film)
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/New_Moon_(1930_film)" title="New Moon (1930 film)">
        New Moon (1930 film)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Two film versions were produced by
       <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">
        MGM
       </a>
       . The first, in
       <a href="/wiki/1930_in_film" title="1930 in film">
        1930
       </a>
       , starred
       <a href="/wiki/Grace_Moore" title="Grace Moore">
        Grace Moore
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Tibbett" title="Lawrence Tibbett">
        Lawrence Tibbett
       </a>
       , with a setting in
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russia
       </a>
       . It added two new songs by
       <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Stothart" title="Herbert Stothart">
        Herbert Stothart
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Clifford_Grey" title="Clifford Grey">
        Clifford Grey
       </a>
       . The second, in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Moon_(1940_film)" title="New Moon (1940 film)">
        1940
       </a>
       , starred
       <a href="/wiki/Jeanette_MacDonald" title="Jeanette MacDonald">
        Jeanette MacDonald
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Eddy" title="Nelson Eddy">
        Nelson Eddy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Recordings">
        Recordings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_New_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recordings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are quite a few recordings of this score. No original Broadway cast recording was made, but the 1928 London cast recorded some selections for
       <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Graphophone_Company" title="Columbia Graphophone Company">
        Columbia
       </a>
       . These 78 rpm records have been transferred to CD on the Pearl Label. The song "Stout-hearted Men" was recorded in 1930 by
       <a href="/wiki/Perry_Askam" title="Perry Askam">
        Perry Askam
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and in 1967 by
       <a href="/wiki/Barbra_Streisand" title="Barbra Streisand">
        Barbra Streisand
       </a>
       , peaking at No. 2 on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Adult_Contemporary" title="Hot Adult Contemporary">
        Easy Listening
       </a>
       chart.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Earl_Wrightson" title="Earl Wrightson">
        Earl Wrightson
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Frances_Greer" title="Frances Greer">
        Frances Greer
       </a>
       starred in
       <a href="/wiki/Al_Goodman" title="Al Goodman">
        Al Goodman
       </a>
       's recording for
       <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">
        RCA Victor
       </a>
       (LK-1011).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Decca_Records" title="Decca Records">
        Decca
       </a>
       made an album in 1953 with Lee Sweetland and Jane Wilson covering six selections from the score, which has been reissued on CD paired with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Desert_Song" title="The Desert Song">
         The Desert Song
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Gordon_MacRae" title="Gordon MacRae">
        Gordon MacRae
       </a>
       recorded a 10-inch LP for
       <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">
        Capitol Records
       </a>
       (Capitol H-219) in 1950 with
       <a href="/wiki/Lucille_Norman" title="Lucille Norman">
        Lucille Norman
       </a>
       . It was repackaged as a split release in 1956 on one side of a 12-inch album with
       <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Friml" title="Rudolf Friml">
        Rudolf Friml
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Vagabond_King" title="The Vagabond King">
         The Vagabond King
        </a>
       </i>
       on the reverse, also with Lucille Norman (Capitol T-219). That release was reissued on CD in 2011 by Vintage Music.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Reader%27s_Digest" title="Reader's Digest">
         Reader's Digest
        </a>
       </i>
       includes the operetta in its 1963 collection,
       <i>
        A Treasury of Great Operettas
       </i>
       , starring
       <a href="/wiki/Jeanette_Scovotti" title="Jeanette Scovotti">
        Jeanette Scovotti
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Palmer_(actor)" title="Peter Palmer (actor)">
        Peter Palmer
       </a>
       and conducted by
       <a href="/wiki/Lehman_Engel" title="Lehman Engel">
        Lehman Engel
       </a>
       . This stereo recording is available on CD.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       As part of a new series of stereo recordings of classic operettas, Capitol had MacRae and
       <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Kirsten" title="Dorothy Kirsten">
        Dorothy Kirsten
       </a>
       record three studio cast albums in 1962 and 1963. Their selections from
       <i>
        The New Moon
       </i>
       (Capitol SW-1966) are available on the EMI CD
       <i>
        Music of Sigmund Romberg
       </i>
       , along with selections from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Student_Prince" title="The Student Prince">
         The Student Prince
        </a>
       </i>
       (Capitol SW-1841), and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Desert_Song" title="The Desert Song">
         The Desert Song
        </a>
       </i>
       (Capitol SW-1842).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Encores! cast recorded the entire score in 2004, using the original orchestrations, for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghostlight_Records" title="Ghostlight Records">
        Ghostlight Records
       </a>
       . A reviewer wrote in
       <i>
        Playbill
       </i>
       that the recording "is eminently enjoyable. ...
       <i>
        The New Moon
       </i>
       is vibrant, full-bodied and – yes – stouthearted."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suskin_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Suskin-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/John_Kenrick_(theatre_writer)" title="John Kenrick (theatre writer)">
        John Kenrick
       </a>
       praised
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodney_Gilfrey" title="Rodney Gilfrey">
        Rodney Gilfrey
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Christiane_Noll" title="Christiane Noll">
        Christiane Noll
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Brandon_Jovanovich" title="Brandon Jovanovich">
        Brandon Jovanovich
       </a>
       , the supporting cast and the conducting, writing: "What a spectacular delight! ... [the] songs soar, exploding with romance and swashbuckling bravado."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
