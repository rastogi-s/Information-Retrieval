      <p>
       "
       <b>
        Blue Moon
       </b>
       " is a classic
       <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">
        popular song
       </a>
       written by
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rodgers" title="Richard Rodgers">
        Richard Rodgers
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lorenz_Hart" title="Lorenz Hart">
        Lorenz Hart
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/1934_in_music" title="1934 in music">
        1934
       </a>
       , and has become a standard
       <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">
        ballad
       </a>
       . It may be the first instance of the familiar "
       <a href="/wiki/50s_progression" title="50s progression">
        50s progression
       </a>
       " in a popular song. The song was a hit twice in 1949 with successful recordings in the US by
       <a href="/wiki/Billy_Eckstine" title="Billy Eckstine">
        Billy Eckstine
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mel_Torm%C3%A9" title="Mel Tormé">
        Mel Tormé
       </a>
       . In 1961, "Blue Moon" became an international number one hit for the
       <a href="/wiki/Doo-wop" title="Doo-wop">
        doo-wop
       </a>
       group
       <a href="/wiki/The_Marcels" title="The Marcels">
        The Marcels
       </a>
       , on the
       <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">
        <i>
         Billboard 100
        </i>
       </a>
       chart and in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Singles_chart" title="UK Singles chart">
        UK Singles chart
       </a>
       . Over the years, "Blue Moon" has been covered by various artists including versions by
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">
        Frank Sinatra
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Billie_Holiday" title="Billie Holiday">
        Billie Holiday
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">
        Elvis Presley
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/The_Mavericks" title="The Mavericks">
        The Mavericks
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dean_Martin" title="Dean Martin">
        Dean Martin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/The_Supremes" title="The Supremes">
        The Supremes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Rod_Stewart" title="Rod Stewart">
        Rod Stewart
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Bing_Crosby" title="Bing Crosby">
        Bing Crosby
       </a>
       included the song in a medley on his album
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/On_the_Happy_Side" title="On the Happy Side">
         On the Happy Side
        </a>
       </i>
       (1962). It is also the anthem of English
       <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">
        Premier League
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">
        football
       </a>
       club
       <a href="/wiki/Manchester_City_F.C." title="Manchester City F.C.">
        Manchester City
       </a>
       , who have adapted the song slightly.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rodgers_and_Hart">
        Rodgers and Hart
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_Moon_(1934_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rodgers and Hart">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_Moon_(1934_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Richard Rodgers and Lorenz Hart were contracted to
       <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">
        Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer
       </a>
       in May 1933. They were soon commissioned to write the songs for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Party_(1934_film)" title="Hollywood Party (1934 film)">
         Hollywood Party
        </a>
       </i>
       , a film that was to star many of the studio's top artists. Richard Rodgers recalled, "One of our ideas was to include a scene in which
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Harlow" title="Jean Harlow">
        Jean Harlow
       </a>
       is shown as an innocent young girl saying—or rather singing—her prayers. How the sequence fitted into the movie I haven't the foggiest notion, but the purpose was to express Harlow's overwhelming ambition to become a movie star ('Oh Lord, if you're not busy up there,/I ask for help with a prayer/So please don't give me the air . . .')." The song was not recorded (nor was the movie released
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) and MGM Song #225 "Prayer (Oh Lord, make me a movie star)" dated June 14, 1933, was registered for copyright as an unpublished work on July 10, 1933.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brent_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brent-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lorenz Hart wrote new lyrics for the tune to create a title song for the 1934 film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Melodrama" title="Manhattan Melodrama">
         Manhattan Melodrama
        </a>
       </i>
       : "Act One:/You gulp your coffee and run;/Into the subway you crowd./Don’t breathe, it isn’t allowed".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kanfer_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kanfer-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The song, which was also titled "It's Just That Kind of Play", was cut from the film before release, and registered for copyright as an unpublished work on March 30, 1934. The studio then asked for a nightclub number for the film. Rodgers still liked the melody so Hart wrote a third lyric: "The Bad in Every Man" ("Oh, Lord . . . /I could be good to a lover,/But then I always discover/The bad in ev’ry man"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kanfer_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kanfer-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), which was sung by
       <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Ross" title="Shirley Ross">
        Shirley Ross
       </a>
       . The song, which was also released as sheet music, was not a hit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brent_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brent-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After the film was released by MGM, Jack Robbins—the head of the studio's publishing company
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martini_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Martini-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       —decided that the tune was suited to commercial release but needed more romantic lyrics and a punchier title. Hart was initially reluctant to write yet another lyric but he was persuaded.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brent_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brent-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The result was "Blue moon/you saw me standing alone/without a dream in my heart/without a love of my own".
      </p>
      <p>
       There is an introductory verse (a common technique employed by songwriters of the 1920s and 1930s) that comes before the first refrain of the song.
       <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">
        Eric Clapton
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Rod_Stewart" title="Rod Stewart">
        Rod Stewart
       </a>
       used it in their 2004 version of the song (

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background_4">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_Moon_(1934_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       "Blue Moon"'s first crossover recording to
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">
        rock and roll
       </a>
       came from
       <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">
        Elvis Presley
       </a>
       in 1956. His cover version of the song was included on his self-titled debut album
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley_(album)" title="Elvis Presley (album)">
         Elvis Presley
        </a>
       </i>
       , issued on
       <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">
        RCA Records
       </a>
       . Presley's remake of "Blue Moon" was the B-side to the single "Just Because". It was produced by
       <a href="/wiki/Sam_Phillips" title="Sam Phillips">
        Sam Phillips
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jarmusch" title="Jim Jarmusch">
        Jim Jarmusch
       </a>
       's 1989 film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mystery_Train_(film)" title="Mystery Train (film)">
         Mystery Train
        </a>
       </i>
       , the three distinct stories that make up the narrative are linked by a portion of Elvis Presley's version of "Blue Moon" (as heard on a radio broadcast) and a subsequent offscreen gunshot, which are heard once during each story, revealing that the three stories occur simultaneously in real time.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background_5">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_Moon_(1934_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Marcels" title="The Marcels">
        The Marcels
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Doo-wop" title="Doo-wop">
        doo-wop
       </a>
       group, also recorded the track for their album
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Blue_Moon_(The_Marcels_album)" title="Blue Moon (The Marcels album)">
         Blue Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       . In 1961, the Marcels had three songs left to record and needed one more. Producer
       <a href="/wiki/Stu_Phillips_(composer)" title="Stu Phillips (composer)">
        Stu Phillips
       </a>
       did not like any of the other songs except one that had the same chord changes as "Heart and Soul" and "Blue Moon". He asked them if they knew either, and one knew "Blue Moon" and taught it to the others, though with the bridge or release (middle section - "I heard somebody whisper...") wrong.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboard_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-billboard-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The famous introduction to the song ("bomp-baba-bomp" and "dip-da-dip") was an excerpt of an original song that the group had in its act.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
        Reception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_Moon_(1934_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The record reached number one on the
       <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">
        Billboard Pop
       </a>
       chart for three weeks and number one on the R&amp;B chart.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitburn_Singles_p376_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Whitburn_Singles_p376-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It also peaked at #1 on the UK Singles Chart. The Marcels' version of "Blue Moon" sold a million copies, and was awarded a
       <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">
        gold disc
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Book_of_Golden_Discs_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_Book_of_Golden_Discs-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is featured in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">
         Rock and Roll Hall of Fame's
        </a>
        500 Songs that Shaped Rock and Roll
       </i>
       . The Marcels
       <a href="/wiki/Doo-wop" title="Doo-wop">
        doo-wop
       </a>
       version is one of three different versions used in the
       <a href="/wiki/1981_in_film" title="1981 in film">
        1981
       </a>
       film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/An_American_Werewolf_in_London" title="An American Werewolf in London">
         An American Werewolf in London
        </a>
       </i>
       with this version appearing at the end credits of the film. A version by
       <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Vinton" title="Bobby Vinton">
        Bobby Vinton
       </a>
       plays during the film's opening titles while a version by
       <a href="/wiki/Sam_Cooke" title="Sam Cooke">
        Sam Cooke
       </a>
       plays during the film's famous werewolf transformation scene. The Marcels' version of the song is referenced in the
       <a href="/wiki/1962_in_film" title="1962 in film">
        1962
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">
        Academy Award
       </a>
       nominated
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animated_short" title="Animated short">
        animated short
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">
        Disney
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Musical_film" title="Musical film">
        musical film
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_Symposium_on_Popular_Songs" title="A Symposium on Popular Songs">
         A Symposium on Popular Songs
        </a>
       </i>
       during the song, "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puppy_Love_Is_Here_to_Stay" title="Puppy Love Is Here to Stay">
        Puppy Love Is Here to Stay
       </a>
       " written by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_%26_Richard_Sherman" title="Robert &amp; Richard Sherman">
        Robert &amp; Richard Sherman
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

      <p>

       <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">
        country music
       </a>
       group

        the Mavericks
       </a>

       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13_(film)" title="Apollo 13 (film)">

        </a>

       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/RPM_(magazine)" title="RPM (magazine)">

        </a>

       Country Tracks chart in Canada. It also charted on the
       <i>

       </i>
       Adult Contemporary Tracks chart, peaking at number 15. A music video was produced, directed by Todd Hallowell.
