      <p>
       <b>
        Adrastea
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         ad-rə-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          STEE
         </span>
         -ə
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Αδράστεια
       </span>
       ), also known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter XV
        </span>
       </b>
       , is the second by distance, and the smallest of the four
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        inner moons of Jupiter
       </a>
       . It was discovered in photographs taken by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1979, making it the first
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       to be discovered from images taken by an interplanetary
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       , rather than through a telescope.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3454_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3454-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was officially named after the mythological
       <a href="/wiki/Adrasteia" title="Adrasteia">
        Adrasteia
       </a>
       , foster mother of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_god" title="Greek god">
        Greek god
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       —the equivalent of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_god" title="Roman god">
        Roman god
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Adrastea is one of the few moons in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       known to orbit its planet in less than the length of that planet's day. It orbits at the edge of
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter#Main_ring" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        Jupiter's Main Ring
       </a>
       and is thought to be the main contributor of material to the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        Rings of Jupiter
       </a>
       . Despite observations made in the 1990s by the
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">
        <i>
         Galileo
        </i>
        spacecraft
       </a>
       , very little is known about the moon's physical characteristics other than its size and the fact that it is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidally_locked" title="Tidally locked">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_observations">
        Discovery and observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adrastea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Adrast%C3%A9e_FDS_20630.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="244" data-file-width="400" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Adrast%C3%A9e_FDS_20630.png/300px-Adrast%C3%A9e_FDS_20630.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4d/Adrast%C3%A9e_FDS_20630.png 1.5x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Adrast%C3%A9e_FDS_20630.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Discovery image of Adrastea, taken on July 8, 1979, by Voyager 2. Adrastea is the fainter dot, in the very middle, straddling the line of the Jovian rings.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Adrastea was discovered by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Jewitt" title="David Jewitt">
        David C. Jewitt
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=G._Edward_Danielson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="G. Edward Danielson (page does not exist)">
        G. Edward Danielson
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       probe photographs taken on July 8, 1979, and received the designation
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/1979 J 1
        </span>
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3454_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3454-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJewitt_Danielson_et_al.1979_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJewitt_Danielson_et_al.1979-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although it appeared only as a dot,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJewitt_Danielson_et_al.1979_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJewitt_Danielson_et_al.1979-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it was the first moon to be discovered by an interplanetary spacecraft. Soon after its discovery, two other of the inner
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">
        moons of Jupiter
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Thebe_(moon)" title="Thebe (moon)">
        Thebe
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Metis_(moon)" title="Metis (moon)">
        Metis
       </a>
       ) were observed in the images taken a few months earlier by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_spacecraft" title="Galileo spacecraft">
        <i>
         Galileo
        </i>
        spacecraft
       </a>
       was able to determine the moon's shape in 1998, but the images remain poor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1983, Adrastea was officially named after the Greek nymph
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrastea_(mythology)" title="Adrastea (mythology)">
        Adrastea
       </a>
       , the daughter of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       and his lover
       <a href="/wiki/Ananke_(mythology)" title="Ananke (mythology)">
        Ananke
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the
       <a href="/wiki/Juno_(spacecraft)" title="Juno (spacecraft)">
        Juno
       </a>
       orbiter, which arrived at Jupiter in 2016, has a camera called
       <a href="/wiki/JunoCam" title="JunoCam">
        JunoCam
       </a>
       , it is almost entirely focused on observations of Jupiter itself. However, if all goes well should be able to capture some limited images of the Jupiter moons
       <a href="/wiki/Metis_(moon)" title="Metis (moon)">
        Metis
       </a>
       and Adrastea.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adrastea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Adrastea has an irregular shape and measures 20×16×14 km across.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A surface area estimate would be between 840 and 1,600 (~1,200) km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       . This makes it the smallest of the four inner moons. The bulk, composition and mass of Adrastea are not known, but assuming that its mean density is like that of
       <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(moon)" title="Amalthea (moon)">
        Amalthea
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       around 0.86 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       its mass can be estimated at about 2 × 10
       <sup>
        15
       </sup>
       kg. Amalthea's density implies that the moon is composed of water
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        ice
       </a>
       with a
       <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">
        porosity
       </a>
       of 10–15%, and Adrastea may be similar.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson_Johnson_et_al.2005-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       No surface details of Adrastea are known, due to the low resolution of available images.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adrastea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Adrastea is the smallest and second-closest member of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        inner Jovian satellite family
       </a>
       . It orbits
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       at a radius of about 129,000 km (1.806 Jupiter radii) at the exterior edge of the planet's
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter#Main_ring" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        Main Ring
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_2-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Adrastea is one of only three moons in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       known to orbit its planet in less than the length of that planet's day—the other two being Jupiter's innermost moon
       <a href="/wiki/Metis_(moon)" title="Metis (moon)">
        Metis
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       ' moon
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       . The orbit has very small
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       —around 0.0015 and 0.03°, respectively. Inclination is relative to the equator of Jupiter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_2-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal locking
       </a>
       , Adrastea rotates synchronously with its orbital period, keeping one face always looking toward the planet. Its long axis is aligned towards Jupiter, this being the lowest energy configuration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The orbit of Adrastea lies inside
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous orbit
       </a>
       radius (as does
       <a href="/wiki/Metis_(moon)" title="Metis (moon)">
        Metis
       </a>
       's), and as a result,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_deceleration" title="Tidal deceleration">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       are slowly causing its orbit to decay so that it will one day impact Jupiter. If its density is similar to Amalthea's then its orbit would actually lie within the fluid
       <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">
        Roche limit
       </a>
       . However, since it is not breaking up, it must still lie outside its rigid Roche limit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_2-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Adrastea is the second-fastest-moving of Jupiter's moons, with an orbital speed of 31.378 km/s.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Relationship_with_Jupiter.27s_rings">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Jupiter's_rings">
        Relationship with Jupiter's rings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adrastea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relationship with Jupiter's rings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Adrastea is the largest contributor to material in
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        Jupiter's rings
       </a>
       . This appears to consist primarily of material that is ejected from the surfaces of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        Jupiter's four small inner satellites
       </a>
       by meteorite impacts. It is easy for the impact ejecta to be lost from these satellites into space. This is due to the satellites' low density and their surfaces lying close to the edge of their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roche_sphere" title="Roche sphere">
        Roche spheres
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_2-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It seems that Adrastea is the most copious source of this ring material, as evidenced by the densest ring (the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter#Main_ring" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        Main Ring
       </a>
       ) being located at and within Adrastea's orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Showalter_et_al.1999_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Showalter_et_al.1999-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       More precisely, the orbit of Adrastea lies near the outer edge of Jupiter's Main Ring.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOckert-Bell_Burns_et_al.1999_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOckert-Bell_Burns_et_al.1999-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The exact extent of visible ring material depends on the phase angle of the images: in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">
        forward-scattered light
       </a>
       Adrastea is firmly outside the Main Ring,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOckert-Bell_Burns_et_al.1999_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOckert-Bell_Burns_et_al.1999-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but in
       <a href="/wiki/Backscatter" title="Backscatter">
        back-scattered light
       </a>
       (which reveals much bigger particles) there appears to also be a narrow ringlet outside Adrastea's orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_2-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
