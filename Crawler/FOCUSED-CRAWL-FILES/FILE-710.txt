      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Explorers on the Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       (French:
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         <b>
          On a marché sur la Lune
         </b>
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is the seventeenth volume of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="The Adventures of Tintin">
         The Adventures of Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       , the comics series by Belgian cartoonist
       <a href="/wiki/Herg%C3%A9" title="Hergé">
        Hergé
       </a>
       . The story was
       <a href="/wiki/Serial_(literature)" title="Serial (literature)">
        serialised
       </a>
       weekly in Belgium's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tintin_(magazine)" title="Tintin (magazine)">
         Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine from October 1952 to December 1953 before being published in a collected volume by
       <a href="/wiki/Casterman" title="Casterman">
        Casterman
       </a>
       in 1954. Completing a story arc begun in the preceding volume,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(comics)" title="Destination Moon (comics)">
         Destination Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       (1953), the narrative tells of the young reporter
       <a href="/wiki/Tintin_(character)" title="Tintin (character)">
        Tintin
       </a>
       , his dog
       <a href="/wiki/Snowy_(character)" title="Snowy (character)">
        Snowy
       </a>
       , and friends
       <a href="/wiki/Captain_Haddock" title="Captain Haddock">
        Captain Haddock
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Professor_Calculus" title="Professor Calculus">
        Professor Calculus
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Thomson_and_Thompson" title="Thomson and Thompson">
        Thomson and Thompson
       </a>
       who are aboard humanity's first manned rocket mission to the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Developed in part through the suggestions of Hergé's friends
       <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Heuvelmans" title="Bernard Heuvelmans">
        Bernard Heuvelmans
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Van_Melkebeke" title="Jacques Van Melkebeke">
        Jacques Van Melkebeke
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       was produced following Hergé's extensive research into the possibility of human space travel – a feat that had yet to be achieved – with the cartoonist seeking for the work to be as realistic as possible. Hergé continued
       <i>
        The Adventures of Tintin
       </i>
       with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Calculus_Affair" title="The Calculus Affair">
         The Calculus Affair
        </a>
       </i>
       , while the series itself became a defining part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Franco-Belgian_comics" title="Franco-Belgian comics">
        Franco-Belgian comics tradition
       </a>
       . Critics have praised the illustrative detail of the book, but have expressed mixed views of the story. The volume was adapted for both the 1957
       <a href="/wiki/Belvision_Studios" title="Belvision Studios">
        Belvision
       </a>
       animated series,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Herg%C3%A9%27s_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="Hergé's Adventures of Tintin">
         Hergé's Adventures of Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       , and for the 1991 animated series
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin_(TV_series)" title="The Adventures of Tintin (TV series)">
         The Adventures of Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse_Programm%C3%A9" title="Ellipse Programmé">
        Ellipse
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nelvana" title="Nelvana">
        Nelvana
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/NERVA" title="NERVA">
        nuclear motor
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Artificial_gravity" title="Artificial gravity">
        artificial gravity
       </a>
       and sending everyone
       <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness#Weightlessness_in_a_spacecraft" title="Weightlessness">
        floating
       </a>
       until Tintin corrects the problem. They then suffered a relapse of the Formula 14 drug (seen in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Black_Gold" title="Land of Black Gold">
         Land of Black Gold
        </a>
       </i>
       (1950)), resulting in their hair growing rapidly in multiple colours. Calculus subsequently administers a cure. Haddock, who has smuggled whisky aboard the rocket, gets drunk and takes an impromptu
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">
        spacewalk
       </a>
       , during which he briefly becoming a
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/2101_Adonis" title="2101 Adonis">
        Adonis
       </a>
       (at which point, Professor Calculus humorously says he will tell Earth that Adonis has a new satellite by the name of Haddock) but Tintin is able to rescue him.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHergé19541-11_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHergé19541-11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The rocket lands in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus_(lunar_crater)" title="Hipparchus (lunar crater)">
        Hipparchus Crater
       </a>
       , with Tintin being the first human to step on the Moon. Three days later, Haddock, Wolff and Tintin take the battery-powered tank to explore some
       <a href="/wiki/Stalactite" title="Stalactite">
        stalactite
       </a>
       caves in the direction of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaeus_(lunar_crater)" title="Ptolemaeus (lunar crater)">
        Ptolemaeus Crater
       </a>
       ; inside a cave Snowy slips into an ice-covered chasm, but Tintin rescues him. Later aboard the ship, Tintin is overwhelmed by a third stowaway,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonel_Jorgen" title="Colonel Jorgen">
        Colonel Jorgen
       </a>
       , a spy who had been smuggled aboard by Wolff, who has been blackmailed by a foreign power for which Jorgen works. With Wolff's help, Jorgen seeks to hijack the ship and return it to Earth, but is foiled by Tintin through emergency sabotage that cuts power to the engine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHergé195412-48_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHergé195412-48-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to the strain on the oxygen supplies, the crew decides to abandon most of the equipment and to cut short the lunar stay. The repair work is completed slightly ahead of schedule, and the rocket cleared for lift-off. Halfway to Earth, Jorgen escapes his bonds thanks to the detectives' bungling and tries to kill Tintin and the others; Wolff seeks to prevent him, and in their struggle over a gun Jorgen is killed. When it is revealed that there will not be enough oxygen aboard for the crew to survive the journey, Wolff sacrifices himself by opening the
       <a href="/wiki/Airlock" title="Airlock">
        airlock
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Space_exposure" title="Space exposure">
        floating out into space
       </a>
       to his death. The crew fall unconscious but Tintin wakes long enough to set the rocket to auto-pilot and it arrives back in
       <a href="/wiki/Syldavia" title="Syldavia">
        Syldavia
       </a>
       safely.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHergé195449-62_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHergé195449-62-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorers_on_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorers_on_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Hergé first devised the idea of sending Tintin on a mission to the Moon while he was working on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_the_Sun" title="Prisoners of the Sun">
         Prisoners of the Sun
        </a>
       </i>
       (1949).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His decision to move into the field of
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       might have been influenced by his friendly rivalry with his colleague
       <a href="/wiki/Edgar_P._Jacobs" title="Edgar P. Jacobs">
        Edgar P. Jacobs
       </a>
       , who had recently had success with his own science fiction comic,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Secret_of_the_Swordfish" title="The Secret of the Swordfish">
         The Secret of the Swordfish
        </a>
       </i>
       (1950–53).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012218_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012218-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He decided that it would be a two-volume story arc, as had proved successful with his earlier arcs,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Secret_of_the_Unicorn" title="The Secret of the Unicorn">
         The Secret of the Unicorn
        </a>
       </i>
       (1943) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Red_Rackham%27s_Treasure" title="Red Rackham's Treasure">
         Red Rackham's Treasure
        </a>
       </i>
       (1944), and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Seven_Crystal_Balls" title="The Seven Crystal Balls">
         The Seven Crystal Balls
        </a>
       </i>
       (1948) and
       <i>
        Prisoners of the Sun
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He had initially intended on beginning this story after the culmination of
       <i>
        Prisoners of the Sun
       </i>
       , but both his wife Germaine Remi and his close friend Marcel Dehaye convinced him to proceed with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Black_Gold" title="Land of Black Gold">
         Land of Black Gold
        </a>
       </i>
       (1950), a story that he had previously left unfinished, instead.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin2009189_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin2009189-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_RH8II_Bild-B0788-42_BSM,_Peenem%C3%BCnde,_Startvorbereitungen_V2.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="794" data-file-width="540" height="323" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Bundesarchiv_RH8II_Bild-B0788-42_BSM%2C_Peenem%C3%BCnde%2C_Startvorbereitungen_V2.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_RH8II_Bild-B0788-42_BSM%2C_Peenem%C3%BCnde%2C_Startvorbereitungen_V2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Bundesarchiv_RH8II_Bild-B0788-42_BSM%2C_Peenem%C3%BCnde%2C_Startvorbereitungen_V2.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_RH8II_Bild-B0788-42_BSM%2C_Peenem%C3%BCnde%2C_Startvorbereitungen_V2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Bundesarchiv_RH8II_Bild-B0788-42_BSM%2C_Peenem%C3%BCnde%2C_Startvorbereitungen_V2.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_RH8II_Bild-B0788-42_BSM%2C_Peenem%C3%BCnde%2C_Startvorbereitungen_V2.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_RH8II_Bild-B0788-42_BSM,_Peenem%C3%BCnde,_Startvorbereitungen_V2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A German V-2 rocket being tested in 1942. The V-2 would serve as a major inspiration for Hergé in his work on both
         <i>
          Destination Moon
         </i>
         and
         <i>
          Explorers on the Moon
         </i>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Seeking advice on the story, Hergé consulted his friend
       <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Heuvelmans" title="Bernard Heuvelmans">
        Bernard Heuvelmans
       </a>
       , who had authored the non-fiction book
       <i>
        L'Homme parmi les étoiles
       </i>
       ("Man Among the Stars") (1944).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012218_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012218-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In autumn 1947, Heuvelmans and
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Van_Melkebeke" title="Jacques Van Melkebeke">
        Jacques Van Melkebeke
       </a>
       developed a script for the story, which they gave to Hergé. This version based Calculus' lunar expedition in a fictional location, Radio City, in the United States. It featured a return of Professor Decimus Phostle, a character who had previously appeared in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Shooting_Star" title="The Shooting Star">
         The Shooting Star
        </a>
       </i>
       (1942), but this time as an antagonist; Phostle had sold the secrets of the mission in order to attain funds to buy a diamond for the actress
       <a href="/wiki/Rita_Hayworth" title="Rita Hayworth">
        Rita Hayworth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991138–139Farr2001138LofficierLofficier200265Peeters2012218_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991138–139Farr2001138LofficierLofficier200265Peeters2012218-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In early 1948, Hergé produced two black-and-white pages of this version of the story before abandoning it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994Thompson1991139Peeters2012218_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994Thompson1991139Peeters2012218-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé retained some elements of this original script in his finished version, namely the scenes in which Haddock drinks whiskey in a gravity-free environment and that in which Haddock goes for a space walk and nearly becomes a satellite of Adonis, which appear on pages 5 and 8 of
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991139Peeters2012220–221_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991139Peeters2012220–221-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nevertheless, Heuvelmans thought his influence on the story to be more significant, stating that "In going through the two books we [he and Van Melkebeke] really had the impression that it was what we had originally done at the beginning. In broad outline, that was it."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009172_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009172-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Hergé hoped for the story to be as realistic as possible, and sought to eschew fantastical elements.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994Farr2001135_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994Farr2001135-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In his own words, it contained "no moonmen, no monsters, no incredible surprises".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To ensure this realism, he collected a wide range of documents about
       <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">
        rockets
       </a>
       and space travel with which to conduct research.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this he was aided by Heuvelmans, who collected pictures of rockets and atomic research facilities for him.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009172Peeters2012222_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009172Peeters2012222-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé's research archive included
       <a href="/wiki/Man_Will_Conquer_Space_Soon!" title="Man Will Conquer Space Soon!">
        an article
       </a>
       from the American magazine
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Collier%27s" title="Collier's">
         Collier's
        </a>
       </i>
       which discussed how humanity could reach the moon,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as well as books by
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Rousseau" title="Pierre Rousseau">
        Pierre Rousseau
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Piccard" title="Auguste Piccard">
        Auguste Piccard
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009170_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009170-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A further work that he used was
       <i>
        L'Astronautique
       </i>
       (1950), a book on putative space travel by the physicist
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ananoff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alexander Ananoff (page does not exist)">
        Alexander Ananoff
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265Goddin20118_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265Goddin20118-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with whom Hergé began a correspondence in April 1950.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012225_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012225-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also visited the
       <a href="/wiki/Ateliers_de_Constructions_Electriques_de_Charleroi" title="Ateliers de Constructions Electriques de Charleroi">
        Ateliers de Constructions Electriques de Charleroi
       </a>
       's Center for Atomic Research, striking up a subsequent correspondence with its director,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Hoyaux&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Max Hoyaux (page does not exist)">
        Max Hoyaux
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009170–171Peeters2012225_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009170–171Peeters2012225-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé incorporated much of this technical information into the story, but juxtaposed it with moments of humour to make it more accessible to his young readership.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991143_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991143-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to literary critics
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Marc_Lofficier" title="Jean-Marc Lofficier">
        Jean-Marc Lofficier
       </a>
       and Randy Lofficier, possible fictional influences on Hergé's story include
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
        Jules Verne
       </a>
       's 1870 novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Around_the_Moon" title="Around the Moon">
         Around the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       and the 1950 American film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(film)" title="Destination Moon (film)">
         Destination Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé was certainly inspired by a number of photographic stills from the
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       film which had been published.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201110_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201110-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The computer system at the Sprodj space centre was visually based upon the
       <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC_I" title="UNIVAC I">
        UNIVAC I
       </a>
       , the first computer to be created for non-military purposes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201122_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201122-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé based his moon rocket on the designs of the
       <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">
        V-2 rocket
       </a>
       which had been developed by German scientists during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136_13-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The red-and-white checker pattern on Hergé's rocket was based upon an illustration of a V-2 which Hergé had come upon in
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leslie_Simon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leslie Simon (page does not exist)">
        Leslie Simon
       </a>
       's 1947 book
       <i>
        German Research in World War II
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He commissioned the construction of a model rocket with detachable parts from his assistant Arthur Van Noeyen. He took the model to
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       where he showed it to Ananoff, asking him if it was a realistic representation of what a moon rocket might look like. He and his assistants then used the model from which to accurately sketch when producing the comic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198995Thompson1991142–143Farr2001136Assouline2009171Peeters2012227_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198995Thompson1991142–143Farr2001136Assouline2009171Peeters2012227-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé introduced into the story the character of Boris Jorgen, who had previously appeared as an antagonist in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/King_Ottokar%27s_Sceptre" title="King Ottokar's Sceptre">
         King Ottokar's Sceptre
        </a>
       </i>
       (1939).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200264_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200264-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He added evidence for water on the moon at the advice of Heuvelmans.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991146Farr2001138_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991146Farr2001138-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Publication">
        Publication
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorers_on_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Publication">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On 7 September 1950, Hergé broke off the story with the statement "end of part one".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012227Goddin201113_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012227Goddin201113-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He felt the need for a break from work, having fallen back into clinical depression. He and his wife Germaine went on holiday to
       <a href="/wiki/Gland,_Switzerland" title="Gland, Switzerland">
        Gland
       </a>
       in Switzerland, before returning to Brussels in late September.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012227–228_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012227–228-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many readers sent letters to
       <i>
        Tintin
       </i>
       magazine asking why
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       was no longer being serialised, with a rumour emerging that Hergé had died.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012230_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012230-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 18 April 1951, he published an open letter in the magazine explaining his absence as a result of illness caused by exhaustion and included an illustration of himself sprawled out on an armchair.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012230_28-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012230-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As Hergé planned his return to work, covers of
       <i>
        Tintin
       </i>
       magazine announced the imminent return of the story.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201117_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201117-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       would resume after an eighteen-month hiatus,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991149Farr2001141_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991149Farr2001141-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       returning in the 9 April 1952 issue, accompanied with a summary of the story so far.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012232_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012232-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its final installment appeared on 31 December 1953.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009172_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009172-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Republication">
        Republication
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorers_on_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Republication">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Upon the serial's publication, Hergé faced criticism for including Wolff's suicide in the story; suicide was widely
       <a href="/wiki/Christian_views_on_suicide#Modern_Catholicism" title="Christian views on suicide">
        viewed as a sin
       </a>
       in Catholic-dominated Belgium. In deference to these critics, for the published book version he added Wolff's line of "perhaps by some miracle I shall escape too", to make the scene seem a less obvious suicide. Years later, Hergé expressed regret that he had capitulated on this issue.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198997Thompson1991148Assouline200974_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198997Thompson1991148Assouline200974-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The story was collected together and published by Editions Casterman as
       <i>
        On a Marché Sur La Lune
       </i>
       in 1954.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200263_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200263-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Casterman were unhappy with this title, which translates as "We Walked on the Moon", but Hergé resolutely refused to make a change.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991149_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991149-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_analysis">
        Critical analysis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorers_on_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Critical analysis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Jean-Marc Lofficier and Randy Lofficier believed that the two-part story "belongs" to Calculus as his "cosmic vision moves the story forward".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200264_24-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200264-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They further expressed the opinion that Wolff was a unique character in the
       <i>
        Adventures of Tintin
       </i>
       , suggesting that he is akin to a character from a
       <a href="/wiki/John_le_Carr%C3%A9" title="John le Carré">
        John le Carré
       </a>
       novel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200264_24-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200264-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Referring specifically to
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       , they opined that it was "a true epic of the human imagination", believing that its depiction of the moon has "withstood the test of time" more than other "proto-space exploration novels".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200266_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200266-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They felt that the moon adventure was "Hergé at his best... a triumphant achievement on every level", awarding both halves of the story five stars out of five.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200266_35-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200266-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Benoit_Peeters_20100329_Salon_du_livre_de_Paris_3.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Benoit_Peeters_20100329_Salon_du_livre_de_Paris_3.jpg/220px-Benoit_Peeters_20100329_Salon_du_livre_de_Paris_3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Benoit_Peeters_20100329_Salon_du_livre_de_Paris_3.jpg/330px-Benoit_Peeters_20100329_Salon_du_livre_de_Paris_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Benoit_Peeters_20100329_Salon_du_livre_de_Paris_3.jpg/440px-Benoit_Peeters_20100329_Salon_du_livre_de_Paris_3.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Benoit_Peeters_20100329_Salon_du_livre_de_Paris_3.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Hergé biographer
         <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Peeters" title="Benoît Peeters">
          Benoît Peeters
         </a>
         <i>
          (pictured, 2010)
         </i>
         felt that Wolff's character brought "a tragic note" to the story.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters201297_36-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters201297-36">
           [36]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Hergé biographer
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Assouline" title="Pierre Assouline">
        Pierre Assouline
       </a>
       felt that the two moon adventures "mark a stage in the development of Hergé's work".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009174_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009174-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé biographer
       <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Peeters" title="Benoît Peeters">
        Benoît Peeters
       </a>
       praised the "gradual introduction into the story of a real dimension of evil" as being something particularly effective.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198997_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198997-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also expressed the view that Wolff brings "a tragic note" to the story, comparing him to the characters in the stories of
       <a href="/wiki/Graham_Greene" title="Graham Greene">
        Graham Greene
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198997_38-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198997-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was critical of the two-part story arc, stating that they had "neither the liveliness and dynamism" of
       <i>
        The Secret of the Unicorn
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Red Rackham's Treasure
       </i>
       , "nor the supernatural quality" of
       <i>
        The Seven Crystal Balls
       </i>
       -
       <i>
        Prisoners of the Sun
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198997_38-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198997-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Harry_Thompson" title="Harry Thompson">
        Harry Thompson
       </a>
       noted that
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       was widely regarded as Hergé's "greatest artistic achievement",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991144_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991144-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       describing the entire moon adventure as "a technical masterpiece" as a result of its "uncannily accurate" depiction of the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991138_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991138-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Thompson expressed his opinion that
       <i>
        Explorers
       </i>
       could be compared to the work of science-fiction writers
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
        Jules Verne
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">
        H. G. Wells
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991146_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991146-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Focusing on the scene in which the Thom(p)sons hair grows rapidly in bright colours, he stated that it provides an abrupt contrast with "the almost scholastic nature of the rest of the story", and that it "injects a few bright splashes" into an otherwise "carefully restrained colour scheme".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991146–147_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991146–147-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Philippe Goddin praised the depiction of the rocket's landing as "a magnificent spectacle, well worth the double space spread given by Hergé",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201129_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201129-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       also highlighting what he perceived as the ending's "unprecedented dramatic tension".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201136_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201136-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In his
       <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">
        psychoanalytical
       </a>
       study of the
       <i>
        Adventures of Tintin
       </i>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Literary_criticism" title="Literary criticism">
        literary critic
       </a>
       Jean-Marie Apostolidès praised the
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       -
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       story arc for its "meticulous attention to scientific facts", but added that this had also resulted in the story's "rather pedagogical tone".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010179_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010179-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He added that in these stories, the main division was "no longer Good and Evil" as it had been in previous
       <i>
        Adventures
       </i>
       , but "Truth and Error".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010179_45-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010179-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Apostolidès opined that despite being a "fussy and somewhat ridiculous character", through his scientific achievements Calculus grows to the "stature of a giant" in this arc, eclipsing Sir Francis Haddock (from
       <i>
        The Secret of the Unicorn
       </i>
       ) as the series' "founding ancestor".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010182_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010182-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He goes on to claim that in becoming the "sacred ancestor", the voyage to the moon becomes "a mystical quest" with science as its guiding religion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010184_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010184-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Drawing comparisons between this arc and the
       <i>
        Prisoners of the Sun
       </i>
       story, he drew symbolic links between the scientific centre and the Inca Temple of the Sun, but noted that here Calculus was the "high priest" rather than the sacrificial victim as he had been in the previous story.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010184–185_48-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010184–185-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moving on to discuss the moon rocket in these stories, Apostolidès described it as a phallic object which penetrated the "virgin territory" of the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010186_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010186-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the same time, he described the rocket as a "maternal belly" in which the space explorers slept.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010186_49-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010186-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Commenting specifically on
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       he commented that the protagonists of the story reverted to childhood when exploring the moon, believing that they had treated it like a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_park" title="Theme park">
        theme park
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010187–188_50-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010187–188-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Literary critic
       <a href="/wiki/Tom_McCarthy_(novelist)" title="Tom McCarthy (novelist)">
        Tom McCarthy
       </a>
       stated that in the
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       -
       <i>
        Explorers of the Moon
       </i>
       story arc, Calculus "embodies Hergé's... own wartime position, spun out into a post-war environment", representing a genius driven by his work whose activities are coincidental to national and political causes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200642_51-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200642-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He suggested that
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       was "perhaps both the most wildly adventurous and the most contemplative" installment in the series.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006172_52-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006172-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He felt that the inclusion of Jorgen being smuggled aboard the rocket as a stowaway reflected the idea of the "stranger" penetrating the "home", something which he thought was present in other
       <i>
        Adventures
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200679_53-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200679-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Commenting on the scene in which Haddock smuggles a whisky bottle inside the
       <i>
        Guide to Astrology
       </i>
       , he states "the text, in this case, is hollow, smuggling something else", which he believed was a reversal of the appearance of a parchment hidden within a model ship in
       <i>
        The Secret of the Unicorn
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200617_54-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200617-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation">
        Adaptation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorers_on_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Adaptation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1957, the animation company
       <a href="/wiki/Belvision_Studios" title="Belvision Studios">
        Belvision Studios
       </a>
       produced
       <a href="/wiki/Herg%C3%A9%27s_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="Hergé's Adventures of Tintin">
        a string of colour adaptations
       </a>
       based on Hergé's original comics, adapting eight of the
       <i>
        Adventures
       </i>
       into a series of daily five-minute episodes.
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       was the second to be adapted in the second animated series; it was directed by Ray Goossens and written by
       <a href="/wiki/Greg_(cartoonist)" title="Greg (cartoonist)">
        Greg
       </a>
       , a well-known cartoonist who was to become editor-in-chief of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tintin_(magazine)" title="Tintin (magazine)">
         Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200287–88_55-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200287–88-55">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1991, a
       <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin_(TV_series)" title="The Adventures of Tintin (TV series)">
        second animated series
       </a>
       based upon
       <i>
        The Adventures of Tintin
       </i>
       was produced, this time as a collaboration between the French studio
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        Ellipse
       </a>
       and the Canadian animation company
       <a href="/wiki/Nelvana" title="Nelvana">
        Nelvana
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       was the fifteenth story to be adapted and was divided into two twenty-minute episodes. Directed by Stéphane Bernasconi, the series has been praised for being "generally faithful" to the original comics, to the extent that the animation was directly adopted from Hergé's original panels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200290_56-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200290-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
