      <p>
       <b>
        Nereid
       </b>
       is the third-largest
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moon
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       . It has a highly eccentric orbit. It was the second moon of Neptune to be discovered, by
       <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Kuiper" title="Gerard Kuiper">
        Gerard Kuiper
       </a>
       in 1949.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">
        Discovery and naming
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nereid_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Nereid was discovered on 1 May 1949 by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerard_P._Kuiper" title="Gerard P. Kuiper">
        Gerard P. Kuiper
       </a>
       on photographic plates taken with the 82-inch telescope at the
       <a href="/wiki/McDonald_Observatory" title="McDonald Observatory">
        McDonald Observatory
       </a>
       . He proposed the name in the report of his discovery. It is named after the
       <a href="/wiki/Nereid" title="Nereid">
        Nereids
       </a>
       , sea-nymphs of
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       and attendants of the god
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune_(mythology)" title="Neptune (mythology)">
        Neptune
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuiper_1949_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kuiper_1949-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was the second and last moon of Neptune to be discovered before the arrival of
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
        Voyager 2
       </a>
       (not counting a single observation of an occultation by
       <a href="/wiki/Larissa_(moon)" title="Larissa (moon)">
        Larissa
       </a>
       in 1981).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1989_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1989-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit_and_rotation">
        Orbit and rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nereid_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit and rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Nereid
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbits
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       direction at an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semimajor_axis" title="Semimajor axis">
        average distance
       </a>
       of 5,513,400 km (3,425,900 mi), but its high
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of 0.7507 takes it as close as 1,372,000 km (853,000 mi) and as far as 9,655,000 km (5,999,000 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL-SSD-Neptune_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-JPL-SSD-Neptune-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_2009-AJ_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson_2009-AJ-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The unusual orbit suggests that it may be either a captured
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       object, or that it was an inner moon in the past and was perturbed during the capture of Neptune's largest moon
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownKoreskoBlake_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BrownKoreskoBlake-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1991, a rotation period of Nereid of about 13.6 hours was determined by an analysis of its light curve.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams1991_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Williams1991-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2003, another rotation period of about
       <span class="nowrap">
        11.52 ± 0.14 hours
       </span>
       was measured.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GravHolmanKavelaars2003_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-GravHolmanKavelaars2003-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, this determination was later disputed, and other researchers for a time failed to detect any periodic modulation in Nereid's light curve from ground-based observations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaefer2008_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Shaefer2008-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2016, a clear rotation period of 11.594 ± 0.017 hours was determined based on observations with the
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(spacecraft)" title="Kepler (spacecraft)">
        Kepler space telescope
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nereid_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Nereid is Neptune's third-largest satellite and has an average radius of about 170 kilometres (110 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL-SSD-sat_phys_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-JPL-SSD-sat_phys-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is rather large for an
       <a href="/wiki/Irregular_moon" title="Irregular moon">
        irregular satellite
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GravHolmanKavelaars2003_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-GravHolmanKavelaars2003-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The shape of Nereid is unknown.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaefer2008_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Shaefer2008-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Since 1987 some photometric observations of Nereid have detected large (by ~1 of magnitude) variations of its brightness, which can happen over years and months, but sometimes even over a few days. They persist even after a correction for distance and phase effects. On the other hand, not all astronomers who have observed Nereid have noticed such variations. This means that they may be quite chaotic. To date there is no credible explanation of the variations, but, if they exist, they are likely related to the rotation of Nereid. Nereid's rotation could be either in the state of forced
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        precession
       </a>
       or even chaotic rotation (like
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(moon)" title="Hyperion (moon)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
       ) due to its highly elliptical orbit.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2016, extended observations with the Kepler space telescope showed only low-amplitude variations (0.033 magnitudes). Thermal modeling based on infrared observations from the
       <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">
        Spitzer
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory" title="Herschel Space Observatory">
        Herschel
       </a>
       space telescopes suggest that Nereid is only moderately elongated which disfavors forced precession of the rotation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The thermal model also indicates that the surface roughness of Nereid is very high, likely similar to the Saturnian moon
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(moon)" title="Hyperion (moon)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        Spectrally
       </a>
       , Nereid appears neutral in colour
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GravHolmanFraser2004_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GravHolmanFraser2004-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        water ice
       </a>
       has been detected on its surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownKoreskoBlake_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-BrownKoreskoBlake-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its spectrum appears to be intermediate between
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       's moons
       <a href="/wiki/Titania_(moon)" title="Titania (moon)">
        Titania
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)" title="Umbriel (moon)">
        Umbriel
       </a>
       , which suggests that Nereid's surface is composed of a mixture of water ice and some spectrally neutral material.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownKoreskoBlake_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-BrownKoreskoBlake-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The spectrum is markedly different from
       <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">
        minor planets
       </a>
       of the outer solar system,
       <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(minor_planet)" title="Centaur (minor planet)">
        centaurs
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/5145_Pholus" title="5145 Pholus">
        Pholus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/2060_Chiron" title="2060 Chiron">
        Chiron
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/10199_Chariklo" title="10199 Chariklo">
        Chariklo
       </a>
       , suggesting that Nereid formed around Neptune rather than being a captured body.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownKoreskoBlake_8-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-BrownKoreskoBlake-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Halimede_(moon)" title="Halimede (moon)">
        Halimede
       </a>
       , which has similar colours, may be a fragment of Nereid that was broken off during a collision.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GravHolmanFraser2004_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-GravHolmanFraser2004-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nereid_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The only spacecraft to visit Nereid is
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       , which passed it at a distance of 4,700,000 km (2,900,000 mi)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JonesB1991_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-JonesB1991-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       between 20 April and 19 August 1989.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson1991_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson1991-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       obtained 83 images with observation accuracies of 70 km (43 mi) to 800 km (500 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson1991_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson1991-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Prior to
       <i>
        Voyager 2'
       </i>
       s arrival, observations of Nereid had been limited to ground-based observations that could only establish its intrinsic brightness and
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_elements" title="Orbital elements">
        orbital elements
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-Pic_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-Pic-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although the images obtained by
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       do not have a high enough resolution to allow surface features to be distinguished,
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       was able to measure the size of Nereid and found that it was grey in colour and had a higher
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       than Neptune's other small satellites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1989_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1989-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">
        In fiction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nereid_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In fiction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the Larry Niven book
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ringworld" title="Ringworld">
         Ringworld
        </a>
       </i>
       , Nereid is described as having been leased by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_outsiders&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The outsiders (page does not exist)">
        the outsiders
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/23rd_century" title="23rd century">
        "half a millennium ago"
       </a>
       . The protagonist,
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_Wu" title="Louis Wu">
        Louis Wu
       </a>
       , speculates that the outsiders evolved on a gas giant moon similar to Nereid.
      </p>
