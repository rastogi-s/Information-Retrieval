      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Moon Mineralogy Mapper
       </b>
       (M
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ) is one of two instruments that
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       contributed to
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       's first mission to the Moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-1" title="Chandrayaan-1">
        Chandrayaan-1
       </a>
       , launched October 22, 2008. The instrument is led by principal investigator Carle Pieters of
       <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">
        Brown University
       </a>
       , and managed by NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">
        Jet Propulsion Laboratory
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Mineralogy_Mapper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       M
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       is an imaging
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
        spectrometer
       </a>
       that has provided the first high-resolution spatial and spectral map of the entire lunar surface, revealing the minerals of which it is made. This information will both provide clues to the early development of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">
        solar system
       </a>
       and guide future
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronauts" title="Astronauts">
        astronauts
       </a>
       to stores of precious resources.
      </p>
      <p>
       This instrument is a
       <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Program" title="Discovery Program">
        Discovery Program
       </a>
       "Mission of Opportunity" (a NASA-designed instrument on board another space agency's spacecraft).
      </p>
      <p>
       Chandrayaan-1 operated for 312 days as opposed to the intended two years but the mission achieved many of its planned objectives.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       M
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       was used to map over 95% of the lunar surface in its low-resolution Global mode, but only a small fraction in the high-resolution Target mode.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After suffering from several technical issues including failure of the star sensors and poor thermal shielding, Chandrayaan-1 stopped sending radio signals at 1:30 AM IST on 29 August 2009 shortly after which, the ISRO officially declared the mission over.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Water_discovered_on_moon">
        Water discovered on moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Mineralogy_Mapper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water discovered on moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Water_Around_Fresh_Moon_Crater.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="352" data-file-width="611" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Water_Around_Fresh_Moon_Crater.jpg/220px-Water_Around_Fresh_Moon_Crater.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Water_Around_Fresh_Moon_Crater.jpg/330px-Water_Around_Fresh_Moon_Crater.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Water_Around_Fresh_Moon_Crater.jpg/440px-Water_Around_Fresh_Moon_Crater.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Water_Around_Fresh_Moon_Crater.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         These images show water in a very young lunar crater on the side of the moon that faces away from
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       On September 24, 2009, the Science Magazine reported that
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's Moon Mineralogy Mapper (M
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ) on Chandrayaan-1 had detected water on the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       But, on 25 September 2009,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISRO" title="ISRO">
        ISRO
       </a>
       announced that the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_Impact_Probe" title="Moon Impact Probe">
        MIP
       </a>
       , another instrument on-board Chandrayaan-1 also had discovered water on the moon just before impact and had discovered it before M
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       . The announcement of this discovery was not made until NASA confirmed it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       M
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       detected absorption features near 2.8-3.0 µm on the surface of the Moon. For silicate bodies, such features are typically attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">
        OH
       </a>
       - and/or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H2o" title="H2o">
        H
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
        O
       </a>
       -bearing materials. On the Moon, the feature is seen as a widely distributed absorption that appears strongest at cooler high latitudes and at several fresh feldspathic craters. The general lack of correlation of this feature in sunlit M
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       data with neutron spectrometer H abundance data suggests that the formation and retention of OH and H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O is an ongoing surficial process. OH/H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O production processes may feed polar cold traps and make the lunar regolith a candidate source of volatiles for human exploration.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon Mineralogy Mapper (M
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ), an imaging spectrometer, was one of the 11 instruments on board Chandrayaan-I that came to a premature end on August 29. M
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       was aimed at providing the first mineral map of the entire lunar surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       Lunar scientists have for decades contended with the possibility of water repositories. They are now increasingly “confident that the decades-long debate is over,” a report says. “The moon, in fact, has water in all sorts of places; not just locked up in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">
        minerals
       </a>
       , but scattered throughout the broken-up
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_surface" title="Planetary surface">
        surface
       </a>
       , and, potentially, in blocks or sheets of ice at depth.” The results from the NASA’s Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter are also “offering a wide array of watery signals.”
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mg-spinel-rich_rock_discovered">
        Mg-spinel-rich rock discovered
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Mineralogy_Mapper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mg-spinel-rich rock discovered">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       M
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       found a rock dominated by Mg-
       <a href="/wiki/Spinel" title="Spinel">
        spinel
       </a>
       with no detectable
       <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">
        pyroxene
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">
        olivine
       </a>
       present (&lt;5%) occurring along the western inner ring of Moscoviense Basin (as one of several discrete areas). The occurrence of this spinel does not easily fit with current lunar-crustal evolution models.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
