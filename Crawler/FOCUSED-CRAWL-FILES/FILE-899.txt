      <p>
       <b>
        Atlas
       </b>
       is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellite" title="Inner satellite">
        inner satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Atlas was discovered by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Terrile" title="Richard Terrile">
        Richard Terrile
       </a>
       in 1980 (some time before November 12) from
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">
        Voyager
       </a>
       photos and was designated
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/1980 S 28
        </span>
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3539_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3539-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1983 it was officially named after
       <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(mythology)" title="Atlas (mythology)">
        Atlas
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       , because it "holds the rings on its shoulders" like the
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">
        Titan
       </a>
       Atlas held the sky up above the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also designated
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Saturn XV
        </span>
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Atlas is the closest satellite to the sharp outer edge of the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#A_Ring" title="Rings of Saturn">
        A ring
       </a>
       , and was long thought to be a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shepherd_satellite" title="Shepherd satellite">
        shepherd satellite
       </a>
       for this ring. However, now it is known that the outer edge of the ring is instead maintained by a 7:6
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       with the larger but more distant moons
       <a href="/wiki/Janus_(moon)" title="Janus (moon)">
        Janus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Epimetheus_(moon)" title="Epimetheus (moon)">
        Epimetheus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELakdawalla2007_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELakdawalla2007-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2004 a faint, thin ring, temporarily designated
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R/2004_S_1" title="R/2004 S 1">
         R/2004 S 1
        </a>
       </span>
       , was discovered in the Atlantean orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8401_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8401-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       High-resolution images taken in June 2005 by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       revealed Atlas to have a roughly spherical centre surrounded by a large, smooth
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_ridge" title="Equatorial ridge">
        equatorial ridge
       </a>
       . The most likely explanation for this unusual and prominent structure is that ring material swept up by the moon accumulates on the moon, with a strong preference for the equator due to the ring's thinness. In fact, the size of the equatorial ridge is comparable with the expected
       <a href="/wiki/Roche_lobe" title="Roche lobe">
        Roche lobe
       </a>
       of the moon. This would mean that for any additional particles impacting the equator, the
       <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force" title="Centrifugal force">
        centrifugal force
       </a>
       will nearly overcome Atlas's tiny gravity, and they will likely be lost.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELakdawalla2007_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELakdawalla2007-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Atlas is significantly perturbed by
       <a href="/wiki/Prometheus_(moon)" title="Prometheus (moon)">
        Prometheus
       </a>
       and to a lesser degree by
       <a href="/wiki/Pandora_(moon)" title="Pandora (moon)">
        Pandora
       </a>
       , leading to excursions in longitude of up to 600 km (~0.25°) away from the precessing Keplerian orbit with a rough period of about 3 years. Because the orbits of Prometheus and Pandora are chaotic, it is suspected that Atlas's may be as well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
