      <p>
       <b>
        Extraterrestrial real estate
       </b>
       refers to claims of land ownership on other
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellites
       </a>
       or parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">
        space
       </a>
       by certain organizations and individuals. Such claims are not recognized by any authority, and have no legal standing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoowns_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-whoowns-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nevertheless, some private individuals and organizations have claimed ownership of celestial bodies, such as the Moon, and are actively involved in "selling" parts of them through certificates of ownership termed "Lunar deeds",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoowns_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-whoowns-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "Martian deeds" or similar.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraterrestrial_real_estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The topic of real estate on celestial objects has been present since the 1890s. Dean Lindsay made claims for all extraterrestrial objects on June 15, 1936. The public sent offers to buy objects from him as well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoowns_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-whoowns-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">
        Legal issues
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraterrestrial_real_estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal issues">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="vertical-navbox nowraplinks hlist" style="float:right;clear:right;width:22.0em;margin:0 0 1.0em 1.0em;background:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;padding:0.2em;border-spacing:0.4em 0;text-align:center;line-height:1.4em;font-size:88%">

      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        United Nations
       </a>
       sponsored 1967 "
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">
        Outer Space Treaty
       </a>
       " established all of outer space as an international commons by describing it as the "province of all mankind" and forbidding all the nations from claiming territorial sovereignty.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Article VI vests the responsibility for activities in space to States Parties, regardless of whether they are carried out by governments or non-governmental entities. The Outer Space Treaty of 1967 has currently been ratified by 102 countries,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-treatystatus_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-treatystatus-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       including all the major space-faring nations. It has also been signed by 26 other nations but not yet ratified.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The international
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_Treaty" title="Moon Treaty">
        Moon Treaty
       </a>
       , finalised in 1979 and entering into force in 1984, forbids private ownership of extraterrestrial real estate.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This agreement has not been widely ratified.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-treatystatus_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-treatystatus-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Several individuals and private organizations claimed ownership of the moon and other extraterrestrial bodies, but no such claims has yet been recognized. A white paper by the Competitive Enterprise Institute suggested legislation whereby the US would recognize claims made by private entities, American and others, which meet certain conditions regarding habitation and transportation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Private_purchase_schemes">
        Private purchase schemes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraterrestrial_real_estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Private purchase schemes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A number of individuals and organisations offer schemes or plans claiming to allow people to purchase portions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       or other celestial bodies. Though the details of some of the schemes' legal arguments vary, one goes so far as to state that although the
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">
        Outer Space Treaty
       </a>
       , which entered force in 1967, forbids countries from claiming celestial bodies, there is no such provision forbidding private individuals from doing so. However, Article VI of this treaty states "The activities of non-governmental entities in outer space, including the moon and other celestial bodies, shall require authorization and continuing supervision by the appropriate State Party to the Treaty." Thus, while it does not explicitly prohibit such schemes, the treaty does require they be authorized by the schemers' government.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">
        short story
       </a>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Sold_the_Moon" title="The Man Who Sold the Moon">
         The Man Who Sold the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
        Robert A. Heinlein
       </a>
       , which was written in 1949, offers a portrayal regarding such plans or schemes, and created the concept of a Lunar Republic. Heinlein's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Stranger_in_a_Strange_Land" title="Stranger in a Strange Land">
         Stranger in a Strange Land
        </a>
       </i>
       also makes reference to a space law case called the
       <i>
        Larkin Decision
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership_of_empty_space">
        Ownership of empty space
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraterrestrial_real_estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ownership of empty space">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Ownership of empty space can be thought of as a different issue from that of land ownership on extraterrestrial bodies, because of its emptiness, the difficulty of defining its bounds, and the difficulty of keeping anything within it. The United Nations "Outer Space Treaty" reserves space out of the atmosphere for the good of mankind, and effectively prohibits private ownership of arbitrary parcels of empty space, although governments which have not signed the relevant treaties may dispute the UN's authority in this matter.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geosynchronous_orbits">
        Geosynchronous orbits
        <span id="Bogota_Declaration">
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraterrestrial_real_estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geosynchronous orbits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A space ownership issue of current practical importance is the allocation of slots for satellites in
       <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">
        geostationary orbit
       </a>
       . This is managed by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">
        International Telecommunication Union
       </a>
       . The 1976 Declaration of the First Meeting of Equatorial Countries, also known as the
       <b>
        Bogotá Declaration
       </b>
       , signed by several countries located on the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       , attempted to assert sovereignty over those portions of the geosynchronous orbit that continuously lie over the signatory nation's territory.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These claims did not receive wider international support or recognition and were subsequently largely abandoned.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_claims">
        Notable claims
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraterrestrial_real_estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notable claims">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Chilean lawyer
       <a href="/wiki/Jenaro_Gajardo_Vera" title="Jenaro Gajardo Vera">
        Jenaro Gajardo Vera
       </a>
       became famous for his 1953 claim of ownership of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoowns_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-whoowns-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Martin Juergens from Germany claims that the Moon has belonged to his family since July 15, 1756, when the Prussian king Frederick the Great presented it to his ancestor Aul Juergens as a symbolic gesture of gratitude for services rendered, and decreed that it should pass to the youngest born son.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A. Dean Lindsay made claims for all extraterrestrial objects on June 15, 1936, and sent a letter to Pittsburgh Notary Public along with a deed and money for establishment of the property. The public sent offers to buy objects from him as well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoowns_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-whoowns-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He had previously made claims on the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/James_T._Mangan" title="James T. Mangan">
        James T. Mangan
       </a>
       (1896–1970) was a famous eccentric, public relations man and best-selling author on self-help topics who publicly claimed ownership of
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">
        outer space
       </a>
       in 1948. Mangan founded what he called the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Nation_of_Celestial_Space" title="Nation of Celestial Space">
         Nation of Celestial Space
        </a>
       </i>
       and registered it with the Recorder of Deeds and Titles of
       <a href="/wiki/Cook_County,_Illinois" title="Cook County, Illinois">
        Cook County, Illinois
       </a>
       , on January 1, 1949.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Robert R. Coles, former chairman of New York's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hayden_Planetarium" title="Hayden Planetarium">
        Hayden Planetarium
       </a>
       , started "the interplanetary Development Corporation"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and sold lots on the moon for one dollar per acre.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dennis Hope, an
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        American
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entrepreneur" title="Entrepreneur">
        entrepreneur
       </a>
       , sells extraterrestrial real estate.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-geo-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1980, he started his own business, the Lunar Embassy Commission.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of 2009 Hope claimed to have sold 2.5M 1-acre plots on the Moon, for around US$20 per acre. He allocates land to be sold by closing his eyes and randomly pointing to a map of the Moon. He claims two former US presidents as customers, stating
       <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">
        Jimmy Carter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">
        Ronald Reagan
       </a>
       had aides purchase them plots on the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1997, Adam Ismail, Mustafa Khalil and Abdullah al-Umari, three men from Yemen, sued
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       for invading Mars. They claim that they "inherited the planet from our ancestors 3,000 years ago", before
       <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">
        Muhammad
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They based their argument on mythologies of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sabaean_language" title="Sabaean language">
        Himyaritic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sabaeans" title="Sabaeans">
        Sabaean
       </a>
       civilizations that existed several thousand years B.C.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Gregory W. Nemitz claimed ownership of Asteroid
       <a href="/wiki/433_Eros" title="433 Eros">
        433 Eros
       </a>
       , on which
       <a href="/wiki/NEAR_Shoemaker" title="NEAR Shoemaker">
        NEAR Shoemaker
       </a>
       landed in 2001. His company, called "Orbital Development",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       issued NASA an invoice of $20 for parking the spacecraft at the asteroid. NASA declined to pay, citing the lack of legal standing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After purchasing the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_2" title="Lunokhod 2">
        Lunokhod 2
       </a>
       lunar lander, computer game designer and astronaut's son
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Garriott" title="Richard Garriott">
        Richard Garriott
       </a>
       jokingly claimed the rest of the Moon in the name of his gaming character,
       <a href="/wiki/Lord_British" title="Lord British">
        Lord British
       </a>
       , under the premise that existing treaties prohibit governments from territorial claims to the Moon, but not individuals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       British singer
       <a href="/wiki/Mike_Scott_(musician)" title="Mike Scott (musician)">
        Mike Scott
       </a>
       claimed in 1982 to be the sole owner of the moon, and wrote a song about it,
       <a href="/wiki/The_Whole_of_the_Moon" title="The Whole of the Moon">
        The Whole of the Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
