      <p>
       A
       <b>
        total
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
         lunar eclipse
        </a>
       </b>
       took place on 4 April 2015. It is the former of two total lunar eclipses in 2015, and the third in a
       <a href="/wiki/Tetrad_(astronomy)" title="Tetrad (astronomy)">
        <i>
         tetrad
        </i>
       </a>
       (four total lunar eclipses in series). Other eclipses in the tetrad are those of
       <a href="/wiki/April_2014_lunar_eclipse" title="April 2014 lunar eclipse">
        15 April 2014
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/October_2014_lunar_eclipse" title="October 2014 lunar eclipse">
        8 October 2014
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/September_2015_lunar_eclipse" title="September 2015 lunar eclipse">
        28 September 2015
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       This is the 30th member of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Saros_132" title="Lunar Saros 132">
        Lunar Saros 132
       </a>
       , and the first total eclipse. The previous event was the
       <a href="/wiki/March_1997_lunar_eclipse" title="March 1997 lunar eclipse">
        March 1997 lunar eclipse
       </a>
       , being slightly partial.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Duration">
        Duration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=April_2015_lunar_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Duration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Totality lasted only 4 minutes and 44 seconds,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       making it the shortest lunar totality in almost five centuries since 17 October 1529 (which lasted 1 minute and 42 seconds). Another shortest occurs on December 28 1917, lasting (11 minutes and 58 seconds). The next very short lunar totality will occur on 26 May 2021 (which will last 14 minutes and 24 seconds). This was the sixth total lunar eclipse out of nine with totality under 5 minutes in a five millennium period between 2,000 BC and 3,000 AD.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, due to the oblateness of the Earth, this lunar eclipse may have actually been (barely) a partial eclipse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This eclipsed moon was 12.9% smaller in apparent diameter than the supermoon
       <a href="/wiki/September_2015_lunar_eclipse" title="September 2015 lunar eclipse">
        September 2015 lunar eclipse
       </a>
       , measured as 29.66' and 33.47' in diameter from the center of the earth. It occurred 3 days before
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
        apogee
       </a>
       at 29.42'.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Visibility">

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <p>
       The eclipse was visible across the Pacific, including all of

        Australia
       </a>
       and

        New Zealand
       </a>
       . It was visible near sunrise for
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       , and after sunset for eastern Asia including
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       .

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=April_2015_lunar_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        Lunar eclipse
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A lunar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes within Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
        umbra
       </a>
       (shadow). As the eclipse begins, Earth's shadow first darkens the Moon slightly. Then, the shadow begins to "cover" part of the Moon, turning it a dark red-brown color (typically - the color can vary based on atmospheric conditions). The Moon appears to be reddish because of
       <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">
        Rayleigh scattering
       </a>
       (the same effect that causes sunsets to appear reddish) and the refraction of that light by Earth's atmosphere into its umbra.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The following simulation shows the approximate appearance of the Moon passing through Earth's shadow. The Moon's brightness is exaggerated within the umbral shadow. The southern portion of the Moon will be closest to the center of the shadow, making it darkest, and most red in appearance.
      </p>

      <p>
       The timing of total lunar eclipses are determined by its contacts:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <b>
         P1
        </b>
        (First contact): Beginning of the penumbral eclipse. Earth's penumbra touches the Moon's outer limb.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         U1
        </b>
        (Second contact): Beginning of the partial eclipse. Earth's umbra touches the Moon's outer limb.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         U2
        </b>
        (Third contact): Beginning of the total eclipse. The Moon's surface is entirely within Earth's umbra.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Greatest eclipse
        </b>
        : The peak stage of the total eclipse. The Moon is at its closest to the center of Earth's umbra.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         U3
        </b>
        (Fourth contact): End of the total eclipse. The Moon's outer limb exits Earth's umbra.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         U4
        </b>
        (Fifth contact): End of the partial eclipse. Earth's umbra leaves the Moon's surface.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         P4
        </b>
        (Sixth contact): End of the penumbral eclipse. Earth's penumbra no longer makes contact with the Moon.
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_year_series">
        Lunar year series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=April_2015_lunar_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Lunar year series">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The eclipse is the one of four lunar eclipses in a short-lived series at the ascending node of the moon's orbit.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_year" title="Lunar year">
        lunar year
       </a>
       series repeats after 12
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">
        lunations
       </a>
       , or 354 days (shifting back about 10 days in sequential years). Because of the date shift, the Earth's shadow will be about 11 degrees west in sequential events.
      </p>
