      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Destination Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       (French:
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         <b>
          Objectif Lune
         </b>
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is the sixteenth volume of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="The Adventures of Tintin">
         The Adventures of Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       , the comics series by Belgian cartoonist
       <a href="/wiki/Herg%C3%A9" title="Hergé">
        Hergé
       </a>
       . The story was initially
       <a href="/wiki/Serial_(literature)" title="Serial (literature)">
        serialised
       </a>
       weekly in Belgium's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tintin_(magazine)" title="Tintin (magazine)">
         Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine from March to September 1950 before being published in a collected volume by
       <a href="/wiki/Casterman" title="Casterman">
        Casterman
       </a>
       in 1953. The plot tells of young reporter
       <a href="/wiki/Tintin_(character)" title="Tintin (character)">
        Tintin
       </a>
       and his friend
       <a href="/wiki/Captain_Haddock" title="Captain Haddock">
        Captain Haddock
       </a>
       who receive an invitation from
       <a href="/wiki/Professor_Calculus" title="Professor Calculus">
        Professor Calculus
       </a>
       to come to
       <a href="/wiki/Syldavia" title="Syldavia">
        Syldavia
       </a>
       , where Calculus is working on a top-secret project in a secure government facility to plan a manned mission to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Developed in part through the suggestions of Hergé's friends
       <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Heuvelmans" title="Bernard Heuvelmans">
        Bernard Heuvelmans
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Van_Melkebeke" title="Jacques Van Melkebeke">
        Jacques Van Melkebeke
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       was produced following Hergé's extensive research into the possibility of human space travel – a feat that had yet to be achieved – with the cartoonist seeking for the work to be as realistic as possible. During the story's serialisation, Hergé established
       <a href="/wiki/Studios_Herg%C3%A9" title="Studios Hergé">
        Studios Hergé
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">
        Brussels
       </a>
       -based team of cartoonists to aid him on the project. Hergé concluded the story arc begun in this volume with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Explorers_on_the_Moon" title="Explorers on the Moon">
         Explorers on the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       , while the series itself became a defining part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Franco-Belgian_comics" title="Franco-Belgian comics">
        Franco-Belgian comics tradition
       </a>
       . Critics have praised the illustrative detail of the book, but have expressed mixed views of the narrative. The story was adapted for both the 1957
       <a href="/wiki/Belvision_Studios" title="Belvision Studios">
        Belvision
       </a>
       animated series,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Herg%C3%A9%27s_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="Hergé's Adventures of Tintin">
         Hergé's Adventures of Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       , and for the 1991 animated series
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin_(TV_series)" title="The Adventures of Tintin (TV series)">
         The Adventures of Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse_Programm%C3%A9" title="Ellipse Programmé">
        Ellipse
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nelvana" title="Nelvana">
        Nelvana
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">
        Synopsis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synopsis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Tintin,
       <a href="/wiki/Snowy_(character)" title="Snowy (character)">
        Snowy
       </a>
       , and Captain Haddock travel to join Professor Calculus, who has been commissioned by the
       <a href="/wiki/Syldavia" title="Syldavia">
        Syldavian
       </a>
       government to secretly build a
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       that will fly to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . Arriving at the Sprodj Atomic Research Centre, they meet the Centre's managing director,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mr._Baxter" title="Mr. Baxter">
        Mr. Baxter
       </a>
       , and Calculus' assistant, the engineer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_Wolff_(comics)" title="Frank Wolff (comics)">
        Frank Wolff
       </a>
       . After witnessing Calculus test out a new multiplex helmet for the planned mission, they are informed of the plan. Haddock is against the idea, but due to his hearing problems, Calculus instead believes he agreed. An unmanned sub-scale prototype of the rocket — the "X-FLR6" — is launched on a
       <a href="/wiki/Circumlunar_trajectory" title="Circumlunar trajectory">
        circumlunar
       </a>
       mission to photograph the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side of the Moon
       </a>
       and test Calculus's
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_thermal_rocket" title="Nuclear thermal rocket">
        nuclear rocket engine
       </a>
       . Before the launch, the centre's radar picks up a plane which drops three paratroopers near to the centre. One of the men dies from a malfunctioning parachute; the incident coincides with the arrival of the police detectives
       <a href="/wiki/Thomson_and_Thompson" title="Thomson and Thompson">
        Thomson and Thompson
       </a>
       , who are initially believed to be the intruders.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHergé19591–19_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHergé19591–19-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Tintin sets out to locate the spies, telling Haddock to follow from the base, as he suspects a mole is on the inside. Wolff follows Haddock out of suspicion. When Tintin spots the paratroopers, he is shot before he is able to do anything. Simultaneously, the base experiences a power outage, and confusion ensues, with neither Haddock nor Wolff capable of explaining clearly what happened.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHergé195920–22_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHergé195920–22-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This incident confirms the Centre's suspicions that the paratroopers were agents of a foreign power, but Tintin fears that efforts to trace any leaked information would be futile. The rocket is successfully launched and orbits the moon as planned, but on its return it is then intercepted by the aforementioned foreign power, who have used the leaked information concerning the rocket's radio control. However, Tintin had anticipated this and asked Calculus to rig a self-destruct mechanism for the rocket, and the Centre destroys the rocket to prevent it from falling into enemy hands. Tintin reasons that there must have been an inside
       <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">
        spy
       </a>
       who leaked information to the paratroopers, but no suspects are found. Preparations are made for the manned expedition to the moon, but after an argument with Haddock, in which Haddock accuses Calculus of "acting the goat", Calculus becomes irate. Usually misunderstanding everything Haddock says, due to being hard of hearing, Calculus was wearing a hearing aid this time, and understood Haddock all too well. Implacable, Calculus angrily gives Haddock a forced tour of the moon rocket. However, in doing so, Calculus forgets to look where he is going, falls down a ladder and suffers
       <a href="/wiki/Amnesia" title="Amnesia">
        amnesia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHergé195923–46_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHergé195923–46-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When Calculus' memory fails to be brought back, Haddock opts to use a violent shock to overcome Calculus' amnesia, though his attempts repeatedly backfire. Eventually, after repeating the phrase "acting the goat", Haddock successfully triggers Calculus' recovery. After regaining his knowledge on the rocket, construction is eventually completed, and the final preparations are made. On the night of the launch, Haddock initially backs out, but after hearing Thomson and Thompson feeling he would be too old to go, he angrily declares he will go. The crew finally board the rocket, and later lose consciousness as the rocket successfully takes off, due to the sudden
       <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">
        g-force
       </a>
       . Despite attempting to make contact, the ground crew are unable to get through, and the book ends with the rocket flying towards the moon while the ground crew repeatedly calls, "Moon Rocket, are you receiving me?".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHergé195947–62_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHergé195947–62-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Hergé first devised the idea of sending Tintin on a mission to the Moon while he was working on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_the_Sun" title="Prisoners of the Sun">
         Prisoners of the Sun
        </a>
       </i>
       (1949).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His decision to move into the field of
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       might have been influenced by his friendly rivalry with his colleague
       <a href="/wiki/Edgar_P._Jacobs" title="Edgar P. Jacobs">
        Edgar P. Jacobs
       </a>
       , who had recently had success with his own science fiction comic,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Secret_of_the_Swordfish" title="The Secret of the Swordfish">
         The Secret of the Swordfish
        </a>
       </i>
       (1950–53).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012218_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012218-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He decided that it would be a two-volume story arc, as had proved successful with his earlier arcs,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Secret_of_the_Unicorn" title="The Secret of the Unicorn">
         The Secret of the Unicorn
        </a>
       </i>
       (1943) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Red_Rackham%27s_Treasure" title="Red Rackham's Treasure">
         Red Rackham's Treasure
        </a>
       </i>
       (1944), and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Seven_Crystal_Balls" title="The Seven Crystal Balls">
         The Seven Crystal Balls
        </a>
       </i>
       (1948) and
       <i>
        Prisoners of the Sun
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He had initially intended on beginning this story after the culmination of
       <i>
        Prisoners of the Sun
       </i>
       , but both his wife Germaine Remi and his close friend Marcel Dehaye convinced him to proceed with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Black_Gold" title="Land of Black Gold">
         Land of Black Gold
        </a>
       </i>
       (1950), a story that he had previously left unfinished, instead.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin2009189_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin2009189-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:V-2_lift-off.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1145" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/V-2_lift-off.jpg/220px-V-2_lift-off.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/V-2_lift-off.jpg/330px-V-2_lift-off.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/V-2_lift-off.jpg/440px-V-2_lift-off.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:V-2_lift-off.jpg" title="Suurenna">
          </a>
         </div>
         A German V-2 rocket being launched in 1945. The V-2 would serve as a major inspiration for Hergé in his work on both
         <i>
          Destination Moon
         </i>
         and
         <i>
          Explorers on the Moon
         </i>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Seeking advice on the story, Hergé consulted his friend
       <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Heuvelmans" title="Bernard Heuvelmans">
        Bernard Heuvelmans
       </a>
       , who had authored the non-fiction book
       <i>
        L'Homme parmi les étoiles
       </i>
       ("Man Among the Stars") (1944).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012218_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012218-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In autumn 1947, Heuvelmans and
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Van_Melkebeke" title="Jacques Van Melkebeke">
        Jacques Van Melkebeke
       </a>
       developed a script for the story, which they gave to Hergé. This version based Calculus' lunar expedition in a fictional location, Radio City, in the United States. It featured a return of Professor Decimus Phostle, a character who had previously appeared in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Shooting_Star" title="The Shooting Star">
         The Shooting Star
        </a>
       </i>
       (1942), but this time as an antagonist; Phostle had sold the secrets of the mission in order to attain funds to buy a diamond for the actress
       <a href="/wiki/Rita_Hayworth" title="Rita Hayworth">
        Rita Hayworth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991138–139Farr2001138LofficierLofficier200265Peeters2012218_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991138–139Farr2001138LofficierLofficier200265Peeters2012218-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In early 1948, Hergé produced two black-and-white pages of this version of the story before abandoning it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994Thompson1991139Peeters2012218_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994Thompson1991139Peeters2012218-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé retained some elements of this original script in his finished version, namely the scenes in which Haddock drinks whiskey in a gravity-free environment and that in which Haddock goes for a space walk and nearly becomes a satellite of Adonis, which appear on pages 5 and 8 of
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991139Peeters2012220–221_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991139Peeters2012220–221-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nevertheless, Heuvelmans thought his influence on the story to be more significant, stating that "In going through the two books we [he and Van Melkebeke] really had the impression that it was what we had originally done at the beginning. In broad outline, that was it."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009172_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009172-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Hergé hoped for the story to be as realistic as possible, and sought to eschew fantastical elements.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994Farr2001135_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994Farr2001135-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In his own words, it contained "no moonmen, no monsters, no incredible surprises".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198994-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To ensure this realism, he collected a wide range of documents about
       <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">
        rockets
       </a>
       and space travel with which to conduct research.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this he was aided by Heuvelmans, who collected pictures of rockets and atomic research facilities for him.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009172Peeters2012222_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009172Peeters2012222-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé's research archive included
       <a href="/wiki/Man_Will_Conquer_Space_Soon!" title="Man Will Conquer Space Soon!">
        an article
       </a>
       from the American magazine
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Collier%27s" title="Collier's">
         Collier's
        </a>
       </i>
       which discussed how humanity could reach the moon,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as well as books by
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Rousseau" title="Pierre Rousseau">
        Pierre Rousseau
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Piccard" title="Auguste Piccard">
        Auguste Piccard
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009170_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009170-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A further work that he used was
       <i>
        L'Astronautique
       </i>
       (1950), a book on putative space travel by the physicist
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Ananoff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alexander Ananoff (page does not exist)">
        Alexander Ananoff
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265Goddin20118_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265Goddin20118-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with whom Hergé began a correspondence in April 1950.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012225_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012225-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also visited the
       <a href="/wiki/Ateliers_de_Constructions_Electriques_de_Charleroi" title="Ateliers de Constructions Electriques de Charleroi">
        Ateliers de Constructions Electriques de Charleroi
       </a>
       's Center for Atomic Research, striking up a subsequent correspondence with its director,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Hoyaux&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Max Hoyaux (page does not exist)">
        Max Hoyaux
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009170–171Peeters2012225_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009170–171Peeters2012225-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé incorporated much of this technical information into the story, but juxtaposed it with moments of humour to make it more accessible to his young readership.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991143_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991143-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to literary critics
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Marc_Lofficier" title="Jean-Marc Lofficier">
        Jean-Marc Lofficier
       </a>
       and Randy Lofficier, possible fictional influences on Hergé's story include
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
        Jules Verne
       </a>
       's 1870 novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Around_the_Moon" title="Around the Moon">
         Around the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       and the 1950 American film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(film)" title="Destination Moon (film)">
         Destination Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé was certainly inspired by a number of photographic stills from the
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       film which had been published.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201110_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201110-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The computer system at the Sprodj space centre was visually based upon the
       <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC_I" title="UNIVAC I">
        UNIVAC I
       </a>
       , the first computer to be created for non-military purposes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201122_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201122-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé based his moon rocket on the designs of the
       <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">
        V-2 rocket
       </a>
       which had been developed by German scientists during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136_14-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001136-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The red-and-white checker pattern on Hergé's rocket was based upon an illustration of a V-2 which Hergé had come upon in
       <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Earl_Simon" title="Leslie Earl Simon">
        Leslie Simon
       </a>
       's 1947 book
       <i>
        German Research in World War II
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He commissioned the construction of a model rocket with detachable parts from his assistant Arthur Van Noeyen. He took the model to
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       where he showed it to Ananoff, asking him if it was realistic representation of what a moon rocket might look like. He and his then used the model from which to accurately sketch when producing the comic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198995Thompson1991142–143Farr2001136Assouline2009171Peeters2012227_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198995Thompson1991142–143Farr2001136Assouline2009171Peeters2012227-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Hergé also inserted a cameo of Jacobs into the story, using him as the basis for a scientist that appears on page 40 of
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       , a nod to Jacobs' inclusion of a reference to Hergé in one of his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Blake_and_Mortimer" title="Blake and Mortimer">
         Blake and Mortimer
        </a>
       </i>
       mysteries,
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mystery_of_the_Great_Pyramid" title="The Mystery of the Great Pyramid">
         The Mystery of the Great Pyramid
        </a>
       </i>
       (1954).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001141Goddin201120Peeters2012243_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001141Goddin201120Peeters2012243-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Publication">
        Publication
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Publication">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:194px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tintin_magazine%27s_On_a_Marche_sur_la_Lune.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="277" data-file-width="192" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e1/Tintin_magazine%27s_On_a_Marche_sur_la_Lune.jpg" width="192"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tintin_magazine%27s_On_a_Marche_sur_la_Lune.jpg" title="Suurenna">
          </a>
         </div>
         The cover of
         <i>
          Tintin
         </i>
         magazine that first announced the impending Moon adventure
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Hergé announced the upcoming story with two consecutive covers of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tintin_(magazine)" title="Tintin (magazine)">
         Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine each depicting the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin20117_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin20117-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The story began serialisation in the Belgium's
       <i>
        Tintin
       </i>
       magazine from 30 March 1950, in the French language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200263_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200263-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It then began serialisation over the border in France, in that country's edition of the magazine, from 11 May 1950.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200263_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200263-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During this time, there were changes to how Hergé conducted his work; on 6 April 1950, he established
       <a href="/wiki/Studios_Herg%C3%A9" title="Studios Hergé">
        Studios Hergé
       </a>
       as a
       <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">
        public company
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001141Assouline2009147Peeters2012226_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001141Assouline2009147Peeters2012226-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Studios were based in his Avenue Delleur house in
       <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">
        Brussels
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012226_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012226-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with Hergé making a newly purchased country house in
       <a href="/wiki/Ottignies-Louvain-la-Neuve" title="Ottignies-Louvain-la-Neuve">
        Céroux-Mousty
       </a>
       his and Germaine's main abode.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012229_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012229-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Studios would provide both personal support to Hergé and technical support for his ongoing work.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009148_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009148-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He hired
       <a href="/wiki/Bob_de_Moor" title="Bob de Moor">
        Bob de Moor
       </a>
       as his primary apprentice at the Studios in March 1951.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009152–153Peeters2012231_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009152–153Peeters2012231-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The story was collected together and published by
       <a href="/wiki/Casterman" title="Casterman">
        Editions Casterman
       </a>
       as
       <i>
        Objectif Lune
       </i>
       in 1953.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200263_27-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200263-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The title had been Hergé's own choice, having rejected Casterman's suggestion of
       <i>
        Tintin and the Nuclear Rocket
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201127_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201127-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For publication in book form, the story was re-coloured, with various changes being made; for instance, in the serialised version, the uniforms worn by staff were green, and they are changed to blue for the book volume.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265_21-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A number of scenes were also deleted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265_21-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200265-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé sent a copy to Ananoff, with a message stating that "Your help, your knowledge, has been invaluable, enabling me to get my little characters to the Moon... and to bring them back safe and sound."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201138_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201138-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An English-language translation of the book was published in 1959.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Marking the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       Moon landings in 1969, Hergé produced an illustration in which
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       astronaut
       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>
       has just arrived on the moon, only to be greeted by Tintin, Snowy, Haddock, and Calculus carrying roses and a sign welcoming him.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That same year, the French magazine
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Paris_Match" title="Paris Match">
         Paris Match
        </a>
       </i>
       commissioned him to produce a short comic documenting the Apollo 12 landings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135LofficierLofficier200266_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001135LofficierLofficier200266-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_analysis">
        Critical analysis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical analysis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Jean-Marc Lofficier and Randy Lofficier believed that the two-part story "belongs" to Calculus, because his "cosmic vision moves the story forward".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200264_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200264-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They further expressed the opinion that Wolff was a unique character in the
       <i>
        Adventures of Tintin
       </i>
       , suggesting that he is akin to a character from a
       <a href="/wiki/John_le_Carr%C3%A9" title="John le Carré">
        John le Carré
       </a>
       novel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200264_36-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200264-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They felt that the moon adventure was "Hergé at his best... a triumphant achievement on every level", awarding both halves of the story five stars out of five.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200266_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200266-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Harry_Thompson" title="Harry Thompson">
        Harry Thompson
       </a>
       described the entire moon adventure as "a technical masterpiece" as a result of its "uncannily accurate" depiction of the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991138_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991138-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hergé biographer
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Assouline" title="Pierre Assouline">
        Pierre Assouline
       </a>
       felt that the two moon adventures "mark a stage in the development of Hergé's work".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009174_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009174-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Conversely, Hergé biographer
       <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Peeters" title="Benoît Peeters">
        Benoît Peeters
       </a>
       was critical of the two-part story arc, stating that they had "neither the liveliness and dynamism" of
       <i>
        The Secret of the Unicorn
       </i>
       -
       <i>
        Red Rackham's Treasure
       </i>
       , "nor the supernatural quality" of
       <i>
        The Seven Crystal Balls
       </i>
       -
       <i>
        Prisoners of the Sun
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters198997_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters198997-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In his
       <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">
        psychoanalytical
       </a>
       study of the
       <i>
        Adventures of Tintin
       </i>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Literary_criticism" title="Literary criticism">
        literary critic
       </a>
       Jean-Marie Apostolidès praised the
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       -
       <i>
        Explorers on the Moon
       </i>
       story arc for its "meticulous attention to scientific facts", but added that this had also resulted in the story's "rather pedagogical tone".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010179_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010179-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He added that in these stories, the main division was "no longer Good and Evil" as it had been in previous
       <i>
        Adventures
       </i>
       , but "Truth and Error".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010179_41-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010179-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Apostolidès opined that despite being a "fussy and somewhat ridiculous character", through his scientific achievements Calculus grows to the "stature of a giant" in this arc, eclipsing Sir Francis Haddock (from
       <i>
        The Secret of the Unicorn
       </i>
       ) as the series' "founding ancestor".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010182_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010182-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He goes on to claim that in becoming the "sacred ancestor", the voyage to the moon becomes "a mystical quest" with science as its guiding religion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010184_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010184-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Drawing comparisons between this arc and the
       <i>
        Prisoners of the Sun
       </i>
       story, he drew symbolic links between the scientific centre and the Inca Temple of the Sun, but noted that here Calculus was the "high priest" rather than the sacrificial victim as he had been in the previous story.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010184–185_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010184–185-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moving on to discuss the moon rocket in these stories, Apostolidès described it as a phallic object which penetrated the "virgin territory" of the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010186_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010186-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the same time, he described the rocket as a "maternal belly" in which the space explorers slept.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010186_45-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolidès2010186-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Literary critic
       <a href="/wiki/Tom_McCarthy_(novelist)" title="Tom McCarthy (novelist)">
        Tom McCarthy
       </a>
       stated that in the
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       -
       <i>
        Explorers of the Moon
       </i>
       story arc, Calculus "embodies Hergé's... own wartime position, spun out into a post-war environment", representing a genius driven by his work whose activities are coincidental to national and political causes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200642_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200642-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He suggested that the scene in which Captain Haddock rides a pantomime
       <a href="/wiki/Hobby_horse" title="Hobby horse">
        hobby horse
       </a>
       can be contrasted with the many scenes in which Haddock is thrown off of a horse's back during the
       <i>
        Adventures
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006132_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006132-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       McCarthy also interpreted the scene in which Calculus cries and tears at his hair in response to the rocket's capture by the enemy as reflecting Hergé's own anxieties at having his work published and reinterpreted by his readers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006189_48-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006189-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was also of the opinion that several scenes in
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       reflected recurring themes throughout the series; he suggested that Haddock's attempts at smuggling whisky into Syldavia echoed the smuggling of treasure in
       <i>
        The Secret of the Unicorn
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006127_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006127-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while the "correction" in which Thompson and Thomson were recognised as not being spies reflected a theme of corrections in the series.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200623_50-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200623-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also believed that there was an example of Hergé's secret vulgarity within
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       , with a diagram above Calculus' head in one scene resembling
       <a href="/wiki/Buttocks" title="Buttocks">
        buttocks
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006109_51-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006109-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation">
        Adaptation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Destination_Moon_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Adaptation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1957, the animation company
       <a href="/wiki/Belvision_Studios" title="Belvision Studios">
        Belvision Studios
       </a>
       produced
       <a href="/wiki/Herg%C3%A9%27s_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="Hergé's Adventures of Tintin">
        a string of colour adaptations
       </a>
       based on Hergé's original comics, adapting eight of the
       <i>
        Adventures
       </i>
       into a series of daily five-minute episodes.
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       was the first to be adapted in the second animated series; it was directed by Ray Goossens and written by
       <a href="/wiki/Greg_(cartoonist)" title="Greg (cartoonist)">
        Greg
       </a>
       , a well-known cartoonist who was to become editor-in-chief of
       <i>
        Tintin
       </i>
       magazine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200287–88_52-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200287–88-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1991, a
       <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin_(TV_series)" title="The Adventures of Tintin (TV series)">
        second animated series
       </a>
       based upon
       <i>
        The Adventures of Tintin
       </i>
       was produced, this time as a collaboration between the French studio
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        Ellipse
       </a>
       and the Canadian animation company
       <a href="/wiki/Nelvana" title="Nelvana">
        Nelvana
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       was the fourteenth story to be adapted and was divided into two twenty-minute episodes. Directed by Stéphane Bernasconi, the series has been praised for being "generally faithful" to the original comics, to the extent that the animation was directly adopted from Hergé's original panels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200290_53-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200290-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
