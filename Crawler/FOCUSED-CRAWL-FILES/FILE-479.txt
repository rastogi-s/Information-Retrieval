   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Moonmilk
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bergmilchkammer_04.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="531" data-file-width="800" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Bergmilchkammer_04.jpg/220px-Bergmilchkammer_04.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Bergmilchkammer_04.jpg/330px-Bergmilchkammer_04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Bergmilchkammer_04.jpg/440px-Bergmilchkammer_04.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bergmilchkammer_04.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Moonmilk ostensibly created from Macromonas bipunctata in the cave Bergmilchkammer
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Moonmilk
       </b>
       (sometimes called
       <b>
        mondmilch
       </b>
       , also known as
       <b>
        montmilch
       </b>
       or as
       <b>
        cave milk
       </b>
       ) is a white, creamy substance found inside limestone
       <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">
        caves
       </a>
       . It is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">
        precipitate
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">
        limestone
       </a>
       comprising aggregates of fine
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">
        crystals
       </a>
       of varying composition usually made of
       <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">
        carbonates
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">
        calcite
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">
        aragonite
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hydromagnesite" title="Hydromagnesite">
        hydromagnesite
       </a>
       , and/or
       <a href="/wiki/Monohydrocalcite" title="Monohydrocalcite">
        monohydrocalcite
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There are several hypotheses concerning the origin of moonmilk. One of these explains moonmilk to be the result of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">
        bacterial
       </a>
       action rather than from chemical reactions. According to this particular hypothesis, moonmilk is thought to have been created by the bacterium
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Macromonas_bipunctata" title="Macromonas bipunctata">
         Macromonas bipunctata
        </a>
       </i>
       . However, no microbiological studies have been carried out so far. Moonmilk was originally explained as created by "
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        moon
       </a>
       rays".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessner_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gessner-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is possible that moonmilk is formed by water that dissolves and softens the
       <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">
        karst
       </a>
       of caves consisting of carbonates, and carries dissolved nutrients that can be used by microbes, such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actinomycetes" title="Actinomycetes">
        Actinomycetes
       </a>
       . As the microbial colonies grow, they trap and accumulate chemically-precipitated crystals in the organic matter-rich matrix formed that way. Perhaps these
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotrophic" title="Heterotrophic">
        heterotrophic
       </a>
       microbes, which produce CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_product" title="Waste product">
        waste product
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">
        respiration
       </a>
       and possibly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_acids" title="Organic acids">
        organic acids
       </a>
       , help to dissolve the carbonate.
      </p>
      <p>
       Being soft, moonmilk was frequently the medium for
       <a href="/wiki/Finger_fluting" title="Finger fluting">
        finger fluting
       </a>
       , a form of
       <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_art" title="Prehistoric art">
        prehistoric art
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The world's largest formation of
       <a href="/wiki/Brushite" title="Brushite">
        brushite
       </a>
       moonmilk is found in the Big Room of
       <a href="/wiki/Kartchner_Caverns_State_Park" title="Kartchner Caverns State Park">
        Kartchner Caverns State Park
       </a>
       in southern Arizona.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the middle of 16th century moonmilk was used as a medicine according to Gessner, and continued to be used as such until the 19th century. It is said to have cured
       <a href="/wiki/Acidosis" title="Acidosis">
        acidosis
       </a>
       and probably
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardialgia" title="Cardialgia">
        cardialgia
       </a>
       by neutralizing an overdose of acid. It had no adverse health effects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-showcasesmoonmilk_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-showcasesmoonmilk-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
