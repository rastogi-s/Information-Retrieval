      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Soviet crewed lunar programs
       </b>
       were a series of unsuccessful programs pursued by the
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet Union
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">
        land a man on the Moon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">
        in competition with the United States
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       to achieve the same goal set publicly by President
       <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">
        John F. Kennedy
       </a>
       on May 25, 1961. The Soviet government publicly denied participating in such a competition, but secretly pursued two programs in the 1960s: crewed lunar flyby missions using
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-L1" title="Soyuz 7K-L1">
        Soyuz 7K-L1
       </a>
       (Zond) spacecraft launched with the
       <a href="/wiki/Proton-K" title="Proton-K">
        Proton-K
       </a>
       rocket, and a crewed lunar landing using
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-LOK" title="Soyuz 7K-LOK">
        Soyuz 7K-LOK
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/LK_(spacecraft)" title="LK (spacecraft)">
        LK Lander
       </a>
       spacecraft launched with the
       <a href="/wiki/N1_(rocket)" title="N1 (rocket)">
        N1 rocket
       </a>
       . Following the dual American successes of the first crewed lunar orbit on December 24–25, 1968 (
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">
        Apollo 8
       </a>
       ) and the first Moon landing on July 20, 1969 (
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       ), and a series of catastrophic N1 failures, both Soviet programs were eventually brought to an end. The Proton-based Zond program was canceled in 1970, and the N1 / L3 program was terminated de facto in 1974 and officially canceled in 1976. Details of both Soviet programs were kept secret until 1990 when the government allowed them to be published under the policy of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Glasnost" title="Glasnost">
         glasnost
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_concepts">
        Early concepts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_crewed_lunar_programs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early concepts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tmk-mavr.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="405" data-file-width="540" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Tmk-mavr.jpg/220px-Tmk-mavr.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Tmk-mavr.jpg/330px-Tmk-mavr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Tmk-mavr.jpg/440px-Tmk-mavr.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tmk-mavr.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Artist's depiction of TMK-MAVR on a
         <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
          Venus
         </a>
         flyby
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       As early as 1961, the Soviet leadership had made public pronouncements about landing a man on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and establishing a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_base" title="Lunar base">
        lunar base
       </a>
       , however serious plans were not made until several years later.
       <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">
        Sergei Korolev
       </a>
       , the senior Soviet rocket engineer, was more interested in launching a heavy orbital station and in
       <a href="/wiki/TMK" title="TMK">
        crewed flights to Mars and Venus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HarveyZakutnyaya2011_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HarveyZakutnyaya2011-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       With this in mind, Korolev began the development of the super-heavy
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-1_rocket" title="N-1 rocket">
        N-1 rocket
       </a>
       with a 75-ton payload.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Soyuz-A-B-C_and_N1">
        Soyuz-A-B-C and N1
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_crewed_lunar_programs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Soyuz-A-B-C and N1">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz-A-B-C_circumlunar_complex_drawing.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1221" data-file-width="1744" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Soyuz-A-B-C_circumlunar_complex_drawing.png/220px-Soyuz-A-B-C_circumlunar_complex_drawing.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Soyuz-A-B-C_circumlunar_complex_drawing.png/330px-Soyuz-A-B-C_circumlunar_complex_drawing.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Soyuz-A-B-C_circumlunar_complex_drawing.png/440px-Soyuz-A-B-C_circumlunar_complex_drawing.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz-A-B-C_circumlunar_complex_drawing.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Soyuz 7K-9K-11K circumlunar concept. The drawing shows Soyuz 7K (right),
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_9K" title="Soyuz 9K">
          Soyuz 9K
         </a>
         booster, and
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_11K" title="Soyuz 11K">
          Soyuz 11K
         </a>
         tanker with twin whip antennas (left)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In its preliminary Moon plans, Korolev's design bureau initially promoted the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">
        Soyuz
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_A" title="Soyuz A">
        A-B-C circumlunar complex
       </a>
       (A-B-V in Russian) concept under which a two-crew spacecraft would rendezvous with other components in Earth orbit to assemble a lunar flyby excursion vehicle. The components would then be delivered by the proven middle
       <a href="/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)" title="R-7 (rocket family)">
        R-7
       </a>
       rocket. After developing the N1, beginning in 1963, Korolev began to plan a Moon landing mission using two launches and docking. Later Korolev managed to increase the payload of the N1 to 92-93 tons (by switching to liquid hydrogen in the upper stage(s) and increasing the number of engines in its first stage from 24 to 30), providing enough power to accomplish the mission with a single launch.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="UR-500K_.2F_LK-1_and_UR-700_.2F_LK-3">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="UR-500K_/_LK-1_and_UR-700_/_LK-3">
        UR-500K / LK-1 and UR-700 / LK-3
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_crewed_lunar_programs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: UR-500K / LK-1 and UR-700 / LK-3">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Another main space design bureau headed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladimir_Chelomei" title="Vladimir Chelomei">
        Vladimir Chelomei
       </a>
       proposed a competing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cislunar" title="Cislunar">
        cislunar
       </a>
       orbiting mission using a heavy UR-500K rocket (later renamed the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_rocket" title="Proton rocket">
        Proton rocket
       </a>
       ) and a two-crew
       <a href="/wiki/LK-1" title="LK-1">
        LK-1
       </a>
       spacecraft. Later, Chelomei also proposed a Moon landing program with a super-heavy
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UR-700" title="UR-700">
        UR-700
       </a>
       rocket and a
       <a href="/wiki/LK-700" title="LK-700">
        LK-700
       </a>
       (
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=LK-3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="LK-3 (page does not exist)">
        LK-3
       </a>
       ) spacecraft.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_to_Apollo">
        Reaction to Apollo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_crewed_lunar_programs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reaction to Apollo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Soviet government issued a response to the American Apollo challenge after three years. According to the first government decree about the Soviet crewed Moon programs (Decree 655-268, '
       <i>
        On Work on the Exploration of the Moon and Mastery of Space
       </i>
       '), adopted in August 1964, Chelomei was instructed to develop a Moon flyby program with a projected first flight by the end of 1966, and Korolev was instructed to develop the Moon landing program with a first flight by the end of 1967.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stabell2012_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stabell2012-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Following the change from
       <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">
        Nikita Khrushchev
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">
        Leonid Brezhnev
       </a>
       in 1964, the Soviet government in September 1965 assigned the flyby program to Korolev, who redesigned the cislunar mission to use his own
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-L1" title="Soyuz 7K-L1">
        Soyuz 7K-L1
       </a>
       spacecraft and Chelomei's Proton rocket.
      </p>
      <p>
       Korolev organized full scale development of both programs, but died after surgery in January 1966.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Publishing2009_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Publishing2009-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to a government decree of February 1967, the first crewed flyby was scheduled for mid-1967, and the first crewed landing for the end of 1968.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Moon_flyby_UR-500K.28Proton.29.2FL1.28Zond.29_program">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Moon_flyby_UR-500K(Proton)/L1(Zond)_program">
        Moon flyby
        <i>
         UR-500K(Proton)/L1(Zond)
        </i>
        program
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_crewed_lunar_programs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Moon flyby UR-500K(Proton)/L1(Zond) program">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ZOND.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2480" data-file-width="3758" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/ZOND.jpg/220px-ZOND.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/ZOND.jpg/330px-ZOND.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/ZOND.jpg/440px-ZOND.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ZOND.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <b>
          <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-L1" title="Soyuz 7K-L1">
           Zond (Soyuz 7K-L1)
          </a>
         </b>
         circumlunar spacecraft.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket_family)" title="Proton (rocket family)">
        Proton (rocket family)
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-L1" title="Soyuz 7K-L1">
        Soyuz 7K-L1
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Zond_program" title="Zond program">
        Zond program
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Launched by a 3-staged Proton rocket, the L1(Zond) was a spacecraft from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">
        Soyuz family
       </a>
       and consisted of two or three modified modules of the main craft
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-OK" title="Soyuz 7K-OK">
        Soyuz 7K-OK
       </a>
       with a total weight of 5.5 tons. The Apollo orbital spacecraft (command ship) for the lunar flyby also had two modules (command and service) but was five times heavier, carried a crew of three and entered lunar orbit, whereas the L1 (Zond) performed a flight around the Moon and came back on a return trajectory. Planned for 8 December 1968 for priority over the US, a first crewed mission of the L1 (Zond) was canceled due to the insufficient readiness of the capsule and rocket. After
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">
        Apollo 8
       </a>
       won the first (lunar flyby) phase of the Moon Race at the end of 1968, the Soviet leadership lost political interest in the L1 (Zond) program. A few reserve units of L1 (Zond) made unpiloted flights, but by the end of 1970, this program was canceled.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Moon_landing_N1.2FL3_program">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Moon_landing_N1/L3_program">
        Moon landing
        <i>
         N1/L3
        </i>
        program
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aleksei_Leonov_%26_Andrei_Sokolov_-_InTheOceanOfStorms.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="325" data-file-width="233" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a7/Aleksei_Leonov_%26_Andrei_Sokolov_-_InTheOceanOfStorms.jpg/220px-Aleksei_Leonov_%26_Andrei_Sokolov_-_InTheOceanOfStorms.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a7/Aleksei_Leonov_%26_Andrei_Sokolov_-_InTheOceanOfStorms.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aleksei_Leonov_%26_Andrei_Sokolov_-_InTheOceanOfStorms.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          In the Ocean of Storms
         </i>
         , a 1967 Soviet painting by
         <a href="/wiki/Alexey_Leonov" title="Alexey Leonov">
          Alexey Leonov
         </a>
         and Andrei Sokolov, depicts a traveler examining the Luna 9 braking rocket and landing capsule which had performed the first uncrewed Moon landing in 1966. Leonov, who made the first
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">
          spacewalk
         </a>
         , was at this time generally viewed as the Soviet cosmonaut most likely to become the first human on the Moon.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>

         </a>
         .

         LK (Lunniy korabl—"lunar craft").
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/LK_(spacecraft)" title="LK (spacecraft)">
        LK (spacecraft)
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/N1_(rocket)" title="N1 (rocket)">
        N1 (rocket)
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-LOK" title="Soyuz 7K-LOK">
        Soyuz 7K-LOK
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The crewed landing plan adopted a similar method to the single launch and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit_rendezvous" title="Lunar orbit rendezvous">
        lunar orbit rendezvous
       </a>
       of the Apollo project. For mission safety, weeks before the crewed mission, an LK-R uncrewed L3 complex and two
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunokhod_program" title="Lunokhod program">
        Lunokhod
       </a>
       automated rovers would be sent to the Moon, to work as radio beacons for crewed LK, with the LK-R used as a reserve escape craft. The Lunokhods were also equipped with manual controls for the cosmonauts, both for transfer to LK-R in necessity and for regular research.
      </p>
      <p>
       The N1 rocket would then carry the
       <b>
        L3 Moon expedition complex
       </b>
       , comprising two spacecraft (LOK and LK) and two (
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Block_G&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Block G (page does not exist)">
        Block G
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Block_D" title="Block D">
        Block D
       </a>
       ) boosters. A variant of the Soyuz craft, the
       <b>
        "Lunniy Orbitalny Korabl" (LOK)
       </b>
       command ship, would carry two men, with three modules like the regular Soyuz 7K-OK, but was heavier by a few tons. The 7K-OK was half the mass of the three-crew Apollo orbital command ship. The
       <b>
        "Lunniy Korabl" (LK)
       </b>
       accommodated only one cosmonaut, so in the Soviet plan, only one cosmonaut would land on the Moon. The mass of the LK was 40% of the mass of the Apollo lunar lander.
      </p>
      <p>
       The L3 complex to be placed in
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
        LEO
       </a>
       by the N1 was 93 tons (compared to
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">
        Saturn V
       </a>
       's 137 tons). The mass of the LOK and LK was 40% of the Apollo complex, but was equivalent to the L3 complex without Block G. The booster for the LEO toward the Moon for the Apollo vehicle was provided by the last stage of the Saturn V, while for the Block D, LOK and LK, this was to be provided by Block G of the same L3 complex.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the L3 complex's journey to the Moon, there would be no need to undock and re-dock the orbital and landing craft as was done in Apollo, because the cosmonaut would transfer from the LOK to LK by a 'spacewalk'. On the Apollo missions, the transfer was done using an internal passage.
      </p>
      <p>
       Block D was to slow the LOK and LK into lunar orbit, while with Apollo this phase was undertaken by firing the engine on the service module to slow the complex and enter lunar orbit since the Apollo complex traveled with the Command Module and Lunar Excursion Module (LEM) facing back towards the Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       Once in orbit, the LK with Block D would separate from the LOK and descend toward the surface of the Moon using the Block D engine. After Block D exhausted its fuel, the LK was to separate and complete landing using its own engine.
      </p>
      <p>
       On the Moon, the cosmonaut would take Moon walks, use Lunokhods, collect rocks, and plant the Soviet flag.
      </p>
      <p>
       After a few hours on the lunar surface, the LK's engine would fire again using its landing structure as a launch pad, as with Apollo. To save weight, the engine used for landing would blast the LK back to lunar orbit for an automated docking with the LOK. The cosmonaut then would spacewalk back to the LOK carrying rock samples. The LK would then be cast off, after which the LOK would fire its rocket for the return to Earth.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmonauts">
        Cosmonauts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_crewed_lunar_programs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cosmonauts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1966, two cosmonaut training groups were formed. One group was commanded by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladimir_Mikhaylovich_Komarov" title="Vladimir Mikhaylovich Komarov">
        Vladimir Komarov
       </a>
       and included
       <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">
        Yuri Gagarin
       </a>
       , and was to prepare for qualification flights of the Soyuz in Earth orbit and a Proton-launched cis-lunar mission (Gagarin,
       <a href="/wiki/Andriyan_Nikolayev" title="Andriyan Nikolayev">
        Nikolayev
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Komarov" title="Vladimir Komarov">
        Komarov
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Valery_Bykovsky" title="Valery Bykovsky">
        Bykovsky
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yevgeny_Khrunov" title="Yevgeny Khrunov">
        Khrunov
       </a>
       ; Engineer-Cosmonauts: Gorbatko, Grechko, Sevastyanov, Kubasov, Volkov). Komarov later died in the
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_1" title="Soyuz 1">
        Soyuz 1
       </a>
       spaceflight when his parachute malfunctioned causing his capsule to smash into the earth at high speed. The second group was led by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexei_Leonov" title="Alexei Leonov">
        Alexei Leonov
       </a>
       and concentrated on the landing mission (Commanders: Leonov,
       <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Popovich" title="Pavel Popovich">
        Popovich
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Belyayev" title="Pavel Belyayev">
        Belyayev
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Boris_Volynov" title="Boris Volynov">
        Volynov
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Klimuk" title="Pyotr Klimuk">
        Klimuk
       </a>
       ; Engineer-cosmonauts: Makarov, Voronov, Rukavishnikov, Artyukhin). As a result, Leonov has the strongest claim to have been the Soviets' first choice for the first man on the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       After Komarov's death in
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_1" title="Soyuz 1">
        Soyuz 1
       </a>
       in 1967, Gagarin was taken out of training and the groups were restructured. Despite the Soyuz 1 setback, the Soviets successfully rehearsed the automated docking of two uncrewed Soyuz craft in Earth orbit in 1968 and with the crewed
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_4" title="Soyuz 4">
        Soyuz 4
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_5" title="Soyuz 5">
        Soyuz 5
       </a>
       joint mission in early 1969 tested the other key mission elements.
      </p>
      <p>
       A total of 18 missions were related to the N1-L3 project.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_developments">
        Later developments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_crewed_lunar_programs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Later developments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       After the US Moon landing in 1969, the justification for the Soviet lunar landing program largely evaporated, although development and testing continued into the early 1970s. In 1970–1971 the LK was ready after three uncrewed test flights in LEO (
       <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_379" title="Kosmos 379">
        Kosmos 379
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_398" title="Kosmos 398">
        Kosmos 398
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_434" title="Kosmos 434">
        Kosmos 434
       </a>
       ). The LOK launched once (
       <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_382" title="Kosmos 382">
        Kosmos 382
       </a>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_7K-L1E&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soyuz 7K-L1E (page does not exist)">
        7K-L1E
       </a>
       , a dummy of
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-LOK" title="Soyuz 7K-LOK">
        7K-LOK
       </a>
       ). The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krechet" title="Krechet">
        Krechet
       </a>
       lunar spacesuit and support systems were tested.
      </p>
      <p>
       Four N1 test launches in 1969 (twice), 1971, and 1972 were failures, despite improvements after each crash. The second launch, on 3 July 1969 (an attempt to upstage
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       by 13 days), resulted in the destruction of the rocket and the entire launch complex, which delayed the N1-L3 program for two years.
      </p>
      <p>
       In an automatic Moon flyby, these first two launches of the N1 carried the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_7K-L1S&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soyuz 7K-L1S (page does not exist)">
        7K-L1S
       </a>
       spacecraft (modified
       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-L1" title="Soyuz 7K-L1">
        7K-L1
       </a>
       ). The dummy 7K-LOK (7K-L1E) and regular 7K-LOK with dummy LKs were used in the third and fourth launches.
      </p>
      <p>
       The complete L3 lunar expedition complex with the 7K-LOK and LK for the Moon flyby and landing was prepared for a fifth launch, using a modified N1 rocket in August 1974. If this mission and the next had been successful, it would have led to the decision to launch up to five Soviet crewed N1-L3 expeditions in 1976–1980. To gain technical and scientific interest in the program, the modified multi-launched N1F-L3M missions were planned to have significantly more time on the Moon's surface than Apollo.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, N1-L3 (as well as N1F-L3M) program was canceled in May 1974, and Soviet crewed space efforts subsequently concentrated on the development of space stations and on several designs and ground preparatory processes for a Mars mission, which continues to the present day, but has unclear objectives.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A Moon base,
       <a href="/wiki/Zvezda_(moonbase)" title="Zvezda (moonbase)">
        Zvezda
       </a>
       , that was proposed later, developed mockups of expedition vehicles
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and surface modules,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and "Vulkan-LEK" project were not adopted for economic reasons. As some recompense and as a replacement for the crewed landing program, the Soviets fulfilled a program of automated delivery of lunar soil and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunokhod" title="Lunokhod">
        Lunokhod
       </a>
       automated Moon rovers.
      </p>
      <p>
       The launch pad and MIK of N1 were redesigned for the
       <a href="/wiki/Buran_programme" title="Buran programme">
        Energia-Buran shuttle program
       </a>
       . Five LKs and three LOKs remain, at least, with some kept in the designer's and producer's company museums. Nearly 150 engines produced for first stages of N1F were kept by the manufacturer (
       <a href="/wiki/Kuznetsov_Design_Bureau" title="Kuznetsov Design Bureau">
        Kuznetsov Design Bureau
       </a>
       ), then sold for use on other launchers beginning around 2000.
      </p>
