      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Yutu
        </b>
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="zh">
        玉兔
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
        pinyin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
         Yùtù
        </span>
       </i>
       ; literally: "
       <i>
        <b>
         Jade Rabbit
        </b>
       </i>
       ") is an unmanned
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_rover" title="Lunar rover">
        lunar rover
       </a>
       that formed part of the Chinese
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_3" title="Chang'e 3">
        Chang'e 3
       </a>
       mission to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . It was launched at 17:30 UTC on 1 December 2013, and reached the Moon's surface on 14 December 2013.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_25356603_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bbc_25356603-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mission marks the first soft landing on the Moon since 1976 and the first
       <a href="/wiki/Rover_(space_exploration)" title="Rover (space exploration)">
        rover
       </a>
       to operate there since the Soviet
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_2" title="Lunokhod 2">
        Lunokhod 2
       </a>
       ceased operations on 11 May 1973.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molnár-May2013_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Molnár-May2013-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The rover encountered operational difficulties toward the end of the second lunar day
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abnormality_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-abnormality-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       after surviving and recovering successflully the first 14-day lunar night (about a month on the Moon)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-back_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-back-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , and was unable to move after the end of the second lunar night, though it continued to gather useful information for some months afterward.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Not_dead_yet_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Not_dead_yet-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In October 2015,
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       set the record for the longest operational period for a rover on the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 31 July 2016,
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       ceased to operate after a total of 31 months, well beyond its original expected lifespan of three months.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       lunar rover was developed by Shanghai Aerospace System Engineering Institute (SASEI) and Beijing Institute of Spacecraft System Engineering (BISSE). The development of the six-wheeled rover began in 2002 and was completed in May 2010.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhuanet_20080424a_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-xinhuanet_20080424a-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhuanet_20080424b_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-xinhuanet_20080424b-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20131202_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt20131202-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The rover deployed from the lander and explored the lunar surface independently. The rover's name was selected in an online poll, and is a reference to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_rabbit" title="Moon rabbit">
        pet rabbit
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e" title="Chang'e">
        Chang'e
       </a>
       , the goddess of the Moon in
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">
        Chinese mythology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20131202_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt20131202-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives">
        Objectives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Objectives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The official mission objective was to achieve China's first soft-landing and roving exploration on the Moon, as well as to demonstrate and develop key technologies for future missions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_China_vol_56_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Science_China_vol_56-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The scientific objectives of Chang'e-3 mainly included lunar surface topography and geological survey, lunar surface material composition and resource survey, Sun-Earth-Moon space environment detection, and lunar-based astronomical observation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_China_vol_56_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Science_China_vol_56-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Chang'e 3 performed the first direct measurement of the structure and depth of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil" title="Lunar soil">
        lunar soil
       </a>
       down to a depth of 30 m (98 ft), and investigated the
       <a href="/wiki/Internal_structure_of_the_Moon" title="Internal structure of the Moon">
        lunar crust structure
       </a>
       down to several hundred meters deep.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhuanet_2012-06_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-xinhuanet_2012-06-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program" title="Chinese Lunar Exploration Program">
        Chinese Lunar Exploration Program
       </a>
       was divided into three main operational phases:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_China_vol_56_15-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Science_China_vol_56-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Orbiting (
        <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_1" title="Chang'e 1">
         Chang'e 1
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_2" title="Chang'e 2">
         Chang'e 2
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Landing (
        <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_3" title="Chang'e 3">
         Chang'e 3
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_4" title="Chang'e 4">
         Chang'e 4
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Sample return (
        <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_5" title="Chang'e 5">
         Chang'e 5
        </a>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications">
        Specifications
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Specifications">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Unlike
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        ESA
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/China_National_Space_Administration" title="China National Space Administration">
        China National Space Administration
       </a>
       reveals little about its missions to the public, so detailed information about
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_3" title="Chang'e 3">
        Chang'e 3
       </a>
       is limited. Aspects of Yutu's design and several of its experiments may have been based on NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rover" title="Mars Exploration Rover">
        Mars Exploration Rovers
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aa_2013_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-aa_2013-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCMP_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SCMP-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its wheel design is believed to have been considerably influenced by what was used on the Russian
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_1" title="Lunokhod 1">
        Lunokhod 1
       </a>
       rover.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCMP_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-SCMP-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       rover has a mass of 140 kg (310 lb), with a payload capacity of 20 kg (44 lb).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-march_12_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-march_12-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_tools_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Science_tools-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xin_200905_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-xin_200905-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is smaller than the Mars Exploration Rovers,
       <i>
        Spirit
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Opportunity
       </i>
       , and carries similar instruments: panoramic cameras, an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_spectrometer" title="Infrared spectrometer">
        infrared spectrometer
       </a>
       and an
       <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle_X-ray_spectrometer" title="Alpha particle X-ray spectrometer">
        alpha particle X-ray spectrometer
       </a>
       (APXS).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molnár-May2013_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Molnár-May2013-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news_163_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-news_163-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       is also equipped with a robotic arm to position its APXS near a target sample. In addition, the rover could transmit live video, and has automatic sensors to prevent it from colliding with other objects.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       was designed to explore an area of 3 square kilometres (1.2 sq mi) during its three-month mission, with a maximum travelling distance of 10 km (6.2 mi). Energy was provided by two
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">
        solar panels
       </a>
       , allowing the rover to operate through lunar days. During the 14-day lunar nights, the rover went into
       <a href="/wiki/Sleep_mode" title="Sleep mode">
        sleep mode
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xi-20131226_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-xi-20131226-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       during which heating was provided by
       <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_heater_unit" title="Radioisotope heater unit">
        radioisotope heater units
       </a>
       (RHU) using
       <a href="/wiki/Plutonium-238" title="Plutonium-238">
        plutonium-238
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pu238_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pu238-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and two-phase fluid loops.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_China_vol_56_15-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Science_China_vol_56-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_payload">
        Scientific payload
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Scientific payload">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       rover carried a
       <a href="/wiki/Ground-penetrating_radar" title="Ground-penetrating radar">
        ground-penetrating radar
       </a>
       and spectrometers to inspect the composition of the soil and the structure of the
       <a href="/wiki/Internal_structure_of_the_Moon" title="Internal structure of the Moon">
        lunar crust
       </a>
       beneath it.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Ground-penetrating_radar_.28GPR.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ground-penetrating_radar_(GPR)">
        Ground-penetrating radar (GPR)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ground-penetrating radar (GPR)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The rover carried a
       <a href="/wiki/Ground-penetrating_radar" title="Ground-penetrating radar">
        ground-penetrating radar
       </a>
       (GPR) on its underside, allowing for the first direct measurement of the structure and depth of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil" title="Lunar soil">
        lunar soil
       </a>
       down to a depth of 30 m (98 ft), and investigation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Internal_structure_of_the_Moon" title="Internal structure of the Moon">
        lunar crust structure
       </a>
       down to several hundred metres deep.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhuanet_2012-06_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-xinhuanet_2012-06-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spectrometers">
        Spectrometers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spectrometers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The rover carried an
       <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle_X-ray_spectrometer" title="Alpha particle X-ray spectrometer">
        alpha particle X-ray spectrometer
       </a>
       (APXS)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news_163_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-news_163-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_spectrometer" title="Infrared spectrometer">
        infrared spectrometer
       </a>
       , intended to analyze the
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
        chemical element
       </a>
       composition of lunar samples. The APXS was the only payload on the robotic arm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APXS_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-APXS-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stereo_cameras">
        Stereo cameras
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Stereo cameras">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There were two panoramic cameras and two navigation cameras on the rover's mast, which stands ~1.5 m (4.9 ft) above the lunar surface, as well as two hazard avoidance cameras installed on the lower front portion of the rover.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spaceflight2_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-spaceflight2-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Each
       <a href="/wiki/Stereo_camera" title="Stereo camera">
        camera pair
       </a>
       was used to capture
       <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">
        stereoscopic
       </a>
       images,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planetary.org_2013-12-23_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-planetary.org_2013-12-23-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or for
       <a href="/wiki/Range_imaging" title="Range imaging">
        range imaging
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Stereo_cameras" title="Stereo cameras">
        triangulation
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       The planned landing site was announced to be

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">
        Activities
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Activities">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="First_lunar_day">
        First lunar day
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: First lunar day">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The rover was successfully deployed from the lander, and made contact with the lunar surface on 14 December, 20:35 UTC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yt.0S7E572zTlc_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-yt.0S7E572zTlc-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 17 December it was announced that all of the scientific instruments apart from the spectrometers had been successfully activated, and that both the lander and rover were "functioning as hoped, despite the unexpectedly rigorous conditions of the lunar environment".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_tools_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Science_tools-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From 16 December to 20 December the rover did not move, having been partially powered down. Direct solar radiation had raised the temperature on the sunlit side of the rover to over 100 °C, while the shaded side simultaneously fell below 0 °C.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yutu_naps_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Yutu_naps-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By 22 December
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       had completed its first tasks: to photograph the lander from several different angles, following a roughly semi-circular route from north to south of the lander, while at the same time being photographed and filmed by the lander. A number of these images have been released, including a
       <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">
        stereoview
       </a>
       of the lander and videos of the rover in motion. The lander and rover then commenced their respective science missions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planetary.org_2013-12-23_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-planetary.org_2013-12-23-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-english.cntv.cn_2013-12-22_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-english.cntv.cn_2013-12-22-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinanews.com_2013-12-23_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-chinanews.com_2013-12-23-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition to successfully deploying its robotic arm,
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       completed checks on 23 December to ensure that it was prepared for the coming lunar night, and moved about 40 metres south of the lander.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfn-20131227_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sfn-20131227-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The lander was also tested the following day. The lander entered
       <a href="/wiki/Sleep_mode" title="Sleep mode">
        sleep mode
       </a>
       first, at around 11 am
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/China_Standard_Time" title="China Standard Time">
        China Standard Time
       </a>
       on 25 December, followed by the rover at 5:23 am on 26 December. Both had to withstand the extreme cold of the two-week-long lunar nights.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xi-20131226_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-xi-20131226-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xinhuanet_2013-12-23_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-xinhuanet_2013-12-23-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Second_lunar_day">
        Second lunar day
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Second lunar day">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On 11 January 2014, following the lunar night, the rover and lander were taken out of
       <a href="/wiki/Sleep_mode" title="Sleep mode">
        sleep mode
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-back_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-back-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and on 16 January the rover completed its first examination of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil" title="Lunar soil">
        lunar soil
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_25763168_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bbc_25763168-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 25 January 2014, near the end of the second lunar day, China's state media announced that the rover had undergone a "mechanical control abnormality" and stated that the problem was caused by the "complicated lunar surface environment".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abnormality_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-abnormality-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Planetary_Society" title="The Planetary Society">
        The Planetary Society
       </a>
       reported that the rover was not responding properly to commands from Earth, so it "could not prepare for the oncoming night properly."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-control_issue_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-control_issue-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_jade_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cnn_jade-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_2014jan27_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-guardian_2014jan27-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-driving_unit_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-driving_unit-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Specifically, the rover suffered a control circuit malfunction in its driving unit, which prevented it from entering normal dormancy and folding its mast and solar panels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xinhua_3March_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Xinhua_3March-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planetary_update_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-planetary_update-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Third_lunar_day">
        Third lunar day
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Third lunar day">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Command Control was expecting the rover to contact Earth on 12 February 2014 had it endured its second lunar night. Since it did not transmit any signals, it was declared permanently inoperative.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rover_Inoperative_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rover_Inoperative-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 13 February, it re-established communication with Command Control.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roverworking_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-roverworking-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-welcomeback_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-welcomeback-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planetary_yutu_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-planetary_yutu-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       China's lunar program spokesman Pei Zhaoyu declared that although Yutu was able to communicate, "it still suffers a mechanical control abnormality."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Not_dead_yet_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Not_dead_yet-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The rover entered its third hibernation period on 22 February. It was still unable to move and serious technical troubles persisted that hampered science operations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kremer_48-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kremer-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Chinese space scientists eventually ascertained that the control circuit had failed, and this prevented Yutu from entering normal dormancy as planned,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-driving_unit_41-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-driving_unit-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xinhua_3March_42-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Xinhua_3March-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but stated that the ground penetrating radar, panoramic and infrared imaging equipment were functioning normally.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kremer_48-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-kremer-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xi.133136885_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-xi.133136885-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gl.848582_50-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gl.848582-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_mission">
        End of mission
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: End of mission">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       While amateur observers were unable to detect transmissions from the lander, Chinese officials reported that the craft was still operating its UV Camera and Telescope as it entered its 14th lunar night on 14 January 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phys-pano_52-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-phys-pano-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 18 April 2014, Wang Jianyu, deputy secretary general of the Chinese Society of Space Research stated that the failure was not mechanical, but electrical, and they were looking to bypass it. He also explained, "The temperature on the Moon is considerably lower than our previous estimation, adding that "certain components may be suffering from "frostbite".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
        <a href="#cite_note-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During 15 April, the Chang'e 3 mission, including its
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       rover, witnessed a
       <a href="/wiki/April_2014_lunar_eclipse" title="April 2014 lunar eclipse">
        total eclipse of the Sun
       </a>
       by the Earth from surface of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-authint.eclipse_54-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-authint.eclipse-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       was unable to move its solar panels back to the insulating position during lunar nights, exposing the internals to the nightly cold. With each night, some capability was lost,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
        <a href="#cite_note-55">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but it exceeded its expected three-month life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exceeded_life_56-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-exceeded_life-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The scientific instruments may have worked, but subsequent science data became very limited as the NIR spectrometer and the ground-penetrating radar were limited to always making the same observation. Mission Control planned to keep on using the
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       until it completely stopped working, as it would provide valuable data on the endurance of its components.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exceeded_life_56-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-exceeded_life-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The rover was still transmitting every Lunar day as of December 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
        <a href="#cite_note-57">
         [57]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
        <a href="#cite_note-58">
         [58]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
        <a href="#cite_note-59">
         [59]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By the end of October 2015,
       <i>
        Yutu
       </i>
       had set the record for the longest operational period of a rover on the Moon, though most of its time was spent immobile.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
        <a href="#cite_note-60">
         [60]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On August 3, 2016, it was reported that the rover had ceased all operations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsci-20160803_61-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nsci-20160803-61">
         [61]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfn-20160804_62-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sfn-20160804-62">
         [62]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_results">
        Scientific results
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yutu_(rover)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Scientific results">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The rover's ground penetrating radar found evidence of at least nine distinct
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_layers" title="Rock layers">
        rock layers
       </a>
       , indicating that the area had surprisingly complex geological processes and is compositionally distinct from the Apollo and Luna landing sites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-layered_63-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-layered-63">
         [63]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
        <a href="#cite_note-64">
         [64]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
