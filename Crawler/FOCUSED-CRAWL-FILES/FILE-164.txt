      <p>
       <b>
        Kerberos
       </b>
       is a small
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Pluto" title="Moons of Pluto">
        natural satellite of Pluto
       </a>
       , about 12 km (7.5 mi) in its longest dimension. It was the fourth moon of Pluto to be discovered and its existence was announced on 20 July 2011.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegram_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-telegram-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was imaged, along with
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and its four other moons, by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft in July 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UniverseToday_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UniverseToday-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first image of Kerberos was released to the public on 22 October 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

         </a>
         discovery images of Kerberos.

      <p>
       Kerberos was discovered by the Pluto Companion Search Team using the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       on 28 June 2011, using the
       <a href="/wiki/Wide_Field_Camera_3" title="Wide Field Camera 3">
        Wide Field Camera 3
       </a>
       , during an attempt to find any
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">
        rings
       </a>
       that Pluto might possess.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [notes 2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosmic_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cosmic-12">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Further observations were made on 3 and 18 July 2011 and Kerberos was verified as a new moon on 20 July 2011.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegram_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-telegram-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-13">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was later '
       <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">
        precovered
       </a>
       ' (identified in earlier archival Hubble images) in images from 15 February 2006 and 25 June 2010.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegram_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-telegram-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Kerberos's brightness is only about 10% of that of
       <a href="/wiki/Nix_(moon)" title="Nix (moon)">
        Nix
       </a>
       , and it was only found because the discovery team took 8-minute exposures; earlier observations had used shorter exposures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakdawalla_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lakdawalla-14">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_naming_conventions#Natural_satellites_of_planets" title="Astronomical naming conventions">
        provisional designation
       </a>
       of the satellite varied based on the source used. The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       announced it as
       <b>
        S/2011 (134340) 1
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegram_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-telegram-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       whereas the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       mission website announced it as
       <b>
        S/2011 P 1
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewHorizons_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NewHorizons-15">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">
        Naming
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerberos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naming">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Upon discovery, Kerberos received the
       <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet_designation" title="Minor planet designation">
        minor planet designation
       </a>
       S/2011 (134340) 1 because it was the first satellite (S) discovered orbiting minor planet (134340) in 2011. It was initially called "P4", meaning the fourth Plutonian moon to be discovered.
      </p>
      <p>
       The convention for naming Plutonian moons is to use names associated with the god
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pluto_(god)" title="Pluto (god)">
        Pluto
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_mythology" title="Classical mythology">
        classical mythology
       </a>
       . To decide on names for P4 and P5,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark_Showalter" title="Mark Showalter">
        Mark Showalter
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/SETI_Institute" title="SETI Institute">
        SETI Institute
       </a>
       , on behalf of the discovery team, conducted a non-binding Internet poll in 2013, in which the general public was invited to vote for their favorite names. The public could choose from a selection of Greek mythological names related to the god Pluto, or could propose their own names.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After the initial announcement,
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shatner" title="William Shatner">
        William Shatner
       </a>
       , the actor who played
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_James_T._Kirk" title="Captain James T. Kirk">
        Captain James T. Kirk
       </a>
       in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">
         Star Trek
        </a>
       </i>
       franchise, proposed the names Vulcan and Romulus, ostensibly referring to the fire god
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulcan_(god)" title="Vulcan (god)">
        Vulcan
       </a>
       (a nephew of Pluto), and to
       <a href="/wiki/Romulus" title="Romulus">
        Romulus
       </a>
       the founder of
       <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       , but also alluding to the fictional planets of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulcan_(Star_Trek_planet)" title="Vulcan (Star Trek planet)">
        Vulcan
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romulans#Homeworld" title="Romulans">
        Romulus
       </a>
       in the
       <i>
        Star Trek
       </i>
       universe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marcia_Dunn_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marcia_Dunn-17">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nature-18">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 'Romulus' suggestion was discounted, because there is already
       <a href="/wiki/Romulus_(moon)" title="Romulus (moon)">
        an asteroid moon of that name
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but Vulcan won the poll after Shatner tweeted about it, with
       <a href="/wiki/Cerberus" title="Cerberus">
        Cerberus
       </a>
       , (the dog that guards Pluto's underworld) coming second and
       <a href="/wiki/Styx" title="Styx">
        Styx
       </a>
       (the goddess of the river of the underworld) coming third. The winning names were submitted to the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nature-18">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, 'Vulcan' was unacceptable to the IAU because it was not the name of an underworld deity and had already been used for a
       <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(hypothetical_planet)" title="Vulcan (hypothetical planet)">
        hypothetical planet
       </a>
       inside the orbit of Mercury, as well as having given its name to the hypothetical
       <a href="/wiki/Vulcanoid" title="Vulcanoid">
        vulcanoids
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marcia_Dunn_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marcia_Dunn-17">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cerberus is already the name of an asteroid,
       <a href="/wiki/1865_Cerberus" title="1865 Cerberus">
        1865 Cerberus
       </a>
       , but the Greek form of the name, Kerberos, was acceptable to the IAU.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 2 July 2013, the IAU announced that it formally approved the names Kerberos for P4 and Styx for P5.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The names of features on the bodies in the Pluto system are related to mythology and the literature and history of exploration. In particular, the names of features on Kerberos must be related to dogs from literature, mythology and history.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">
        Physical properties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerberos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physical properties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Kerberos has a double-lobed shape and is approximately 7.4 mi (11.9 km) across in its long dimension and 2.8 mi (4.5 km) in its shortest dimension. Kerberos has an
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       similar to the other three small moons. Until the
       <i>
        New Horizons
       </i>
       flyby it was thought that Kerberos was larger and darker.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Like the other small Plutonian satellites, Kerberos is not tidally locked; its rotational period was about 5.33 days at the time of the
       <i>
        New Horizons
       </i>
       flyby, although this varies quickly over geological timescales.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerberos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Observations indicate a circular, equatorial orbit with a radius of approximately 58,000 km (36,000 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShowalterHamilton2015_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ShowalterHamilton2015-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Kerberos orbits between Nix and
       <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(moon)" title="Hydra (moon)">
        Hydra
       </a>
       and makes a complete orbit around Pluto roughly every 32.1 days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegram_1-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-telegram-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakdawalla_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lakdawalla-14">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This period is close to a 1:5
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       , with the timing discrepancy being about 0.7%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [notes 3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As with the near resonances between Nix or Hydra and Charon (1:4 and 1:6, respectively), determining how close this relationship is to a true resonance will require more-accurate knowledge of Kerberos's orbit, in particular its rate of
       <a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">
        precession
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerberos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Like Pluto's other satellites,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stern-28">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it is suspected that Kerberos coalesced from the
       <a href="/wiki/Collisional_family" title="Collisional family">
        debris of a massive collision
       </a>
       between Pluto and another
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       object, similar to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        giant impact
       </a>
       thought to have created the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosmic_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-cosmic-12">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerberos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft took images of Kerberos during its flyby of the Pluto system on 14 July 2015. Three months later, on 22 October, the first image of the moon was published. It is the last moon to have its picture released.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_science_fiction">
        In science fiction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerberos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In science fiction">
         edit
        </a>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Kerberos was where the plot of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voltron:_Legendary_Defender" title="Voltron: Legendary Defender">
         Voltron: Legendary Defender
        </a>
       </i>
       began, though it is incorrectly depicted as spherical. The moon's description was not properly taken into account, yet it remained such a minor detail that the issue was dropped when the show aired.
