   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Gravitation of the Moon
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MoonLP150Q_grav_150.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Radial gravity anomaly at the surface of the Moon in
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gal_(acceleration)" title="Gal (acceleration)">
          Gal (acceleration)
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration" title="Gravitational acceleration">
        acceleration due to gravity
       </a>
       on the surface of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       is about 1.625 m/s
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       , about 16.6% that on Earth's surface or 0.16
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_gravity" title="Standard gravity">
        <span class="texhtml">
         <i>
          ɡ
         </i>
        </span>
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Over the entire surface, the variation in gravitational acceleration is about 0.0253 m/s
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       (1.6% of the acceleration due to gravity). Because
       <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">
        weight
       </a>
       is directly dependent upon gravitational acceleration, things on the Moon will weigh only 16.6% of what they weigh on the Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
        gravitational field
       </a>
       of the Moon has been measured by tracking the radio signals emitted by orbiting spacecraft. The principle used depends on the
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">
        Doppler effect
       </a>
       , whereby the line-of-sight spacecraft acceleration can be measured by small shifts in frequency of the radio signal, and the measurement of the distance from the spacecraft to a station on Earth. Since the gravitational field of the Moon affects the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       of a spacecraft, one can use these tracking data to detect
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_anomaly" title="Gravity anomaly">
        gravity anomalies
       </a>
       . However, because of the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchronous_rotation" title="Synchronous rotation">
        synchronous rotation
       </a>
       it is not possible to track spacecraft from Earth much beyond the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_limb" title="Lunar limb">
        limbs of the Moon
       </a>
       , so until the recent
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Recovery_and_Interior_Laboratory" title="Gravity Recovery and Interior Laboratory">
        GRAIL
       </a>
       mission the far-side gravity field was not accurately known.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_gravity_acceleration_map_LGM2011.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1395" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Moon_gravity_acceleration_map_LGM2011.jpg/300px-Moon_gravity_acceleration_map_LGM2011.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Moon_gravity_acceleration_map_LGM2011.jpg/450px-Moon_gravity_acceleration_map_LGM2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Moon_gravity_acceleration_map_LGM2011.jpg/600px-Moon_gravity_acceleration_map_LGM2011.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_gravity_acceleration_map_LGM2011.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Gravity acceleration at the surface of the Moon in m/s
         <sup>
          2
         </sup>
         . Near side on the left, far side on the right. Map from
         <a class="external text" href="http://geodesy.curtin.edu.au/research/models/lgm2011/" rel="nofollow">
          Lunar Gravity Model 2011
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A major feature of the Moon's gravitational field is the presence of
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_concentration_(astronomy)" title="Mass concentration (astronomy)">
        mascons
       </a>
       , which are large positive gravity anomalies associated with some of the giant
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact basins
       </a>
       . These anomalies significantly influence the orbit of spacecraft around the Moon, and an accurate gravitational model is necessary in the planning of both manned and unmanned missions. They were initially discovered by the analysis of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter" title="Lunar Orbiter">
        Lunar Orbiter
       </a>
       tracking data:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       navigation tests prior to the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       showed positioning errors much larger than mission specifications.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mascons are in part due to the presence of dense
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        mare
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basaltic
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
        lava flows
       </a>
       that fill some of the impact basins.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, lava flows by themselves cannot fully explain the gravitational variations, and uplift of the
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       interface is required as well. Based on
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       gravitational models, it has been suggested that some mascons exist that do not show evidence for mare basaltic
       <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
        volcanism
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The huge expanse of mare basaltic volcanism associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Oceanus Procellarum
       </a>
       does not cause a positive gravity anomaly. The center of gravity of the Moon does not coincide exactly with its geometric center, but is displaced toward the Earth by about 2 kilometers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
