      <p>
       The
       <b>
        two moons of
        <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
         Mars
        </a>
       </b>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Both moons were discovered in August 1877 by
       <a href="/wiki/Asaph_Hall" title="Asaph Hall">
        Asaph Hall
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and are named after the characters
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(mythology)" title="Phobos (mythology)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       (panic/fear) and
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(deity)" title="Deimos (deity)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       (terror/dread) who, in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       , accompanied their father
       <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">
        Ares
       </a>
       , god of war, into battle. Ares was known as Mars to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Romans
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Of the two moons,
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       orbits closer to
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       with a semi-major axis of 9,377 km (5,827 mi).
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       orbits Mars with a semi-major axis of 23,460 km (14,580 mi).
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early speculation">

      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:245px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA17352-MarsMoons-PhobosPassesDeimos-RealTime.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="156" data-file-width="243" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2f/PIA17352-MarsMoons-PhobosPassesDeimos-RealTime.gif" width="243"/>

          <a href="/wiki/Curiosity_(rover)" title="Curiosity (rover)">

          </a>
         </i>
         view of the Martian moons:
         <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
          Phobos
         </a>
         passing in front of
         <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">

         – in

         </i>
         (video-gif, 1 August 2013)
        </div>

      <p>
       The existence of the moons of Mars had been speculated about since the moons of Jupiter were discovered. It is known that when
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
        Galileo
       </a>
       , as a hidden report about him having observed two bumps on the sides of Saturn (later discovered to be its rings), used the anagram
       <i>
        smaismrmilmepoetaleumibunenugttauiras
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        Altissimum planetam tergeminum observavi
       </i>
       ("I have observed the most distant planet to have a triple form"),
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">
        Johannes Kepler
       </a>
       had misinterpreted it to mean
       <i>
        Salve umbistineum geminatum Martia proles
       </i>
       (Hello, furious twins, sons of Mars).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Perhaps inspired by Kepler (and quoting
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">
        Kepler's third law of planetary motion
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Swift" title="Jonathan Swift">
        Jonathan Swift
       </a>
       's satire
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels" title="Gulliver's Travels">
         Gulliver's Travels
        </a>
       </i>
       (1726) refers to two moons in Part 3, Chapter 3 (the "Voyage to
       <a href="/wiki/Laputa" title="Laputa">
        Laputa
       </a>
       "), in which Laputa's astronomers are described as having discovered two satellites of Mars orbiting at distances of 3 and 5 Martian diameters with periods of 10 and 21.5 hours. The actual orbital distances of Phobos and Deimos are 1.4 and 3.5 Martian diameters, and their respective orbital periods are 7.66 and 30.35 hours.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-galileo_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-galileo-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESA-Phobos_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ESA-Phobos-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the 20th century, V. G. Perminov, a spacecraft designer of early Soviet Mars and Venus spacecraft, speculated Swift found and deciphered records that Martians left on Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the view of most astronomers is that Swift was simply employing a common argument of the time, that as the inner planets Venus and Mercury had no satellites, Earth had one and Jupiter had four (known at the time), that Mars by analogy must have two. Furthermore, as they had not yet been discovered, it was reasoned that they must be small and close to Mars. This would lead Swift to making a roughly accurate estimate of their orbital distances and revolution periods. In addition Swift could have been helped in his calculations by his friend, the mathematician
       <a href="/wiki/John_Arbuthnot" title="John Arbuthnot">
        John Arbuthnot
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">
        Voltaire
       </a>
       's 1752 short story "
       <a href="/wiki/Microm%C3%A9gas" title="Micromégas">
        Micromégas
       </a>
       ", about an alien visitor to Earth, also refers to two moons of Mars. Voltaire was presumably influenced by Swift.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In recognition of these 'predictions', two craters on Deimos are named
       <a href="/wiki/Swift_(Deimian_crater)" title="Swift (Deimian crater)">
        Swift
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Voltaire_(crater)" title="Voltaire (crater)">
        Voltaire
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while on Phobos there is one named
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_geology#Regio_(astronomy)" title="Planetary geology">
        regio
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Laputa Regio
       </i>
       , and one named
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planitia" title="Planitia">
        planitia
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Lagado Planitia
       </i>
       , both of which are named after places in
       <i>
        Gulliver's Travels
       </i>
       (the fictional
       <a href="/wiki/Laputa" title="Laputa">
        Laputa
       </a>
       , a flying island, and
       <a href="/wiki/Lagado" title="Lagado">
        Lagado
       </a>
       , imaginary capital of the fictional nation
       <a href="/wiki/Balnibarbi" title="Balnibarbi">
        Balnibarbi
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many of the
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)#Craters_on_Phobos" title="Phobos (moon)">
        craters on Phobos
       </a>
       are also named after characters in
       <i>
        Gulliver's Travels
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhobosCraters01a_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PhobosCraters01a-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        <span id="Discovery">
        </span>
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Asaph Hall discovered Deimos on 12 August 1877 at about 07:48
       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">
        UTC
       </a>
       and Phobos on 18 August 1877, at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Naval_Observatory" title="US Naval Observatory">
        US Naval Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">
        Washington, D.C.
       </a>
       , at about 09:14
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GMT" title="GMT">
        GMT
       </a>
       (contemporary sources, using the pre-1925 astronomical convention that began the day at noon, give the time of discovery as 11 August 14:40 and 17 August 16:06
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_mean_time" title="Washington mean time">
        Washington mean time
       </a>
       respectively).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morley1989_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morley1989-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the time, he was deliberately searching for Martian moons. Hall had previously seen what appeared to be a Martian moon on 10 August, but due to bad weather, he could not definitively identify them until later.
      </p>
      <p>
       Hall recorded his discovery of Phobos in his notebook as follows:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Usno-telescope-equalized-1.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1339" data-file-width="1922" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Usno-telescope-equalized-1.png/220px-Usno-telescope-equalized-1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Usno-telescope-equalized-1.png/330px-Usno-telescope-equalized-1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Usno-telescope-equalized-1.png/440px-Usno-telescope-equalized-1.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Usno-telescope-equalized-1.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The telescope used to discover the Martian moons
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <dl>
        <dd>
         "I repeated the examination in the early part of the night of 11th [August 1877], and again found nothing, but trying again some hours later I found a faint object on the following side and a little north of the planet. I had barely time to secure an observation of its position when fog from the River stopped the work. This was at half past two o'clock on the night of the 11th. Cloudy weather intervened for several days.
        </dd>
        <dd>
         "On 15 August the weather looking more promising, I slept at the Observatory. The sky cleared off with a thunderstorm at 11 o'clock and the search was resumed. The atmosphere however was in a very bad condition and Mars was so blazing and unsteady that nothing could be seen of the object, which we now know was at that time so near the planet as to be invisible.
        </dd>
        <dd>
         "On 16 August the object was found again on the following side of the planet, and the observations of that night showed that it was moving with the planet, and if a satellite, was near one of its elongations. Until this time I had said nothing to anyone at the Observatory of my search for a satellite of Mars, but on leaving the observatory after these observations of the 16th, at about three o'clock in the morning, I told my assistant, George Anderson, to whom I had shown the object, that I thought I had discovered a satellite of Mars. I told him also to keep quiet as I did not wish anything said until the matter was beyond doubt. He said nothing, but the thing was too good to keep and I let it out myself. On 17 August between one and two o'clock, while I was reducing my observations, Professor Newcomb came into my room to eat his lunch and I showed him my measures of the faint object near Mars which proved that it was moving with the planet.
        </dd>
        <dd>
         "On 17 August while waiting and watching for the outer moon, the inner one was discovered. The observations of the 17th and 18th put beyond doubt the character of these objects and the discovery was publicly announced by Admiral Rodgers."
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The telescope used for the discovery was the 26-inch (66 cm) refractor (telescope with a lens) then located at Foggy Bottom.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1893 the lens was remounted and put in a new dome, where it remains into the 21st century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The names, originally spelled
       <i>
        Phobus
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Deimus
       </i>
       , respectively, were suggested by
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_George_Madan" title="Henry George Madan">
        Henry Madan
       </a>
       (1838–1901), Science Master of
       <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">
        Eton
       </a>
       , from Book XV of the
       <a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">
        Iliad
       </a>
       , where
       <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">
        Ares
       </a>
       summons Fear and Fright.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_moon_hoax">
        Mars moon hoax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mars moon hoax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1959,
       <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott_Houston" title="Walter Scott Houston">
        Walter Scott Houston
       </a>
       perpetrated a celebrated
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/April_Fool" title="April Fool">
        April Fool
       </a>
       's hoax in the April edition of the
       <i>
        Great Plains Observer
       </i>
       , claiming that "Dr. Arthur Hayall of the University of the Sierras reports that the moons of Mars are actually artificial satellites". Both Dr. Hayall and the University of the Sierras were fictitious. The hoax gained worldwide attention when Houston's claim was repeated in earnest by a Soviet scientist,
       <a href="/wiki/Iosif_Shklovsky" title="Iosif Shklovsky">
        Iosif Shklovsky
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who, based on a later-disproven density estimate,
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)#Shklovsky's_&quot;Hollow_Phobos&quot;_hypothesis" title="Phobos (moon)">
        suggested Phobos was a hollow metal shell
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_surveys">
        Recent surveys
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recent surveys">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Searches have been conducted for additional satellites. Most recently,
       <a href="/wiki/Scott_S._Sheppard" title="Scott S. Sheppard">
        Scott S. Sheppard
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/David_C._Jewitt" title="David C. Jewitt">
        David C. Jewitt
       </a>
       surveyed the
       <a href="/wiki/Hill_sphere" title="Hill sphere">
        Hill sphere
       </a>
       of Mars for irregular satellites. The search covered nearly the entire Hill sphere, but scattered light from Mars excluded the inner few arcminutes where the satellites Phobos and Deimos reside. No new satellites were found to an apparent limiting red magnitude of 23.5, which corresponds to radii of about 0.09 km using an albedo of 0.07.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA17351-ApparentSizes-MarsDeimosPhobos-EarthMoon.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="300" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/PIA17351-ApparentSizes-MarsDeimosPhobos-EarthMoon.jpg/250px-PIA17351-ApparentSizes-MarsDeimosPhobos-EarthMoon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0c/PIA17351-ApparentSizes-MarsDeimosPhobos-EarthMoon.jpg 1.5x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA17351-ApparentSizes-MarsDeimosPhobos-EarthMoon.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Apparent sizes of the moons of Mars,
         <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">
          Deimos
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
          Phobos
         </a>
         , and the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         as viewed from the
         <i>
          surface
         </i>
         of their respective planets (
         <a href="/wiki/Curiosity_(rover)" title="Curiosity (rover)">
          Curiosity rover
         </a>
         , 1 August 2013)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       If viewed from Mars's surface near its equator, full Phobos looks about one third as big as a full moon on Earth. It has an
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        angular diameter
       </a>
       of between 8' (rising) and 12' (overhead). It would look smaller when the observer is further away from the Martian equator, and is completely invisible from Mars's polar ice caps. Deimos looks more like a bright star or planet for an observer on Mars, only slightly bigger than
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       looks from Earth; it has an angular diameter of about 2'. The Sun's angular diameter as seen from Mars, by contrast, is about 21'. Thus there are no total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       on Mars, as the moons are far too small to completely cover the Sun. On the other hand, total
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipses
       </a>
       of Phobos are very common, happening almost every night.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The motions of Phobos and Deimos would appear very different from that of our own Moon. Speedy Phobos rises in the west, sets in the east, and rises again in just eleven hours, while Deimos, being only just outside
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous orbit
       </a>
       , rises as expected in the east but very slowly. Despite its 30-hour orbit, it takes 2.7 days to set in the west as it slowly falls behind the rotation of Mars.
      </p>
      <p>
       Both moons are
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       , always presenting the same face towards Mars. Since Phobos orbits Mars faster than the planet itself rotates, tidal forces are slowly but steadily decreasing its orbital radius. At some point in the future, when it approaches Mars closely enough (see
       <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">
        Roche limit
       </a>
       ), Phobos will be broken up by these tidal forces.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Several strings of craters on the Martian surface, inclined further from the equator the older they are, suggest that there may have been other small moons that suffered the fate expected of Phobos, and that the Martian crust as a whole shifted between these events.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Deimos, on the other hand, is far enough away that its orbit is being slowly boosted instead,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as in the case of our own Moon.
      </p>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MoonsOfMarsImproved3.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Animation illustrating the asteroid-belt origin for the moons

      <p>
       The origin of the Martian moons is still controversial.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burns_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-burns-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Phobos and Deimos both have much in common with carbonaceous
       <a href="/wiki/C-type_asteroid" title="C-type asteroid">
        C-type asteroids
       </a>
       , with
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        spectra
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       very similar to those of C- or D-type asteroids.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Based on their similarity, one hypothesis is that both moons may be captured
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main-belt_asteroids" title="Main-belt asteroids">
        main-belt asteroids
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESA-Phobos_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ESA-Phobos-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landis_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-landis-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Both moons have very circular orbits which lie almost exactly in Mars's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_plane" title="Equatorial plane">
        equatorial plane
       </a>
       , and hence a capture origin requires a mechanism for
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_circularization" title="Tidal circularization">
        circularizing
       </a>
       the initially highly eccentric orbit, and adjusting its inclination into the equatorial plane, most probably by a combination of atmospheric drag and
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cazenave_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cazenave-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although it is not clear that sufficient time is available for this to occur for Deimos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burns_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-burns-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Capture also requires dissipation of energy. The current
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">
        atmosphere of Mars
       </a>
       is too thin to capture a Phobos-sized object by atmospheric braking.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burns_27-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-burns-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Landis" title="Geoffrey Landis">
        Geoffrey Landis
       </a>
       has pointed out that the capture could have occurred if the original body was a
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_asteroid" title="Binary asteroid">
        binary asteroid
       </a>
       that separated under tidal forces.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landis_29-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-landis-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Phobos could be a second-generation Solar System object that
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">
        coalesced
       </a>
       in orbit after Mars formed, rather than forming concurrently out of the same birth cloud as Mars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESA2010_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ESA2010-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another hypothesis is that Mars was once surrounded by many Phobos- and Deimos-sized bodies, perhaps ejected into orbit around it by a collision with a large
       <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">
        planetesimal
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Craddock_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Craddock-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The high porosity of the interior of Phobos (based on the density of 1.88 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       , voids are estimated to comprise 25 to 35 percent of Phobos' volume) is inconsistent with an asteroidal origin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andert_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Andert-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Observations of Phobos in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_infrared" title="Thermal infrared">
        thermal infrared
       </a>
       suggest a composition containing mainly
       <a href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals#Phyllosilicates" title="Silicate minerals">
        phyllosilicates
       </a>
       , which are well known from the surface of Mars. The spectra are distinct from those of all classes of
       <a href="/wiki/Chondrite" title="Chondrite">
        chondrite
       </a>
       meteorites, again pointing away from an asteroidal origin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giuranna_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Giuranna-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Both sets of findings support an origin of Phobos from material ejected by an impact on Mars that reaccreted in Martian orbit,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blast_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blast-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       similar to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        prevailing theory
       </a>
       for the origin of Earth's moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       The moons of Mars may have started with a huge collision with a protoplanet one third the mass of Mars that formed a ring around Mars. The inner part of the ring formed a large moon. Gravitational interactions between this moon and the outer ring formed Phobos and Deimos. Later, the large moon crashed into Mars, but the two small moons remained in orbit. This theory agrees with the fine-grained surface of the moons and their high porosity. The outer disk would create fine-grained material.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moons_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)#Exploration" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos (moon) § Exploration
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)#Exploration" title="Deimos (moon)">
        Deimos (moon) § Exploration
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Mars" title="Exploration of Mars">
        Exploration of Mars
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       While many Martian probes provided images and other data about Phobos and Deimos, only few were dedicated to these satellites and intended to perform a flyby or landing on the surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       Two probes under the Soviet
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_program" title="Phobos program">
        Phobos program
       </a>
       were successfully launched in 1988, but neither conducted the intended jumping landings on Phobos and Deimos due to failures (although
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Phobos_2" title="Phobos 2">
         Phobos 2
        </a>
       </i>
       successfully photographed Phobos). The post-Soviet Russian
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Fobos-Grunt" title="Fobos-Grunt">
         Fobos-Grunt
        </a>
       </i>
       probe was intended to be the first
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_return_mission" title="Sample return mission">
        sample return mission
       </a>
       from Phobos, but a rocket failure left it stranded in Earth orbit in 2011.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1997 and 1998, the Aladdin mission was selected as a finalist in the NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Program" title="Discovery Program">
        Discovery Program
       </a>
       . The plan was to visit both Phobos and Deimos, and launch projectiles at the satellites. The probe would collect the ejecta as it performed a slow flyby. These samples would be returned to Earth for study three years later. Ultimately, NASA rejected this proposal in favor of
       <a href="/wiki/MESSENGER" title="MESSENGER">
        MESSENGER
       </a>
       , a probe to Mercury.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2007, the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        European Space Agency
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EADS_Astrium" title="EADS Astrium">
        EADS Astrium
       </a>
       proposed and developed a mission to Phobos in 2016 with a lander and sample return, but this mission was never flown. Since 2007 the
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Space_Agency" title="Canadian Space Agency">
        Canadian Space Agency
       </a>
       has been considering the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_Reconnaissance_and_International_Mars_Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Phobos Reconnaissance and International Mars Exploration (page does not exist)">
        PRIME
       </a>
       mission to Phobos with orbiter and lander. Since 2008
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_Glenn_Research_Center" title="NASA Glenn Research Center">
        NASA Glenn Research Center
       </a>
       began studying a Phobos and Deimos sample return mission. Since 2013 NASA developed the
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_Surveyor" title="Phobos Surveyor">
        Phobos Surveyor
       </a>
       mission concept with an orbiter and a small rover that is proposed to launch sometime after 2023.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Proposed NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_And_Deimos_%26_Mars_Environment" title="Phobos And Deimos &amp; Mars Environment">
        PADME
       </a>
       mission proposes to launch in 2020 and reach Mars orbit in 2021 to conduct multiple flybys of the Martian moons.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Also, NASA is assessing the
       <a href="/wiki/OSIRIS-REx#OSIRIS-REx_II" title="OSIRIS-REx">
        OSIRIS-REx II
       </a>
       , concept mission for a sample return from Phobos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another sample return mission from Deimos, called Gulliver. has been conceptualized.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Russia plans to repeat
       <a href="/wiki/Fobos-Grunt" title="Fobos-Grunt">
        Fobos-Grunt
       </a>
       mission around 2024.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
