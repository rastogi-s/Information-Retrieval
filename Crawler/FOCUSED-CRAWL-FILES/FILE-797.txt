      <p>
       <b>
        Ananke
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="/ŋ/: 'ng' in 'sing'">
            ŋ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         ə-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          NANG
         </span>
         -kee
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Ανάγκη
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">
        moon of Jupiter
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Seth_Barnes_Nicholson" title="Seth Barnes Nicholson">
        Seth Barnes Nicholson
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory" title="Mount Wilson Observatory">
        Mount Wilson Observatory
       </a>
       in 1951
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicolson1951_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nicolson1951-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and is named after the mythological
       <a href="/wiki/Ananke_(mythology)" title="Ananke (mythology)">
        Ananke
       </a>
       , the personification of Necessity, and the mother of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moirai" title="Moirai">
        Moirai
       </a>
       (Fates) by
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       . The adjectival form of the name is
       <i>
        Anankean
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Ananke did not receive its present name
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson1938_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nicholson1938-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       until 1975;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_2846_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_2846-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       before then, it was simply known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter XII
        </span>
       </b>
       . It was sometimes called "Adrastea"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaposchkin_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gaposchkin-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       between 1955 and 1975 (
       <a href="/wiki/Adrastea_(moon)" title="Adrastea (moon)">
        Adrastea
       </a>
       is now the name of another satellite of Jupiter).
      </p>
      <p>
       Ananke gives its name to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ananke_group" title="Ananke group">
        Ananke group
       </a>
       , retrograde irregular moons which orbit Jupiter between 19.3 and 22.7 Gm, at inclinations of roughly 150°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardJewittPorco2004_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardJewittPorco2004-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ananke_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Ananke orbits Jupiter on a high eccentricity and high inclination retrograde orbit. Eight irregular satellites orbiting Jupiter have been discovered since 2000 following similar orbits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardJewittPorco2004_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardJewittPorco2004-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The orbital elements are as of January 2000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_2000_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson_2000-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are continuously changing due to
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Solar
       </a>
       and planetary perturbations. The diagram illustrates Ananke's orbit in relation to other retrograde irregular satellites of Jupiter. The eccentricity of selected orbits is represented by the yellow segments (extending from the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        pericentre
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        apocentre
       </a>
       ). The outermost regular satellite
       <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">
        Callisto
       </a>
       is located for reference.
      </p>
      <p>
       Given these orbital elements and the physical characteristics known so far, Ananke is thought to be the largest remnant
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardJewitt2003_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardJewitt2003-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of an original break-up forming the
       <a href="/wiki/Ananke_group" title="Ananke group">
        Ananke group
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nesvorny2004_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nesvorny2004-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2003_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2003-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ananke_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the visible
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       , Ananke appears neutral to light-red (
       <a href="/wiki/Color_index" title="Color index">
        colour indices
       </a>
       B-V=0.90 V-R=0.38).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2003_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2003-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       spectrum is similar to
       <a href="/wiki/P-type_asteroid" title="P-type asteroid">
        P-type asteroids
       </a>
       but with a possible indication of water.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2004_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2004-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
