      <p>
       <b>
        Callirrhoe
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪr/: 'irr' in 'mirror'">
            ɪr
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">
            ˌ
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         kə-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          LIRR
         </span>
         -o-ee
        </i>
       </a>
       ; Greek:
       <i>
        Καλλιρρόη
       </i>
       ), also known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter XVII
        </span>
       </b>
       (17), is one of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       's outermost
       <i>
        named
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellites
       </a>
       . It is an
       <a href="/wiki/Irregular_moon" title="Irregular moon">
        irregular moon
       </a>
       that orbits in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_and_direct_motion" title="Retrograde and direct motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       direction. Callirrhoe was imaged by
       <a href="/wiki/Spacewatch" title="Spacewatch">
        Spacewatch
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Kitt_Peak_National_Observatory" title="Kitt Peak National Observatory">
        Kitt Peak National Observatory
       </a>
       from October 6 through November 4, 1999,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pr_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-pr-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and originally designated as
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        1999 UX
        <sub>
         18
        </sub>
       </span>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon1999_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon1999-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was discovered to be in orbit around Jupiter by
       <a href="/wiki/Timothy_B._Spahr" title="Timothy B. Spahr">
        Tim Spahr
       </a>
       on July 18, 2000, and then given the designation
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/1999 J 1
        </span>
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iauc7460_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-iauc7460-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was the 17th confirmed moon of Jupiter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pr_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-pr-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Callirrhoe has an
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent magnitude
       </a>
       of 20.7,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jplssd_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-jplssd-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       making it even fainter than
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       at magnitude 18.7.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eris-AstDys_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Eris-AstDys-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Jupiter is about 2.5 billion times brighter than Callirrhoe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Callirrhoe is about 8.6 kilometers in diameter,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jplssd_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-jplssd-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and orbits Jupiter at an average distance of 24.1 million kilometers in 758 days, at an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       of 141° to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       (140° to Jupiter's equator) with an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of 0.28. This object was probably captured long ago from a heliocentric orbit and the Sun's gravitational influence makes this orbit highly erratic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pr_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-pr-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was named in October 2002 after
       <a href="/wiki/Callirrhoe_(Oceanid)" title="Callirrhoe (Oceanid)">
        Callirrhoe
       </a>
       , daughter of the river god
       <a href="/wiki/Achelous" title="Achelous">
        Achelous
       </a>
       , one of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       ' (Jupiter's) many conquests.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It belongs to the
       <a href="/wiki/Pasiphae_group" title="Pasiphae group">
        Pasiphae group
       </a>
       , irregular retrograde moons orbiting Jupiter at distances ranging between 22.8 and 24.1 million kilometers, and with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclinations
       </a>
       ranging between 144.5° and 158.3°.
      </p>
      <p>
       As a navigation exercise, the
       <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
        New Horizons
       </a>
       spacecraft imaged Callirrhoe on January 10, 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
