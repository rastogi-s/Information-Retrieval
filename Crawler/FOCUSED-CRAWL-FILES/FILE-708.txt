      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Rebel Moon Rising
        </b>
       </i>
       is a PC game made by Fenris Wolf and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GT_Interactive" title="GT Interactive">
        GT Interactive
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_(console)" title="PlayStation (console)">
        PlayStation
       </a>
       version was also announced,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but never released. In the game, the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       has been colonized, and due to political conflicts, the Lunar colonies are rebelling against the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        United Nations
       </a>
       . The player is on the side of the Lunar alliance, fighting against United Nation forces. The game later takes a twist, when an alien species is discovered.
      </p>
      <p>
       This game is the sequel to
       <i>
        Rebel Moon
       </i>
       . With the same basis as Rising, the player takes on 27 levels in the original
       <i>
        Rebel Moon
       </i>
       , quite a few more levels than Rising.
       <i>
        Rebel Moon
       </i>
       was only released in a bonus disk with the Creative Labs hardware "3D BLASTER PCI". The series was also planned to have a third game, "Rebel Moon Revolution", but it was cancelled by GT Interactive. Due to insufficient communications about the cancellation, Fenris Wolf instated a lawsuit against GT Interactive.
      </p>
      <p>
       The PC shareware version of
       <i>
        Rebel Moon Rising
       </i>
       is included on disc 2 of the EIDOS Interactive game
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Blood_(video_game)" title="Blood (video game)">
         Blood
        </a>
       </i>
       , copyright 1997.
      </p>
      <p>
       A novelization of Rebel Moon, written by
       <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Bethke" title="Bruce Bethke">
        Bruce Bethke
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodore_Beale" title="Theodore Beale">
        Vox Day
       </a>
       (the latter being a pseudonym of one of the game's developers), was published in 1996.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
        Reception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebel_Moon_Rising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_Gaming_World" title="Computer Gaming World">
        Computer Gaming World
       </a>
       described Rebel Moon Rising as technologically innovative and "highly creative", and declared that its escort missions broke new ground in 3D shooter mission design.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Computer_Gaming_World,_September_1997_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Computer_Gaming_World,_September_1997-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The game was commercially unsuccessful, according to Day, because the release of
       <a href="/wiki/Quake_(video_game)" title="Quake (video game)">
        Quake
       </a>
       at about the same time made gaming consumers look for 3D games, rather than the "2.5D" of RMR.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valizadeh_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-valizadeh-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
