      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">
        mythology
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        lunar deity
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/God_(male_deity)" title="God (male deity)">
        god
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Goddess" title="Goddess">
        goddess
       </a>
       associated with, or symbolic of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        moon
       </a>
       . These deities can have a variety of functions and traditions depending upon the culture, but they are often related. Some form of moon worship can be found in most ancient religions.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Moon_in_religion_and_mythology">
        Moon in religion and mythology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_lunar_deities&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Moon in religion and mythology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The monthly cycle of the moon, in contrast to the annual cycle of the sun's path, has been implicitly linked to women's
       <a href="/wiki/Menstrual_cycle" title="Menstrual cycle">
        menstrual cycles
       </a>
       by many cultures, as evident in the links between the words for menstruation and for moon in many
       <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">
        resultant
       </a>
       languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many of the most well-known mythologies feature female lunar deities, such as the Greek goddesses
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(mythology)" title="Phoebe (mythology)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">
        Artemis
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Selene" title="Selene">
        Selene
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Hecate" title="Hecate">
        Hecate
       </a>
       as well as the Chinese goddess
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e" title="Chang'e">
        Chang'e
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Male
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_god" title="Lunar god">
        lunar gods
       </a>
       are also frequent, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Sin_(mythology)" title="Sin (mythology)">
        Sin
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">
        Mesopotamians
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mani_(god)" title="Mani (god)">
        Mani
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_tribes" title="Germanic tribes">
        Germanic tribes
       </a>
       , and the Japanese god
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsukuyomi" title="Tsukuyomi">
        Tsukuyomi
       </a>
       . These cultures usually featured female
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_deity" title="Solar deity">
        Sun goddesses
       </a>
       . There are also many lunar deities that were prevalent in Greek and Egyptian civilizations. For example, Ibis and Chonsu of Thebes were both lunar deities. Thoth was also a lunar deity, but his character is considerably more complex than Ibis and Chonsu.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thoth,_the_Hermes_of_Egypt:_a_study_of_some_aspects_of_theological_thought_in_ancient_Egypt,_page_75_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Thoth,_the_Hermes_of_Egypt:_a_study_of_some_aspects_of_theological_thought_in_ancient_Egypt,_page_75-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Set represented the Moon in the Egyptian Calendar of Lucky and Unlucky Days of papyrus Cairo 86637.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The original
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_religion" title="Proto-Indo-European religion">
        Proto-Indo-European
       </a>
       lunar deity appears to have been a male god.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Several goddesses, like
       <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">
        Artemis
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Hecate" title="Hecate">
        Hecate
       </a>
       , did not originally have lunar aspects, and only acquired them late in antiquity, due to syncretism with
       <a href="/wiki/Selene" title="Selene">
        Selene
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_(goddess)" title="Luna (goddess)">
        Luna
       </a>
       , the de facto Greco-Latin lunar deity. In traditions with male gods, there is little evidence of such syncretism, though the Greco-Roman
       <a href="/wiki/Hermes" title="Hermes">
        Hermes
       </a>
       has been equated with male Egyptian lunar gods like
       <a href="/wiki/Thoth" title="Thoth">
        Thoth
       </a>
       . In Greece proper, remnants of male moon gods are also seen with
       <a href="/wiki/Menelaus" title="Menelaus">
        Menelaus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Also of significance is that many religions and societies are oriented chronologically by the moon, as opposed to the sun. One common example is
       <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">
        Hinduism
       </a>
       in which the word
       <a href="/wiki/Chandra" title="Chandra">
        Chandra
       </a>
       means "moon" and has religious significance during many Hindu festivals (e.g.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karwa_Chauth" title="Karwa Chauth">
        Karwa Chauth
       </a>
       , Sankasht Chaturthi, and during the eclipses).
      </p>
      <p>
       The moon is also worshipped in
       <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">
        witchcraft
       </a>
       , both in its modern form, and in Medieval times (I.E. in the cult of
       <a href="/wiki/Madonna_Oriente" title="Madonna Oriente">
        Madonna Oriente
       </a>
       ). The moon features prominently in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_in_art_and_literature" title="Moon in art and literature">
        art and literature
       </a>
       , and also has a purported influence in human affairs, a belief that consistently remains a feature of
       <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">
        astrology
       </a>
       and theology.
      </p>
