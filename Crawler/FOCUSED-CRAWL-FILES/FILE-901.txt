      <p>
       <b>
        Pandora
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">
            ɔːr
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         pan-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          DOR
         </span>
         -ə
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el">
        Πανδώρα
       </span>
       ) is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellite" title="Inner satellite">
        inner satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . It was discovered in 1980 from photos taken by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       probe, and was provisionally designated
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/1980 S 26
        </span>
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3532_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3532-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In late 1985 it was officially named after
       <a href="/wiki/Pandora" title="Pandora">
        Pandora
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_4157_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_4157-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also designated as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Saturn XVII
        </span>
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUSGS:_Planet_and_Satellite_Names_and_Discoverers_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUSGS:_Planet_and_Satellite_Names_and_Discoverers-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Pandora was thought to be an outer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shepherd_satellite" title="Shepherd satellite">
        shepherd satellite
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#F_Ring" title="Rings of Saturn">
        F Ring
       </a>
       . However, recent studies indicate that it does not play such a role, and that only
       <a href="/wiki/Prometheus_(moon)" title="Prometheus (moon)">
        Prometheus
       </a>
       , the inner shepherd, contributes to the confinement of the narrow ring.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LakdawallaE2014_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LakdawallaE2014-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuzzi2014_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cuzzi2014-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is more heavily cratered than nearby Prometheus, and has at least two large craters 30 kilometres (19 mi) in diameter. The majority of craters on Pandora are shallow as a result of being filled with debris. Ridges and grooves are also present on moon's surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESolar_System,_NASA:_Pandora_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESolar_System,_NASA:_Pandora-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The orbit of Pandora appears to be chaotic, as a consequence of a series of four 118:121
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance#Types_of_resonances" title="Orbital resonance">
        mean-motion resonances
       </a>
       with Prometheus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERenner_et_al._2005_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERenner_et_al._2005-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The most appreciable changes in their orbits occur approximately every 6.2 years,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006-2">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       when the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">
        periapsis
       </a>
       of Pandora lines up with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apoapsis" title="Apoapsis">
        apoapsis
       </a>
       of Prometheus and the moons approach to within about 1,400 kilometres (870 mi). Pandora also has a 3:2 mean-motion resonance with
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006-2">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From its very low density and relatively high
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       , it seems likely that Pandora is a very porous icy body. There is a lot of uncertainty in these values, however, so this remains to be confirmed.
      </p>
