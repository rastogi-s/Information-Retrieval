      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">
        Sergei Korolev
       </a>
       initially promoted the Soyuz A-B-V circumlunar complex (
       <i>
        7K-9K-11K
       </i>
       ) concept (also known as L1) in which a two-man craft Soyuz 7K would rendezvous with other components (9K and 11K) in Earth orbit to assemble a lunar excursion vehicle, the components being delivered by the proven
       <a href="/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)" title="R-7 (rocket family)">
        R-7
       </a>
       rocket.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Besides the Soyuz 7K spacecraft, the complex would feature a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_9K" title="Soyuz 9K">
        Soyuz 9K
       </a>
       booster and a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_11K" title="Soyuz 11K">
        Soyuz 11K
       </a>
       tanker with twin whip antennas.
      </p>
      <p>
       The 7K would have been equipped with cameras and sensors to study the lunar surface during the flyby, at a distance of 1,000 to 20,000 km from the Moon's surface. Total flight time would have been 7 to 8 days.
      </p>
