      <p>
       A
       <b>
        dark moon
       </b>
       describes the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       during the period it is invisible against the backdrop of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">
        sky
       </a>
       . The duration of a dark moon varies between 1.5 and 3.5 days, depending on its
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">
        ecliptic latitude
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical" title="Astronomical">
        astronomical
       </a>
       usage, the
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       occurs in the middle of this period,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       when the Moon and Sun are in
       <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy)" title="Conjunction (astronomy)">
        conjunction
       </a>
       . This definition has entered popular usage, so that calendars will typically indicate the date of the "new moon" rather than the "dark moon". However, "new moon" originally referred to the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        crescent phase
       </a>
       on the first night it is visible, one or two days after conjunction. Maritime records from the 19th century distinguish the dark moon (no moon) from the new moon.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dark_moon_as_the_30th_division">
        Dark moon as the 30th division
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dark_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Dark moon as the 30th division">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
         Oxford English Dictionary
        </a>
       </i>
       defines the
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       as “the first visible crescent of the Moon, after conjunction with the Sun”.
       <i>
        Dark Moon
       </i>
       is a term used for a waning crescent moon. When the Moon is divided into 30 portions, as the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        ancient Greeks
       </a>
       did in the time of Homer, the
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">
        Babylonians
       </a>
       did, and the Indians still do today (calling them
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tithi" title="Tithi">
         tithi
        </a>
       </i>
       ), the last phase is called the Dark Moon. In Greek, it was called the Old Moon and associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Hecate" title="Hecate">
        Hecate
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In India, it is called
       <a href="/wiki/Amavasya" title="Amavasya">
        Amavasya
       </a>
       and associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Kali" title="Kali">
        Kali
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Both of these goddesses have a dark connotation, hence the Dark Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Babylonian, Greek, and Indian culture, the Dark Moon occurs within the 12° of
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_distance" title="Angular distance">
        angular distance
       </a>
       between the Moon and Sun before
       <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy)" title="Conjunction (astronomy)">
        conjunction
       </a>
       (a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Syzygy_(astronomy)" title="Syzygy (astronomy)">
        syzygy
       </a>
       ). The New Moon occurs within the 12° after syzygy. This 12°
       <a href="/wiki/Arc_(geometry)" title="Arc (geometry)">
        arc
       </a>
       is called
       <i>
        uma
       </i>
       by the Babylonians and
       <i>
        tithi
       </i>
       by the Indians. The Moon takes a mean duration of 23 hours and 37 minutes to cover this length, but this period can vary from 21 to 26 hours because of the Moon's orbital
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_theory" title="Lunar theory">
        anomaly
       </a>
       . This means that the Dark Moon lasts approximately 23 hours and 37 minutes before the time marked as New Moon on a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">
        lunar calendar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
