      <p>
       A
       <b>
        quake
       </b>
       is the result when the surface of a
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        moon
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       begins to shake, usually as the consequence of a sudden release of energy transmitted as
       <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">
        seismic
       </a>
       waves, and potentially with great violence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The types of quakes include:
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Earthquake">
        Earthquake
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quake_(natural_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Earthquake">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">
        Earthquake
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       An earthquake is a
       <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">
        phenomenon
       </a>
       that results from the sudden release of stored energy in the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       that creates
       <a href="/wiki/Seismic_wave" title="Seismic wave">
        seismic waves
       </a>
       . At the Earth's surface, earthquakes may manifest themselves by a shaking or displacement of the ground and sometimes cause
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsunamis" title="Tsunamis">
        tsunamis
       </a>
       , which may lead to loss of life and destruction of property. An earthquake is caused by tectonic plates (sections of the Earth's crust) getting stuck and putting a strain on the ground. The strain becomes so great that rocks give way and fault lines occur.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Moonquake">
        Moonquake
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quake_(natural_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Moonquake">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       "Moonquake" redirects here. For other uses, see
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Moonquake_(disambiguation)" title="Moonquake (disambiguation)">
        Moonquake (disambiguation)
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       A moonquake is the lunar equivalent of an earthquake (i.e., a quake on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       ). They were first discovered by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">
        Apollo
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        astronauts
       </a>
       . The largest moonquakes are much weaker than the largest earthquakes, though they can last for up to an hour, due to the lack of water to dampen seismic vibrations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Information about moonquakes comes from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismometers" title="Seismometers">
        seismometers
       </a>
       placed on the Moon by Apollo astronauts from 1969 through 1972. The instruments placed by the Apollo 12, 14, 15 and 16 functioned perfectly until they were switched off in 1977.
      </p>
      <p>
       According to
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       , there are at least four different kinds of moonquakes:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Deep moonquakes (~700 km below the surface, probably tidal in origin)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">
         Meteorite
        </a>
        impact vibrations
       </li>
       <li>
        Thermal moonquakes (the frigid lunar crust expands when sunlight returns after the two-week lunar night)
       </li>
       <li>
        Shallow moonquakes (20 or 30 kilometers below the surface)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The first three kinds of moonquakes mentioned above tend to be mild; however, shallow moonquakes can register up to 5.5 on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richter_scale" title="Richter scale">
        Richter scale
       </a>
       . Between 1972 and 1977, 28 shallow moonquakes were observed. On Earth, quakes of magnitude 4.5 and above can cause damage to buildings and other rigid structures.
      </p>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selenoseismology" title="Selenoseismology">
        Selenoseismology
       </a>
      </div>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Marsquake">
        Marsquake
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quake_(natural_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marsquake">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A marsquake is a quake that occurs on the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       . A recent study suggests that marsquakes occur every million years. This suggestion is related to recently found evidence of
       <a href="/wiki/Volcanology_of_Mars#Tectonic_boundaries" title="Volcanology of Mars">
        Mars's tectonic boundaries
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mars_seismic_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-mars_seismic-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Venusquake">
        Venusquake
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quake_(natural_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Venusquake">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A venusquake is a quake that occurs on the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A venusquake may have caused a new scarp and a
       <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">
        landslide
       </a>
       to form. An image of the landslides was taken in November 1990 during the first flight around Venus by the
       <a href="/wiki/Magellan_(spacecraft)" title="Magellan (spacecraft)">
        <i>
         Magellan
        </i>
        spacecraft
       </a>
       . Another image was taken on July 23, 1991 as the
       <i>
        Magellan
       </i>
       revolved around Venus for the second time. Each image was 24 kilometres (15 mi) across and 38 kilometres (24 mi) long, and was centered at 2° south latitude and 74° east longitude. The pair of
       <i>
        Magellan
       </i>
       images shows a region in
       <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite_Terra" title="Aphrodite Terra">
        Aphrodite Terra
       </a>
       , within a steeply sloping valley that is cut by many fractures (faults).
      </p>

      <p>
       Planetquake is the generic term for quakes occurring on terrestrial planets, since current observational technology cannot penetrate to the solid core of gaseous planets.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sunquake">
        Sunquake
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quake_(natural_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sunquake">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">
        Helioseismology
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A sunquake is a quake that occurs on the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Seismic waves produced by sunquakes occur in the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       and can travel at velocities of 35,000 kilometres per hour (22,000 mph) for distances up to 400,000 kilometres (250,000 mi) before fading away.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solar_Flare_Leaves_Sun_Quaking_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Solar_Flare_Leaves_Sun_Quaking-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On July 9, 1996, a sunquake was produced by an X2.6 class
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flare
       </a>
       and its corresponding
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejection
       </a>
       . According to researchers who reported the event in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">
         Nature
        </a>
       </i>
       , this sunquake was comparable to an earthquake of a magnitude 11.3 on the Richter scale. That represents a release of energy approximately 40,000 times greater than that of the devastating
       <a href="/wiki/1906_San_Francisco_earthquake" title="1906 San Francisco earthquake">
        1906 San Francisco earthquake
       </a>
       , and far greater than that of any earthquake ever recorded. Such an event contains the energy of 100-110 billion tons of TNT or 2 million modest sized nuclear bombs. It is unclear how such a relatively modest flare could have liberated sufficient energy to generate such powerful seismic waves.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solar_Flare_Leaves_Sun_Quaking_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Solar_Flare_Leaves_Sun_Quaking-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        ESA
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       spacecraft
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO
       </a>
       records sunquakes as part of its mission to study the Sun.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Starquake">
        Starquake
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quake_(natural_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Starquake">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroseismology" title="Asteroseismology">
        Asteroseismology
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A starquake is an
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
        astrophysical
       </a>
       phenomenon that occurs when the crust of a
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">
        neutron star
       </a>
       undergoes a sudden adjustment, analogous to an earthquake on Earth. Starquakes are thought to result from two different mechanisms. One is the huge stresses exerted on the surface of the neutron star produced by twists in the ultra-strong interior
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic fields
       </a>
       . A second cause is a result of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spindown&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spindown (page does not exist)">
        spindown
       </a>
       . As the neutron star loses angular velocity due to
       <a href="/wiki/Frame-dragging" title="Frame-dragging">
        frame-dragging
       </a>
       and by the bleeding off of energy due to it being a rotating magnetic dipole, the crust develops an enormous amount of stress. Once that exceeds a certain amount, the shape adjusts itself to a shape closer to non-rotating equilibrium: a perfect sphere. The actual change is believed to be on the order of micrometers or less, and occurs in less than a millionth of a second.
      </p>
      <p>
       The largest recorded starquake was detected on December 27, 2004 from the ultracompact stellar corpse (
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetar" title="Magnetar">
        magnetar
       </a>
       )
       <a href="/wiki/SGR_1806-20" title="SGR 1806-20">
        SGR 1806-20
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which created a quake equivalent to a magnitude 32.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="Space.com and BBC references do not state this (November 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The quake, which occurred 50,000 light years from Earth, released gamma rays equivalent to 10
       <sup>
        37
       </sup>
       kW in intensity. Had it occurred within a distance of 10 light years from Earth, the quake would have possibly triggered a mass extinction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
