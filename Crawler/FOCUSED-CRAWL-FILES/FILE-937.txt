      <p>
       <b>
        Chiron
       </b>
       (Greek:
       <i>
        <b>
         Χείρων
        </b>
       </i>
       ) is the name given to a supposed
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moon
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       sighted by
       <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Goldschmidt" title="Hermann Goldschmidt">
        Hermann Goldschmidt
       </a>
       in 1861. It has since been determined that no such moon exists.
      </p>
      <p>
       Hermann Goldschmidt announced the discovery of the ninth moon of Saturn in April 1861, which, he said, orbited between
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">
        Titan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(moon)" title="Hyperion (moon)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
       . Goldschmidt's discovery was never confirmed, and Chiron was never seen again.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1898,
       <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Pickering" title="William Henry Pickering">
        William Henry Pickering
       </a>
       discovered
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)" title="Phoebe (moon)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       , which is now considered the ninth moon of Saturn. Strangely, in 1905, Pickering believed that he had discovered another moon of Saturn, which, he reported, orbited the
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       between Titan and Hyperion. He called this new moon
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Themis_(moon)" title="Themis (moon)">
        Themis
       </a>
       . Themis, like Chiron, was never sighted again.
      </p>
      <p>
       An object, now classified as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centaur_(planetoid)" title="Centaur (planetoid)">
        centaur
       </a>
       , which was discovered in 1977, is named
       <a href="/wiki/2060_Chiron" title="2060 Chiron">
        2060 Chiron
       </a>
       .
      </p>
