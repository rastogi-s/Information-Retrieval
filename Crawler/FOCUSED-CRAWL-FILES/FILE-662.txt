      <p>
       This is a
       <b>
        list of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hijri_years" title="Hijri years">
         Hijri years
        </a>
       </b>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         anno Hegirae
        </span>
       </i>
       or
       <b>
        AH
       </b>
       ) with the corresponding
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_era" title="Common era">
        common era
       </a>
       years where applicable. For Hijri years since 1298 (AD 1880/1881) the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregorian_date" title="Gregorian date">
        Gregorian date
       </a>
       of 1
       <a href="/wiki/Muharram" title="Muharram">
        Muharram
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_New_Year" title="Islamic New Year">
        first day of the year
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
        Islamic calendar
       </a>
       , is given.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first Hijri year (AH 1) was retrospectively considered to have begun on the
       <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">
        Julian calendar
       </a>
       date 15 July
       <a href="/wiki/622" title="622">
        622
       </a>
       (known as the ‘astronomical’ or ‘Thursday’ epoch,
       <a href="/wiki/Julian_day" title="Julian day">
        Julian day
       </a>
       1,948,439) or 16 July 622 (the ‘civil’ or ‘Friday’ epoch, Julian day 1,948,440), denoted as "1 Muharram, AH 1". Years prior to this are reckoned in English as
       <b>
        BH
       </b>
       ("Before the Hijra").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In principle, each Islamic month begins with sighting of the new crescent moon (after a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Moon" title="New Moon">
        New Moon
       </a>
       ) at sunset. Because of this, the calendar is dependent on observational conditions and cannot be predicted or reconstructed with certainty, but
       <a href="/wiki/Tabular_Islamic_calendar" title="Tabular Islamic calendar">
        tabular calendars
       </a>
       are in use which determine the dates algorithmically. Because of this, dates may vary by up to two days between traditions or countries.
      </p>

      </p>
