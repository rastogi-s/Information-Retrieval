      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Rabbit and the Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       is a 1998 book by
       <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Wood_(naturalist)" title="Douglas Wood (naturalist)">
        Douglas Wood
       </a>
       . It is an adaption of a
       <a href="/wiki/Cree" title="Cree">
        Cree
       </a>
       legend about how
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_rabbit" title="Moon rabbit">
        Rabbit
       </a>
       reached the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , and how the
       <a href="/wiki/Whooping_crane" title="Whooping crane">
        Whooping crane
       </a>
       got its long legs and red head marking.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
        Reception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rabbit_and_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BookList" title="BookList">
         BookList
        </a>
        '
       </i>
       , in a review of
       <i>
        Rabbit and the Moon
       </i>
       , wrote "The watercolor illustrations have a fuzzy, sleepy quality, yet are clear enough that the animals depict a range of emotions .. The story itself is told in fairly short, easy-to-understand sentences, making this a good a choice for a bedtime story or for older students studying folktales."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buffalo_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-buffalo-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/School_Library_Journal" title="School Library Journal">
         School Library Journal
        </a>
       </i>
       called it a "satisfying story", but "An uneven offering."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buffalo_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-buffalo-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Kirkus_Reviews" title="Kirkus Reviews">
         Kirkus Reviews
        </a>
       </i>
       was somewhat critical, writing, "Though Wood pays homage to Rabbit as a trickster in the source note, there's no mischief in the story and Rabbit is portrayed as polite and unassuming. Baker's watercolors are another disappointment; Rabbit's limbs change length and proportions unpredictably, so that sometimes his shape is that of a natural-looking rabbit, and other times that of a human child in a fur suit."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Rabbit and the Moon
       </i>
       has also been reviewed by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Horn_Book_Magazine" title="The Horn Book Magazine">
         The Horn Book Magazine
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Publishers_Weekly" title="Publishers Weekly">
         Publishers Weekly
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
