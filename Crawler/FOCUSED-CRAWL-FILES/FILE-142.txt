      <p>
       <b>
        Umbriel
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">
        moon of Uranus
       </a>
       discovered on October 24, 1851, by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Lassell" title="William Lassell">
        William Lassell
       </a>
       . It was discovered at the same time as
       <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(moon)" title="Ariel (moon)">
        Ariel
       </a>
       and named after a character in
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">
        Alexander Pope
       </a>
       's poem
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Rape_of_the_Lock" title="The Rape of the Lock">
         The Rape of the Lock
        </a>
       </i>
       . Umbriel consists mainly of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        ice
       </a>
       with a substantial fraction of
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">
        rock
       </a>
       , and may be differentiated into a rocky
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_(geology)" title="Core (geology)">
        core
       </a>
       and an icy
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       . The surface is the darkest among Uranian moons, and appears to have been shaped primarily by impacts. However, the presence of canyons suggests early endogenic processes, and the moon may have undergone an early endogenically driven resurfacing event that obliterated its older surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       Covered by numerous
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact craters
       </a>
       reaching 210 km (130 mi) in diameter, Umbriel is the second most heavily cratered satellite of Uranus after
       <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(moon)" title="Oberon (moon)">
        Oberon
       </a>
       . The most prominent surface feature is a ring of bright material on the floor of
       <a href="/wiki/Wunda_(crater)" title="Wunda (crater)">
        Wunda
       </a>
       crater. This moon, like all moons of Uranus, probably formed from an
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">
        accretion disk
       </a>
       that surrounded the planet just after its formation. The Uranian system has been studied up close only once, by the spacecraft
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       in January 1986. It took several images of Umbriel, which allowed mapping of about 40% of the moon’s surface.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_name">
        Discovery and name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbriel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Umbriel, along with another Uranian satellite,
       <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(moon)" title="Ariel (moon)">
        Ariel
       </a>
       , was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Lassell" title="William Lassell">
        William Lassell
       </a>
       on October 24, 1851.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell_1851_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell_1851-13">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell,_letter_1851_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell,_letter_1851-14">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although
       <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">
        William Herschel
       </a>
       , the discoverer of
       <a href="/wiki/Titania_(moon)" title="Titania (moon)">
        Titania
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(moon)" title="Oberon (moon)">
        Oberon
       </a>
       , claimed at the end of the 18th century that he had observed four additional moons of Uranus,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herschel_1798_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Herschel_1798-15">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       his observations were not confirmed and those four objects are now thought to be spurious.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Struve_1848_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Struve_1848-16">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       All of Uranus's moons are named after characters created by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        William Shakespeare
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">
        Alexander Pope
       </a>
       . The names of all four satellites of Uranus then known were suggested by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">
        John Herschel
       </a>
       in 1852 at the request of Lassell.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell_1852_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell_1852-17">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Umbriel is the "dusky melancholy sprite" in Alexander Pope's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Rape_of_the_Lock" title="The Rape of the Lock">
         The Rape of the Lock
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuiper_1949_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kuiper_1949-18">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the name suggests the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
         umbra
        </a>
       </i>
       , meaning
       <i>
        shadow
       </i>
       . The moon is also designated
       <b>
        Uranus II
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell,_letter_1851_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell,_letter_1851-14">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbriel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Umbriel orbits Uranus at the distance of about 266,000 km (165,000 mi), being the third farthest from the planet among its
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">
        five major moons
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [e]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Umbriel's orbit has a small
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       and is
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclined
       </a>
       very little relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       of Uranus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orbit_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-orbit-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       is around 4.1 Earth days, coincident with its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotational_period" title="Rotational period">
        rotational period
       </a>
       . In other words, Umbriel is a
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidally_locked" title="Tidally locked">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       satellite, with one face always pointing toward its parent planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Umbriel's orbit lies completely inside the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Uranus" title="Magnetosphere of Uranus">
        Uranian magnetosphere
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-11">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is important, because the trailing
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere#Hemisphere" title="Sphere">
        hemispheres
       </a>
       of airless satellites orbiting inside a magnetosphere (like Umbriel) are struck by magnetospheric
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       , which co-rotates with the planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_Acuña_et_al._1986_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness_Acuña_et_al._1986-20">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This bombardment may lead to the darkening of the trailing hemispheres, which is actually observed for all Uranian moons except
       <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(moon)" title="Oberon (moon)">
        Oberon
       </a>
       (see below).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-11">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Umbriel also serves as a sink of the magnetospheric charged particles, which creates a pronounced dip in energetic particle count near the moon's orbit as observed by
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       in 1986.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krimigis_Armstrong_et_al._1986_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Krimigis_Armstrong_et_al._1986-21">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because Uranus orbits the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       almost on its side, and its moons orbit in the planet's equatorial plane, they (including Umbriel) are subject to an extreme seasonal cycle. Both northern and southern
       <a href="/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies#Geographic_poles" title="Poles of astronomical bodies">
        poles
       </a>
       spend 42 years in complete darkness, and another 42 years in continuous sunlight, with the Sun rising close to the
       <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">
        zenith
       </a>
       over one of the poles at each
       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">
        solstice
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_11-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-11">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       flyby coincided with the southern hemisphere's 1986 summer solstice, when nearly the entire northern hemisphere was unilluminated. Once every 42 years, when Uranus has an
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinox
       </a>
       and its equatorial plane intersects the Earth, mutual
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occultations
       </a>
       of Uranus's moons become possible. In 2007–2008 a number of such events were observed including two occultations of Titania by Umbriel on August 15 and December 8, 2007 as well as of Ariel by Umbriel on August 19, 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller_Chanover_2009_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Miller_Chanover_2009-22">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arlot_Dumas_et_al._2008_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Arlot_Dumas_et_al._2008-23">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Currently Umbriel is not involved in any
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       with other Uranian satellites. Early in its history, however, it may have been in a 1:3 resonance with
       <a href="/wiki/Miranda_(moon)" title="Miranda (moon)">
        Miranda
       </a>
       . This would have increased Miranda's orbital eccentricity, contributing to the internal heating and geological activity of that moon, while Umbriel's orbit would have been less affected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990-24">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Due to Uranus's lower
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">
        oblateness
       </a>
       and smaller size relative to its satellites, its moons can escape more easily from a mean motion resonance than those of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . After Miranda escaped from this resonance (through a mechanism that probably resulted in its anomalously high orbital inclination), its eccentricity would have been damped, turning off the heat source.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1989_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1989-25">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malhotra_Dermott_1990_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Malhotra_Dermott_1990-26">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_and_internal_structure">
        Composition and internal structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbriel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition and internal structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Umbriel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="729" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Umbriel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png/250px-Umbriel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Umbriel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png/375px-Umbriel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Umbriel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png/500px-Umbriel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Umbriel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Size comparison of
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         , the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         , and Umbriel.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Umbriel is the third largest and fourth most massive of Uranian moons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [f]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The moon's density is 1.39 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_Campbell_et_al._1992_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson_Campbell_et_al._1992-6">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which indicates that it mainly consists of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        water ice
       </a>
       , with a dense non-ice component constituting around 40% of its mass.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-29">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The latter could be made of
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">
        rock
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbonaceous
       </a>
       material including heavy
       <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">
        organic compounds
       </a>
       known as
       <a href="/wiki/Tholin" title="Tholin">
        tholins
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The presence of water ice is supported by
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic" title="Spectroscopic">
        spectroscopic
       </a>
       observations, which have revealed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">
        crystalline
       </a>
       water ice on the surface of the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_11-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-11">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Water ice
       <a href="/wiki/Absorption_band" title="Absorption band">
        absorption bands
       </a>
       are stronger on Umbriel's leading hemisphere than on the trailing hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_11-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-11">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The cause of this asymmetry is not known, but it may be related to the bombardment by charged particles from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Uranus" title="Magnetosphere of Uranus">
        magnetosphere of Uranus
       </a>
       , which is stronger on the trailing hemisphere (due to the plasma's co-rotation).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_11-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-11">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The energetic particles tend to
       <a href="/wiki/Sputtering" title="Sputtering">
        sputter
       </a>
       water ice, decompose
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       trapped in ice as
       <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_hydrate" title="Clathrate hydrate">
        clathrate hydrate
       </a>
       and darken other organics, leaving a dark, carbon-rich
       <a href="/wiki/Residue_(chemistry)" title="Residue (chemistry)">
        residue
       </a>
       behind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_11-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-11">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Except for water, the only other compound identified on the surface of Umbriel by the infrared spectroscopy is
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
        carbon dioxide
       </a>
       , which is concentrated mainly on the trailing hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_11-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-11">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The origin of the carbon dioxide is not completely clear. It might be produced locally from
       <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">
        carbonates
       </a>
       or organic materials under the influence of the energetic charged particles coming from the magnetosphere of Uranus or the solar
       <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
        ultraviolet
       </a>
       radiation. This hypothesis would explain the asymmetry in its distribution, as the trailing hemisphere is subject to a more intense magnetospheric influence than the leading hemisphere. Another possible source is the
       <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">
        outgassing
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primordial_element" title="Primordial element">
        primordial
       </a>
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       trapped by water ice in Umbriel's interior. The escape of CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       from the interior may be a result of past geological activity on this moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_11-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-11">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Umbriel may be differentiated into a rocky
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_(geology)" title="Core (geology)">
        core
       </a>
       surrounded by an icy
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_29-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-29">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If this is the case, the radius of the core (317 km) is about 54% of the radius of the moon, and its mass is around 40% of the moon’s mass—the parameters are dictated by the moon's composition. The pressure in the center of Umbriel is about 0.24
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPa" title="GPa">
        GPa
       </a>
       (2.4
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kbar" title="Kbar">
        kbar
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_29-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-29">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The current state of the icy mantle is unclear, although the existence of a subsurface ocean is considered unlikely.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_29-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-29">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_features">
        Surface features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbriel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Surface features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Umbriel's surface is the darkest of the Uranian moons, and reflects less than half as much light as Ariel, a sister satellite of similar size.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPLSSD_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-JPLSSD-27">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Umbriel has a very low
       <a href="/wiki/Bond_albedo" title="Bond albedo">
        Bond albedo
       </a>
       of only about 10% as compared to 23% for Ariel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoschka_2001,_Hubble_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Karkoschka_2001,_Hubble-10">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The reflectivity of the moon's surface decreases from 26% at a phase angle of 0° (
       <a href="/wiki/Geometric_albedo" title="Geometric albedo">
        geometric albedo
       </a>
       ) to 19% at an angle of about 1°. This phenomenon is called
       <a href="/wiki/Opposition_surge" title="Opposition surge">
        opposition surge
       </a>
       . The surface of Umbriel is slightly blue in color,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_McCord_1991_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bell_McCord_1991-30">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while fresh bright impact deposits (in
       <a href="/wiki/Wunda_(crater)" title="Wunda (crater)">
        Wunda
       </a>
       crater, for instance)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       are even bluer. There may be an asymmetry between the leading and trailing hemispheres; the former appears to be redder than the latter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buratti_Mosher_1991_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buratti_Mosher_1991-32">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The reddening of the surfaces probably results from
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weathering" title="Space weathering">
        space weathering
       </a>
       from bombardment by charged particles and
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">
        micrometeorites
       </a>
       over the age of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_McCord_1991_30-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bell_McCord_1991-30">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the color asymmetry of Umbriel is likely caused by accretion of a reddish material coming from outer parts of the Uranian system, possibly, from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellites
       </a>
       , which would occur predominately on the leading hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buratti_Mosher_1991_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buratti_Mosher_1991-32">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The surface of Umbriel is relatively homogeneous—it does not demonstrate strong variation in either albedo or color.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_McCord_1991_30-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bell_McCord_1991-30">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Scientists have so far recognized only one class of geological feature on Umbriel—
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        craters
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_33-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-usgs-33">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The surface of Umbriel has far more and larger craters than do Ariel and
       <a href="/wiki/Titania_(moon)" title="Titania (moon)">
        Titania
       </a>
       . It shows the least geological activity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In fact, among the Uranian moons only Oberon has more impact craters than Umbriel. The observed crater diameters range from a few kilometers at the low end to 210 kilometers for the largest known crater, Wokolo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_33-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-usgs-33">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       All recognized craters on Umbriel have central peaks,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but no crater has
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_system" title="Ray system">
        rays
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Near Umbriel's equator lies the most prominent surface feature: Wunda crater, which has a diameter of about 131 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgsWunda_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-usgsWunda-36">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunt_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hunt-37">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Wunda has a large ring of bright material on its floor, which may be an impact deposit
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or deposit of carbon dioxide ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sori2017_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sori2017-38">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nearby, seen along the
       <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">
        terminator
       </a>
       , are the craters
       <a href="/wiki/Vuver_(crater)" title="Vuver (crater)">
        Vuver
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Skynd_(crater)" title="Skynd (crater)">
        Skynd
       </a>
       , which lack bright rims but possess bright central peaks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunt_37-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-hunt-37">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Study of limb profiles of Umbriel revealed a possible very large impact feature having the diameter of about 400 km and depth of approximately 5 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore_Schenk_et_al._2004_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moore_Schenk_et_al._2004-39">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Much like other moons of Uranus, the surface of Umbriel is cut by a system of canyons trending northeast–southwest.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft1989_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Croft1989-40">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are not, however, officially recognized due to the poor imaging resolution and generally bland appearance of this moon, which hinders
       <a href="/wiki/Geologic_map" title="Geologic map">
        geological mapping
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Umbriel's heavily cratered surface has probably been stable since the
       <a href="/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment" title="Late Heavy Bombardment">
        Late Heavy Bombardment
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The only signs of the ancient internal activity are canyons and dark polygons—dark patches with complex shapes measuring from tens to hundreds of kilometers across.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfenstein_Thomas_et_al._1989_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Helfenstein_Thomas_et_al._1989-41">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The polygons were identified from precise photometry of
       <i>
        Voyager 2'
       </i>
       s images and are distributed more or less uniformly on the surface of Umbriel, trending northeast–southwest. Some polygons correspond to depressions of a few kilometers deep and may have been created during an early episode of tectonic activity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfenstein_Thomas_et_al._1989_41-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Helfenstein_Thomas_et_al._1989-41">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Currently there is no explanation for why Umbriel is so dark and uniform in appearance. Its surface may be covered by a relatively thin layer of dark material (so called
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
         umbral
        </a>
        material
       </i>
       ) excavated by an impact or expelled in an explosive volcanic eruption.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [h]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buratti_Mosher_1991_32-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buratti_Mosher_1991-32">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Alternatively, Umbriel's crust may be entirely composed of the dark material, which prevented formation of bright features like crater rays. However, the presence of the bright feature within Wunda seems to contradict this hypothesis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_evolution">
        Origin and evolution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbriel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Origin and evolution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Umbriel_usgsx2.jpg">
         <img alt="A spherical blueish body with its surface covered by craters and polygons. The lower right part is smooth." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="702" data-file-width="702" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Umbriel_usgsx2.jpg/220px-Umbriel_usgsx2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Umbriel_usgsx2.jpg/330px-Umbriel_usgsx2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Umbriel_usgsx2.jpg/440px-Umbriel_usgsx2.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Umbriel_usgsx2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Map of Umbriel showing polygons
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Umbriel is thought to have formed from an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disc" title="Accretion disc">
        accretion disc
       </a>
       or subnebula; a disc of gas and dust that either existed around Uranus for some time after its formation or was created by the giant impact that most likely gave Uranus its large
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        obliquity
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mousis_2004_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mousis_2004-43">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The precise composition of the subnebula is not known; however, the higher density of Uranian moons compared to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Saturn" title="Moons of Saturn">
        moons of Saturn
       </a>
       indicates that it may have been relatively water-poor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [i]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Significant amounts of
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
        nitrogen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbon
       </a>
       may have been present in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
        carbon monoxide
       </a>
       (CO) and
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
        molecular nitrogen
       </a>
       (N
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       ) instead of
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       and methane.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mousis_2004_43-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mousis_2004-43">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The moons that formed in such a subnebula would contain less water ice (with CO and N
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       trapped as clathrate) and more rock, explaining the higher density.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Umbriel's accretion probably lasted for several thousand years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mousis_2004_43-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mousis_2004-43">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The impacts that accompanied accretion caused heating of the moon's outer layer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988-45">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The maximum temperature of around 180 K was reached at the depth of about 3 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988_45-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988-45">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After the end of formation, the subsurface layer cooled, while the interior of Umbriel heated due to decay of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">
        radioactive elements
       </a>
       present in its rocks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The cooling near-surface layer contracted, while the interior expanded. This caused strong
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)" title="Stress (mechanics)">
        extensional stresses
       </a>
       in the moon's crust, which may have led to cracking.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hillier_&amp;_Squyres_1991_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hillier_&amp;_Squyres_1991-46">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This process probably lasted for about 200 million years, implying that any endogenous activity ceased billions of years ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The initial
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">
        accretional heating
       </a>
       together with continued decay of radioactive elements may have led to melting of the ice
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988_45-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988-45">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       if an
       <a href="/wiki/Antifreeze" title="Antifreeze">
        antifreeze
       </a>
       like ammonia (in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">
        ammonia hydrate
       </a>
       ) or some salt was present.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_29-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-29">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The melting may have led to the separation of ice from rocks and formation of a rocky core surrounded by an icy mantle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A layer of liquid water (ocean) rich in dissolved ammonia may have formed at the core–mantle boundary. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutectic_temperature" title="Eutectic temperature">
        eutectic temperature
       </a>
       of this mixture is 176 K. The ocean, however, is likely to have frozen long ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_29-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-29">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Among Uranian moons Umbriel was least subjected to endogenic resurfacing processes,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although it may like other Uranian moons have experienced a very early resurfacing event.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfenstein_Thomas_et_al._1989_41-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Helfenstein_Thomas_et_al._1989-41">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Voyager_spacecraft.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2494" data-file-width="3017" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Voyager_spacecraft.jpg/200px-Voyager_spacecraft.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Voyager_spacecraft.jpg/300px-Voyager_spacecraft.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Voyager_spacecraft.jpg/400px-Voyager_spacecraft.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Voyager_spacecraft.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The Voyager 2 spacecraft
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Uranus" title="Exploration of Uranus">
        Exploration of Uranus
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       So far the only close-up images of Umbriel have been from the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       probe, which photographed the moon during its flyby of Uranus in January 1986. Since the closest distance between
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       and Umbriel was 325,000 km (202,000 mi),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone_1987_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stone_1987-47">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the best images of this moon have a spatial resolution of about 5.2 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The images cover about 40% of the surface, but only 20% was photographed with the quality required for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_mapping" title="Geological mapping">
        geological mapping
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_1987_31-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_1987-31">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the time of the flyby the southern hemisphere of Umbriel (like those of the other moons) was pointed towards the Sun, so the northern (dark) hemisphere could not be studied.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       No other spacecraft has ever visited Uranus (and Umbriel), and no mission to Uranus and its moons are planned.
      </p>
