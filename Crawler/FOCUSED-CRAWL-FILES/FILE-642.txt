      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Package
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        ALSEP
       </b>
       ) comprised a set of scientific instruments placed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        astronauts
       </a>
       at the landing site of each of the five
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo missions
       </a>
       to land on the Moon following
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       (Apollos
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        12
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">
        14
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        15
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">
        16
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        17
       </a>
       ). Apollo 11 left a smaller package called the
       <b>
        Early Apollo Scientific Experiments Package
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        EASEP
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiments_Package&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The instrumentation and experiments that would comprise ALSEP were decided in February 1966. Specifically, the experiments, institutions responsible, and principal investigators and coinvestigators were:
       <sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_EAweb">
        <a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiments_Package#endnote_EAweb">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Passive Lunar Seismic Experiment:
        <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">
         Massachusetts Institute of Technology
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Frank_Press" title="Frank Press">
         Frank Press
        </a>
        ;
        <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">
         Columbia University
        </a>
        , George Sutton;
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgia_Tech" title="Georgia Tech">
         Georgia Tech
        </a>
        , Robert Hostetler
       </li>
       <li>
        Lunar Tri-axis Magnetometer:
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_Ames_Research_Center" title="NASA Ames Research Center">
         NASA Ames Research Center
        </a>
        , C. P. Sonett;
        <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">
         Marshall Space Flight Center
        </a>
        , Jerry Modisette.
       </li>
       <li>
        Medium-Energy
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
         Solar Wind
        </a>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">
         Jet Propulsion Laboratory
        </a>
        , C. W. Snyder and M. M. Neugebauer.
       </li>
       <li>
        Suprathermal Ion Detection:
        <a href="/wiki/Rice_University" title="Rice University">
         Rice University
        </a>
        , J. W. Freeman, Jr.; Marshall Space Flight Center,
        <a href="/wiki/Curt_Michel" title="Curt Michel">
         Curt Michel
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Lunar Heat Flow Management: Columbia University, M. Langseth;
        <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">
         Yale University
        </a>
        , S. Clark.
       </li>
       <li>
        Low-Energy Solar Wind: Rice University, B. J. O'Brien.
       </li>
       <li>
        Active Lunar Seismic Experiment:
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-active_1-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-active-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ninfinger_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-ninfinger-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">
         Stanford University
        </a>
        , R. L. Kovach;
        <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">
         United States Geological Survey
        </a>
        , J. S. Watkins.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The ALSEP was built and tested by
       <a href="/wiki/Bendix_Corporation" title="Bendix Corporation">
        Bendix Aerospace
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Ann_Arbor,_Michigan" title="Ann Arbor, Michigan">
        Ann Arbor, Michigan
       </a>
       . The instruments were designed to run autonomously after the astronauts left and to make long term studies of the lunar environment. They were arrayed around a Central Station which supplied power generated by a
       <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">
        radioisotope thermoelectric generator
       </a>
       (RTG) to run the instruments and communications so data collected by the experiments could be relayed to Earth. Thermal control was achieved by passive elements (insulation, reflectors, thermal coatings) as well as power dissipation resistors and heaters. Data collected from the instruments were converted into a telemetry format and transmitted to Earth.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Deployment">
        Deployment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiments_Package&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Deployment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The ALSEP was stored in the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
        Lunar Module
       </a>
       's Scientific Equipment (SEQ) Bay in two separate subpackages. The base of the first subpackage formed the Central Station while the base of the second subpackage was part of the RTG. A subpallet was also attached to the second subpackage which usually carried one or two of the experiments and the antenna gimbal assembly. On Apollo 12, 13, and 14, the second subpackage also stored the Lunar Hand Tool Carrier (HTC). The exact deployment of experiments differed by mission. The following pictures show a typical procedure from Apollo 12.

        <td>
         The picture shows the Central Station from Apollo 16's ALSEP.
         <br/>
         The Central Station was essentially the command center for the entire ALSEP station. It received commands from Earth, transmitted data, and distributed power to each experiment. Communications with Earth were achieved through a 58 cm long, 3.8 cm diameter modified axial-helical antenna mounted on top of the Central Station and pointed towards Earth by the astronauts. Transmitters, receivers, data processors and multiplexers were housed within the Central Station. The Central Station was a 25 kg box with a stowed volume of 34,800 cubic cm. In addition, on Apollos 12 to 15, a Dust Detector was mounted on the Central Station which measured the accumulation of Lunar dust.

         The LEAM was designed to detect secondary particles that had been ejected by meteorite impacts on the lunar surface and to detect primary micrometeorites themselves.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
          <a href="#cite_note-5">
           [5]
          </a>
         </sup>
         See
         <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil#Moon_dust_fountains_and_electrostatic_levitation" title="Lunar soil">
          Lunar soil
         </a>
         for some experiment results.
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Lunar Seismic Profiling Experiment (LSPE)
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ALSEP_Lunar_Seismic_Profiling_Experiment_Charge.gif">
          <img alt="ALSEP Lunar Seismic Profiling Experiment Charge.gif" data-file-height="509" data-file-width="586" height="109" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/ALSEP_Lunar_Seismic_Profiling_Experiment_Charge.gif/125px-ALSEP_Lunar_Seismic_Profiling_Experiment_Charge.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/ALSEP_Lunar_Seismic_Profiling_Experiment_Charge.gif/188px-ALSEP_Lunar_Seismic_Profiling_Experiment_Charge.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/ALSEP_Lunar_Seismic_Profiling_Experiment_Charge.gif/250px-ALSEP_Lunar_Seismic_Profiling_Experiment_Charge.gif 2x" width="125"/>
         </a>
         <br/>
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NASA_Apollo17_LSPE_explosive.jpg">
          <img alt="NASA Apollo17 LSPE explosive.jpg" data-file-height="239" data-file-width="338" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/NASA_Apollo17_LSPE_explosive.jpg/125px-NASA_Apollo17_LSPE_explosive.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/NASA_Apollo17_LSPE_explosive.jpg/188px-NASA_Apollo17_LSPE_explosive.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/NASA_Apollo17_LSPE_explosive.jpg/250px-NASA_Apollo17_LSPE_explosive.jpg 2x" width="125"/>
         </a>
        </td>
        <td>
         The LSPE was similar to the ASE except the expected depth was to be several kilometers. It consisted of three major components. A set of four geophones was laid out near the ALSEP by an astronaut.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brzostowski_pp_414-416_4-1">
          <a href="#cite_note-Brzostowski_pp_414-416-4">
           [4]
          </a>
         </sup>
         The LSPE antenna was used to send signals to the charges. There were eight charges, each consisting of various sizes ranging from 1/8 to 6 lbs. The charges were deployed during the
         <a href="/wiki/Lunar_rover" title="Lunar rover">
          rover
         </a>
         traverses.
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Lunar Surface Gravimeter (LSG)
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Gravimeter.gif">
          <img alt="ALSEP Lunar Surface Gravimeter.gif" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="388" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Gravimeter.gif/125px-ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Gravimeter.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Gravimeter.gif/188px-ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Gravimeter.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Gravimeter.gif/250px-ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Gravimeter.gif 2x" width="125"/>
         </a>
        </td>
        <td>
         The LSG was designed to make very accurate measurements of lunar gravity and its change over time. It was hoped the data could be used to prove the existence of
         <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">
          gravitational waves
         </a>
         .
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Lunar Surface Magnetometer (LSM)
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Magnetometer.svg">
          <img alt="ALSEP Lunar Surface Magnetometer.svg" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="610" height="72" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Magnetometer.svg/125px-ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Magnetometer.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Magnetometer.svg/188px-ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Magnetometer.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Magnetometer.svg/250px-ALSEP_Lunar_Surface_Magnetometer.svg.png 2x" width="125"/>
         </a>
        </td>
        <td>
         The LSM was designed to measure the
         <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field_of_the_Moon" title="Magnetic field of the Moon">
          Lunar magnetic field
         </a>
         . The data could be used to determine electrical properties of the subsurface. It was also used to study the interaction of solar plasma and the Lunar surface.
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Passive Seismic Experiment (PSE)
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment.jpg">
          <img alt="ALSEP Passive Seismic Experiment.jpg" data-file-height="460" data-file-width="800" height="72" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment.jpg/125px-ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment.jpg/188px-ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment.jpg/250px-ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment.jpg 2x" width="125"/>
         </a>
        </td>
        <td>
         The PSE was designed to detect "
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moonquake" title="Moonquake">
          moonquakes
         </a>
         ," either naturally or artificially created, to help study the structure of the subsurface.
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Passive Seismic Experiment Package (PSEP)
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment_Package.gif">
          <img alt="ALSEP Passive Seismic Experiment Package.gif" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="740" height="82" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment_Package.gif/125px-ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment_Package.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment_Package.gif/188px-ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment_Package.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment_Package.gif/250px-ALSEP_Passive_Seismic_Experiment_Package.gif 2x" width="125"/>
         </a>
        </td>
        <td>
         Similar to the PSE, except it was self-supporting. This meant it carried its own power source (
         <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">
          solar arrays
         </a>
         ), electronics, and communications equipment. In addition, the PSEP also carried a Dust Detector.
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Solar Wind Spectrometer Experiment (SWS)
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ALSEP_Solar_Wind_Spectrometer-en.svg">
          <img alt="ALSEP Solar Wind Spectrometer-en.svg" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="512" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/ALSEP_Solar_Wind_Spectrometer-en.svg/125px-ALSEP_Solar_Wind_Spectrometer-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/ALSEP_Solar_Wind_Spectrometer-en.svg/188px-ALSEP_Solar_Wind_Spectrometer-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/ALSEP_Solar_Wind_Spectrometer-en.svg/250px-ALSEP_Solar_Wind_Spectrometer-en.svg.png 2x" width="125"/>
         </a>
        </td>
        <td>
         The SWS was designed to study solar wind properties and its effects on the Lunar environment.
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Suprathermal Ion Detector Experiment (SIDE)
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
        </td>
        <td>
         The SIDE was designed to measure various properties of positive ions in the Lunar environment, provide data on the plasma interaction between solar wind and the Moon, and to determine the electrical potential of the Lunar surface.
        </td>
       </tr>

      <h3>
       <span id="Apollo_11_.28EASEP.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_11_(EASEP)">
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
         Apollo 11
        </a>
        (EASEP)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiments_Package&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Apollo 11 (EASEP)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Buzz_Aldrin_carries_the_EASEP.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2362" data-file-width="2349" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Buzz_Aldrin_carries_the_EASEP.jpg/200px-Buzz_Aldrin_carries_the_EASEP.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Buzz_Aldrin_carries_the_EASEP.jpg/300px-Buzz_Aldrin_carries_the_EASEP.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Buzz_Aldrin_carries_the_EASEP.jpg/400px-Buzz_Aldrin_carries_the_EASEP.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Buzz_Aldrin_carries_the_EASEP.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         On Apollo 11,
         <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
          Buzz Aldrin
         </a>
         simply carried the EASEP to the deployment site by using handles. This is different from the carrybar used on later missions.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Because of the risk of an early abort on the Moon, geologists persuaded NASA to permit only experiments that could be set up or completed in 10 minutes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lindoh_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lindoh-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a result, Apollo 11 did not leave a full ALSEP package, but left a simpler version called the Early Apollo Surface Experiments Package (EASEP). Since there was only one 2 hour 40 minute EVA planned, the crew would not have enough time to deploy a full ALSEP, which usually took one to two hours to deploy. Both packages were stored in the LM's SEQ bay.
      </p>
      <p>
       Engineers designed the EASEP to deploy with one squeeze handle, and the Laser Ranging Retro Reflector (LRRR) also deployed within ten minutes. Despite the simpler design, the seismometer was sensitive enough to detect
       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>
       's movements during sleep.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lindoh_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-lindoh-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="After_Apollo">
        After Apollo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiments_Package&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: After Apollo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The ALSEP system and instruments were controlled by commands from Earth. The stations ran from deployment until they were turned off on 30 September 1977 due primarily to budgetary considerations. Additionally, by 1977 the power packs could not run both the transmitter and any other instrument, and the ALSEP control room was needed for the attempt to reactivate
       <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">
        Skylab
       </a>
       . ALSEP systems are visible in several images taken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       during its orbits over Apollo landing sites.
      </p>
