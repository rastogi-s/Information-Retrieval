      <p>
       <b>
        Methone
       </b>
       is a very small
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       orbiting between the orbits of
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enceladus_(moon)" title="Enceladus (moon)">
        Enceladus
       </a>
       . It was discovered in 2004, and in 2012 was imaged more closely by the Cassini orbiter probe
      </p>

      <p>
       Methone was first seen by the Cassini Imaging Team
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECassini_Imaging_Team_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECassini_Imaging_Team-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8389_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8389-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPorco_Baker_et_al._2005_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorco_Baker_et_al._2005-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and given the temporary designation
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/2004 S 1
        </span>
       </b>
       . Methone is also named
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Saturn XXXII
        </span>
       </b>
       (32). The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft has made two visits to Methone and its closest approach was made on May 20, 2012 with a minimum distance of 1,900 km (1,181 mi) from it.
      </p>
      <p>
       The name Methone was approved by the IAU Working Group on Planetary System Nomenclature on January 21, 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8471_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8471-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was ratified at the IAU General Assembly in 2006.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methone_(mythology)" title="Methone (mythology)">
        Methone
       </a>
       (Greek
       <i>
        Μεθώνη
       </i>
       ) was one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Alkyonides" title="Alkyonides">
        Alkyonides
       </a>
       , the seven beautiful daughters of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_(mythology)" title="Giant (mythology)">
        Giant
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyoneus" title="Alkyoneus">
        Alkyoneus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Methone_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Methone's orbit is visibly affected by a perturbing 14:15 mean-longitude
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        resonance
       </a>
       with the much larger
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       . This causes its
       <a href="/wiki/Osculating_orbit" title="Osculating orbit">
        osculating orbital
       </a>
       elements to vary with an amplitude of about 20 km (12 mi) in semi-major axis, and 5° in
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude_of_the_periapsis" title="Longitude of the periapsis">
        longitude of its periapsis
       </a>
       on a timescale of about 450 days. Its eccentricity also varies on different timescales between 0.0011 and 0.0037, and its inclination between about 0.003° and 0.020°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Methone_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In May 2012, the
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       spacecraft obtained its first close-up photographs of Methone, revealing an egg-shaped moonlet with a remarkably smooth surface, with no visible craters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELakdawalla,_E.2012_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELakdawalla,_E.2012-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The moons
       <a href="/wiki/Pallene_(moon)" title="Pallene (moon)">
        Pallene
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Aegaeon_(moon)" title="Aegaeon (moon)">
        Aegaeon
       </a>
       are thought to be similarly smooth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBattersby,_2013_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBattersby,_2013-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Methone has two different sharply defined
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       regions, one distinctly (~13%) darker centered on Methone's leading point.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas2013_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas2013-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It brighter area has an albedo of ~0.70.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas2013_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas2013-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       UV and IR spectra gave no indication of a color difference between the two regions, suggesting that a physical rather than compositional difference may be responsible.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas2013_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas2013-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Increased exposure to electrons from
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Saturn" title="Magnetosphere of Saturn">
        Saturn's magnetosphere
       </a>
       has been proposed to be responsible for thermal anomalies on the leading hemispheres of Mimas and
       <a href="/wiki/Tethys_(moon)" title="Tethys (moon)">
        Tethys
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchenk_Hamilton_et_al.2011745–46_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchenk_Hamilton_et_al.2011745–46-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and a similar irradiation anisotropy might be behind Methone's albedo pattern.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas2013_4-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas2013-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Methone's mean radius is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7003145000000000000♠
        </span>
        1.45
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.03 km
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas2013_4-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas2013-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Assuming that Methone is in
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">
        hydrostatic equilibrium
       </a>
       , i.e. that its elongated shape simply reflects the balance between the
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal force
       </a>
       exerted by Saturn and Methone's gravity, its density can be estimated:
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7002310000000000000♠
        </span>
        0.31
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;">
         <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">
          +0.05
          <br/>
          −0.03
         </span>
        </span>
        g/cm
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
       </span>
       , among the lowest density values obtained or inferred for a Solar System body. This indicates that Methone is composed of icy fluff, material that might be mobile enough to explain the lack of craters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas2013_4-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas2013-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBattersby,_2013_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBattersby,_2013-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Relationship_with_Saturn.27s_rings">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Saturn's_rings">
        Relationship with Saturn's rings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Methone_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relationship with Saturn's rings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#Methone_Ring_Arc" title="Rings of Saturn">
        Rings of Saturn § Methone Ring Arc
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Material blasted off Methone by
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">
        micrometeoroid
       </a>
       impacts is believed to the source of the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#Methone_Ring_Arc" title="Rings of Saturn">
        Methone Ring Arc
       </a>
       , a faint partial ring around Saturn co-orbital with Methone that was discovered in September 2006.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPorco_et_al.,_2008_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorco_et_al.,_2008-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHedman_et_al.,_2009_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHedman_et_al.,_2009-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
