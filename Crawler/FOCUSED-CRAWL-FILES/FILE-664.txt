     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       An
       <b>
        ecclesiastical full moon
       </b>
       is formally the 14th day of the ecclesiastical lunar month (an ecclesiastical moon) in an
       <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">
        ecclesiastical lunar calendar
       </a>
       . The ecclesiastical lunar calendar spans the year with lunar months of 30 and 29 days which are intended to approximate the observed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phases_of_the_moon" title="Phases of the moon">
        phases of the moon
       </a>
       . Since a true
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_month" title="Synodic month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       has a length that can vary from about 29.27 to 29.83 days, the moment of astronomical opposition tends to be roughly 14.75 days after the previous conjunction of the sun and moon. The ecclesiastical full moons of the Gregorian lunar calendar tend to agree with the dates of astronomical opposition, referred to a day beginning at midnight at 0 degrees longitude, to within a day or so. However, the astronomical opposition happens at a single moment for the entire Earth: The hour and day at which the opposition is measured as having taken place will vary with longitude. In the ecclesiastical calendar, the 14th day of the lunar month, reckoned in local time, is considered the day of the full moon at each longitude.
      </p>
      <p>
       Schematic lunar calendars can and do get out of step with the moon. A useful way of checking their performance is to compare the variation of the astronomical new moon with a standard time of 6 a.m. on the last day of a 30-day month and 6 p.m. (end of day) on the last day of a 29-day month.
      </p>
      <p>
       Beginning in the medieval period the age of the ecclesiastical moon was announced daily in the office of
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_(liturgy)" title="Prime (liturgy)">
        Prime
       </a>
       at the reading of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_martyrology" title="Roman martyrology">
        martyrology
       </a>
       . This is still done today by those using the extraordinary form of the Roman Rite adhering to the 1962 Roman Breviary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">
        Book of Common Prayer
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">
        Protestant Episcopal Church of the United States of America
       </a>
       , the dates of the paschal full moons for the 19 years of the Gregorian Easter cycle are indicated by the placement of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_numbers" title="Golden numbers">
        Golden Number
       </a>
       to the left of the date in March or April on which the paschal full moon falls in that year of the cycle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The same practice is followed in some editions of the Book of Common Prayer of the Church of England.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Paschal_full_moon">
        Paschal full moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecclesiastical_full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Paschal full moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Notionally, the
       <i>
        paschal full moon
       </i>
       refers to the ecclesiastical full moon of the northern spring used in the determination of the date of
       <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">
        Easter
       </a>
       . The name "paschal" is derived from "
       <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">
        Pascha
       </a>
       ", a transliteration of the Aramaic word meaning Passover. The date of Easter is determined as the first Sunday after the "paschal full moon" falling on or after the Spring Equinox (March 21). This "full moon" does not currently correspond directly to any
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomical
       </a>
       event, but is instead the 14th day of a lunar month, determined from tables. It may differ from the date of the actual full moon by up to two days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The use of tables instead of actual observations of the full moon is useful and necessary since the full moon may occur on different dates depending where one is in the world.
      </p>
      <p>
       The calculations to determine the date of the paschal full moon are somewhat complex, but can be described briefly as follows:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Nineteen civil calendar years are divided into 235 lunar months of 30 and 29 days each (the so-called "ecclesiastical moon".)
       </li>
       <li>
        The period of 19 years (the
        <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">
         metonic cycle
        </a>
        ) is used because it produces a set of civil calendar dates for the ecclesiastical moons that repeats every nineteen years while still providing a reasonable approximation to the astronomical facts.
       </li>
       <li>
        The first day of each of these lunar months is the
        <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_new_moon" title="Ecclesiastical new moon">
         ecclesiastical new moon
        </a>
        . Exactly one ecclesiastical new moon in each year falls on a date between March 8 and April 5, both inclusive. This begins the paschal lunar month for that year, and thirteen days later (that is, between March 21 and April 18, both inclusive) is the paschal full moon, Saturday Paschal Full Moon can come as late as April 19 Easter date, then April 20 Easter date only ranges from Sunday to Friday for the paschal full moon.
       </li>
       <li>
        Easter is the Sunday following the paschal full moon.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In other words, Easter falls from one to seven days after the paschal full moon, so that if the paschal full moon is on Sunday, Easter is the following Sunday. Thus the earliest possible date of Easter is March 22, while the latest possible date is April 25.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Earliest_Easter">
        Earliest Easter
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecclesiastical_full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Earliest Easter">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1818, as a paschal full moon fell on Saturday March 21 (the Spring Equinox), Easter was the following day—Sunday March 22—the earliest date possible. It will not fall on this date again until 2285, a span of 467 years.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Latest_Easter">
        Latest Easter
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecclesiastical_full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Latest Easter">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1943 a full moon fell on Saturday March 20. As this was before the Equinox, the next full moon, which fell on Sunday April 18, determined the date of Easter—the following Sunday, April 25. It will not fall on this date again until 2038, a span of 95 years, however the Saturday Full Moon can come late as April 19, since the Paschal Full Moon is ranged from March 21 to April 18.
      </p>
      <p>
       For a detailed discussion of the paschal computations, see
       <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">
        Computus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Easter_tables">
        Easter tables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecclesiastical_full_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Easter tables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       By the middle of the third century AD computists of some churches, among which were the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">
        Church of Rome
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">
        one of Alexandria
       </a>
       , had begun to calculate their own periodic sequences of dates of paschal full moon, to be able to determine their own dates of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Easter_Sunday" title="Easter Sunday">
        Easter Sunday
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The motivation for these experiments was a dissatisfaction with the Jewish calendars that Christians had hitherto relied on to fix the date of Easter. These Jewish calendars, according to their Christian critics, sometimes placed Nisan 14, the paschal full moon and the day of preparation for the Jewish Passover, before the spring equinox (see
       <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">
        Easter
       </a>
       ). The Christians who began the experiments with independent computations held that the paschal full moon should never precede the equinox.
      </p>
      <p>
       The computational principles developed at Alexandria eventually became normative, but their reception was a centuries-long process during which Alexandrian Easter tables competed with other tables incorporating different arithmetical parameters. So for a period of several centuries the sequences of dates of the paschal full moon applied by different churches could show great differences (see
       <a href="/wiki/Easter_controversy" title="Easter controversy">
        Easter controversy
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
