      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">
        Discovery and naming
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Themisto_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Themisto was first discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_T._Kowal" title="Charles T. Kowal">
        Charles T. Kowal
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Roemer" title="Elizabeth Roemer">
        Elizabeth Roemer
       </a>
       on September 30, 1975, reported on October 3, 1975,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and designated
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/1975 J 1
        </span>
       </b>
       . However, not enough observations were made to establish an orbit and it was subsequently lost. (See also
       <a href="/wiki/Lost_minor_planet" title="Lost minor planet">
        lost minor planet
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       Themisto appeared as a footnote in astronomy textbooks into the 1980s. Then, in 2000, a seemingly new satellite was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Scott_S._Sheppard" title="Scott S. Sheppard">
        Scott S. Sheppard
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/David_C._Jewitt" title="David C. Jewitt">
        David C. Jewitt
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yanga_R._Fern%C3%A1ndez" title="Yanga R. Fernández">
        Yanga R. Fernández
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eugene_A._Magnier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eugene A. Magnier (page does not exist)">
        Eugene A. Magnier
       </a>
       , and was designated
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/2000 J 1
        </span>
       </b>
       . It was soon confirmed that this was the same as the one observed in 1975.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This observation was immediately correlated with an observation on August 6, 2000, by the team of
       <a href="/wiki/Brett_J._Gladman" title="Brett J. Gladman">
        Brett J. Gladman
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/John_J._Kavelaars" title="John J. Kavelaars">
        John J. Kavelaars
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean-Marc_Petit" title="Jean-Marc Petit">
        Jean-Marc Petit
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hans_Scholl_(astronomer)" title="Hans Scholl (astronomer)">
        Hans Scholl
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Matthew_J._Holman" title="Matthew J. Holman">
        Matthew J. Holman
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Brian_G._Marsden" title="Brian G. Marsden">
        Brian G. Marsden
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_D._Nicholson" title="Philip D. Nicholson">
        Philip D. Nicholson
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_A._Burns" title="Joseph A. Burns">
        Joseph A. Burns
       </a>
       , which was reported to the Minor Planet Center but not published as an IAU Circular (IAUC).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In October 2002 it was officially named after
       <a href="/wiki/Themisto" title="Themisto">
        Themisto
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       daughter of the river god
       <a href="/wiki/Inachus" title="Inachus">
        Inachus
       </a>
       and lover of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       (Jupiter) in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Themisto_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TheIrregulars_JUPITER_GROUPS.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="400" height="363" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/TheIrregulars_JUPITER_GROUPS.svg/290px-TheIrregulars_JUPITER_GROUPS.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/TheIrregulars_JUPITER_GROUPS.svg/435px-TheIrregulars_JUPITER_GROUPS.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/TheIrregulars_JUPITER_GROUPS.svg/580px-TheIrregulars_JUPITER_GROUPS.svg.png 2x" width="290"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TheIrregulars_JUPITER_GROUPS.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Diagram illustrating Themisto's orbit (top left) among those of the other irregular satellites of Jupiter. The satellites above the horizontal axis are
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_motion" title="Direct motion">
          prograde
         </a>
         , the satellites beneath it are
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
          retrograde
         </a>
         . The yellow segments extend from the
         <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
          pericentre
         </a>
         to the
         <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
          apocentre
         </a>
         , showing the
         <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
          orbital eccentricity
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Themisto's orbit is unusual: unlike most of
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">
        Jupiter's moons
       </a>
       , which orbit in distinct groups, Themisto orbits alone. The moon is located midway between the
       <a href="/wiki/Galilean_moons" title="Galilean moons">
        Galilean moons
       </a>
       and the first group of prograde irregular moons, called the
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_group" title="Himalia group">
        Himalia group
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Themisto is about 8 kilometers (5 miles) in diameter (assuming an
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       of 0.04).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardJewitt2003_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardJewitt2003-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That figure can be used to find a surface area of between 200 and 380 square kilometers.
      </p>
