      <p>
       <b>
        Shoemaker
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_craters" title="Lunar craters">
        lunar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       that is located near the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_south_pole" title="Lunar south pole">
        southern pole
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , within half a crater diameter of
       <a href="/wiki/Shackleton_(crater)" title="Shackleton (crater)">
        Shackleton
       </a>
       . It lies to the south of the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Malapert_(crater)" title="Malapert (crater)">
        Malapert
       </a>
       , to the east of
       <a href="/wiki/Haworth_(crater)" title="Haworth (crater)">
        Haworth
       </a>
       , and just to the west of the similar-sized
       <a href="/wiki/Faustini_(crater)" title="Faustini (crater)">
        Faustini
       </a>
       . The rim of Shoemaker is circular and worn, with some small craters along the inner wall. Due to the lack of illumination (it is a
       <a href="/wiki/Crater_of_eternal_darkness" title="Crater of eternal darkness">
        crater of eternal darkness
       </a>
       ), the
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       of the interior floor surface remains unknown.
      </p>
      <p>
       Prior to being given its current name by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       , this formation had been informally named Mawson (after the
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       explorer
       <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Mawson" title="Douglas Mawson">
        Douglas Mawson
       </a>
       ). It was officially named Shoemaker in honor of
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Merle_Shoemaker" title="Eugene Merle Shoemaker">
        Eugene Shoemaker
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">
        geologist
       </a>
       whose remains were on board the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       spacecraft that impacted this crater floor.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_trap_deposits">
        Cold trap deposits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shoemaker_(lunar_crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cold trap deposits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This crater became of interest to scientists when the Lunar Prospector detected unusually high concentrations of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       on the floor of this and other nearby craters using a
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">
        neutron
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
        spectrometer
       </a>
       . The floor of this crater is kept in permanent shadow from the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , and thus maintains a temperature below 100
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        K
       </a>
       . Thus the floor forms a cold trap, and any molecules of water that wander into the crater from
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        cometary
       </a>
       impacts can deposit on the floor and remain there almost permanently. The instruments on board Lunar Prospector gave a concentration of about 146
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">
        ppm
       </a>
       of hydrogen, compared to 50 ppm for the average lunar surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       The topography of this crater was subsequently measured using the
       <a href="/wiki/Goldstone_Deep_Space_Communications_Complex" title="Goldstone Deep Space Communications Complex">
        Goldstone
       </a>
       70-meter
       <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">
        antenna
       </a>
       to irradiate it with 3.5 cm
       <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
        radar
       </a>
       . Two 34-meter antennae, located 20 kilometers apart, received the echoes, and the results were used to generate a
       <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">
        topographic map
       </a>
       with 150 m resolution.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Margot1999_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Margot1999-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On July 31, 1999, Lunar Prospector was deliberately crashed into the floor of this crater to determine if a plume of water vapor would be detected. The apparent detection of water and the shallow rim of this crater made it a suitable target for this experiment. However the outcome was unsuccessful. Subsequent tests have failed to confirm the presence of water in the crater floor, so the hydrogen found in the crater bottom is likely to prove more difficult to extract than had been originally hoped.
      </p>
