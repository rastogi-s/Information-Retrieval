       <b>
        Hyperion
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'h' in 'hi'">
            h
           </span>
           <span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">
            aɪ
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪər/: 'ear' in 'near'">
            ɪər
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         hy-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          PEER
         </span>
         -ee-ən
        </i>
       </a>
       ; Greek:
       <i>
        Ὑπερίων
       </i>
       ), also known as
       <b>
        Saturn VII
       </b>
       (7), is a
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Saturn" title="Moons of Saturn">
        moon of Saturn
       </a>
       discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Cranch_Bond" title="William Cranch Bond">
        William Cranch Bond
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/George_Phillips_Bond" title="George Phillips Bond">
        George Phillips Bond
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/William_Lassell" title="William Lassell">
        William Lassell
       </a>
       in 1848. It is distinguished by its irregular shape, its chaotic rotation, and its unexplained sponge-like appearance. It was the first non-
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">
        round
       </a>
       moon to be discovered.

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperion_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The moon is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(mythology)" title="Hyperion (mythology)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">
        Titan
       </a>
       god of watchfulness and observation – the elder brother of
       <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">
        Cronus
       </a>
       , the Greek equivalent of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn_(mythology)" title="Saturn (mythology)">
        Saturn
       </a>
       – in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       . It is also designated
       <b>
        Saturn VII
       </b>
       . The adjectival form of the name is
       <i>
        Hyperionian
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Hyperion's discovery came shortly after
       <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">
        John Herschel
       </a>
       had suggested names for the seven previously-known satellites of Saturn in his 1847 publication
       <i>
        Results of Astronomical Observations made at the Cape of Good Hope
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell_1848_Observations_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell_1848_Observations-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/William_Lassell" title="William Lassell">
        William Lassell
       </a>
       , who saw Hyperion two days after
       <a href="/wiki/William_Cranch_Bond" title="William Cranch Bond">
        William Bond
       </a>
       , had already endorsed Herschel's naming scheme and suggested the name Hyperion in accordance with it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also beat Bond to publication.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperion_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Shape">
        Shape
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperion_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shape">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Hyperion is one of the largest bodies known to be highly irregularly shaped (non-ellipsoidal, i.e. not in
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">
        hydrostatic equilibrium
       </a>
       ) in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nb_larger_objects_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nb_larger_objects-13">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The only larger moon known to be irregular in shape is Neptune's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Proteus_(moon)" title="Proteus (moon)">
        Proteus
       </a>
       . Hyperion has about 15% of the mass of
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       , the least massive known ellipsoidal body. The largest
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        crater
       </a>
       on Hyperion is approximately 121.57 km (75.54 mi) in diameter and 10.2 km (6.3 mi) deep. A possible explanation for the irregular shape is that Hyperion is a fragment of a larger body that was broken up by a large impact in the distant past.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matthews1992_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matthews1992-14">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A proto-Hyperion could have been 350–1,000 km (220–620 mi) in diameter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farinella1997_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Farinella1997-15">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Over about 1,000 years, ejecta from a presumed Hyperion breakup would have impacted
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">
        Titan
       </a>
       at low speeds, building up
       <a href="/wiki/Volatiles" title="Volatiles">
        volatiles
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Titan" title="Atmosphere of Titan">
        atmosphere of Titan
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farinella1997_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Farinella1997-15">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:197px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hyperion_in_natural_colours.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="242" data-file-width="262" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Hyperion_in_natural_colours.jpg/195px-Hyperion_in_natural_colours.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e0/Hyperion_in_natural_colours.jpg 1.5x" width="195"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hyperion_in_natural_colours.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         True-color image of Hyperion, taken by the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini_spacecraft" title="Cassini spacecraft">
          <i>
           Cassini
          </i>
          spacecraft
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
        Composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperion_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Like most of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturn%27s_natural_satellites" title="Saturn's natural satellites">
        Saturn's moons
       </a>
       , Hyperion's low
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       indicates that it is composed largely of water ice with only a small amount of rock. It is thought that Hyperion may be similar to a loosely accreted pile of rubble in its physical composition. However, unlike most of Saturn's moons, Hyperion has a low
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       (0.2–0.3), indicating that it is covered by at least a thin layer of dark material. This may be material from
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)" title="Phoebe (moon)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       (which is much darker) that got past
       <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)" title="Iapetus (moon)">
        Iapetus
       </a>
       . Hyperion is
       <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">
        redder
       </a>
       than Phoebe and closely matches the color of the dark material on Iapetus.
      </p>
      <p>
       Hyperion has a
       <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">
        porosity
       </a>
       of about 0.46.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas2007_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Thomas2007-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_features">
        Surface features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperion_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Surface features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_geological_features_on_Hyperion" title="List of geological features on Hyperion">
        List of geological features on Hyperion
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       passed through the Saturn system, but photographed Hyperion only from a distance. It discerned individual craters and an enormous ridge, but was not able to make out the texture of Hyperion's surface. Early images from the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       orbiter suggested an unusual appearance, but it was not until
       <i>
        Cassini'
       </i>
       s first targeted flyby of Hyperion on 25 September 2005 that Hyperion's oddness was revealed in full.
      </p>
      <p>
       Hyperion's surface is covered with deep, sharp-edged
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        craters
       </a>
       that give it the appearance of a giant sponge. Dark material fills the bottom of each crater. The reddish substance contains long chains of
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       and appears very similar to material found on other Saturnian satellites, most notably
       <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)" title="Iapetus (moon)">
        Iapetus
       </a>
       . Scientists attribute Hyperion's unusual, sponge-like appearance to the fact that it has an unusually low density for such a large object. Its low density makes Hyperion quite porous, with a weak surface gravity. These characteristics mean impactors tend to compress the surface, rather than excavating it, and most material that is blown off the surface never returns.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-next2015_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-next2015-16">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The latest analyses of data obtained by
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       during its flybys of Hyperion in 2005 and 2006 show that about 40 percent of it is empty space. It was suggested in July 2007 that this
       <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">
        porosity
       </a>
       allows craters to remain nearly unchanged over the eons. The new analyses also confirmed that Hyperion is composed mostly of water ice with very little rock.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation">
        Rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperion_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hyperion_false_color.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2953" data-file-width="2988" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Hyperion_false_color.jpg/190px-Hyperion_false_color.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Hyperion_false_color.jpg/285px-Hyperion_false_color.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Hyperion_false_color.jpg/380px-Hyperion_false_color.jpg 2x" width="190"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hyperion_false_color.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Hyperion with image processing to bring out details. Taken by the
         <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
          <i>
           Cassini
          </i>
         </a>
         space probe.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       images and subsequent ground-based
       <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">
        photometry
       </a>
       indicated that Hyperion's rotation is
       <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">
        chaotic
       </a>
       , that is, its axis of rotation wobbles so much that its orientation in space is unpredictable. Its
       <a href="/wiki/Lyapunov_time" title="Lyapunov time">
        Lyapunov time
       </a>
       is around 30 days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hyperion, together with
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Pluto" title="Moons of Pluto">
        Pluto's moons
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Nix_(moon)" title="Nix (moon)">
        Nix
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(moon)" title="Hydra (moon)">
        Hydra
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is among only a few moons in the Solar System known to rotate chaotically, although it is expected to be common in
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_asteroid" title="Binary asteroid">
        binary asteroids
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also the only
       <a href="/wiki/Regular_moon" title="Regular moon">
        regular planetary natural satellite
       </a>
       in the Solar System known not to be
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Hyperion is unique among the large moons in that it is very irregularly shaped, has a fairly eccentric orbit, and is near a much larger moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">
        Titan
       </a>
       . These factors combine to restrict the set of conditions under which a stable rotation is possible. The 3:4
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       between Titan and Hyperion may also make a chaotic rotation more likely. The fact that its rotation is not locked probably accounts for the relative uniformity of Hyperion's surface, in contrast to many of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturn%27s_natural_satellites" title="Saturn's natural satellites">
        Saturn's other moons
       </a>
       , which have contrasting trailing and leading hemispheres.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperion_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Hyperion has been imaged several times from moderate distances by the
       <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
        <i>
         Cassini
        </i>
        orbiter
       </a>
       . The first close targeted flyby occurred at a distance of 500 km (310 mi) on 26 September 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-next2015_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-next2015-16">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       made another two close approaches to Hyperion on 25 August 2011 and on 16 September 2011, when it passed at 58,000 km (36,000 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Cassini'
       </i>
       s last flyby was on 31 May 2015 at a distance of about 34,000 km (21,000 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-next2015_16-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-next2015-16">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
