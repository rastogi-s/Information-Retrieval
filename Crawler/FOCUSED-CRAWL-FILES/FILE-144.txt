       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Domenico_Cassini" title="Giovanni Domenico Cassini">
        Giovanni Domenico Cassini
       </a>
       in 1684.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassini1686–1692_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cassini1686–1692-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is named after the
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">
        Titaness
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Dione_(Titaness)" title="Dione (Titaness)">
        Dione
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       . It is also designated
       <b>
        Saturn IV
       </b>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dione_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Domenico_Cassini" title="Giovanni Domenico Cassini">
        Giovanni Domenico Cassini
       </a>
       named the four moons he discovered (
       <a href="/wiki/Tethys_(moon)" title="Tethys (moon)">
        Tethys
       </a>
       , Dione,
       <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(moon)" title="Rhea (moon)">
        Rhea
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)" title="Iapetus (moon)">
        Iapetus
       </a>
       )
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sidera_Lodoicea" title="Sidera Lodoicea">
         Sidera Lodoicea
        </a>
       </i>
       ("the stars of Louis") to honor king
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">
        Louis XIV
       </a>
       . Cassini found Dione in 1684 using a large
       <a href="/wiki/Aerial_telescope" title="Aerial telescope">
        aerial telescope
       </a>
       he set up on the grounds of the
       <a href="/wiki/Paris_Observatory" title="Paris Observatory">
        Paris Observatory
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The satellites of Saturn were not named until 1847, when
       <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">
        William Herschel
       </a>
       's son
       <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">
        John Herschel
       </a>
       published
       <i>
        Results of Astronomical Observations made at the Cape of Good Hope,
       </i>
       suggesting that the names of the
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">
        Titans
       </a>
       (sisters and brothers of
       <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">
        Cronus
       </a>
       ) be used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dione_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Dione orbits Saturn with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semimajor_axis" title="Semimajor axis">
        semimajor axis
       </a>
       about 2% less than that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . However, reflecting
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       's greater mass (95 times that of Earth's), Dione's orbital period is one tenth that of the Moon. Dione is currently in a 1:2 mean-motion
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       with moon
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enceladus_(moon)" title="Enceladus (moon)">
        Enceladus
       </a>
       , completing one orbit of Saturn for every two orbits completed by Enceladus. This resonance maintains Enceladus's orbital eccentricity (0.0047), providing a source of heat for Enceladus's extensive geological activity, which shows up most dramatically in its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryovolcanic" title="Cryovolcanic">
        cryovolcanic
       </a>
       geyser-like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plume_(hydrodynamics)" title="Plume (hydrodynamics)">
        jets
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porco_Helfenstein_et_al._2006_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Porco_Helfenstein_et_al._2006-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The resonance also maintains a smaller eccentricity in Dione's orbit (0.0022),
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_heating" title="Tidal heating">
        tidally heating
       </a>
       it as well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassini_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cassini-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dione has two co-orbital, or
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_(astronomy)" title="Trojan (astronomy)">
        trojan
       </a>
       , moons,
       <a href="/wiki/Helene_(moon)" title="Helene (moon)">
        Helene
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Polydeuces_(moon)" title="Polydeuces (moon)">
        Polydeuces
       </a>
       . They are located within Dione's
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrangian points
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         5
        </sub>
       </a>
       , 60 degrees ahead of and behind Dione respectively.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics_and_interior">
        Physical characteristics and interior
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dione_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics and interior">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_geological_features_on_Dione" title="List of geological features on Dione">
        List of geological features on Dione
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dione,_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2026" data-file-width="3000" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Dione%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg/250px-Dione%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Dione%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg/375px-Dione%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Dione%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg/500px-Dione%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dione,_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Size comparison of
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         , the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         , and Dione.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       At
       <span style="white-space: nowrap">
        1
        <span style="margin-left: 0.25em">
         122
        </span>
       </span>
       km (697 mi) in diameter, Dione is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_moons" title="List of moons">
        15th largest moon
       </a>
       in the Solar System, and is more massive than all known moons smaller than itself combined.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       About two thirds of Dione's mass is water ice, and the remaining is a dense core, probably
       <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">
        silicate
       </a>
       rock.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Data gathered by
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini-Huygens" title="Cassini-Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       indicates that Dione has an internal liquid water ocean (a situation similar to that of its orbital resonance partner, Enceladus).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_strategy_2015_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA_strategy_2015-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Downward bending of the surface associated with the 1.5 km high ridge
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_mountains_in_the_Solar_System" title="List of tallest mountains in the Solar System">
        Janiculum Dorsa
       </a>
       can most easily be explained by the presence of such an ocean.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammond2013_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hammond2013-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sci_Am_2017_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sci_Am_2017-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Gravity and shape data points to a 99 ± 23 km thick ice shell crust on top of a 65 ± 30 internal liquid water global ocean.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howell2016_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Howell2016-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beuthe2016_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beuthe2016-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Neither moon has a shape close to
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">
        hydrostatic equilibrium
       </a>
       ; the deviations are maintained by
       <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
        isostasy
       </a>
       . Dione's ice shell is thought to vary in thickness by less than 5%, with the thinnest areas at the poles, where
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_heating" title="Tidal heating">
        tidal heating
       </a>
       of the crust is greatest.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beuthe2016_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beuthe2016-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Though somewhat smaller and denser, Dione is otherwise very similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(moon)" title="Rhea (moon)">
        Rhea
       </a>
       . They both have similar albedo features and varied terrain, and both have dissimilar
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leading_hemisphere" title="Leading hemisphere">
        leading and trailing hemispheres
       </a>
       . Dione's leading hemisphere is heavily cratered and is uniformly bright. Its trailing hemisphere, however, contains an unusual and distinctive surface feature: a network of bright ice cliffs.
      </p>
      <p>
       Scientists recognise Dionean
       <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
        geological
       </a>
       features of the following types:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Chasma" title="Chasma">
         Chasmata
        </a>
        (chasms; long, deep, steep-sided depressions)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wrinkle-ridge" title="Wrinkle-ridge">
         Dorsa
        </a>
        (ridges)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Fossa_(geology)" title="Fossa (geology)">
         Fossae
        </a>
        (long narrow depressions)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
         Craters
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Crater_chain" title="Crater chain">
         Catenae
        </a>
        (crater chains)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span id="The_ice_cliffs_.28formerly_.27wispy_terrain.27.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_ice_cliffs_(formerly_'wispy_terrain')">
        The ice cliffs (formerly 'wispy terrain')
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dione_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The ice cliffs (formerly 'wispy terrain')">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dione_Carthage_Linea.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="508" data-file-width="508" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Dione_Carthage_Linea.jpg/220px-Dione_Carthage_Linea.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Dione_Carthage_Linea.jpg/330px-Dione_Carthage_Linea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Dione_Carthage_Linea.jpg/440px-Dione_Carthage_Linea.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dione_Carthage_Linea.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Fractures bisecting older craters on Dione. Those running from upper right to lower left are the Carthage Fossae, whereas Pactolus Catena runs more horizontally at lower right.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       When the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">
         Voyager
        </a>
       </i>
       space probe photographed Dione in 1980, it showed what appeared to be wispy features covering its trailing hemisphere. The origin of these features was mysterious, because all that was known was that the material has a high
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       and is thin enough that it does not obscure the surface features underneath. One hypothesis was that shortly after its formation Dione was geologically active, and some process such as cryovolcanism resurfaced much of its surface, with the streaks forming from eruptions along cracks in Dione's surface that fell back to the surface as snow or ash. Later, after the internal activity and resurfacing ceased, cratering continued primarily on the leading hemisphere and wiped out the streak patterns there.
      </p>
      <p>
       This hypothesis was proven wrong by the
       <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
        <i>
         Cassini
        </i>
        probe
       </a>
       flyby of December 13, 2004, which produced close-up images. These revealed that the 'wisps' were, in fact, not ice deposits at all, but rather bright ice cliffs created by tectonic fractures (
       <i>
        chasmata
       </i>
       ). Dione has been revealed as a world riven by enormous fractures on its trailing hemisphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       orbiter performed a closer flyby of Dione at 500 km (310 mi) on October 11, 2005, and captured
       <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">
        oblique
       </a>
       images of the cliffs, showing that some of them are several hundred metres high.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Craters">
        Craters
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dione_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Craters">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA18345-SaturnMoons-DioneEnceladus-CassiniHuygens-20150908.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1020" data-file-width="1020" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/PIA18345-SaturnMoons-DioneEnceladus-CassiniHuygens-20150908.jpg/200px-PIA18345-SaturnMoons-DioneEnceladus-CassiniHuygens-20150908.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/PIA18345-SaturnMoons-DioneEnceladus-CassiniHuygens-20150908.jpg/300px-PIA18345-SaturnMoons-DioneEnceladus-CassiniHuygens-20150908.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/PIA18345-SaturnMoons-DioneEnceladus-CassiniHuygens-20150908.jpg/400px-PIA18345-SaturnMoons-DioneEnceladus-CassiniHuygens-20150908.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA18345-SaturnMoons-DioneEnceladus-CassiniHuygens-20150908.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Dione before
         <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">
          Enceladus
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Dione's icy surface includes heavily cratered terrain, moderately cratered plains, lightly cratered plains, and areas of tectonic fractures. The heavily cratered terrain has numerous craters greater than 100 kilometres (62 mi) in diameter. The plains areas tend to have craters less than 30 kilometres (19 mi) in diameter. Some of the plains are more heavily cratered than others. Much of the heavily cratered terrain is located on the trailing hemisphere, with the less cratered plains areas present on the leading hemisphere. This is the opposite of what some scientists expected;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Shoemaker" title="Eugene Shoemaker">
        Shoemaker
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_F._Wolfe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ruth F. Wolfe (page does not exist)">
        Wolfe
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       proposed a cratering model for a
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       satellite with the highest cratering rates on the leading hemisphere and the lowest on the trailing hemisphere. This suggests that during the period of heavy bombardment, Dione was tidally locked to Saturn in the opposite orientation. Because Dione is relatively small, an impact causing a 35 kilometer crater could have spun the satellite. Because there are many craters larger than 35 kilometres (22 mi), Dione could have been repeatedly spun during its early heavy bombardment. The pattern of cratering since then and the bright albedo of the leading side suggests that Dione has remained in its current orientation for several billion years.
      </p>
      <p>
       Like
       <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">
        Callisto
       </a>
       , Dione's craters lack the high-relief features seen on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       ; this is probably due to slumping of the weak icy crust over geologic time.
      </p>

      <div class="thumb tright">

          Cassini
         </i>
         enhanced-color composite of Dione, showing the darker, fractured terrain of the trailing hemisphere. The Padua Chasmata trace an arc on the left, interrupted near the top by a
         <a href="/wiki/Complex_crater" title="Complex crater">
          central peak crater
         </a>

      <p>
       On April 7, 2010, instruments on board the unmanned
       <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
        <i>
         Cassini
        </i>
        probe
       </a>
       , which flew by Dione, detected a thin layer of molecular oxygen ions (
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        O
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         +
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       ) around Dione, so thin that scientists prefer to call it an
       <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">
        exosphere
       </a>
       rather than a tenuous atmosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ghosh_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ghosh-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tokar_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tokar-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The density of molecular oxygen ions determined from the
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       plasma spectrometer data ranges from 0.01 to 0.09 per cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tokar_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tokar-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simon_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Simon-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       probe instruments were unable to directly detect water from the exosphere due to high background levels,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tokar_21-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tokar-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but it seems that highly charged particles from the planet's powerful radiation belts could split the water in the ice into hydrogen and oxygen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ghosh_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ghosh-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dione_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Dione was first imaged by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">
         Voyager
        </a>
       </i>
       space probes. It has also been probed five times from close distances by the
       <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
        <i>
         Cassini
        </i>
        orbiter
       </a>
       . There was a close targeted flyby, at a distance of 500 km (310 mi) on 11 October 2005;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       another flyby was performed on 7 April 2010 also at a distance of 500 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A third flyby was performed on 12 December 2011 at a distance of 99 km (62 mi). The following flyby was on 16 June 2015 at a distance of 516 km (321 mi),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the last
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       flyby was performed on 17 August 2015 at a distance of 474 km (295 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In May 2013, it was announced that NASA's spacecraft
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       had provided scientists with evidence that Dione is more active than previously realized. Using topographic data, NASA teams deduced that crustal depression associated with a prominent mountain ridge on the leading hemisphere is best explained if there was a global subsurface liquid ocean like that of Enceladus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammond2013_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hammond2013-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins2010_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Collins2010-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The ridge Janiculum Dorsa has a height of 1 to 2 km (0.6 to 1.2 miles); Dione's crust seems to pucker 0.5 km (0.3 miles) under it, suggesting that the icy crust was warm when the ridge formed, probably due to the presence of a subsurface liquid ocean, which increases tidal flexing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Activity2013_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Activity2013-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
