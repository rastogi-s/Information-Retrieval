   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunar standstill
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       At a major
       <b>
        lunar standstill
       </b>
       , which takes place every 18.6 years, the Moon's range of
       <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">
        declination
       </a>
       , and consequently its range of
       <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">
        azimuth
       </a>
       at moonrise and moonset, reaches a maximum. As a result, viewed from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_latitude" title="Middle latitude">
        middle latitudes
       </a>
       , the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altitude_(astronomy)" title="Altitude (astronomy)">
        altitude
       </a>
       at upper
       <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">
        culmination
       </a>
       (the daily moment when the object appears to contact the observer's
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(astronomy)" title="Meridian (astronomy)">
        meridian
       </a>
       ) changes in just two weeks – from high in the sky to low over the horizon. Similarly, its azimuth at moonrise changes from northeast to southeast and at moonset from northwest to southwest.
      </p>
      <p>
       This time appears to have had special significance for the
       <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">
        Bronze Age
       </a>
       societies, who built the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megalithic_monuments" title="Megalithic monuments">
        megalithic monuments
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Britain" title="Prehistoric Britain">
        Britain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Ireland" title="Prehistoric Ireland">
        Ireland
       </a>
       . It also has significance for some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neopagan" title="Neopagan">
        neopagan
       </a>
       religions. Evidence also exists that alignments to the moonrise or moonset on the days of lunar standstills can be found in ancient sites of other ancient cultures, such as at
       <a href="/wiki/Chimney_Rock_National_Monument" title="Chimney Rock National Monument">
        Chimney Rock
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">
        Colorado
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Hopewell_sites" title="List of Hopewell sites">
        Hopewell Sites
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">
        Ohio
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_name">
        Origin of name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_standstill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin of name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        lunar standstill
       </i>
       was apparently first used by archeologist
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Thom" title="Alexander Thom">
        Alexander Thom
       </a>
       in his 1971 book
       <i>
        Megalithic Lunar Observatories
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vincent3_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-vincent3-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">
         solstice
        </a>
       </i>
       , which derives from the Latin
       <i>
        solstitium
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        sol
       </i>
       - (sun) + -
       <i>
        stitium
       </i>
       (a stoppage), describes the similar extremes in the Sun's varying declination. Neither the Sun nor the Moon stands still, obviously; what stops, momentarily, is the change in declination. The word
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tropic" title="wiktionary:tropic">
         tropic
        </a>
       </i>
       , as in
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">
        Tropic of Capricorn
       </a>
       , comes from ancient Greek meaning "to turn", referring to how ascending (or descending) motion turns to descending (or ascending) motion at the solstice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Informal_explanation">
        Informal explanation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_standstill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Informal explanation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_coordinate_system" title="Equatorial coordinate system">
        Equatorial coordinate system
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_standstill.GIF">
         <img alt="Lunar standstill.GIF" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="471" data-file-width="363" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Lunar_standstill.GIF/220px-Lunar_standstill.GIF" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Lunar_standstill.GIF/330px-Lunar_standstill.GIF 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/Lunar_standstill.GIF 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_standstill.GIF" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       As
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
        Earth rotates
       </a>
       on its axis, the stars in the
       <a href="/wiki/Night_sky" title="Night sky">
        night sky
       </a>
       appear to follow circular paths around the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_pole" title="Celestial pole">
        celestial poles
       </a>
       . It appears to us as if all the stars are
       <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">
        fixed
       </a>
       on a
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       surrounding us. In the same way that we
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system" title="Spherical coordinate system">
        measure positions
       </a>
       on the Earth using
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       , we measure the
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_place" title="Apparent place">
        apparent places
       </a>
       of stars on this sphere in
       <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">
        right ascension
       </a>
       (equivalent to longitude) and
       <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">
        declination
       </a>
       (equivalent to latitude). Viewed from a place on Earth at latitude 50°, stars that daily pass
       <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">
        directly overhead
       </a>
       have a declination of +50°.
      </p>
      <p>
       Unlike the stars, the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       do not have a fixed declination. As Earth orbits the Sun, with its rotational axis tilted at about 23.5° from a line perpendicular to the plane of the orbit (the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       ), the Sun's declination changes from +23.5° on the
       <a href="/wiki/June_solstice" title="June solstice">
        June solstice
       </a>
       to −23.5° on the
       <a href="/wiki/December_solstice" title="December solstice">
        December solstice
       </a>
       . Therefore in June, in the northern hemisphere, the midday Sun is higher in the sky and the days are longer, than in December. In the southern hemisphere, the situation is reversed. This is the cause of the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">
        seasons
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon also changes in declination, but it does so every lunar
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nodal_Period" title="Nodal Period">
        nodal period
       </a>
       or 27.212 days. So it goes from a positive declination to a negative one in under two weeks. Thus in under a month, the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altitude_(astronomy)" title="Altitude (astronomy)">
        altitude
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">
        culmination
       </a>
       (when it contacts the observer's
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(astronomy)" title="Meridian (astronomy)">
        meridian
       </a>
       ) can shift from higher in the sky to lower over the horizon and back.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon differs from most satellites of other planets in that its orbit is close to the plane of the ecliptic, and not to Earth's equatorial plane. The Moon's maximum and minimum declination vary because the plane of the Moon's orbit around the Earth is inclined by about 5.1° to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       (the plane of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">
        Earth's orbit
       </a>
       around the Sun), and the direction in space of lunar orbit inclination gradually changes over an 18.6-year cycle, alternately adding to or subtracting from the 23.5° tilt of the Earth's axis. In consequence, the maximum declination of the Moon varies from roughly (23.5° − 5° =) 18.5° to (23.5° + 5° =) 28.5°. At the minor lunar standstill, the Moon will change its declination during the nodal period from +18.5° to −18.5° for a total movement of 37°. Then, 9.3 years later, during the major lunar standstill, the Moon will change its declination during the nodal period from +28.5° to −28.5°, which is a total movement of 57°. This is enough to take its
       <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">
        culmination
       </a>
       from high in the sky to low on the horizon in just two weeks (half an orbit).
      </p>
      <p>
       Strictly speaking, the lunar standstill is a moving position in space relative to the direction of the Earth's axis and in relation to the rotation of the lunar orbital plane caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_precession" title="Lunar precession">
        nodal prescession
       </a>
       over a period of 18.6 years. The standstill position does not persist over the two weeks that the Moon takes to move from its maximum (positive) declination to minimum (negative) declination, and it most likely will not exactly coincide with either extreme. However, because the 18.6-year cycle of standstills is so much longer than the Moon's orbital period, the change in the declination range over periods as short as half an orbit is very small. The period of lunar orbit turning in space is slightly shorter than the lunar standstill interval due to precession altering the inclination of Earth rotation over a very long period relative to the lunar orbit's direction of rotation. Simply, the standstill cycle results from the combination of the two inclinations.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apparent_position_of_the_Moon_during_standstill">
        Apparent position of the Moon during standstill
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_standstill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apparent position of the Moon during standstill">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">
        azimuth
       </a>
       (direction) of moonrise and moonset varies according to the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">
        moon's nodal period
       </a>
       of 27.212 days, while the azimuth variation during each nodal period varies with the lunar standstill period (18.613 years). For a northern
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       of 55° on Earth, the following table shows moonrise and moonset azimuths for the Moon's narrowest and widest arc paths across the sky. The azimuths are given in degrees from
       <a href="/wiki/True_north" title="True north">
        true north
       </a>
       and apply when the horizon is unobstructed. Figures for a time midway between major and minor standstill are also given. The arc path of the full Moon generally reaches its widest in midwinter and narrowest in midsummer. The widest arc path of the new Moon (when it near the Sun in the sky) is in the summer, as for the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burl_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-burl-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Detailed_explanation">
        Detailed explanation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_standstill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Detailed explanation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_orbit.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="408" data-file-width="484" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Lunar_orbit.png/300px-Lunar_orbit.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Lunar_orbit.png/450px-Lunar_orbit.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/34/Lunar_orbit.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_orbit.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Apparent paths of the Sun and Moon on the
         <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
          celestial sphere
         </a>
         (angles exaggerated for clarity)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A more detailed explanation is best considered in terms of the paths of the Sun and Moon on the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       , as shown in the first diagram. This shows the abstract sphere surrounding the Earth at the center. The Earth is oriented so that its axis is vertical.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Sun is, by definition, always seen on the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       (the Sun's apparent path across they sky) while Earth is tilted at an angle of
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       = 23.5° to the plane of that path and completes one orbit around the Sun in 365.25636 days, slightly longer than one year due to precession altering the direction of Earth's inclination.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon's orbit around Earth (shown dotted) is inclined at an angle of
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       = 5.14° relative to the ecliptic. The Moon completes one orbit around the Earth in 27.32166 days. The two points at which Moon crosses the ecliptic are known as its
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_node" title="Orbital node">
        orbital nodes
       </a>
       , shown as "N1" and "N2" (ascending node and descending node, respectively), and the line connecting them is known as the
       <i>
        line of nodes
       </i>
       . Due to
       <a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">
        precession
       </a>
       of the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        orbital inclination
       </a>
       , these crossing points, the nodes and the positions of eclipses, gradually shift around the ecliptic in a period of 18.59992 years.
      </p>
      <p>
       Looking at the diagram, note that when the Moon's line of nodes (N1 &amp; N2) rotates a little more than shown, and aligns with Earth's equator, (from front to back, N1, Earth, and N2, seem to be the same dot), the Moon's orbit will reach its steepest angle with the Earth's equator, and in 9.3 years (from front to back, N2, Earth, N1 seem to be the same dot) it will be the shallowest: the 5.14° declination (tilt) of the Moon's orbit either adds to (major standstill) or subtracts from (minor standstill) the inclination of earth's rotation axis (23.439°).
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LunarDec2006.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="824" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/LunarDec2006.png/300px-LunarDec2006.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/LunarDec2006.png/450px-LunarDec2006.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/LunarDec2006.png/600px-LunarDec2006.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LunarDec2006.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The declination of the Moon in 2005–06
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The effect of this on the declination of the Moon is shown in the second diagram. During the course of the nodal period, as the Moon orbits the Earth, its declination swings from –
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       ° to +
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       °, where
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       is a number in the range (
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       –
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       ) ≤
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       ≤ (
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       +
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       ). At a minor standstill (e.g., in 2015), its declination during the month varies from –(
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       –
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       ) = –18.5° to +(
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       –
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       ) = 18.5°. During a major standstill (e.g., in 2005-2006), the declination of the Moon varied during each month from about –(
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       +
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       ) = –28.5° to +(
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       +
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       ) = 28.5°.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LunarMaxima2006.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="598" data-file-width="818" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/LunarMaxima2006.png/300px-LunarMaxima2006.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/LunarMaxima2006.png/450px-LunarMaxima2006.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/LunarMaxima2006.png/600px-LunarMaxima2006.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LunarMaxima2006.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The maximum declinations of the Moon in 2005–06
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       However, an additional subtlety further complicates the picture. The Sun's gravitational attraction on the Moon pulls it toward the plane of the ecliptic, causing a slight wobble of about 9 arcmin within a 6-month period. In 2006, the effect of this was that, although the 18.6-year maximum occurred in June, the maximum declination of the Moon was not in June but in September, as shown in the third diagram.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_complications">
        Other complications
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_standstill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other complications">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because the Moon is relatively close to the Earth,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_parallax" title="Lunar parallax">
        lunar parallax
       </a>
       alters declination up to 0.95° when observed from Earth's surface versus geocentric declination, the declination of the Moon from the center of the Earth. Geocentric declination may be up to about 0.95° different from the observed declination. The amount of this parallax varies with latitude, hence the observed maximum of each standstill cycle varies according to position of observation.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">
        Atmospheric refraction
       </a>
       – the bending of the light from the Moon as it passes through the Earth's atmosphere – alters the observed declination of the Moon, more so at low elevation, where the atmosphere is thicker (deeper).
      </p>
      <p>
       Not all the maxima are observable from all places in the world – the Moon may be below the horizon at a particular observing site during the maximum, and by the time it rises, it may have a lower declination than an observable maximum at some other date.
      </p>
