      <p>
       <b>
        Lunar Mission One
       </b>
       is an international, publicly funded, robotic mission to the Moon, led by Lunar Missions Limited.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Mission_One&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The mission, which is in its early development stages, aims to send a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_lander" title="Lunar lander">
        lunar lander
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       in 2024. The lander will drill below the surface of the Lunar South Pole to a depth of up to 100m, in the hope of accessing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_rock" title="Lunar rock">
        lunar rock
       </a>
       up to 4.5 billion years old. The lunar lander will contain scientific instruments to explore the science and
       <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
        geology
       </a>
       behind the origins of the Moon and the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After drilling, the module will place a
       <a href="/wiki/Time_capsule" title="Time capsule">
        time capsule
       </a>
       into the borehole. This time capsule will contain a public archive, with a record of Earth’s biosphere and a history of
       <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">
        human
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civilisation" title="Civilisation">
        civilisation
       </a>
       , and a private archive consisting of millions of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_memory" title="Digital memory">
        digital memory
       </a>
       boxes. Consumers will be able to purchase digital memory boxes, and fill them with digital data such as photos or videos. They will also be able to store their
       <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">
        DNA
       </a>
       via a strand of hair. Lunar Missions Limited has set the total cost of the mission at £500 million and it is aiming to raise these funds through global sales of digital memory boxes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Funding for the initial development phase was raised on the international crowd-funding platform,
       <a href="/wiki/Kickstarter" title="Kickstarter">
        Kickstarter
       </a>
       . The fundraising was successfully completed on 17 December 2014,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with £672,447 ($1,017,000 approx.) being pledged, exceeding the minimum target of £600,000 ($900,000 approx.).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lunar Missions Limited is a company chaired by
       <a href="/wiki/Ian_Taylor_(British_politician)" title="Ian Taylor (British politician)">
        Ian Taylor
       </a>
       , former
       <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Science" title="Ministry of Science">
        UK Minister for Science and Technology
       </a>
       . Directors of the company include David Iron and Angela Lamont. The technical advisor for the first stage of the project is
       <a href="/wiki/Rutherford_Appleton_Laboratory" title="Rutherford Appleton Laboratory">
        RAL Space
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lunar Mission One is overseen by the Lunar Missions Trust which is also responsible for the education program to be developed around Lunar Mission One, primarily focusing on STEM subjects. The Trust is chaired by
       <a href="/wiki/Graeme_Davies" title="Graeme Davies">
        Sir Graeme Davies
       </a>
       and trustees include
       <a href="/wiki/Monica_Grady" title="Monica Grady">
        Monica Grady
       </a>
       and David Iron.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
