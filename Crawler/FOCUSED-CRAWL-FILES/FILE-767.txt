      <p>
       <b>
        Moon Motor Car Company
       </b>
       (1905 - 1930) was an
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        American
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">
        automobile
       </a>
       company that was located in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">
        St. Louis, Missouri
       </a>
       . The company had a venerable reputation among the buying public, as it was known for fully assembled, easily affordable mid-level cars using high-quality parts. Often this meant the manufacturing process required more human intervention, leading to operating losses.
      </p>
      <p>
       The company was founded by carriage maker Joseph W. Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McConnell_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-McConnell-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moon produced both cars and trucks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McConnell_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-McConnell-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moon Motor's peak production year was 1925 when the company produced 10,271 vehicles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McConnell_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-McConnell-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Motor_Car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In addition to the Moon name, Moons were sold under the
       <a href="/wiki/Hol-Tan" title="Hol-Tan">
        Hol-Tan
       </a>
       name in 1908. Moon produced the
       <i>
        Diana
       </i>
       via its subsidiary the
       <a href="/wiki/Diana_Motors_Company" title="Diana Motors Company">
        Diana Motors Company
       </a>
       in 1925 - 1928. Another subsidiary produced the
       <a href="/wiki/Windsor_(automobile)" title="Windsor (automobile)">
        Windsor
       </a>
       in 1929-1930.
      </p>
      <p>
       The firm also produced a
       <a href="/wiki/Cotton_picker" title="Cotton picker">
        cotton picker
       </a>
       built under contract from the American Cottonpicker Corporation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Beginning in 1924, Moon was increasingly unable to meet dealership orders. The company went out of business at the start of the
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">
        Great Depression
       </a>
       ; the Moon factory was purchased by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruxton_automobile" title="Ruxton automobile">
        Ruxton automobile
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon automobiles are on display at the
       <a href="/wiki/Missouri_History_Museum" title="Missouri History Museum">
        Missouri History Museum
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Transportation" title="Museum of Transportation">
        Museum of Transportation
       </a>
       in St. Louis, Missouri and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneervillage.com&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pioneervillage.com (page does not exist)">
        Pioneer Village Foundation Museum in Minden, NE
       </a>
       . The amusement park
       <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags_St._Louis" title="Six Flags St. Louis">
        Six Flags St. Louis
       </a>
       also features a ride using the Moon automobile body.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">
        Walt Disney
       </a>
       famously had to sell his Moon Roadster to help finance the production of
       <a href="/wiki/Steamboat_Willie" title="Steamboat Willie">
        Steamboat Willie
       </a>
       in 1928.
      </p>
