      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Lunar Receiving Laboratory
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        LRL
       </b>
       ) was a facility at
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Space_Center" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center">
        Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center
       </a>
       (Building 37) that was constructed to
       <a href="/wiki/Quarantine" title="Quarantine">
        quarantine
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronauts" title="Astronauts">
        astronauts
       </a>
       and material brought back from the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       to mitigate the risk of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Back-contamination" title="Back-contamination">
        back-contamination
       </a>
       . After recovery at sea, crews from
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        Apollo 12
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">
        Apollo 14
       </a>
       walked from their
       <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">
        helicopter
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Quarantine_Facility" title="Mobile Quarantine Facility">
        Mobile Quarantine Facility
       </a>
       on the deck of an
       <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">
        aircraft carrier
       </a>
       and were brought to the LRL for quarantine. Samples of rock and
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       that the astronauts collected and brought back were flown directly to the LRL and initially analyzed in
       <a href="/wiki/Glovebox" title="Glovebox">
        glovebox
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_chamber" title="Vacuum chamber">
        vacuum chambers
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       <dd>

      <p>
       The quarantine requirement was dropped beginning with
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       and the LRL was used for study, distribution and safe storage of the lunar samples. Between 1969 and 1972, six Apollo space flight missions brought back 382 kilograms (842 pounds) of lunar rocks, core samples, pebbles, sand and dust from the lunar surface. The six space flights returned 2200 separate samples from six different exploration sites on the lunar surface. (Other lunar samples were returned to Earth by three automated Soviet spacecraft, named
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_16" title="Luna 16">
        Luna 16
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_20" title="Luna 20">
        Luna 20
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_24" title="Luna 24">
        Luna 24
       </a>
       , which returned samples totaling 300 grams (approximately 3/4 pound) from three other lunar sites.)
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1976, a portion of the samples were moved to
       <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Air_Force_Base" title="Brooks Air Force Base">
        Brooks Air Force Base
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" title="San Antonio, Texas">
        San Antonio, Texas
       </a>
       for second-site storage. In 1979, a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Sample_Laboratory_Facility" title="Lunar Sample Laboratory Facility">
        Lunar Sample Laboratory Facility
       </a>
       was built to serve as the chief repository for the Apollo samples. It was constructed to provide permanent storage of the lunar sample collection in a physically secure and non-contaminating environment. The facility consists of storage vaults for the samples, laboratories for sample preparation and study, a vault for sample data and records, and machinery to supply nitrogen to the cabinets in which the samples are stored and processed.
       <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www-curator.jsc.nasa.gov/lunar/lun-fac.cfm" rel="nofollow">
        [1]
       </a>
       The Lunar Receiving Laboratory building is currently occupied by the Life Sciences division. It contains biomedical and environment labs, and is used for experiments involving human adaptation to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microgravity" title="Microgravity">
        microgravity
       </a>
       .
      </p>
