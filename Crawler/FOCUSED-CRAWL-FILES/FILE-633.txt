    Trans-lunar injection
   </h1>

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        trans-lunar injection
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        TLI
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_maneuver" title="Orbital maneuver">
        propulsive maneuver
       </a>
       used to set a
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       on a
       <a href="/wiki/Trajectory" title="Trajectory">
        trajectory
       </a>
       that will cause it to arrive at the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Typical lunar transfer trajectories approximate
       <a href="/wiki/Hohmann_transfer_orbit" title="Hohmann transfer orbit">
        Hohmann transfers
       </a>
       , although
       <a href="/wiki/Low-energy_transfer" title="Low-energy transfer">
        low-energy transfers
       </a>
       have also been used in some cases, as with the
       <a href="/wiki/Hiten" title="Hiten">
        Hiten
       </a>
       probe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For short duration missions without significant
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_theory" title="Perturbation theory">
        perturbations
       </a>
       from sources outside the Earth-Moon system, a fast Hohmann transfer is typically more practical.
      </p>
      <p>
       A spacecraft performs TLI to begin a lunar transfer from a low circular
       <a href="/wiki/Parking_orbit" title="Parking orbit">
        parking orbit
       </a>
       around
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       . The large TLI
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_maneuver" title="Orbital maneuver">
        burn
       </a>
       , usually performed by a chemical
       <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">
        rocket
       </a>
       engine, increases the spacecraft's velocity, changing its orbit from a circular
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
        low Earth orbit
       </a>
       to a highly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical_orbit" title="Elliptical orbit">
        eccentric orbit
       </a>
       . As the spacecraft begins coasting on the lunar transfer arc, its trajectory approximates an elliptical orbit about the Earth with an
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        apogee
       </a>
       near to the radius of the Moon's orbit. The TLI burn is sized and timed to precisely target the Moon as it revolves around the Earth. The burn is timed so that the spacecraft nears apogee as the Moon approaches. Finally, the spacecraft enters the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)" title="Sphere of influence (astrodynamics)">
        sphere of influence
       </a>
       , making a hyperbolic lunar swingby.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modeling">
        Modeling
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trans-lunar_injection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Modeling">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Constellation_trans-lunar_injection.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="640" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Constellation_trans-lunar_injection.jpg/220px-Constellation_trans-lunar_injection.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Constellation_trans-lunar_injection.jpg/330px-Constellation_trans-lunar_injection.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Constellation_trans-lunar_injection.jpg/440px-Constellation_trans-lunar_injection.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Constellation_trans-lunar_injection.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Artist’s concept of NASA’s
         <a href="/wiki/Constellation_program" title="Constellation program">
          Constellation
         </a>
         stack performing the trans-lunar injection burn
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Patched_conics">
        Patched conics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trans-lunar_injection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Patched conics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       TLI targeting and lunar transfers are a specific application of the
       <a href="/wiki/N-body_problem" title="N-body problem">
        n body problem
       </a>
       , which may be approximated in various ways. The simplest way to explore lunar transfer trajectories is by the method of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patched_Conics" title="Patched Conics">
        patched conics
       </a>
       . The spacecraft is assumed to accelerate only under classical 2 body dynamics, being dominated by the Earth until it reaches the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)" title="Sphere of influence (astrodynamics)">
        sphere of influence
       </a>
       . Motion in a patched-conic system is deterministic and simple to calculate, lending itself for rough mission design and "
       <a href="/wiki/Back-of-the-envelope_calculation" title="Back-of-the-envelope calculation">
        back of the envelope
       </a>
       " studies.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Restricted_circular_three_body_.28RC3B.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Restricted_circular_three_body_(RC3B)">
        Restricted circular three body (RC3B)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trans-lunar_injection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Restricted circular three body (RC3B)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       More realistically, however, the spacecraft is subject to gravitational forces from many bodies. Gravitation from Earth and Moon dominate the spacecraft's acceleration, and since the spacecraft's own mass is negligible in comparison, the spacecraft's trajectory may be better approximated as a
       <a href="/wiki/Euler%27s_three-body_problem" title="Euler's three-body problem">
        restricted three-body problem
       </a>
       . This model is a closer approximation but lacks an analytic solution,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       requiring numerical calculation via methods such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runge_kutta" title="Runge kutta">
        Runge-Kutta
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Further_accuracy">
        Further accuracy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trans-lunar_injection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Further accuracy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       More detailed simulation involves modeling the Moon's true orbital motion; gravitation from other astronomical bodies; the non-uniformity of the Earth's and Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_field" title="Gravitational field">
        gravity
       </a>
       ; including
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar radiation pressure
       </a>
       ; and so on. Propagating spacecraft motion in such a model is numerically intensive, but necessary for true mission accuracy.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Free_return">
        Free return
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trans-lunar_injection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Free return">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In some cases it is possible to design a TLI to target a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_return_trajectory" title="Free return trajectory">
        free return trajectory
       </a>
       , so that the spacecraft will loop around behind the Moon and return to Earth without need for further propulsive maneuvers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Such free return trajectories add a margin of safety to
       <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">
        human spaceflight
       </a>
       missions, since the spacecraft will return to Earth "for free" after the initial TLI burn.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trans-lunar_injection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The first space probe to successfully perform TLI was the
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet Union
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_1" title="Luna 1">
        Luna 1
       </a>
       on January 2, 1959. The first human-crewed mission to successfully perform this procedure, and thus becoming the first humans to leave the Earth's influence, was
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">
        Apollo 8
       </a>
       on December 21, 1968.
      </p>
      <p>
       For the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       lunar missions, the restartable
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J-2_(rocket_engine)" title="J-2 (rocket engine)">
        J-2
       </a>
       engine in the third (
       <a href="/wiki/S-IVB" title="S-IVB">
        S-IVB
       </a>
       ) stage of the
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">
        Saturn V
       </a>
       rocket performed TLI. This particular TLI
       <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">
        burn
       </a>
       lasted approximately 350 seconds, providing 3.05 to 3.25 km/s (10,000 to 10,600 ft/s) of
       <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">
        delta-v
       </a>
       , at which point the spacecraft was traveling at approximately 10.4 km/s (34150 ft/s) relative to the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Apollo 8 TLI was spectacularly observed from the Hawaiian Islands in the pre-dawn sky south of Waikiki, photographed and reported in the papers the next day.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1969, the Apollo 10 pre-dawn TLI was visible from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloncurry" title="Cloncurry">
        Cloncurry
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShadowOfTheMoon372_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ShadowOfTheMoon372-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was described as resembling car headlights coming over a hill in fog, with the spacecraft appearing as a bright comet with a greenish tinge.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShadowOfTheMoon372_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ShadowOfTheMoon372-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
