      <p>
       <b>
        Lunar soil
       </b>
       is the fine fraction of the
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       found on the
       <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Moon" title="Geology of the Moon">
        surface of the Moon
       </a>
       . Its properties can differ significantly from those of terrestrial
       <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">
        soil
       </a>
       . The physical properties of lunar soil are primarily the result of mechanical disintegration of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basaltic" title="Basaltic">
        basaltic
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anorthositic" title="Anorthositic">
        anorthositic
       </a>
       rock, caused by continuous meteoric impact and bombardment by interstellar charged atomic particles over years. The process is largely one of mechanical weathering in which the particles are ground to finer and finer size over time. This situation contrasts fundamentally to terrestrial soil formation, mediated by the presence of
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        molecular oxygen
       </a>
       (O
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       ), humidity, atmospheric wind, and a robust array of contributing biological processes. Some have argued that the term "soil" is not correct in reference to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       because on the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , soil is defined as having
       <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">
        organic
       </a>
       content, whereas the Moon has none. However, standard usage among lunar scientists is to ignore that distinction.
      </p>
      <p>
       The term lunar soil is often used interchangeably with "
       <b>
        lunar regolith
       </b>
       " but typically refers to only the finer fraction of regolith, that which is composed of grains one centimeter in diameter or less.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <b>
        Lunar dust
       </b>
       generally connotes even finer materials than lunar soil. There is no official definition of what size fraction constitutes "dust"; some place the cutoff at less than 50 micrometres in diameter, others at less than 10.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_processes">
        Formation processes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_soil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation processes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_17_orange_soil.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="913" data-file-width="904" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Apollo_17_orange_soil.jpg/220px-Apollo_17_orange_soil.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Apollo_17_orange_soil.jpg/330px-Apollo_17_orange_soil.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Apollo_17_orange_soil.jpg/440px-Apollo_17_orange_soil.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_17_orange_soil.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Orange soil found on Apollo 17, the result of volcanic glass beads
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Moon" title="Geology of the Moon">
        Geology of the Moon
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The major processes involved in the formation of lunar soil are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Comminution" title="Comminution">
         Comminution
        </a>
        : mechanical breaking of rocks and minerals into smaller particles by meteorite and
        <a href="/wiki/Micrometeorite" title="Micrometeorite">
         micrometeorite
        </a>
        impact;
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Agglutination_(biology)" title="Agglutination (biology)">
         Agglutination
        </a>
        : welding of mineral and rock fragments together by micrometeorite-impact-produced glass;
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
         Solar wind
        </a>
        spallation and implantation: sputtering caused by impacts of ions and high energy particles.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These processes not only form lunar soil, they also continue to change the physical and optical properties of the soil over time; this process is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weathering" title="Space weathering">
        space weathering
       </a>
       . In addition, fire fountaining, whereby volcanic lava is lofted and cools into small glass beads before falling back to the surface, can create small but important deposits in some locations, such as the orange soil found at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shorty_Crater" title="Shorty Crater">
        Shorty Crater
       </a>
       in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taurus-Littrow" title="Taurus-Littrow">
        Taurus-Littrow
       </a>
       valley by
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        Apollo 17
       </a>
       , and the green glass found at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley%E2%80%93Apennine_(Moon)" title="Hadley–Apennine (Moon)">
        Hadley-Apennine
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       . Deposits of volcanic beads are also thought to be the origin of Dark Mantle Deposits (DMD) in other locations around the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMD_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DMD-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mineralogy_and_composition">
        Mineralogy and composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_soil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mineralogy and composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lunar soil is composed of various types of particles including rock fragments, mono-mineralic fragments, and various kinds of glasses including
       <a href="/wiki/Agglutination_(biology)" title="Agglutination (biology)">
        agglutinate
       </a>
       particles and volcanic and impact spherules. The agglutinates form at the lunar surface by micrometeorite impacts that cause small-scale melting which fuses adjacent materials together with tiny specks of metallic iron (Fe
       <sup>
        0
       </sup>
       ) embedded in each dust particle’s glassy shell.
      </p>
      <p>
       Over time, material is mixed both vertically and horizontally (a process known as "gardening") by impact processes. However, the contribution of material from great distances is relatively minor, such that the soil composition at any given location largely reflects the local bedrock composition.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are two profound differences in the chemistry of lunar regolith and soil from terrestrial materials. The first is that the Moon is very dry. As a result, those minerals with water as part of their structure such as
       <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">
        clay
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">
        mica
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphiboles" title="Amphiboles">
        amphiboles
       </a>
       are totally absent from the Moon. The second difference is that lunar regolith and crust are chemically
       <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">
        reduced
       </a>
       , rather than being significantly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidized" title="Oxidized">
        oxidized
       </a>
       like the Earth's crust. In the case of the regolith, this is due in part to the constant bombardment of the lunar surface with protons (i.e. hydrogen (H) nuclei) from the solar wind. One consequence is that iron on the Moon is found in the metallic 0 and +2 oxidation state, whereas on Earth iron is found primarily in the +2 and +3 oxidation state.
      </p>

      <p>
       The significance of acquiring appropriate knowledge of lunar soil properties is great. The potential for construction of structures,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ground transportation networks, and waste disposal systems, to name a few examples, will depend on real-world experimental data obtained from testing lunar soil samples. The load-carrying capability of the soil is an important parameter in the design of such structures on Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to myriad meteorite impacts (with velocities in the range of 20 km/s), the lunar surface is covered with a thin layer of dust. The dust is electrically charged and sticks to any surface it comes in contact with. The density of lunar regolith is about 1.5 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The soil becomes very dense beneath the top layer of regolith. Other factors which may affect the properties of lunar soil include large
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_thermal_analysis" title="Differential thermal analysis">
        differentials
       </a>
       , the presence of a hard
       <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">
        vacuum
       </a>
       , and the absence of a significant lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       (thereby allowing charged solar wind particles to continuously hit the surface of the Moon).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Moon_dust_fountains_and_electrostatic_levitation">
        Moon dust fountains and electrostatic levitation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_soil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Moon dust fountains and electrostatic levitation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There is some evidence that the Moon may have a tenuous atmosphere of moving dust particles constantly leaping up from and falling back to the Moon's surface, giving rise to a "dust atmosphere" that looks static but is composed of dust particles in constant motion. The term "Moon fountain" has been used to describe this effect by analogy with the stream of molecules of water in a fountain following a ballistic trajectory while appearing static due to the constancy of the stream. According to a model proposed in 2005 by the Laboratory for Extraterrestrial Physics at
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">
        Goddard Space Flight Center
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       this is caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_levitation" title="Electrostatic levitation">
        electrostatic levitation
       </a>
       . On the daylit side of the Moon, solar
       <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
        ultraviolet
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-ray
       </a>
       radiation is energetic enough to knock
       <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">
        electrons
       </a>
       out of atoms and molecules in the lunar soil. Positive charges build up until the tiniest particles of lunar dust (measuring 1 micrometre and smaller) are repelled from the surface and lofted anywhere from metres to kilometres high, with the smallest particles reaching the highest altitudes. Eventually they fall back toward the surface where the process is repeated. On the night side, the dust is negatively charged by electrons in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       . Indeed, the fountain model suggests that the night side would achieve greater voltage differences than the day side, possibly launching dust particles to higher velocities and altitudes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonfountains_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonfountains-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This effect could be further enhanced during the portion of the Moon's orbit where it passes through Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetotail" title="Magnetotail">
        magnetotail
       </a>
       ; see
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field_of_the_Moon" title="Magnetic field of the Moon">
        Magnetic field of the Moon
       </a>
       for more detail.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On the terminator there could be significant horizontal electric fields forming between the day and night areas, resulting in horizontal dust transport - a form of "moon storm".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonfountains_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonfountains-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonstorms_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonstorms-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_17_twilight_ray_sketch.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="620" data-file-width="450" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Apollo_17_twilight_ray_sketch.jpg/220px-Apollo_17_twilight_ray_sketch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Apollo_17_twilight_ray_sketch.jpg/330px-Apollo_17_twilight_ray_sketch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Apollo_17_twilight_ray_sketch.jpg/440px-Apollo_17_twilight_ray_sketch.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_17_twilight_ray_sketch.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Lunar "twilight rays" sketched by Apollo 17 astronauts
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       This effect was anticipated in 1956 by science fiction author
       <a href="/wiki/Hal_Clement" title="Hal Clement">
        Hal Clement
       </a>
       in his short story "Dust Rag", published in
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astounding_Science_Fiction" title="Astounding Science Fiction">
         Astounding Science Fiction
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonfountains_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonfountains-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_Regolith_in_Moscow.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Moon_Regolith_in_Moscow.JPG/220px-Moon_Regolith_in_Moscow.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Moon_Regolith_in_Moscow.JPG/330px-Moon_Regolith_in_Moscow.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Moon_Regolith_in_Moscow.JPG/440px-Moon_Regolith_in_Moscow.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_Regolith_in_Moscow.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A piece of
         <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
          regolith
         </a>
         from
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
          Apollo 11
         </a>
         presented to
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Soviet_Union" title="The Soviet Union">
          the Soviet Union
         </a>
         and exhibited in the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memorial_Museum_of_Astronautics" title="Memorial Museum of Astronautics">
          Memorial Museum of Astronautics
         </a>
         in
         <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">
          Moscow
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       There is some evidence for this effect. In the early 1960s,
       <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_7" title="Surveyor 7">
        Surveyor 7
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and several prior
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surveyor_spacecraft" title="Surveyor spacecraft">
        Surveyor spacecraft
       </a>
       that soft-landed on the Moon returned photographs showing an unmistakable twilight glow low over the lunar horizon persisting after the Sun had set.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonfountains_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonfountains-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moreover, the distant horizon between land and sky did not look razor-sharp, as would have been expected in a vacuum where there was no atmospheric haze. Apollo 17 astronauts orbiting the Moon in 1972 repeatedly saw and sketched what they variously called "bands," "streamers" or "twilight rays" for about 10 seconds before lunar sunrise or lunar sunset. Such rays were also reported by astronauts aboard Apollo 8, 10, and 15. These may have been similar to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crepuscular_ray" title="Crepuscular ray">
        crepuscular rays
       </a>
       on Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonfountains_6-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonfountains-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Apollo 17 also placed an experiment on the Moon's surface called
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiments_Package" title="Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Package">
        LEAM
       </a>
       , short for Lunar Ejecta and Meteorites. It was designed to look for dust kicked up by small meteoroids hitting the Moon's surface. It had three sensors that could record the speed, energy, and direction of tiny particles: one each pointing up, east, and west. LEAM saw a large number of particles every morning, mostly coming from the east or west—rather than above or below—and mostly slower than speeds expected for lunar ejecta. In addition, the experiment's temperature increased to near 100 degrees Celsius a few hours after each lunar sunrise, so the unit had to be turned off temporarily because it was overheating. It is speculated that this could have been a result of electrically-charged moondust sticking to LEAM, darkening its surface so the experiment package absorbed rather than reflected sunlight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonstorms_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonstorms-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, scientists were unable to make a definite determination of the source of the problem, as LEAM operated only briefly before the Apollo program ended.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dirt_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dirt-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is possible that these storms have been spotted from Earth: For centuries, there have been reports of strange glowing lights on the Moon, known as "
       <a href="/wiki/Transient_lunar_phenomenon" title="Transient lunar phenomenon">
        Transient lunar phenomenon
       </a>
       " or TLPs. Some TLPs have been observed as momentary flashes—now generally accepted to be visible evidence of meteoroids impacting the lunar surface. But others have appeared as amorphous reddish or whitish glows or even as dusky hazy regions that change shape or disappear over seconds or minutes. These may have been a result of sunlight reflecting from suspended lunar dust.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonstorms_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonstorms-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Harmful_effects_of_lunar_dust">
        Harmful effects of lunar dust
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_soil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Harmful effects of lunar dust">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Adverse_health_effects_from_lunar_dust_exposure" title="Adverse health effects from lunar dust exposure">
        Adverse health effects from lunar dust exposure
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A 2005 NASA study listed 20 risks that required further study before humans should commit to a human Mars expedition, and ranked "dust" as the #1 challenge. The report urged study of its mechanical properties, corrosiveness, grittiness, and effect on electrical systems. Most scientists think the only way to answer the questions definitively is by returning samples of Martian soil and rock to Earth well before launching any astronauts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dirt_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dirt-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although that report addressed Martian dust, the concerns are equally valid concerning lunar dust. The dust found on the lunar surface could cause harmful effects on any manned outpost technology and crew members:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Darkening of surfaces, leading to a considerable increase in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiative_heat_transfer" title="Radiative heat transfer">
         radiative heat transfer
        </a>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        Abrasive nature of the dust particles may rub and wear down surfaces through friction;
       </li>
       <li>
        Negative effect on coatings used on gaskets to seal equipment from space, optical lenses, solar panels, and windows as well as wiring;
       </li>
       <li>
        Possible damage to an astronaut's lungs, nervous, and cardiovascular systems.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The principles of astronautical hygiene should be used to assess the risks of exposure to lunar dust during exploration on the Moon's surface and thereby determine the most appropriate measures to control exposure. These may include removing the spacesuit in a three-stage airlock, "vacuuming" the suit with a magnet
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       before removal, and using local exhaust ventilation with a high–efficiency particulate filter to remove dust from the spacecraft's atmosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The harmful properties of lunar dust are not well known. However, based on studies of dust found on Earth, it is expected that exposure to lunar dust will result in greater risks to health both from direct exposure (acute) and if exposure is over time (chronic). This is because lunar dust is more chemically reactive and has larger surface areas composed of sharper jagged edges than Earth dust.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If the chemical reactive particles are deposited in the lungs, they may cause respiratory disease. Long-term exposure to the dust may cause a more serious respiratory disease similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Silicosis" title="Silicosis">
        silicosis
       </a>
       . During lunar exploration, the astronauts' spacesuits will become contaminated with lunar dust. The dust will be released into the atmosphere when the suits are removed. The methods used to mitigate exposure will include providing high air recirculation rates in the airlock, the use of a "Double Shell Spacesuit", the use of dust shields, the use of high–grade magnetic separation, and the use of solar flux to sinter and melt the regolith.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present_availability">
        Present availability
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_soil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Present availability">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Apollo astronauts brought back some 360 kilograms (800 pounds) of lunar rocks from six landing sites. Although this material has been isolated in vacuum-packed bottles, it is now unusable for detailed chemical or mechanical analysis—the gritty particles deteriorated the knife-edge
       <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">
        indium
       </a>
       seals of the vacuum bottles; air has slowly leaked in. Every sample brought back from the moon has been contaminated by Earth’s air and humidity. The dust has acquired a patina of rust, and, as a result of bonding with terrestrial water and oxygen molecules, its chemical reactivity is long gone. The chemical and electrostatic properties of the soil no longer match what future astronauts will encounter on the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dirt_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dirt-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon dust-contaminated items finally became available to the public in 2014, when the US government approved
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the sale of private material owned, and collected, by astronauts. Since then only one item has been produced for sale with genuine moon dust collected after the item spent over 32 hours on the Moon. A luggage strap, exposed to the elements of the Moon for 32 hours, a piece of Charles "Pete" Conrad's spacesuit on the Apollo 12 mission, was sold by his estate to a private purchaser at auction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2017 lunar soil collected by
       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>
       in 1969 was put up for auction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While many jewelry- and watch-makers claim their product contains "moon dust", the products only contain pieces of, or dust from, meteorites believed to have originated from the Moon.
      </p>

           <q>
            The particle size distribution of the lunar dust from Apollo 17 sample 77051 has been determined using SEM imaging analysis. The size-distribution data features an approximate Gaussian distribution with a single mode at around 300-nm. The reactivation surface area of highly porous “Swiss-cheese” particles is about 26% higher than a sphere. The morphologies of dust grains have been classified based upon their four types: 1) spherical; 2) angular blocks; 3) glass shards; and 4) irregular (ropey or Swiss-cheese). These data will assist the medical researchers in their studies of the toxicological effects of inhalation of lunar dust by humans.
