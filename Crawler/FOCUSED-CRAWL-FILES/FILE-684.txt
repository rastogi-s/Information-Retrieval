      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         The First Men in the Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_romance" title="Scientific romance">
        scientific romance
       </a>
       by the English author
       <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">
        H. G. Wells
       </a>
       , originally serialised in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Strand_Magazine" title="The Strand Magazine">
         The Strand Magazine
        </a>
       </i>
       from December 1900 to August 1901 and published in hardcover in 1901,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who called it one of his "fantastic stories".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The novel tells the story of a journey to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        moon
       </a>
       undertaken by the two protagonists, a businessman narrator, Mr. Bedford, and an eccentric scientist, Mr. Cavor. Bedford and Cavor discover that the moon is inhabited by a sophisticated
       <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">
        extraterrestrial
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civilisation" title="Civilisation">
        civilisation
       </a>
       of insect-like creatures they call "Selenites".
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">
        Plot summary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_First_Men_in_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The narrator is a London businessman who withdraws to the countryside to write a play, by which he hopes to alleviate his financial problems. Bedford rents a small countryside house in
       <a href="/wiki/Lympne" title="Lympne">
        Lympne
       </a>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">
        Kent
       </a>
       , where he wants to work in peace. He is bothered every afternoon, however, at precisely the same time, by a passer-by making odd noises. After two weeks Bedford accosts the man, who proves to be a reclusive physicist named Mr. Cavor. Bedford befriends Cavor when he learns he is developing a new material,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_shielding" title="Gravitational shielding">
         cavorite
        </a>
       </i>
       , which can negate the force of
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       When a sheet of cavorite is prematurely processed, it makes the air above it weightless and shoots off into space. Bedford sees in the commercial production of cavorite a possible source of "wealth enough to work any sort of social revolution we fancied; we might own and order the whole world".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cavor hits upon the idea of a spherical spaceship made of "steel, lined with glass", and with sliding "windows or blinds" made of cavorite by which it can be steered, and persuades a reluctant Bedford to undertake a voyage to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        moon
       </a>
       ; Cavor is certain there is no life there.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On the way to the moon, they experience
       <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">
        weightlessness
       </a>
       , which Bedford finds "exceedingly restful".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On the surface of the moon the two men discover a desolate landscape, but as the sun rises, the thin, frozen
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       vaporises and strange plants begin to grow with extraordinary rapidity. Bedford and Cavor leave the capsule, but in romping about get lost in the rapidly growing jungle. They hear for the first time a mysterious booming coming from beneath their feet. They encounter "great beasts", "monsters of mere fatness", that they dub "mooncalves", and five-foot-high "Selenites" tending them. At first they hide and crawl about, but growing hungry partake of some "monstrous coralline growths" of fungus that inebriate them. They wander drunkenly until they encounter a party of six extraterrestrials, who capture them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The insectoid lunar natives (referred to as "Selenites", after
       <a href="/wiki/Selene" title="Selene">
        Selene
       </a>
       , the moon goddess) are part of a complex and technologically sophisticated society that lives underground, but this is revealed only in radio communications received from Cavor after Bedford's return to earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bedford and Cavor break out of captivity beneath the surface of the moon and flee, killing several Selenites. In their flight they discover that gold is common on the moon. In their attempt to find their way back to the surface and to their sphere, they come upon some Selenites carving up mooncalves but fight their way past. Back on the surface, they split up to search for their spaceship. Bedford finds it but returns to Earth without Cavor, who injured himself in a fall and was recaptured by the Selenites, as Bedford learns from a hastily scribbled note he left behind.
      </p>
      <p>
       Chapter 19, "Mr. Bedford in Infinite Space", plays no role in the plot but is a remarkable set piece in which the narrator describes experiencing a quasi-mystical "pervading doubt of my own
       <a href="/wiki/Identity_(social_science)" title="Identity (social science)">
        identity
       </a>
       . . . the doubts within me could still argue: 'It is not you that is reading, it is Bedford—but
       <i>
        you are not Bedford,
       </i>
       you know. That's just where the mistake comes in.' 'Counfound it!' I cried, 'and if I am not Bedford, what
       <i>
        am
       </i>
       I? But in that direction no light was forthcoming, though the strangest fancies came drifting into my brain, queer remote suspicions like shadow seem from far away... Do you know I had an idea that really I was something quite outside not only the world, but all worlds, and out of space and time, and that this poor Bedford was just a peephole through which I looked at life..."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By good fortune, the narrator lands in the sea off the coast of Britain, near the seaside town of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Littlestone" title="Littlestone">
        Littlestone
       </a>
       , not far from his point of departure. His fortune is made by some gold he brings back, but he loses the sphere when a curious boy named Tommy Simmons climbs into the unattended sphere and shoots off into space. Bedford writes and publishes his story in
       <i>
        The Strand Magazine
       </i>
       , then learns that "Mr. Julius Wendigee, a Dutch electrician, who has been experimenting with certain apparatus akin to the apparatus used by
       <a href="/wiki/Nikola_Tesla" title="Nikola Tesla">
        Mr. Tesla
       </a>
       in America", has picked up fragments of radio communications from Cavor sent from inside the moon. During a period of relative freedom Cavor has taught two Selenites English and learned much about lunar society.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cavor's account explains that Selenites exist in thousands of forms and find fulfilment in carrying out the specific social function for which they have been brought up:
       <a href="/wiki/Division_of_labour" title="Division of labour">
        specialisation
       </a>
       is the essence of Selenite society. "With knowledge the Selenites grew and changed; mankind stored their knowledge about them and remained brutes—equipped," remarks the Grand Lunar, when he finally meets Cavor and hears about life on Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Unfortunately, Cavor reveals humanity's propensity for war; the lunar leader and those listening to the interview are "stricken with amazement". Bedford infers that it is for this reason that Cavor has been prevented from further broadcasting to Earth. Cavor's transmissions are cut off as he is trying to describe how to make cavorite. His final fate is unknown, but Bedford is sure that "we shall never… receive another message from the moon".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_C._S._Lewis">
        Influence on C. S. Lewis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_First_Men_in_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Influence on C. S. Lewis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">
        C. S. Lewis
       </a>
       explicitly stated that his science fiction books were both inspired by and written as an antithesis to those of H. G. Wells. Specifically, he acknowledged
       <i>
        The First Men in The Moon
       </i>
       to be "the best of the sort [of science fiction] I have read...." (From a letter to
       <a href="/wiki/Roger_Lancelyn_Green" title="Roger Lancelyn Green">
        Roger Lancelyn Green
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The influence of Wells's book is especially visible in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Out_of_the_Silent_Planet" title="Out of the Silent Planet">
         Out of the Silent Planet
        </a>
       </i>
       , the first book of Lewis's
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Trilogy" title="Space Trilogy">
         Space Trilogy
        </a>
       </i>
       . There, too, a central role in the story line is played by a partnership between a worldly businessman interested in the material gains from space travel (and specifically, in importing extraterrestrial gold to Earth) and a scientist with wider cosmic theories.
      </p>
      <p>
       Also in Lewis's book, the two quietly build themselves a spaceship in the seclusion of an English country house, and take off into space without being noticed by the rest of the world. (It may be noted that both Wells and Lewis, like virtually all science fiction writers until the 1950s, grossly underestimated the resources needed for even the smallest jaunt outside Earth's gravitational field.) Like Wells's book, Lewis's reaches its climax with the Earth scientist speaking to the wise ruler of an alien world (in this case
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oyarsa" title="Oyarsa">
        Oyarsa
       </a>
       , the ruler of Malacandra/Mars) and blurting out the warlike and predatory nature of humanity.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, in Lewis's book the businessman-scientist pair are the villains of the piece. Moreover, his scientist,
       <a href="/wiki/Professor_Weston" title="Professor Weston">
        Professor Weston
       </a>
       , has a philosophy diametrically opposite to Cavor's, being an outspoken proponent of human colonisation of other planets, up to and including extermination of "primitive natives".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Film_adaptations">
        Film adaptations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_First_Men_in_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Film adaptations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        The First Men in the Moon
       </i>
       has been adapted to film four times, and once prior to that as a mash-up Verne-Wells film:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/A_Trip_to_the_Moon" title="A Trip to the Moon">
          A Trip to the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        (1902) was released one year after the publication of Wells's source material. It incorporated two of Jules Verne's novels (
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon" title="From the Earth to the Moon">
          From the Earth to the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Around_the_Moon" title="Around the Moon">
          Around the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        ) while the adventures on the Moon were taken from Wells's book.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/The_First_Men_in_the_Moon_(1919_film)" title="The First Men in the Moon (1919 film)">
         first adaptation
        </a>
        was made in 1919; the first film made from a science fiction novel.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
         <a href="#cite_note-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/First_Men_in_the_Moon_(1964_film)" title="First Men in the Moon (1964 film)">
         second adaptation
        </a>
        was made in 1964. In this version, the men wear
        <a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">
         diving suits
        </a>
        as spacesuits, which they did not in the original novel.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/The_First_Men_in_the_Moon_(2010_film)" title="The First Men in the Moon (2010 film)">
         third adaptation
        </a>
        was made for TV in 2010; this is the version most faithful to the novel.
       </li>
       <li>
        The fourth adaptation, in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-D_film" title="3-D film">
         3D
        </a>
        , by David Rosler, was in production from 2009 to 2010.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
        Criticism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_First_Men_in_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criticism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Soon after the publication of
       <i>
        The First Men in the Moon
       </i>
       , Wells was accused by the Irish writer
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cromie" title="Robert Cromie">
        Robert Cromie
       </a>
       of having stolen from his novel
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A_Plunge_into_Space&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A Plunge into Space (page does not exist)">
         A Plunge into Space
        </a>
       </i>
       (1890), which used an antigravity device similar to that in Chrysostom Trueman's
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_History_of_a_Voyage_to_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The History of a Voyage to the Moon (page does not exist)">
         The History of a Voyage to the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       (1864).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Both novels had certain elements in common, such as a globular spaceship built in secret after inventing a way to overcome Earth's gravity. Wells simply replied: "I have never heard of Mr Cromie nor of the book he attempts to advertise by insinuations of plagiarism on my part."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
        Jules Verne
       </a>
       was publicly hostile to Wells's novel mainly due to Wells having his characters go to the moon via a totally fictional creation of an anti-gravitational material rather than the actual use of technology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
