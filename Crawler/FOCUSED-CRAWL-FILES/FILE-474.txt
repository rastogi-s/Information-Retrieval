   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunar Orbiter program
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Lunar Orbiter program
       </b>
       was a series of five
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unmanned_space_mission" title="Unmanned space mission">
        unmanned
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        lunar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">
        orbiter
       </a>
       missions launched by the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       from 1966 through 1967. Intended to help select
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       landing sites by mapping the Moon's surface,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       they provided the first photographs from lunar orbit.
      </p>
      <p>
       All five missions were successful, and 99% of the Moon was mapped from photographs taken with a resolution of 60 meters (200 ft) or better. The first three missions were dedicated to imaging 20 potential manned lunar landing sites, selected based on Earth-based observations. These were flown at low-inclination orbits. The fourth and fifth missions were devoted to broader scientific objectives and were flown in high-altitude polar orbits. Lunar Orbiter 4 photographed the entire nearside and 9% of the far side, and Lunar Orbiter 5 completed the far side coverage and acquired medium (20 m (66 ft)) and high (2 m (6 ft 7 in)) resolution images of 36 pre-selected areas. All Lunar Orbiter craft were launched by an
       <a href="/wiki/Atlas-Agena" title="Atlas-Agena">
        Atlas-Agena
       </a>
       D launch vehicle.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Lunar Orbiters had an ingenious imaging system, which consisted of a dual-lens
       <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">
        camera
       </a>
       , a film processing unit, a readout scanner, and a film handling apparatus. Both lenses, a 610 mm (24 in) narrow angle high resolution (HR) lens and an 80 mm (3.1 in) wide angle medium resolution (MR) lens, placed their frame exposures on a single roll of
       <a href="/wiki/70_mm_film" title="70 mm film">
        70 mm film
       </a>
       . The axes of the two cameras were coincident so the area imaged in the HR frames were centered within the MR frame areas. The film was moved during exposure to compensate for the
       <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">
        spacecraft velocity
       </a>
       , which was estimated by an electro-optical sensor. The film was then processed, scanned, and the images transmitted back to Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the Lunar Orbiter missions, the first pictures of Earth as a whole were taken, beginning with Earth-rise over the lunar surface by Lunar Orbiter 1 in August, 1966. The first full picture of the whole Earth was taken by Lunar Orbiter 5 on 8 August 1967.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A second photo of the whole Earth was taken by Lunar Orbiter 5 on 10 November 1967.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_and_subsystems">
        Spacecraft and subsystems
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Orbiter_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spacecraft and subsystems">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Orbiter_diagram.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="587" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Lunar_Orbiter_diagram.png/220px-Lunar_Orbiter_diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Lunar_Orbiter_diagram.png/330px-Lunar_Orbiter_diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Lunar_Orbiter_diagram.png/440px-Lunar_Orbiter_diagram.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Orbiter_diagram.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Lunar Orbiter diagram (NASA)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Boeing-Eastman Kodak proposal was announced by NASA on 20 December 1963. The main bus of the Lunar Orbiter had the general shape of a truncated cone, 1.65 m (5 ft 5 in) tall and 1.5 m (4 ft 11 in) in diameter at the base. The spacecraft was composed of three decks supported by trusses and an arch. The equipment deck at the base of the craft held the battery, transponder, flight programmer,
       <a href="/wiki/Inertial_navigation_system" title="Inertial navigation system">
        inertial reference unit
       </a>
       (IRU),
       <a href="/wiki/Canopus" title="Canopus">
        Canopus
       </a>
       star tracker, command decoder, multiplex encoder, traveling wave tube amplifier (TWTA), and the photographic system. Four solar panels were mounted to extend out from this deck with a total span across of 3.72 m (12.2 ft). Also extending out from the base of the spacecraft were a high gain antenna on a 1.32 m (4 ft 4 in) boom and a low-gain antenna on a 2.08 m (6 ft 10 in) boom. Above the equipment deck, the middle deck held the velocity control engine, propellant, oxidizer and pressurization tanks, Sun sensors, and micro-meteoroid detectors. The third deck consisted of a heat shield to protect the spacecraft from the firing of the velocity control engine. The nozzle of the engine protruded through the center of the shield. Mounted on the perimeter of the top deck were four
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attitude_control_system" title="Attitude control system">
        attitude control
       </a>
       thrusters.
      </p>
      <p>
       Power of 375 W was provided by the four solar arrays containing 10,856 n/p solar cells which would directly run the spacecraft and also charge the 12 A·h
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-cadmium_batteries" title="Nickel-cadmium batteries">
        nickel-cadmium
       </a>
       battery. The batteries were used during brief periods of occultation when no solar power was available. Propulsion for major maneuvers was provided by the gimballed velocity control engine, a hypergolic 100 pound-force (445 N) thrust
       <a href="/wiki/Marquardt_Corporation" title="Marquardt Corporation">
        Marquardt
       </a>
       rocket motor. Three axis stabilization and attitude control were provided by four one lbf (4 N) nitrogen gas jets. Navigational knowledge was provided by five Sun sensors, Canopus star sensor, and the IRU equipped with internal gyros. Communications were via a 10 W transmitter and the directional 1 m diameter high gain antenna for transmission of photographs and a 0.5 W transmitter and omnidirectional low-gain antenna for other communications. Both antennas operated in S-band at 2295 MHz. Thermal control was maintained by a multilayer aluminized
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mylar" title="Mylar">
        Mylar
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dacron" title="Dacron">
        Dacron
       </a>
       thermal blanket which enshrouded the main bus, special paint, insulation, and small heaters.
      </p>
      <p>
       The photographic system was provided by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak" title="Eastman Kodak">
        Eastman Kodak
       </a>
       and derived from a system, provided by the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Reconnaissance_Office" title="National Reconnaissance Office">
        National Reconnaissance Office
       </a>
       , designed for the
       <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">
        U-2
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird" title="Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird">
        SR-71
       </a>
       reconnaissance aircraft.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-day20101129_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-day20101129-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The camera used two lenses to simultaneously expose a wide-angle and a high-resolution image on the same film. The wide-angle, medium resolution mode used an 80 mm F 2.8
       <a href="/wiki/Schneider_Kreuznach#Xenotar" title="Schneider Kreuznach">
        Xenotar
       </a>
       lens manufactured by
       <a href="/wiki/Schneider_Kreuznach" title="Schneider Kreuznach">
        Schneider Kreuznach
       </a>
       , Germany. The high-resolution mode used a 610 mm F 5.6 Panoramic lens manufactured by the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Optical_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pacific Optical Company (page does not exist)">
        Pacific Optical Company
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The film was developed on-orbit in a semi-dry process, and then scanned by a photomultiplier for transmission to Earth. This system was adapted under permission of NRO from
       <a href="/wiki/Samos_(satellite)" title="Samos (satellite)">
        SAMOS
       </a>
       E-1 reconnaissance camera, built by Kodak for a short-lived USAF near-realtime satellite imaging project.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Originally, the Air Force had offered NASA several spare cameras from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KH-7_GAMBIT" title="KH-7 GAMBIT">
        KH-7 GAMBIT
       </a>
       program, but then became concerned over security surrounding the classified cameras, including the possibility of images of the Moon giving away their resolution. Some proposals were made that NASA not publish the orbital parameters of the Lunar Orbiter probes so that the resolution of the images could not be calculated through their altitude, but in the end, NASA's existing camera systems, while lower resolution, proved adequate for the needs of the mission.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Backup">
        Backup
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Orbiter_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Backup">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       As a backup for Lunar Orbiter, NASA and NRO cooperated on the Lunar Mapping and Survey System (LM&amp;SS), based on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KH-7" title="KH-7">
        KH-7
       </a>
       reconnaissance satellite. Replacing the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Module" title="Lunar Module">
        Lunar Module
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">
        Saturn V
       </a>
       , Apollo astronauts would operate LM&amp;SS remotely in lunar orbit. NASA canceled the project in the summer of 1967 after Lunar Orbiter's success.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-day20101129_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-day20101129-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Results">
        Results
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Orbiter_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Results">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Lunar Orbiter program consisted of five spacecraft which returned photography of 99 percent of the surface of the Moon (near and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far_side_(Moon)" title="Far side (Moon)">
        far side
       </a>
       ) with resolution down to 1 meter (3 ft 3 in). Altogether the Orbiters returned 2180 high resolution and 882 medium resolution frames. The micro-meteoroid experiments recorded 22 impacts showing the average micro-meteoroid flux near the Moon was about two orders of magnitude greater than in interplanetary space, but slightly less than the near-Earth environment. The radiation experiments confirmed that the design of Apollo hardware would protect the astronauts from average and greater than average short term exposure to solar particle events. The use of Lunar Orbiters for tracking to evaluate the
       <a href="/wiki/Manned_Space_Flight_Network" title="Manned Space Flight Network">
        Manned Space Flight Network
       </a>
       tracking stations and Apollo Orbit Determination Program was successful, with three Lunar Orbiters (2, 3, and 5) being tracked simultaneously from August to October 1967. The Lunar Orbiters were all eventually commanded to crash on the Moon before their attitude control fuel ran out so they would not present navigational or communications hazards to later Apollo flights. The Lunar Orbiter program was managed by NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Langley_Research_Center" title="Langley Research Center">
        Langley Research Center
       </a>
       at a total cost of roughly $200 million.
      </p>
      <p>
       Doppler tracking of the five orbiters allowed mapping of the gravitational field of the moon and discovery of
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_concentration_(astronomy)" title="Mass concentration (astronomy)">
        mass concentrations
       </a>
       (mascons), or gravitational highs, which were located in the centers of some (but not all) of the lunar maria.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Data_availability">
        Data availability
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Orbiter_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Data availability">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Lunar Orbiter orbital photographs were transmitted to Earth as analog data after onboard scanning of the original film into a series of strips. The data were written to magnetic tape and also to film. The film data were used to create hand-made mosaics of Lunar Orbiter frames. Each LO exposure resulted in two photographs: medium-resolution frames recorded by the 80-mm focal-length lens and high-resolution frames recorded by the 610-mm focal length lens. Due to their large size, HR frames were divided into three sections, or sub-frames. Large-format prints (16 by 20 inches (410 mm × 510 mm)) from the mosaics were created and several copies were distributed across the U.S. to NASA image and data libraries known as
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.lpi.usra.edu/library/RPIF/" rel="nofollow">
        Regional Planetary Information Facilities
       </a>
       . The resulting outstanding views were of generally very high spatial resolution and covered a substantial portion of the lunar surface, but they suffered from a “venetian blind” striping, missing or duplicated data, and frequent saturation effects that hampered their use. For many years these images have been the basis of much of lunar scientific research. Because they were obtained at low to moderate Sun angles, the Lunar Orbiter photographic mosaics are particularly useful for studying the morphology of lunar topographic features.
      </p>
      <p>
       Several atlases and books featuring Lunar Orbiter photographs have been published. Perhaps the most definitive was that of
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.lpi.usra.edu/resources/lunar_orbiter/book/lopam.pdf" rel="nofollow">
        Bowker and Hughes
       </a>
       (1971); it contained 675 photographic plates with approximately global coverage of the Moon. In part because of high interest in the data and in part because that atlas is out of print, the task was undertaken at the
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.lpi.usra.edu/" rel="nofollow">
        Lunar and Planetary Institute
       </a>
       to scan the large-format prints of Lunar Orbiter data.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These were made available online as the
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.lpi.usra.edu/resources/lunar_orbiter/" rel="nofollow">
        Digital Lunar Orbiter Photographic Atlas of the Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Data_recovery_and_digitization">
        Data recovery and digitization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Orbiter_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Data recovery and digitization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Comparison_between_original_and_LOIRP_result.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="800" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Comparison_between_original_and_LOIRP_result.jpg/220px-Comparison_between_original_and_LOIRP_result.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Comparison_between_original_and_LOIRP_result.jpg/330px-Comparison_between_original_and_LOIRP_result.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Comparison_between_original_and_LOIRP_result.jpg/440px-Comparison_between_original_and_LOIRP_result.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Comparison_between_original_and_LOIRP_result.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A detail of an original image at the top, compared to a reprocessed version at the bottom created by
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LOIRP" title="LOIRP">
          LOIRP
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 2000, the
       <a href="/wiki/Astrogeology_Research_Program" title="Astrogeology Research Program">
        Astrogeology Research Program
       </a>
       of the US Geological Survey in Flagstaff, Arizona was funded by NASA (as part of the
       <a class="external text" href="https://astrogeology.usgs.gov/Projects/LunarOrbiterDigitization/" rel="nofollow">
        Lunar Orbiter Digitization Project
       </a>
       ) to scan at 25 micrometer resolution archival LO positive film strips that were produced from the original data.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The goal was to produce a
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.mapaplanet.org/explorer-bin/explorer.cgi?map=Moon&amp;layers=moon_lo&amp;west=-180&amp;south=-90&amp;east=180&amp;north=90&amp;center=0&amp;defaultcenter=on&amp;grid=none&amp;stretch=auto&amp;projection=SIMP&amp;advoption=NO&amp;info=NO&amp;resolution=2" rel="nofollow">
        global mosaic
       </a>
       of the Moon using the best available
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter" title="Lunar Orbiter">
        Lunar Orbiter
       </a>
       frames (largely the same coverage as that of Bowker and Hughes, 1971). The frames were constructed from scanned film strips; they were digitally constructed, geometrically controlled, and map-projected without the stripes that had been noticeable in the original photographic frames. Because of its emphasis on construction of a global mosaic, this project only scanned about 15% of the available Lunar Orbiter photographic frames. Data from Lunar Orbiter missions
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_3" title="Lunar Orbiter 3">
        III
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_4" title="Lunar Orbiter 4">
        IV
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_5" title="Lunar Orbiter 5">
        V
       </a>
       were included in the global mosaic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition, the USGS digitization project created frames from very high resolution Lunar Orbiter images for several 'sites of scientific interest.' These sites had been identified in the 1960s when the Apollo landing sites were being selected. Frames for sites such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        Apollo 12
       </a>
       landing site, the
       <a href="/wiki/Marius_(crater)" title="Marius (crater)">
        Marius
       </a>
       Hills, and the Sulpicius Gallus rille have been released.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2007, the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_Image_Recovery_Project" title="Lunar Orbiter Image Recovery Project">
        Lunar Orbiter Image Recovery Project
       </a>
       (LOIRP) began a process to convert the Lunar Orbiter Images directly from the original
       <a href="/wiki/Ampex" title="Ampex">
        Ampex
       </a>
       FR-900 analog video recordings of the spacecraft data to digital image format, a change which provided vastly improved resolution over the original images released in the 1960s. The first of these restored images were released in late 2008.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Almost all of the Lunar Orbiter images have been successfully recovered as of February 2014 and are undergoing digital processing before being submitted to NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_Data_System" title="Planetary Data System">
        Planetary Data System
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
