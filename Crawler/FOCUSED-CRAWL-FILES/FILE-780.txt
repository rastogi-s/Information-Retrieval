      <p>
       <b>
        Thebe
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/θ/: 'th' in 'thigh'">
            θ
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'b' in 'buy'">
            b
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          THEE
         </span>
         -bee
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Θήβη
       </span>
       ) also known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter XIV
        </span>
       </b>
       , is the fourth of
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">
        Jupiter's moons
       </a>
       by distance from the
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_P._Synnott" title="Stephen P. Synnott">
        Stephen P. Synnott
       </a>
       in images from the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       space probe taken on March 5, 1979, while making its flyby of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESynnott1980_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESynnott1980-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1983 it was officially named after the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        mythological
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">
        nymph
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_mythological_figures_named_Thebe" title="List of mythological figures named Thebe">
        Thebe
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872-10">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The second largest of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        inner satellites of Jupiter
       </a>
       , Thebe orbits within the outer edge of the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter#Thebe_gossamer_ring" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        Thebe gossamer ring
       </a>
       that is formed from dust ejected from its surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-6">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is irregularly shaped and reddish in colour, and is thought like
       <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(moon)" title="Amalthea (moon)">
        Amalthea
       </a>
       to consist of porous water ice with unknown amounts of other materials. Its surface features include large craters and high mountains—some of them are comparable to the size of the moon itself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Thebe was photographed in 1979 by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">
         Voyager 1 and 2
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft, and later, in more detail, by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_spacecraft" title="Galileo spacecraft">
        <i>
         Galileo
        </i>
        orbiter
       </a>
       in the 1990s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_observations">
        Discovery and observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Thebe was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_P._Synnott" title="Stephen P. Synnott">
        Stephen P. Synnott
       </a>
       in images from the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       space probe taken on March 5, 1979, and was initially given the provisional designation
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/1979 J 2
        </span>
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESynnott1980_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESynnott1980-9">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3470_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3470-11">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1983 it was officially named after the mythological
       <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">
        nymph
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_mythological_figures_named_Thebe" title="List of mythological figures named Thebe">
        Thebe
       </a>
       who was a lover of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       —the Greek equivalent of Jupiter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3872-10">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After its discovery by Voyager 1, Thebe was photographed by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       space probe in 1979.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-6">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, before the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_spacecraft" title="Galileo spacecraft">
        <i>
         Galileo
        </i>
        spacecraft
       </a>
       arrived at Jupiter, knowledge about it was extremely limited.
       <i>
        Galileo
       </i>
       imaged almost all of the surface of Thebe and helped clarify its composition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Thebe is the outermost of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        inner Jovian moons
       </a>
       , and orbits Jupiter at a distance of about 222,000 km (3.11 Jupiter radii). Its orbit has an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of 0.018, and an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       of 1.08° relative to the equator of Jupiter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECooper_Murray_et_al.2006_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECooper_Murray_et_al.2006-3">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These values are unusually high for an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellite" title="Inner satellite">
        inner satellite
       </a>
       and can be explained by the past influence of the innermost
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galilean_satellite" title="Galilean satellite">
        Galilean satellite
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-6">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in the past, several
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        mean-motion resonances
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       would have passed through Thebe's orbit as Io gradually receded from Jupiter, and these excited Thebe's orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_6-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-6">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The orbit of Thebe lies near the outer edge of the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter#Thebe_gossamer_ring" title="Rings of Jupiter">
        Thebe gossamer ring
       </a>
       , which is composed of the dust ejected from the satellite. After ejection the dust drifts in the direction of the planet under the action of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poynting%E2%80%93Robertson_drag" title="Poynting–Robertson drag">
        Poynting–Robertson drag
       </a>
       forming a ring inward of the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Showalter_et_al.1999_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Showalter_et_al.1999-12">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Thebe is irregularly shaped, with the closest
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">
        ellipsoidal
       </a>
       approximation being 116×98×84 km. Its surface area is probably between 31,000 and 59,000 (~45,000) km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       . Its bulk density and mass are not known, but assuming that its mean
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       is like that of Amalthea (around 0.86 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       its mass can be estimated at roughly 4.3 × 10
       <sup>
        17
       </sup>
       kg.
      </p>
      <p>
       Similarly to all inner satellites of Jupiter, Thebe
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchronous_rotation" title="Synchronous rotation">
        rotates synchronously
       </a>
       with its orbital motion, thus keeping one face always looking toward the planet. Its orientation is such that the long axis always points to Jupiter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_6-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-6">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the surface points closest to and furthest from Jupiter, the surface is thought to be near the edge of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">
        Roche limit
       </a>
       , where Thebe's gravity is only slightly larger than the
       <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force" title="Centrifugal force">
        centrifugal force
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_6-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-6">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a result, the
       <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">
        escape velocity
       </a>
       in these two points is very small, thus allowing dust to escape easily after meteorite impacts, and ejecting it into the Thebe gossamer ring.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_6-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-6">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Zethus Crater is the largest (diameter about 40 km) crater on and the only named surface feature of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       's moon Thebe. There are several bright spots at the
       <a href="/wiki/Rim_(craters)" title="Rim (craters)">
        rim
       </a>
       of this crater.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998_4-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas_Burns_et_al.1998-4">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is located on the far side of Thebe, facing away from Jupiter. It was discovered by the
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">
        <i>
         Galileo
        </i>
       </a>
       spacecraft. It is named for
       <a href="/wiki/Amphion_and_Zethus" title="Amphion and Zethus">
        Zethus
       </a>
       , the husband of the nymph
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_mythological_figures_named_Thebe" title="List of mythological figures named Thebe">
        Thebe
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-USGS-13">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The surface of Thebe is dark and appears to be reddish in color.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimonelli_Rossier_et_al.2000_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimonelli_Rossier_et_al.2000-8">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is a substantial asymmetry between leading and trailing
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        hemispheres
       </a>
       : the leading hemisphere is 1.3 times brighter than the trailing one. The asymmetry is probably caused by the higher velocity and frequency of
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impacts
       </a>
       on the leading hemisphere, which excavate a bright material (probably ice) from the interior of the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimonelli_Rossier_et_al.2000_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimonelli_Rossier_et_al.2000-8">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The surface of Thebe is heavily cratered and it appears that there are at least three or four
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact craters
       </a>
       that are very large, each being roughly comparable in size to Thebe itself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004_6-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns_Simonelli_et_al.2004-6">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
