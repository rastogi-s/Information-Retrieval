   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunar effect
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

         Biologists as well as artists and poets have thought about the Moon's influence on living creatures.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kiefer_14-04-2014_Vollmond_fullmoon_(13856458823).jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="947" data-file-width="1263" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Kiefer_14-04-2014_Vollmond_fullmoon_%2813856458823%29.jpg/200px-Kiefer_14-04-2014_Vollmond_fullmoon_%2813856458823%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Kiefer_14-04-2014_Vollmond_fullmoon_%2813856458823%29.jpg/300px-Kiefer_14-04-2014_Vollmond_fullmoon_%2813856458823%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Kiefer_14-04-2014_Vollmond_fullmoon_%2813856458823%29.jpg/400px-Kiefer_14-04-2014_Vollmond_fullmoon_%2813856458823%29.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kiefer_14-04-2014_Vollmond_fullmoon_(13856458823).jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Despite all of the beliefs, no valid scientific study has ever found a significant extraordinary effect of the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_Moon" title="Full Moon">
          full Moon
         </a>
         on life on Earth.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The term
       <b>
        lunar effect
       </b>
       refers to real or imaginary correlations between specific stages of the roughly 29.5-day
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_cycle" title="Lunar cycle">
        lunar cycle
       </a>
       and behavior and physiological changes in living beings on Earth, including humans. In some cases the purported effect may depend on external cues, such as the amount of
       <a href="/wiki/Moonlight" title="Moonlight">
        moonlight
       </a>
       . In other cases, such as the approximately-monthly cycle of
       <a href="/wiki/Menstruation" title="Menstruation">
        menstruation
       </a>
       in humans (but not other mammals), the coincidence in timing reflects no known lunar influence.
      </p>
      <p>
       A considerable number of studies have examined the effect on humans. By the late 1980s, there were at least 40 published studies on the purported lunar-lunacy connection,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RottonKelly1985_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RottonKelly1985-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and at least 20 published studies on the purported lunar-birthrate connection.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MartensKelly1988_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MartensKelly1988-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This has allowed several extensive literature reviews and meta-analyses to be produced, which have found no correlation between the lunar cycle and human biology or behavior.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RottonKelly1985_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-RottonKelly1985-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MartensKelly1988_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-MartensKelly1988-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelly_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kelly-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FosterRoenneberg2008_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FosterRoenneberg2008-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <br/>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_the_belief">
        Origins of the belief
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins of the belief">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Examples of the belief have been found in ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">
        Assyrian
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">
        Babylonian
       </a>
       writing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skepdic_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-skepdic-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contexts">
        Contexts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contexts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Claims of a lunar connection have appeared in the following contexts:
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fertility">
        Fertility
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fertility">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Menstrual_cycle#The_Moon" title="Menstrual cycle">
        Menstrual cycle § The Moon
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       It is widely believed that the Moon has a relationship with
       <a href="/wiki/Fertility" title="Fertility">
        fertility
       </a>
       due to the corresponding human
       <a href="/wiki/Menstrual_cycle" title="Menstrual cycle">
        menstrual cycle
       </a>
       , which averages 28 days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skepdic_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-skepdic-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-straightdope-menstruation_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-straightdope-menstruation-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, no connection between lunar rhythms and menstrual onset has been conclusively shown to exist, and the similarity in length between the two cycles is most likely coincidental.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_rate">
        Birth rate
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Birth rate">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Three studies carried out between 1959 and 1973 reported a 1 percent increase in births in New York following a full Moon.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       However, multiple studies have found no connection between birth rate and lunar phases. A 1957 analysis of 9,551 births in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danville,_PA" title="Danville, PA">
        Danville, PA
       </a>
       , found no correlation between birth rate and the phase of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abell1979_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Abell1979-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Records of 11,961 live births and 8,142 natural births (not induced by drugs or cesarean section) over a 4-year period (1974-1978) at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UCLA" title="UCLA">
        UCLA
       </a>
       hospital did not correlate in any way with the cycle of lunar phases.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AbellGreenspan1979_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AbellGreenspan1979-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Analysis of 3,706 spontaneous births (excluding births resulting from induced labor) in 1994 showed no correlation with lunar phase.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JoshiBharadwaj1998_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-JoshiBharadwaj1998-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The distribution of 167,956 spontaneous vaginal deliveries, at 37 to 40 weeks gestation, in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenix,_AZ" title="Phoenix, AZ">
        Phoenix, AZ
       </a>
       , between 1995 and 2000, showed no relationship with lunar phase.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morton-PradhanBay2005_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morton-PradhanBay2005-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Analysis of 564,039 births (1997 to 2001) in
       <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">
        North Carolina
       </a>
       showed no predictable influence of the lunar cycle on deliveries or complications.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArlissKaplan2005_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ArlissKaplan2005-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Analysis of 6,725 deliveries (2000 to 2006) in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hannover,_Germany" title="Hannover, Germany">
        Hannover
       </a>
       revealed no significant correlation of birth rate to lunar phases.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StaboulidouSoergel2008_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-StaboulidouSoergel2008-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A 2001 analysis of 70,000,000 birth records from the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Health_Statistics" title="National Center for Health Statistics">
        National Center for Health Statistics
       </a>
       revealed no correlation between birth rate and lunar phase.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caton2001_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Caton2001-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An extensive review of 21 studies from 7 different countries showed that the majority of studies reported no relationship to lunar phase, and that the positive studies were inconsistent with each other.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MartensKelly1988_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-MartensKelly1988-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A review of 6 additional studies from 5 different countries similarly showed no evidence of relationship between birth rate and lunar phase.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KellyMartens1994_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-KellyMartens1994-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Blood_loss">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

         ]
        </span>
       </span>

      <p>
       It is sometimes know as blue blood. And claimed that surgeons used to refuse to operate during the full Moon because of the increased risk of death of the patient through blood loss.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RomanSoriano2004_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RomanSoriano2004-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
          <span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (September 2013)">
           not in citation given
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">

       <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">

       <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_bleeding" title="Gastrointestinal bleeding">

       </a>
       , but only when comparing full Moon days to all non-full Moon days lumped together.

        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]

         ]
        </span>

      <p>
       Two studies found evidence that those with mental disorders i.e. Schizophrenia generally exhibit 1.8% of increased violent or aggressive episodes during the full Moon,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drum1986_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Drum1986-21">

        </a>

        <a href="#cite_note-Owen1998-23">
         [23]
        </a>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schizophrenic" title="Schizophrenic">

      <p>
       A study into epilepsy found a significant negative correlation between the mean number of seizures per day and the fraction of the Moon that is illuminated, but this correlation disappeared when the local clarity of the night sky was controlled for, suggesting that it is the brightness of the night sky, and not the lunar phase per se, that influences the occurrence of epileptic seizures with advanced photosensitive epilepsy.

A police officer responsible for the research told the BBC that "From my experience of 19 years of being a police officer, undoubtedly on full moons we do seem to get people with sort of strange behavior - more fractious, argumentative."

      </p>

        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">

      <p>
       Several studies have argued that the stock market’s average returns are much higher during the half of the month closest to the new moon than the half closest to the full moon. The reasons for this have not been studied, but the authors suggest this may be due to lunar influences on mood.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YuanZheng2001_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-YuanZheng2001-33">

        <a href="#cite_note-DichevJanes2001-35">
         [35]
        </a>

       Another study has found contradictory results and questioned these claims.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herbst2007_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Herbst2007-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meta-analyses">
        Meta-analyses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Meta-analyses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       A meta-analysis of thirty-seven studies that examined relationships between the Moon's four phases and human behavior revealed no significant correlation. The authors found that, of twenty-three studies that had claimed to show correlation, nearly half contained at least one
       <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">
        statistical
       </a>
       error.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RottonKelly1985_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-RottonKelly1985-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelly_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-kelly-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Similarly, in a review of twenty studies examining correlations between Moon phase and suicides, most of the twenty studies found no correlation, and the ones that did report positive results were inconsistent with each other.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelly_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-kelly-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A 1978 review of the literature also found that lunar phases and human behavior are not related.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CampbellBeets1978_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CampbellBeets1978-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_quality">
        Sleep quality
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sleep quality">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A July 2013 study carried out at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Basel" title="University of Basel">
        University of Basel
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       suggests a correlation between the full Moon and human sleep quality.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CajochenAltanay-Ekici2013_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CajochenAltanay-Ekici2013-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Professor Cajochen and colleagues presented evidence that a lunar rhythm can modulate sleep structure in humans when measured under the highly controlled conditions of a circadian laboratory study protocol without time cues. Studying 33 volunteer subjects, the researchers found that subjective and objective measures of sleep varied according to lunar phase and thus may reflect human circalunar rhythmicity. Stringently controlled laboratory conditions, in a cross-sectional setting, were employed to exclude confounding effects such as increased light at night or the potential bias in perception. Measures of lunar influence on sleep structure,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electroencephalograph" title="Electroencephalograph">
        electroencephalographic
       </a>
       activity during
       <a href="/wiki/Non-rapid_eye_movement_sleep" title="Non-rapid eye movement sleep">
        non-rapid eye movement sleep
       </a>
       (NREM), and secretion of the hormones
       <a href="/wiki/Melatonin" title="Melatonin">
        melatonin
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cortisol" title="Cortisol">
        cortisol
       </a>
       , were retrospectively analyzed. At no point, during and after the study, were volunteers or investigators aware of the posteriori analysis relative to lunar phase. Around full Moon it was found that electroencephalogram (EEG) delta activity during NREM sleep, an indicator of deep sleep, decreased by 30%, time to fall asleep increased by five minutes, and EEG-assessed total sleep duration was reduced by 20 minutes. These changes were associated with a decrease in subjective sleep quality and diminished endogenous melatonin levels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CajochenAltanay-Ekici2013_38-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-CajochenAltanay-Ekici2013-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cajochen said: "The lunar cycle seems to influence human sleep, even when one does not 'see' the Moon and is not aware of the actual Moon phase."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are suggestions that the 2013 Cajochen study is faulty because of a relatively small (n=33) sample size and inappropriate controls for age and sex.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cordi2014_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cordi2014-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A 2014 study with larger sample sizes (n1=366, n2=29, n3=870) and better experimental controls found no effect of the lunar phase on sleep quality metrics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cordi2014_40-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cordi2014-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A 2015 study of 795 children found a 3-minute
       <i>
        increase
       </i>
       in sleep duration near the full moon,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sjodin2015_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sjodin2015-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but a 2016 study of 5,812 children found a 5-minute
       <i>
        decrease
       </i>
       in sleep duration near the full moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaput2016_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chaput2016-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       No other modification in activity behaviors were reported,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaput2016_42-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chaput2016-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the lead scientist concluded: "Our study provides compelling evidence that the moon does not seem to influence people's behavior."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FrontPed_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FrontPed-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_animals">
        In animals
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In animals">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Grunion" title="Grunion">
        California Grunion
       </a>
       fish have an unusual mating and spawning ritual during the spring and summer months. The egg laying takes place on four consecutive nights, beginning on the nights of the full and new Moons, when tides are highest.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grunion_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-grunion-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, this is a well understood reproductive strategy that is more related to tides than it is to lunar phase. It happens to correlate with the lunar phase because tides are highest when the Sun, Earth, and Moon are aligned, i.e., at new Moon or full Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Correlation between hormonal changes in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testis" title="Testis">
        testis
       </a>
       and lunar periodicity was found in
       <a href="/wiki/Streamlined_spinefoot" title="Streamlined spinefoot">
        streamlined spinefoot
       </a>
       (a type of fish), which spawns synchronously around the last Moon quarter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zimecki_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-zimecki-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Orange-spotted_spinefoot" title="Orange-spotted spinefoot">
        orange-spotted spinefoot
       </a>
       , lunar phases affect the levels of
       <a href="/wiki/Melatonin" title="Melatonin">
        melatonin
       </a>
       in the blood.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zimecki_45-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-zimecki-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Evidence for lunar effect in reptiles, birds and mammals is scant,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zimecki_45-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-zimecki-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but among reptiles
       <a href="/wiki/Marine_iguana" title="Marine iguana">
        marine iguanas
       </a>
       (which live in the
       <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">
        Galápagos Islands
       </a>
       ) time their trips to the sea in order to arrive at low tide.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In insects, the lunar cycle may affect hormonal changes early in
       <a href="/wiki/Phylogenesis" title="Phylogenesis">
        phylogenesis
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zimecki_45-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-zimecki-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The body weight of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honeybees" title="Honeybees">
        honeybees
       </a>
       peaks during
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Moon" title="New Moon">
        new Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zimecki_45-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-zimecki-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Midge" title="Midge">
        midge
       </a>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Clunio_marinus" title="Clunio marinus">
         Clunio marinus
        </a>
       </i>
       has a biological clock synchronized with the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CajochenAltanay-Ekici2013_38-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-CajochenAltanay-Ekici2013-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Spawning of coral
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Platygyra_lamellina" title="Platygyra lamellina">
         Platygyra lamellina
        </a>
       </i>
       occurs at night during the summer on a date determined by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_of_the_Moon" title="Phase of the Moon">
        phase of the Moon
       </a>
       ; in the
       <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">
        Red Sea
       </a>
       , this is the three- to five-day period around the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Moon" title="New Moon">
        new Moon
       </a>
       in July and the similar period in August.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Acropora" title="Acropora">
         Acropora
        </a>
       </i>
       coral time their simultaneous release of sperm and eggs to just one or two days a year, after sundown with a full moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Palolo_worm" title="Palolo worm">
        Palolo worm
       </a>
       that lives in the seas of Indonesia and Polynesia loose the terminal part of their bodies during a waning moon at a certain time of year. These parts float to the surface and release sperm and eggs. The terminal parts are gathered by people as a special food. The event would be predicted by the local priests, and the lunar calendar was set by the event.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because the Palolo adjust their spawning time between October and November, and because of inter-island differences in spawning times, there are factors other than the Moon that control the timing. Such factor may include seawater temperatures, tides, weather, or other biological signals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A relationship between the moon and the birth rate of cows was reported in a 2016 study.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_plants">
        In plants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In plants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The popular belief that the moon has an effect on plants is unsubstantiated. When witnessed, the effects have been indirectly attributed to "moon gardeners"' attentive care.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
        <a href="#cite_note-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Serious doubts have been raised
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
        <a href="#cite_note-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
       about the claim that a species of
       <a href="/wiki/Ephedra_(plant)" title="Ephedra (plant)">
        Ephedra
       </a>
       synchronizes its pollination peak to the full moon in July.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
        <a href="#cite_note-55">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_explanations">
        Proposed explanations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Proposed explanations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal">
        Tidal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Tidal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Believers in the lunar theory suggest several different mechanisms by which the behaviour of the Moon could influence the behaviour of human beings. A common suggestion is that, since the Moon affects large bodies of water such as the ocean (a phenomenon known as "
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal force
       </a>
       "), the Moon should be expected to have an analogous effect on human beings, whose bodies contain a great deal of water.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skepdic_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-skepdic-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-straightdope_56-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-straightdope-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, this is a misconception that fails to take into account differences in scale. The tidal force is in fact very weak and should be expected to exercise no more gravitational pull on the human body than a mosquito.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skepdic_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-skepdic-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Besides this, the "suggestion" failed to account for the dependence of
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        tides
       </a>
       from both the phase of the Moon and the
       <i>
        time of day
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Believers (
       <a href="/wiki/David_Tredinnick_(politician)" title="David Tredinnick (politician)">
        David Tredinnick
       </a>
       being a prominent example) often support their claims by noting that many police officers, teachers, and nurses have observed a lunar effect in the course of their work. To the extent that nurses and police officers do indeed claim to observe patterns, this is most likely to be explained in terms of
       <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_bias" title="Confirmation bias">
        confirmation bias
       </a>
       : People notice if something dramatic happens during a full Moon, but do not notice when nothing dramatic happens;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-straightdope_56-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-straightdope-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilovich1993_57-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gilovich1993-57">
         [57]
        </a>
       </sup>
       furthermore, dramatic occurrences that do not occur during full Moons are typically not counted as evidence against the belief.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skepdic_5-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-skepdic-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Believers are further bolstered in their belief through
       <a href="/wiki/Communal_reinforcement" title="Communal reinforcement">
        communal reinforcement
       </a>
       : The more people talk about the effect, the more people notice spurious relationships.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skepdic_5-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-skepdic-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary">
        Evolutionary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Evolutionary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Nocturnal carnivores are widely believed to have played an important role in human evolution, driving the need for nighttime shelter, the control of fire and our innate fear of darkness. We performed an extensive analysis of predatory behavior across the lunar cycle on the largest dataset of lion attacks ever assembled and found that African lions are as sensitive to moonlight when hunting humans as when hunting herbivores and that lions are most dangerous to humans when the Moon is faint or below the horizon.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         <cite class="citation" id="CITEREFC._PackerA._SwansonD._IkandaH._Kushnir2011">
          C. Packer; A. Swanson; D. Ikanda &amp; H. Kushnir (2011), "Fear of Darkness, the Full Moon and the Nocturnal Ecology of African Lions.",
          <i>
           PLoS ONE
          </i>
          ,
          <b>
           6
          </b>
          (7): e22285,
          <a href="/wiki/Bibcode" title="Bibcode">
           Bibcode
          </a>
          :
          <a class="external text" href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2011PLoSO...622285P" rel="nofollow">
           2011PLoSO...622285P
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="/wiki/Digital_object_identifier" title="Digital object identifier">
           doi
          </a>
          :
          <a class="external text" href="//doi.org/10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0022285" rel="nofollow">
           10.1371/journal.pone.0022285
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="/wiki/PubMed_Central" title="PubMed Central">
           PMC
          </a>
          <span class="plainlinks">
           <a class="external text" href="//www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3140494" rel="nofollow">
            3140494
           </a>
           <img alt="Freely accessible" data-file-height="813" data-file-width="512" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Lock-green.svg/9px-Lock-green.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Lock-green.svg/14px-Lock-green.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Lock-green.svg/18px-Lock-green.svg.png 2x" title="Freely accessible" width="9"/>
          </span>
          ,
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PubMed_Identifier" title="PubMed Identifier">
           PMID
          </a>
          <a class="external text" href="//www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21799812" rel="nofollow">
           21799812
          </a>
         </cite>
         <span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal&amp;rft.genre=article&amp;rft.jtitle=PLoS+ONE&amp;rft.atitle=Fear+of+Darkness%2C+the+Full+Moon+and+the+Nocturnal+Ecology+of+African+Lions.&amp;rft.volume=6&amp;rft.issue=7&amp;rft.pages=e22285&amp;rft.date=2011&amp;rft_id=%2F%2Fwww.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov%2Fpmc%2Farticles%2FPMC3140494&amp;rft_id=info%3Apmid%2F21799812&amp;rft_id=info%3Adoi%2F10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0022285&amp;rft_id=info%3Abibcode%2F2011PLoSO...622285P&amp;rft.au=C.+Packer&amp;rft.au=A.+Swanson&amp;rft.au=D.+Ikanda&amp;rft.au=H.+Kushnir&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3ALunar+effect">
          <span style="display:none;">
          </span>
         </span>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       For some 3–4 million years, bipedal hominins in the East African Rift valley were evolving in potential conflict and competition with fearsome carnivores including sabre-toothed cats equipped with excellent night-vision. Using the largest data set ever recorded – 1,000 lion attacks on humans across Tanzania between 1988 and 2009 – Craig Packer and his colleagues showed that there is a peak of attacks by lions upon humans during the evening dark hours following full Moon. According to Packer, this may help explain why so many myths and superstitions attribute fearsome dangers and nightmarish potencies to the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
        <a href="#cite_note-58">
         [58]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While not all archaeologists accept that lunar periodicity was ever relevant to human evolution, those favouring the idea include Curtis Marean, who heads excavations at the important Middle Stone Age site of
       <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Point" title="Pinnacle Point">
        Pinnacle Point
       </a>
       , South Africa. Marean argues that anatomically modern humans around 165,000 years ago – when inland regions of the continent were dry, arid and uninhabitable – became restricted to small populations clustered around coastal refugia, reliant on marine resources including shellfish whose safe harvesting at spring low tides presupposed careful tracking of lunar phase. Against this background, if Marean is right, humans who ignored or misread the Moon might frequently have been drowned.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
        <a href="#cite_note-59">
         [59]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        With gradual offshore platforms during spring low tides, substantial areas of the intertidal zone are revealed, and these are the most productive and safest shellfish collecting times... Foragers should schedule visits to coastal residential sites at times during the lunar month when spring tides are present and then move slightly inland during neaps to broaden the size of the exploitable terrestrial area.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         <cite class="citation">
          Marean, C. 2010. Pinnacle Point Cave 13B (Western Cape Province, South Africa) in context: The Cape Floral kingdom, shellfish, and modern human origins.
          <i>
           Journal of Human Evolution
          </i>
          <b>
           59
          </b>
          : 425e-443 .
         </cite>
         <span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook&amp;rft.genre=book&amp;rft.btitle=Marean%2C+C.+2010.+Pinnacle+Point+Cave+13B+%28Western+Cape+Province%2C+South+Africa%29+in+context%3A+The+Cape+Floral+kingdom%2C+shellfish%2C+and+modern+human+origins.+Journal+of+Human+Evolution+59%3A+425e-443+.&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3ALunar+effect">
          <span style="display:none;">
          </span>
         </span>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Positive_ions">
        Positive ions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Positive ions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A further suggestion is that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_ions" title="Positive ions">
        positive ions
       </a>
       increase in abundance during a full Moon and that this should be expected to influence human behavior. However, this is a pseudo-scientific claim. Not only is the increase in frequency extremely slight (much smaller than that caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">
        air conditioning
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">
        air pollution
       </a>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-straightdope_56-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-straightdope-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but ionic charge – positive or negative – has no effect on human behavior, and no physiological effect other than static electric shock.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
        <a href="#cite_note-60">
         [60]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Studies have shown that with regard to certain types of depression such as
       <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_affective_disorder" title="Seasonal affective disorder">
        seasonal affective disorder
       </a>
       (SAD),
       <a href="/wiki/Negative_air_ionization_therapy" title="Negative air ionization therapy">
        negative air ionization therapy
       </a>
       has no statistically significant benefits beyond placebo. Moreover, such treatments require ionization many orders of magnitude greater than any differences produced by the lunar cycle, and there are superior effects found by using similar treatments with bright light instead of ion generators.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
        <a href="#cite_note-61">
         [61]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
