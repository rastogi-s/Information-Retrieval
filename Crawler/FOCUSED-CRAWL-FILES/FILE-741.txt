      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         The Moon Is Blue
        </b>
       </i>
       is a 1953 American
       <a href="/wiki/Romantic_comedy_film" title="Romantic comedy film">
        romantic comedy film
       </a>
       produced and directed by
       <a href="/wiki/Otto_Preminger" title="Otto Preminger">
        Otto Preminger
       </a>
       and starring
       <a href="/wiki/William_Holden" title="William Holden">
        William Holden
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/David_Niven" title="David Niven">
        David Niven
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Maggie_McNamara" title="Maggie McNamara">
        Maggie McNamara
       </a>
       . Written by
       <a href="/wiki/F._Hugh_Herbert" title="F. Hugh Herbert">
        F. Hugh Herbert
       </a>
       and based on his 1951 play of the same title, the film is about a young woman who meets an architect on the observation deck of the
       <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">
        Empire State Building
       </a>
       and quickly turns his life upside down. Herbert's play had also been a huge success in Germany, and Preminger decided to film English- and German-language versions simultaneously, using the same sets but different casts. The German film version is
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Die_Jungfrau_auf_dem_Dach" title="Die Jungfrau auf dem Dach">
         Die Jungfrau auf dem Dach
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
        Plot
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_Is_Blue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Comedy_of_manners" title="Comedy of manners">
        comedy of manners
       </a>
       , the film centers on virtuous actress Patty O'Neill, who meets playboy architect Donald Gresham on the top of the Empire State Building and accepts his invitation to join him for drinks and dinner in his apartment. There she meets Donald's upstairs neighbors, his ex-fiancée Cynthia and her father, roguish David Slater.
      </p>
      <p>
       Both men are determined to bed the young woman, but they quickly discover Patty is more interested in engaging in spirited discussions about the pressing moral and sexual issues of the day than surrendering her virginity to either one of them. After resisting their amorous advances throughout the night, Patty leaves and returns to the Empire State Building, where she finds Donald who has missed her and worried all night about her. Donald declares his love for her and proposes marriage to her.
      </p>

        Johanna Matz
       </a>
       , the stars of the German adaptation, briefly appear in the English-language version as tourists. Krüger and Matz appear as the young couple waiting to use the coin-operated telescope at the top of the Empire State Building,
       <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">
        cameo roles
       </a>
       which Holden and McNamara played in the German version.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
        Production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_Is_Blue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Otto Preminger had directed the 1951
       <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">
        Broadway
       </a>
       production of F. Hugh Herbert's play with
       <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Bel_Geddes" title="Barbara Bel Geddes">
        Barbara Bel Geddes
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Donald_Cook_(actor)" title="Donald Cook (actor)">
        Donald Cook
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Barry_Nelson" title="Barry Nelson">
        Barry Nelson
       </a>
       in the lead roles. Its successful run of 924 performances prompted him to contract with
       <a href="/wiki/United_Artists" title="United Artists">
        United Artists
       </a>
       to finance and distribute a screen adaptation over which he would have complete control. He deferred his producer's and director's salaries in exchange for 75% of the film's profits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Preminger cast
       <a href="/wiki/David_Niven" title="David Niven">
        David Niven
       </a>
       over the objection of studio executives, who felt the actor's career was in decline. The director cast him in a West Coast production of the play to prepare him for the film.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Its_Double_p._142_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Its_Double_p._142-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Last of the leads to be cast was
       <a href="/wiki/Maggie_McNamara" title="Maggie McNamara">
        Maggie McNamara
       </a>
       , making her screen debut in a role she had played on stage in Chicago and briefly in New York.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Its_Double_p._142_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Its_Double_p._142-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Herbert's play had been a huge success in Germany, and Preminger decided to film English- and German-language versions simultaneously, using the same sets but different casts. The director estimated this method would increase the filming schedule by only eight to ten days and production costs by only 10 to 15 percent. The budget for both films was $373,445.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is estimated that deferred costs came to nearly $500,000 but in return United Artists gave the producers Herbert and Preminger 75% of the profits, 20% of which they gave to William Holden.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tino_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-tino-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On July 13, 1951, the
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Breen" title="Joseph Breen">
        Breen office
       </a>
       contacted Herbert and advised him his screenplay was in violation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Production_Code" title="Motion Picture Production Code">
        Motion Picture Production Code
       </a>
       because of its "light and gay treatment of the subject of illicit sex and seduction." On December 26, Preminger submitted a revised draft of the script which, due to numerous lines of dialogue exhibiting "an unacceptably light attitude towards seduction, illicit sex, chastity, and virginity," was rejected on January 2, 1952. Contrary to later legend, the words "virgin," "mistress," and "pregnant," all of which had been in the original play's dialogue, were not singled out as objectionable. On January 6, Preminger and Herbert advised the Breen office they disagreed with its decision and would film the screenplay without further changes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In a display of solidarity, United Artist heads
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_B._Krim" title="Arthur B. Krim">
        Arthur B. Krim
       </a>
       and Robert S. Benjamin amended their contract and deleted the clause requiring Preminger to deliver a film that would be granted a Production Code seal of approval. After ten days of rehearsals for each of his casts, Preminger began principal photography of both films on January 21, filming an English language scene and then its German equivalent in quick succession. Preminger later stated he much preferred
       <i>
        The Moon is Blue
       </i>
       to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Die_Jungfrau_auf_dem_Dach" title="Die Jungfrau auf dem Dach">
         Die Jungfrau auf dem Dach
        </a>
       </i>
       because he felt the psychology of the plot did not translate well. Filming was completed in twenty-four days and it previewed in
       <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">
        Pasadena
       </a>
       on April 8. Two days later, Breen notified Preminger the film would not be approved. Outraged at Breen's "unwarranted and unjustified attack" on "a harmless story," the director joined forces with UA executives to appeal the decision with the
       <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Association_of_America" title="Motion Picture Association of America">
        Motion Picture Association of America
       </a>
       board of directors, who ruled against them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       United Artists decided to release the film without the seal of approval, initially in major urban markets where they hoped its success would encourage exhibitors in rural areas to book the film. The film premiered for an "adults only" audience in one movie house in Chicago on June 22 and opened in one theater in San Francisco on June 25. One small mid-western town only showed the movie to men and women separately. On June 30,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">
         Variety
        </a>
       </i>
       reported three major nationwide theater chains were willing to exhibit the film, and it went into general release on July 8. (In its year-end report,
       <i>
        Variety
       </i>
       said the film had ranked #15 at the box office with a gross of $3.5 million.)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Kansas, Ohio, and Maryland banned the film, and Preminger and United Artists decided to bring suit in a Maryland court. On December 7, 1953, Judge Herman Moser reversed the State Censor Board. In his ruling, he called the film "a light comedy telling a tale of wide-eyed, brash, puppy-like innocence." Preminger and UA then appealed in Kansas, but the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Kansas" title="Supreme Court of Kansas">
        Supreme Court of Kansas
       </a>
       upheld the state board of review's decision to ban the film. Determined to win, the director and studio took their case to the
       <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">
        Supreme Court of the United States
       </a>
       , which overturned the finding of the Kansas Supreme Court on October 24, 1955.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The success of the film was instrumental in weakening the influence of the Production Code. On June 27, 1961, the PCA granted both
       <i>
        The Moon is Blue
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Man_with_the_Golden_Arm" title="The Man with the Golden Arm">
         The Man with the Golden Arm
        </a>
       </i>
       , Preminger's similarly controversial 1955 release, the seals of approval they initially withheld.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In later years, the film was the focus of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/M*A*S*H_(TV_series)" title="M*A*S*H (TV series)">
         M*A*S*H
        </a>
       </i>
       episode, "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Moon_is_Not_Blue" title="The Moon is Not Blue">
        The Moon is Not Blue
       </a>
       ", in which the characters, having heard about the controversy surrounding it, attempt to get a copy shipped to their mobile hospital in Korea. The film actually was released in the closing days of the
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">
        Korean War
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Theatres in many small towns in the United States would not show the film to men and women together, restricting audiences to men only or to women only.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">
        Critical reception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_Is_Blue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Critical reception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Bosley_Crowther" title="Bosley Crowther">
        Bosley Crowther
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       observed it "is not outstanding, either as a romance or as film. The wit of Mr. Herbert . . . is turned almost wholly on his freeness with the startling idea or phrase, as glibly tossed off (for the most part) by a young lady who appears a wide-eyed child. And Otto Preminger's lifting of the play from the stage to the screen is much too rigidly respectful of its conversational form. As a consequence, the movement is restricted and the talk is exceedingly long. At times, it gets awfully tedious, considering its limited range."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
         Time
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine found the film to be pleasant.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time.com_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-time.com-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic_Legion_of_Decency" title="Catholic Legion of Decency">
        Catholic Legion of Decency
       </a>
       gave the movie a "C", "Condemned" rating, despite giving the original play a milder "B", "Unobjectionable for adults" rating.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time.com_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-time.com-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In his review of the DVD release of the film, Tim Purtell of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">
         Entertainment Weekly
        </a>
       </i>
       called the film a "trifle" that "seems overly talky and slight".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1953 this movie was banned in Jersey City, New Jersey because it portrayed New Jersey as "indecent and obscene".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_nominations">

       but lost to

       </a>

       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Roman_Holiday" title="Roman Holiday">

        </a>

       .

       </a>
       was nominated for the
       <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Film_Editing" title="Academy Award for Best Film Editing">
        Academy Award for Best Film Editing
       </a>
       but lost to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_A._Lyon" title="William A. Lyon">

       </a>
       for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/From_Here_to_Eternity" title="From Here to Eternity">
         From Here to Eternity
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Burke_Gilbert" title="Herschel Burke Gilbert">
        Herschel Burke Gilbert
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Fine" title="Sylvia Fine">
        Sylvia Fine
       </a>
       were nominated for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Song" title="Academy Award for Best Song">
        Academy Award for Best Song
       </a>
       for the title tune but lost to
       <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Fain" title="Sammy Fain">
        Sammy Fain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Francis_Webster" title="Paul Francis Webster">
        Paul Francis Webster
       </a>
       for "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secret_Love_(1953_song)" title="Secret Love (1953 song)">
        Secret Love
       </a>
       " from
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calamity_Jane_(1953_film)" title="Calamity Jane (1953 film)">
         Calamity Jane
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       F. Hugh Herbert was nominated for the
       <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America_Award" title="Writers Guild of America Award">
        Writers Guild of America Award
       </a>
       for Best Written Comedy but lost to
       <a href="/wiki/Ian_McLellan_Hunter" title="Ian McLellan Hunter">
        Ian McLellan Hunter
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dalton_Trumbo" title="Dalton Trumbo">
        Dalton Trumbo
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/John_Dighton" title="John Dighton">
        John Dighton
       </a>
       for
       <i>
        Roman Holiday
       </i>
       .
