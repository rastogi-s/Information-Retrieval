      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Larissa_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       It was first discovered by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harold_J._Reitsema" title="Harold J. Reitsema">
        Harold J. Reitsema
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_B._Hubbard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William B. Hubbard (page does not exist)">
        William B. Hubbard
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Larry_A._Lebofsky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Larry A. Lebofsky (page does not exist)">
        Larry A. Lebofsky
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/David_J._Tholen" title="David J. Tholen">
        David J. Tholen
       </a>
       , based on fortuitous ground-based stellar occultation observations
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reitsema1982_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Reitsema1982-9">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       on May 24, 1981, given the temporary designation
       <b>
        S/1981 N 1
       </b>
       and announced on May 29, 1981.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_3608_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_3608-10">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The moon was recovered and confirmed to be the only object in its orbit during the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       flyby in 1989
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithSoderblom1989_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SmithSoderblom1989-11">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       after which it received the additional designation
       <b>
        S/1989 N 2
       </b>
       on August 2, 1989.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_4824_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_4824-12">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The announcement by
       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_P._Synnott" title="Stephen P. Synnott">
        Stephen P. Synnott
       </a>
       spoke of “10 frames taken over 5 days”, which gives a recovery date sometime before July 28. The name was given on September 16, 1991.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_5347_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_5347-13">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Larissa_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Larissa.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="198" data-file-width="450" height="97" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Larissa.jpg/220px-Larissa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Larissa.jpg/330px-Larissa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Larissa.jpg/440px-Larissa.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>

         Front and back of Larissa
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Larissa_map.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="596" data-file-width="320" height="410" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Larissa_map.gif/220px-Larissa_map.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a8/Larissa_map.gif 1.5x" width="220"/>

      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Simulated_view_of_Larissa.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1005" data-file-width="1280" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Simulated_view_of_Larissa.jpg/220px-Simulated_view_of_Larissa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Simulated_view_of_Larissa.jpg/330px-Simulated_view_of_Larissa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Simulated_view_of_Larissa.jpg/440px-Simulated_view_of_Larissa.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Simulated_view_of_Larissa.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A
         <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">
          simulated
         </a>
         view of Larissa orbiting
         <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
          Neptune
         </a>
         . The surface details are fictional.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The fourth-largest satellite of Neptune, Larissa is irregular (non-spherical) in shape and appears to be heavily
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        cratered
       </a>
       , with no sign of any geological modification. Little else is known about it. It is likely that Larissa, like the other satellites inward of
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       , is a
       <a href="/wiki/Rubble_pile" title="Rubble pile">
        rubble pile
       </a>
       re-accreted from fragments of Neptune's original satellites, which were smashed up by perturbations from Triton soon after that moon's capture into a very eccentric initial orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BanfieldMurray1992_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BanfieldMurray1992-14">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Larissa's orbit is circular but not perfect and lies below Neptune's
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous orbit
       </a>
       radius, so it is slowly spiralling inward due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration#Tidal_deceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">
        tidal deceleration
       </a>
       and may eventually impact Neptune's atmosphere, or break up into a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">
        planetary ring
       </a>
       upon passing its
       <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">
        Roche limit
       </a>
       due to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_forces" title="Tidal forces">
        tidal stretching
       </a>
       , similar to how
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       will crash into
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       or break into a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">
        planetary ring
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

         ]
        </span>

       Larissa has only been visited by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       . The probe was able to get photographs of Larissa.
      </p>
