      <p>
       <b>
        Tarvos
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/ɑːr/: 'ar' in 'far'">
            ɑːr
           </span>
           <span title="'v' in 'vie'">
            v
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          TAR
         </span>
         -vəs
        </i>
       </a>
       ), or
       <b>
        Saturn XXI
       </b>
       , is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/John_J._Kavelaars" title="John J. Kavelaars">
        John J. Kavelaars
       </a>
       et al. on September 23, 2000, and given the temporary designation
       <b>
        S/2000 S 4
       </b>
       . The name, given in August 2003, is after
       <a href="/wiki/Tarvos_Trigaranus" title="Tarvos Trigaranus">
        Tarvos
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">
        deity
       </a>
       depicted as a
       <a href="/wiki/Bull" title="Bull">
        bull
       </a>
       god carrying three
       <a href="/wiki/Crane_(bird)" title="Crane (bird)">
        cranes
       </a>
       alongside its back from
       <a href="/wiki/Gaulish_language" title="Gaulish language">
        Gaulish
       </a>
       mythology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Tarvos orbits Saturn at an average distance of 18 million km in 926 days and is about 15
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Km" title="Km">
        km
       </a>
       in diameter (assuming an albedo of 0.04). It has the most
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentric
       </a>
       orbit around Saturn.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-JPL-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is a member of the
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn%27s_Gallic_group_of_satellites" title="Saturn's Gallic group of satellites">
        Gallic group
       </a>
       of irregular satellites.
      </p>
      <p>
       With a similar orbit and displaying a similar light-red colour, Tarvos is thought to have its origin in the break-up of a common progenitor
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2003_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2003-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gladman2001_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gladman2001-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or to be a fragment of
       <a href="/wiki/Albiorix_(moon)" title="Albiorix (moon)">
        Albiorix
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2006_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2006-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
