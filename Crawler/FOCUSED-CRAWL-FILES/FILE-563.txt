      <p>
       <b>
        Third-party evidence for Apollo Moon landings
       </b>
       is evidence, or analysis of evidence, about
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">
        Moon landings
       </a>
       that does not come from either
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       or the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._government" title="U.S. government">
        U.S. government
       </a>
       (the first party), or the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing_conspiracy_theories" title="Moon landing conspiracy theories">
        Apollo Moon landing hoax theorists
       </a>
       (the second party). This evidence serves as independent confirmation of NASA's account of the Moon landings.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_evidence">
        Independent evidence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-party_evidence_for_Apollo_Moon_landings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Independent evidence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In this section are only those observations that are completely independent of NASA—no NASA facilities were used, and there was no NASA funding. Each of the countries mentioned in this section (
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet Union
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
        Japan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       ) has its own
       <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">
        space program
       </a>
       , builds its own
       <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">
        space probes
       </a>
       which are launched on their own
       <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">
        launch vehicles
       </a>
       , and has its own
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Network_(disambiguation)" title="Deep Space Network (disambiguation)">
        deep space communication network
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SELENE_photographs">
        SELENE photographs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-party_evidence_for_Apollo_Moon_landings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: SELENE photographs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2008, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency">
        Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency
       </a>
       (JAXA)
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
        SELENE
       </a>
       lunar probe obtained several
       <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">
        photographs
       </a>
       showing evidence of Moon landings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On the left are two photos taken on the lunar surface by the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        astronauts
       </a>
       August 2, 1971 during EVA 3 at station 9A near Hadley Rille. On the right is a 2008 reconstruction from images taken by the SELENE terrain camera and
       <a href="/wiki/3D_computer_graphics" title="3D computer graphics">
        3D
       </a>
       projected to the same vantage point as the surface photos. The terrain is a close match within the SELENE camera resolution of 10 metres.
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-party_evidence_for_Apollo_Moon_landings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Apollo 13">

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chabot_Observatory" title="Chabot Observatory">
        Chabot Observatory
       </a>
       calendar records an application of optical tracking during the final phases of Apollo 13, on April 17, 1970:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Rachel, Chabot Observatory's 20-inch refracting telescope, helps bring Apollo 13 and its crew home. One last burn of the lunar lander engines was needed before the crippled spacecraft's re-entry into the Earth's atmosphere. In order to compute that last burn, NASA needed a precise position of the spacecraft, obtainable only by telescopic observation. All the observatories that could have done this were clouded over, except Oakland's Chabot Observatory, where members of the Eastbay Astronomical Society had been tracking the Moon flights. EAS members received an urgent call from NASA Ames Research Station, which had ties with Chabot's educational program since the 60's, and they put the Observatory's historic 20-inch refractor to work. They were able to send the needed data to Ames, and the Apollo crew was able to make the needed correction and to return safely to Earth on this date in 1970.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astr.ua.edu_6-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-astr.ua.edu-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Existence_and_age_of_Moon_rocks">
        Existence and age of Moon rocks
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-party_evidence_for_Apollo_Moon_landings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Existence and age of Moon rocks">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A total of 382 kilograms (842 lb) of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_rocks" title="Moon rocks">
        Moon rocks
       </a>
       and dust were collected during the Apollos 11, 12, 14, 15, 16 and 17 missions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Papike98_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Papike98-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some 10 kg (22 lb) of the Moon rocks have been used in hundreds of experiments performed by both NASA researchers and planetary scientists at research institutions unaffiliated with NASA. These experiments have confirmed the age and origin of the rocks as lunar, and were used to identify lunar meteorites collected later from
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The oldest Moon rocks are up to 4.5 billion years old,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Papike98_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Papike98-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       making them 200 million years older than the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oldest_rock" title="Oldest rock">
        oldest Earth rocks
       </a>
       , which are from the
       <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">
        Hadean
       </a>
       eon and dated 3.8 to 4.3 billion years ago. The rocks returned by Apollo are very close in composition to the samples returned by the independent Soviet
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">
        Luna programme
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A rock brought back by Apollo 17 was accurately dated to be 4.417 billion years old, with a
       <a href="/wiki/Margin_of_error" title="Margin of error">
        margin of error
       </a>
       of plus or minus 6 million years. The test was done by a group of researchers headed by Alexander Nemchin at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curtin_University_of_Technology" title="Curtin University of Technology">
        Curtin University of Technology
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Bentley,_Western_Australia" title="Bentley, Western Australia">
        Bentley, Australia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">

      <p>
       Quoting from James Hansen's biography of
       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/First_Man:_The_Life_of_Neil_A._Armstrong" title="First Man: The Life of Neil A. Armstrong">
         First Man: The Life of Neil A. Armstrong
        </a>
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        "For those few misguided souls who still cling to the belief that the Moon landings never happened, examination of the results of five decades of
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Laser_Ranging_experiment" title="Lunar Laser Ranging experiment">
         LRRR experiments
        </a>
        should evidence how delusional their rejection of the Moon landing really is."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
         <a href="#cite_note-35">
          [35]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The NASA-independent
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Azur_Observatory" title="Côte d'Azur Observatory">
        <i>
         Observatoire de la Côte d'Azur
        </i>
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/McDonald_Observatory" title="McDonald Observatory">
        McDonald
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Apache_Point_Observatory_Lunar_Laser-ranging_Operation" title="Apache Point Observatory Lunar Laser-ranging Operation">
        Apache Point
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Haleakala_Observatory" title="Haleakala Observatory">
        Haleakalā
       </a>
       observatories regularly use the Apollo LRRR.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lick Observatory attempted to detect from Apollo 11's retroreflector while Armstrong and Aldrin were still on the Moon but did not succeed until August 1, 1969.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Apollo 14 astronauts deployed a retroreflector on February 5, 1971, and McDonald Observatory detected it the same day. The Apollo 15 retroreflector was deployed on July 31, 1971, and was detected by McDonald Observatory within a few days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The image on the left shows what is considered some of the most unambiguous evidence. This experiment repeatedly fires a laser at the Moon, at the spots where the Apollo landings were reported. The dots show when
       <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
        photons
       </a>
       are received from the Moon. The dark line shows that a large number come back at a specific time, and hence were reflected by something quite small (well under a metre in size). Photons reflected from the surface come back over a much broader range of times (the whole vertical range of the plot corresponds to only 30 metres or so in range). The concentration of photons at a specific time appears when the laser is aimed at the Apollos 11, 14 or 15 landing sites; otherwise the expected featureless distribution is observed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Apollo reflectors are still in use.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lunar-geophysics_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lunar-geophysics-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Strictly speaking, although the reflectors are strong evidence that human-manufactured artifacts currently exist on the Moon, and their locations are consistent with NASA's claims, they do not prove
       <i>
        humans
       </i>
       have visited the Moon. Smaller retroreflectors were carried by the unmanned landers
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_1" title="Lunokhod 1">
        Lunokhod 1
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_2" title="Lunokhod 2">
        Lunokhod 2
       </a>
       . The Lunokhod 2 reflector has been in use since 1973.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lunar-geophysics_40-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-lunar-geophysics-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The location of Lunokhod 1 was unknown for nearly 40 years but it was rediscovered in 2010 in photographs by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       (LRO) and its retroreflector is now in use. Both the United States and the USSR had the capability to soft-land objects on the surface of the Moon for several years before that. The USSR successfully landed its first unmanned probe (
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_9" title="Luna 9">
        Luna 9
       </a>
       ) on the Moon in February 1966, and the United States followed with
       <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_1" title="Surveyor 1">
        Surveyor 1
       </a>
       in June 1966, but no unmanned landers carried retroreflectors before Lunokhod 1 in November 1970.
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="New_lunar_missions">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-party_evidence_for_Apollo_Moon_landings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: New lunar missions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lroc_apollo11_landing_site_20091109_zoom.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="309" data-file-width="540" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Lroc_apollo11_landing_site_20091109_zoom.jpg/220px-Lroc_apollo11_landing_site_20091109_zoom.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Lroc_apollo11_landing_site_20091109_zoom.jpg/330px-Lroc_apollo11_landing_site_20091109_zoom.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Lroc_apollo11_landing_site_20091109_zoom.jpg/440px-Lroc_apollo11_landing_site_20091109_zoom.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lroc_apollo11_landing_site_20091109_zoom.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Apollo 11 landing site photographed by
         <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
          LRO
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Post-Apollo lunar exploration missions have located and imaged artifacts of the Apollo program remaining on the Moon's surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       Images taken by the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter mission beginning in July 2009 show the six Apollo
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Module" title="Lunar Module">
        Lunar Module
       </a>
       descent stages,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiment_Package" title="Apollo Lunar Surface Experiment Package">
        Apollo Lunar Surface Experiment Package
       </a>
       (ALSEP) science experiments, astronaut footpaths, and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_rover" title="Lunar rover">
        lunar rover
       </a>
       tire tracks. These images are the most effective proof to date to rebut the "landing hoax" theories.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LRO1_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LRO1-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astronomy-magazine_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-astronomy-magazine-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although this probe was indeed launched by NASA, the camera and the interpretation of the images are under the control of an academic group — the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        LROC Science Operations Center
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Arizona_State_University" title="Arizona State University">
        Arizona State University
       </a>
       , along with many other academic groups.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At least some of these groups, such as
       <a href="/wiki/German_Aerospace_Center" title="German Aerospace Center">
        German Aerospace Center
       </a>
       , Berlin, are not located in the USA, and are not funded by the US government.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After the images shown here were taken, the LRO mission moved into a lower orbit for higher resolution camera work. All of the sites have since been re-imaged at higher resolution.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">

        </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">

        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_missions_tracked_by_non-NASA_personnel">
        Apollo missions tracked by non-NASA personnel
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-party_evidence_for_Apollo_Moon_landings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Apollo missions tracked by non-NASA personnel">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       This section contains reports of the lunar missions from facilities that had significant numbers of non-NASA employees. This includes facilities such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Network" title="Deep Space Network">
        Deep Space Network
       </a>
       , which employed (and still employs) many local citizens in
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       and Australia, and facilities such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Parkes_Observatory" title="Parkes Observatory">
        Parkes Observatory
       </a>
       , which were hired by NASA for specific tasks, but staffed by non-NASA personnel.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observers_of_all_missions_2">
        Observers of all missions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-party_evidence_for_Apollo_Moon_landings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Observers of all missions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Manned_Space_Flight_Network" title="Manned Space Flight Network">
        Manned Space Flight Network
       </a>
       (MSFN) was a worldwide network of stations that tracked the
       <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">
        Mercury
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">
        Gemini
       </a>
       , Apollo and
       <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">
        Skylab
       </a>
       missions. Most MSFN stations were only needed during the launch, Earth orbit and landing phases of the lunar missions, but three "deep space" sites with larger antennas provided continuous coverage during the trans-lunar, trans-Earth and lunar mission phases. Today, these three sites form the NASA
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Network" title="Deep Space Network">
        Deep Space Network
       </a>
       : the
       <a href="/wiki/Goldstone_Deep_Space_Communications_Complex" title="Goldstone Deep Space Communications Complex">
        Goldstone Deep Space Communications Complex
       </a>
       near Goldstone, California; the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madrid_Deep_Space_Communication_Complex" title="Madrid Deep Space Communication Complex">
        Madrid Deep Space Communication Complex
       </a>
       near
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       , Spain; and the
       <a href="/wiki/Canberra_Deep_Space_Communication_Complex" title="Canberra Deep Space Communication Complex">
        Canberra Deep Space Communication Complex
       </a>
       , adjacent to the
       <a href="/wiki/Tidbinbilla_Nature_Reserve" title="Tidbinbilla Nature Reserve">
        Tidbinbilla Nature Reserve
       </a>
       , near
       <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">
        Canberra
       </a>
       , Australia.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although most MSFN stations were NASA-owned, they employed many local citizens. NASA also contracted the
       <a href="/wiki/Parkes_Observatory" title="Parkes Observatory">
        Parkes Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">
        New South Wales
       </a>
       , Australia, to supplement the three deep space sites, most famously during the Apollo 11 EVA as documented by radio astronomer John Sarkissian
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and portrayed (humorously and not quite accurately) in the movie
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Dish" title="The Dish">
         The Dish
        </a>
       </i>
       . The Parkes Observatory is not NASA-owned; it is, and always has been, owned and operated by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Scientific_and_Industrial_Research_Organisation" title="Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation">
        Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation
       </a>
       (CSIRO), a research agency of the
       <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Australia" title="Government of Australia">
        Australian government
       </a>
       . It would have been relatively easy for NASA to avoid using the Parkes Observatory to receive the Apollo 11 EVA television signals by scheduling the EVA at an earlier time when the Goldstone station could provide complete coverage.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_11_2">
        Apollo 11
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

       </a>
      </div>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The Madrid Apollo Station, now part of the Deep Space Network, built in
        <a href="/wiki/Fresnedillas_de_la_Oliva" title="Fresnedillas de la Oliva">
         Fresnedillas
        </a>
        , near Madrid, Spain, tracked Apollo 11.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
         <a href="#cite_note-52">
          [52]
         </a>
        </sup>
        A large majority of the people working at this station were not employees of NASA, but of Spain's
        <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Nacional_de_T%C3%A9cnica_Aeroespacial" title="Instituto Nacional de Técnica Aeroespacial">
         Instituto Nacional de Técnica Aeroespacial
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
         <a href="#cite_note-53">
          [53]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_12_2">
        Apollo 12
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        Apollo 12
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Surveyor3camera.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1756" data-file-width="1148" height="459" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Surveyor3camera.jpg/300px-Surveyor3camera.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Surveyor3camera.jpg/450px-Surveyor3camera.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Surveyor3camera.jpg/600px-Surveyor3camera.jpg 2x" width="300"/>

       <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_3" title="Surveyor 3">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
        <a href="#cite_note-55">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plans">
        Plans
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Third-party_evidence_for_Apollo_Moon_landings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Plans">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As new research facilities such as orbiters and telescopes are built, the question arises whether they can see Apollo artifacts on the Moon.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In 2002, astronomers suggested using the
        <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">
         Very Large Telescope
        </a>
        to search for the landing sites.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
         <a href="#cite_note-56">
          [56]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
