      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naiad_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Naiad was discovered sometime before mid-September 1989 from the images taken by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">
        probe
       </a>
       . The last moon to be discovered during the flyby, it was designated
       <b>
        S/1989 N 6
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_4867_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_4867-8">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The discovery was announced on September 29, 1989, in the IAU Circular No. 4867, but the text only talks of "25 frames taken over 11 days", giving a discovery date of sometime before September 18. The name was given on 16 September 1991.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_5347_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_5347-9">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naiad_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Simulated_view_of_Naiad.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="845" data-file-width="1152" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Simulated_view_of_Naiad.jpg/210px-Simulated_view_of_Naiad.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Simulated_view_of_Naiad.jpg/315px-Simulated_view_of_Naiad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Simulated_view_of_Naiad.jpg/420px-Simulated_view_of_Naiad.jpg 2x" width="210"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Simulated_view_of_Naiad.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A
         <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">
          simulated
         </a>
         view of Naiad orbiting Neptune with the
         <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
          Sun
         </a>
         in the distance.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Naiad is irregularly shaped and probably has not been modified by any internal geological processes since its formation. It is likely that it is a rubble pile re-accreted from fragments of Neptune's original satellites, which were smashed up by perturbations from
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       soon after that moon's capture into a very eccentric initial orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BanfieldMurray1992_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BanfieldMurray1992-10">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_characteristics">
        Orbital characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naiad_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbital characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Naiad orbits about 23,500 km above Neptune's cloud tops. Since this is below the
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous orbit
       </a>
       radius, its orbit is slowly decaying due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration#Tidal_deceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">
        tidal deceleration
       </a>
       and it may eventually impact Neptune's atmosphere, or break up into a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">
        planetary ring
       </a>
       upon passing its
       <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">
        Roche limit
       </a>
       due to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_forces" title="Tidal forces">
        tidal stretching
       </a>
       . Naiad orbits Neptune well within its fluid Roche limit, and its density is expected to be low enough that it may be very close to its actual Roche limit already.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Naiad_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Since the
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       flyby, the Neptune system has been extensively studied from ground-based
       <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">
        observatories
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       as well. In 2002–03 the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_telescopes" title="Keck telescopes">
        Keck telescope
       </a>
       observed the system using
       <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">
        adaptive optics
       </a>
       and detected easily the largest four inner satellites.
       <a href="/wiki/Thalassa_(moon)" title="Thalassa (moon)">
        Thalassa
       </a>
       was found with some image processing, but Naiad was not located.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis2004May-AAS_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchis2004May-AAS-11">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hubble has the ability to detect all the known satellites and possible new satellites even dimmer than those found by
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       . On October 8, 2013 the SETI Institute announced that Naiad had been located in archived Hubble imagery from 2004.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The suspicion that the loss of positioning was due to considerable errors in Naiad's
       <a href="/wiki/Ephemeris" title="Ephemeris">
        ephemeris
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2005Aug-AAS_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Showalter2005Aug-AAS-13">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       proved correct as Naiad was ultimately located 80 degrees from its expected position.
      </p>
