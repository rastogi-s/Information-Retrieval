      <p>
       <b>
        Dia
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">
            aɪ
           </span>
           <span title="/./: syllable break">
            .
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ), also known as
       <b>
        Jupiter LIII
       </b>
       , is the second-outermost known
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Irregular_moon" title="Irregular moon">
        irregular satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       . Provisionally known as
       <b>
        S/2000 J 11
       </b>
       , it received its name on March 7, 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBET4075_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CBET4075-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Dia_(mythology)" title="Dia (mythology)">
        Dia
       </a>
       , daughter of Deioneus (or Eioneus), wife of Ixion. According to Homer, she was seduced by Zeus in stallion form; Pirithous was the issue.
      </p>
      <p>
       The satellite is the only known small body in the
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_group" title="Himalia group">
        Himalia group
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardJewitt2003_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardJewitt2003-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dia is thought to be about 4 kilometres in diameter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardJewittPorco2004_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardJewittPorco2004-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It orbits Jupiter at an average distance of 12 million km in 274 days, at an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       of 28° (to Jupiter's equator), and with an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of 0.21.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEC2012-R22_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPEC2012-R22-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observational_history">
        Observational history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dia_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observational history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Dia was discovered by a team of astronomers from the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">
        University of Hawaii
       </a>
       led by
       <a href="/wiki/Scott_S._Sheppard" title="Scott S. Sheppard">
        Scott S. Sheppard
       </a>
       in 2000 with an observation arc of 26 days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Initial observations were not followed up, and Dia was not observed for more than a decade after 2000. This apparent disappearance led some astronomers to consider the moon lost.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL-SSD_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-JPL-SSD-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One theory was that it had crashed into
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_(moon)" title="Himalia (moon)">
        Himalia
       </a>
       , creating a faint ring around Jupiter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, it was finally recovered in observations made in 2010 and 2011.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEC2012-R22_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPEC2012-R22-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
