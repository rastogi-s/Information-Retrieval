   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Atmosphere of the Moon
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

         At sunrise and sunset many Apollo crews saw glows and light rays.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
          <a href="#cite_note-1">
           [1]
          </a>
         </sup>
         This
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
          Apollo 17
         </a>
         sketch depicts the mysterious twilight rays.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        atmosphere of the Moon
       </b>
       is a very scant presence of gases surrounding the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . For most practical purposes, the Moon is considered to be surrounded by
       <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">
        vacuum
       </a>
       . The elevated presence of atomic and molecular particles in its vicinity compared to
       <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_medium" title="Interplanetary medium">
        interplanetary medium
       </a>
       , referred to as "lunar atmosphere" for scientific objectives, is negligible in comparison with the gaseous envelopes surrounding
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and most
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       . The pressure of this small mass is around 3
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        −15
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">
        atm
       </a>
       (0.3
       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">
        nPa
       </a>
       ), varying throughout the day, and in total weighs less than 10 metric tonnes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon-faq_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon-faq-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Otherwise, the Moon is considered not to have an atmosphere because it cannot absorb measurable quantities of radiation, does not appear layered or self-circulating, and requires constant replenishment due to the high rate at which its gases are lost to space.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">
        Sources
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One source of the lunar atmosphere is
       <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">
        outgassing
       </a>
       : the release of gases such as
       <a href="/wiki/Radon" title="Radon">
        radon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       resulting from
       <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">
        radioactive decay
       </a>
       within the
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       . Another important source is the bombardment of the lunar surface by
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">
        micrometeorites
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
        sunlight
       </a>
       , in a process known as
       <a href="/wiki/Sputtering" title="Sputtering">
        sputtering
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L06_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-L06-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Losses">
        Losses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Losses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Gases can either:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        be re-implanted into the
        <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
         regolith
        </a>
        as a result of the
        <a href="/wiki/Gravitation_of_the_Moon" title="Gravitation of the Moon">
         Moon's gravity
        </a>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        escape the Moon entirely if the particle is moving at or above the lunar
        <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">
         escape velocity
        </a>
        of 2.38 km/s (1.48 mi/s);
       </li>
       <li>
        be lost to space either by solar
        <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">
         radiation pressure
        </a>
        or, if the gases are
        <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">
         ionized
        </a>
        , by being swept away in the solar wind's
        <a href="/wiki/Stellar_magnetic_field" title="Stellar magnetic field">
         magnetic field
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
        Composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       What little atmosphere the Moon has consists of some unusual gases, including
       <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">
        sodium
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">
        potassium
       </a>
       , which are not found in the atmospheres of Earth,
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       . At sea level on Earth, each cubic centimeter of the atmosphere contains approximately 10
       <sup>
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         19
        </span>
       </sup>
       molecules; by comparison the lunar atmosphere contains fewer than 10
       <sup>
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         6
        </span>
       </sup>
       molecules in the same volume. On Earth, this is considered to be a very good vacuum. In fact, the density of the atmosphere at the Moon's surface is comparable to the density of the outermost fringes of Earth's atmosphere, where the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">
        International Space Station
       </a>
       orbits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The elements sodium and potassium have been detected in the Moon's atmosphere using Earth-based
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectroscopic methods
       </a>
       , whereas the isotopes
       <a href="/wiki/Radon" title="Radon">
        radon
       </a>
       -222 and
       <a href="/wiki/Polonium" title="Polonium">
        polonium
       </a>
       -210 have been inferred from data obtained by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">
        alpha particle
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
        spectrometer
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">
        Argon
       </a>
       -40,
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       -4,
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       and/or
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       (CH
       <span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;">
        <br/>
        4
       </span>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
        nitrogen
       </a>
       (N
       <span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;">
        <br/>
        2
       </span>
       ) and/or
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
        carbon monoxide
       </a>
       (CO), and
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
        carbon dioxide
       </a>
       (CO
       <span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;">
        <br/>
        2
       </span>
       )) were detected by in-situ detectors placed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       astronauts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern1999_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stern1999-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The average daytime abundances of the elements known to be present in the lunar atmosphere, in atoms per cubic centimeter, are as follows:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Argon: 20,000–100,000
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_8-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-nasa-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Helium: 5,000–30,000
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_8-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-nasa-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Neon: up to 20,000
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_8-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-nasa-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20150817_9-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20150817-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Sodium: 70
       </li>
       <li>
        Potassium: 17
       </li>
       <li>
        Hydrogen: fewer than 17
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This yields approximately 80,000 total atoms per cubic centimeter, marginally higher than the quantity posited to exist in the
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mercury" title="Atmosphere of Mercury">
        atmosphere of Mercury
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern1999_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stern1999-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While this greatly exceeds the density of the solar wind, which is usually on the order of just a few protons per cubic centimeter, it is virtually a vacuum in comparison with the atmosphere of the Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon may also have a tenuous "atmosphere" of
       <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_levitation" title="Electrostatic levitation">
        electrostatically-levitated
       </a>
       dust. See
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_dust#Moon_fountains_and_electrostatic_levitation" title="Moon dust">
        Moon dust
       </a>
       for more details.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Past_thicker_atmosphere">
        Past thicker atmosphere
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Past thicker atmosphere">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In October 2017,
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       scientists at the
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">
        Marshall Space Flight Center
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_and_Planetary_Institute" title="Lunar and Planetary Institute">
        Lunar and Planetary Institute
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">
        Houston
       </a>
       announced their finding, based on studies of Moon magma samples retrieved by the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       missions, that the Moon had once possessed a relatively thick atmosphere for a period of 70 million years between 3 and 4 billion years ago. This atmosphere, sourced from gases ejected from lunar volcanic eruptions, was twice the thickness of that of present-day
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       . The ancient lunar atmosphere was eventually stripped away by solar winds and dissipated into space.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
