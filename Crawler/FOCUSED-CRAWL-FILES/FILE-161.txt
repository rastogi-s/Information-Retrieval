       <b>
        Deimos
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_naming_conventions#Natural_satellites" title="Astronomical naming conventions">
        systematic designation
       </a>
       :
       <b>
        Mars II
       </b>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blunck_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blunck-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is the smaller and outer of the two
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Mars" title="Moons of Mars">
        natural satellites
       </a>
       of the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       , the other being
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       . Deimos has a mean radius of 6.2 km (3.9 mi)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Horizons-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and takes 30.3 hours
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons_1-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Horizons-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to orbit Mars. In Greek mythology,
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(deity)" title="Deimos (deity)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       is the twin brother of
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(mythology)" title="Phobos (mythology)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       and personified
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terror" title="Terror">
        terror
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Deimos is 23,460 km (14,580 mi) from Mars, much farther than Mars's other moon, Phobos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAS-2016_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SAS-2016-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deimos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Mars#Discovery" title="Moons of Mars">
        Moons of Mars § Discovery
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deimos-viking1.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1033" data-file-width="1015" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Deimos-viking1.jpg/220px-Deimos-viking1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Deimos-viking1.jpg/330px-Deimos-viking1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Deimos-viking1.jpg/440px-Deimos-viking1.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deimos-viking1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Deimos (
         <a href="/wiki/Viking_2" title="Viking 2">
          Viking 2
         </a>
         , 5 October 1977)
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-photo_8-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-photo-8">
           [8]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Deimos was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Asaph_Hall" title="Asaph Hall">
        Asaph Hall, III
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Observatory" title="United States Naval Observatory">
        United States Naval Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C" title="Washington, D.C">
        Washington, D.C
       </a>
       on 12 August 1877, at about 07:48
       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">
        UTC
       </a>
       (given in contemporary sources as "11 August 14:40"
       <a href="/wiki/Washington_Mean_Time" title="Washington Mean Time">
        Washington Mean Time
       </a>
       , using an astronomical convention of beginning a day at noon, so 12 hours must be added to get the actual local mean time).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morley1989_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morley1989-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hall also discovered Phobos on 18 August 1877, at about 09:14 GMT, after deliberately searching for Martian moons.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(deity)" title="Deimos (deity)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       , a figure representing
       <a href="/wiki/Fear" title="Fear">
        dread
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blunck_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blunck-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The names, at first spelled
       <i>
        Phobus
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Deimus
       </i>
       , were suggested by
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_George_Madan" title="Henry George Madan">
        Henry Madan
       </a>
       (1838–1901),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blunck_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blunck-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Science Master of
       <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">
        Eton
       </a>
       , from Book XV of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">
         Iliad
        </a>
       </i>
       , where
       <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">
        Ares
       </a>
       (the Roman god
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">
        Mars
       </a>
       ) summons Dread (
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(deity)" title="Deimos (deity)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       ) and Fear (
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(mythology)" title="Phobos (mythology)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deimos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deimos_Surface.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1068" data-file-width="986" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Deimos_Surface.png/125px-Deimos_Surface.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Deimos_Surface.png/188px-Deimos_Surface.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Deimos_Surface.png/250px-Deimos_Surface.png 2x" width="125"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deimos_Surface.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The surface of Deimos from 30 km (
         <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">
          Viking
         </a>
         , 1977)
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
          <a href="#cite_note-14">
           [14]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Deimos, like Mars's other moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       , has
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        spectra
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedos
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        densities
       </a>
       similar to those of a
       <a href="/wiki/C-type_asteroid" title="C-type asteroid">
        C-
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/D-type_asteroid" title="D-type asteroid">
        D-type asteroid
       </a>
       . Like most bodies of its size, Deimos is highly non-spherical with triaxial dimensions of 15 × 12.2 × 11 km,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sea_and_Sky_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sea_and_Sky-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       making it 0.56 times the size of Phobos. Deimos is composed of rock rich in carbonaceous material, much like C-type
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carbonaceous_chondrite" title="Carbonaceous chondrite">
        carbonaceous chondrite
       </a>
       meteorites. It is
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        cratered
       </a>
       , but the surface is noticeably smoother than that of Phobos, caused by the partial filling of craters with
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       . The regolith is highly
       <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">
        porous
       </a>
       and has a radar-estimated density of only 1.471 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michaelbusch_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Michaelbusch-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">
        Escape velocity
       </a>
       from Deimos is 5.6 m/s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent magnitude
       </a>
       of Deimos is 12.45.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPLSSD_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-JPLSSD-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </div>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_characteristics">
        Orbital characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deimos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbital characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orbits_of_Phobos_and_Deimos.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="120" data-file-width="160" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/db/Orbits_of_Phobos_and_Deimos.gif" width="160"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         Orbits of
         <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
          Phobos
         </a>
         and Deimos (to scale)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Deimos's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       is nearly circular and is close to Mars's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_plane" title="Equatorial plane">
        equatorial plane
       </a>
       . Deimos is possibly an asteroid that was perturbed by
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       into an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       that allowed it to be captured by Mars, though this hypothesis is still controversial and disputed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burns_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-burns-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Both Deimos and Phobos have very circular orbits which lie almost exactly in Mars's equatorial plane, and hence a capture origin requires a mechanism for circularizing the initially highly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentric_orbit" title="Eccentric orbit">
        eccentric orbit
       </a>
       , and adjusting its inclination into the equatorial plane, most likely by a combination of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">
        atmospheric drag
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cazenave_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cazenave-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it is not clear that sufficient time was available for this to have occurred for Deimos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burns_19-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-burns-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:245px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA17352-MarsMoons-PhobosPassesDeimos-RealTime.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="156" data-file-width="243" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2f/PIA17352-MarsMoons-PhobosPassesDeimos-RealTime.gif" width="243"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA17352-MarsMoons-PhobosPassesDeimos-RealTime.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Curiosity_(rover)" title="Curiosity (rover)">
           Curiosity's
          </a>
         </i>
         view of the
         <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Mars" title="Moons of Mars">
          Mars moons
         </a>
         :
         <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
          Phobos
         </a>
         passing in front of Deimos – in
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Real_time_(media)" title="Real time (media)">
           real-time
          </a>
         </i>
         (video-gif, 1 August 2013)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       As seen from Mars, Deimos would have an
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        angular diameter
       </a>
       of no more than 2.5 minutes (sixty minutes make one degree), one twelfth of the width of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       as seen from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , and would therefore appear almost star-like to the naked eye.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richardson_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-richardson-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At its brightest ("full moon") it would be about as bright as
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       is from Earth; at the first- or third-quarter phase it would be about as bright as
       <a href="/wiki/Vega" title="Vega">
        Vega
       </a>
       . With a small
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       , a Martian observer could see Deimos's phases, which take 1.2648 days (Deimos's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_period" title="Synodic period">
        synodic period
       </a>
       ) to run their course.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richardson_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-richardson-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Unlike Phobos, which orbits so fast that it rises in the west and sets in the east, Deimos rises in the east and sets in the west. The Sun-synodic orbital period of Deimos of about 30.4 hours exceeds the Martian solar day ("
       <a href="/wiki/Timekeeping_on_Mars" title="Timekeeping on Mars">
        sol
       </a>
       ") of about 24.7 hours by such a small amount that 2.7 days elapse between its rising and setting for an equatorial observer.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because Deimos's orbit is relatively close to Mars and has only a very small inclination to Mars's equator, it cannot be seen from Martian latitudes greater than 82.7°.
      </p>
      <p>
       Deimos's orbit is slowly getting larger, because it is far enough away from Mars and because of
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">
        tidal acceleration
       </a>
       . It is expected to eventually escape Mars's gravity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_transits">
        Solar transits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deimos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solar transits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Transit_of_Deimos_from_Mars.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="360" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Transit_of_Deimos_from_Mars.jpg/220px-Transit_of_Deimos_from_Mars.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Transit_of_Deimos_from_Mars.jpg/330px-Transit_of_Deimos_from_Mars.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/62/Transit_of_Deimos_from_Mars.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Transit_of_Deimos_from_Mars.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Deimos
         <a href="/wiki/Transit_(astronomy)" title="Transit (astronomy)">
          transits
         </a>
         the
         <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
          Sun
         </a>
         – as viewed by the Mars rover
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opportunity_rover" title="Opportunity rover">
          <i>
           Opportunity
          </i>
         </a>
         (4 March 2004)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Deimos_from_Mars" title="Transit of Deimos from Mars">
        Transit of Deimos from Mars
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Deimos regularly
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_(astronomy)" title="Transit (astronomy)">
        passes in front of the Sun
       </a>
       as seen from Mars. It is too small to cause a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_eclipse" title="Total eclipse">
        total eclipse
       </a>
       , appearing only as a small black dot moving across the Sun. Its angular diameter is only about 2.5 times the angular diameter of Venus during a
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">
        transit of Venus
       </a>
       from Earth. On 4 March 2004 a transit of Deimos was photographed by Mars rover
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_(rover)" title="Opportunity (rover)">
         Opportunity
        </a>
       </i>
       , and on 13 March 2004 a transit was photographed by Mars rover
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Spirit_(rover)" title="Spirit (rover)">
         Spirit
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deimos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NASA-Deimos-MarsMoon-19951003.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="397" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/NASA-Deimos-MarsMoon-19951003.jpg/200px-NASA-Deimos-MarsMoon-19951003.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/NASA-Deimos-MarsMoon-19951003.jpg/300px-NASA-Deimos-MarsMoon-19951003.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5f/NASA-Deimos-MarsMoon-19951003.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NASA-Deimos-MarsMoon-19951003.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Deimos imaged by one of the
         <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">
          Viking orbiters
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The origin of Mars's moons is unknown and the hypotheses are controversial.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burns_19-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-burns-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The main hypotheses are that they formed either by
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_capture" title="Asteroid capture">
        capture
       </a>
       or by
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">
        accretion
       </a>
       . Because of the similarity to the composition of C- or D-type asteroids, one hypothesis is that the moons may be objects captured into Martian orbit from the
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">
        asteroid belt
       </a>
       , with orbits that have been circularized either by atmospheric drag or
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cazenave-2_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cazenave-2-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as capture requires dissipation of energy. The current
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martian_atmosphere" title="Martian atmosphere">
        Martian atmosphere
       </a>
       is too thin to capture a Phobos-sized object by atmospheric braking.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burns_19-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-burns-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_A._Landis" title="Geoffrey A. Landis">
        Geoffrey Landis
       </a>
       has pointed out that the capture could have occurred if the original body was a
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_asteroid" title="Binary asteroid">
        binary asteroid
       </a>
       that separated due to tidal forces.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landis_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-landis-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The main alternative hypothesis is that the moons accreted in the present position. Another hypothesis is that Mars was once surrounded by many Phobos- and Deimos-sized bodies, perhaps ejected into orbit around it by a collision with a
       <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">
        planetesimal
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div style="clear:left;">
      </div>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deimos_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA17351-ApparentSizes-MarsDeimosPhobos-EarthMoon.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="300" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/PIA17351-ApparentSizes-MarsDeimosPhobos-EarthMoon.jpg/220px-PIA17351-ApparentSizes-MarsDeimosPhobos-EarthMoon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0c/PIA17351-ApparentSizes-MarsDeimosPhobos-EarthMoon.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA17351-ApparentSizes-MarsDeimosPhobos-EarthMoon.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <i>
          relative
         </i>
         sizes of Deimos and Phobos as seen from Mars, compared to the
         <i>
          relative
         </i>
         size of the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         as seen from Earth
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Overall, its exploration history is similar to those
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Mars" title="Exploration of Mars">
        of Mars
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)#Exploration" title="Phobos (moon)">
        of Phobos
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Deimos has been photographed in close-up by several spacecraft whose primary mission has been to photograph Mars. No landings on Deimos have been made.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Soviet
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_program" title="Phobos program">
        Phobos program
       </a>
       sent two probes to Phobos. In case
       <i>
        Phobos 1
       </i>
       succeeded,
       <i>
        Phobos 2
       </i>
       could have been sent to Deimos. Both probes launched successfully in July 1988. The first was lost en route to Mars, whereas the second returned some data and images but failed shortly before beginning its detailed examination of Phobos's surface, including a lander.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1997 and 1998, the proposed
       <i>
        Aladdin
       </i>
       mission was selected as a finalist in the NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Program" title="Discovery Program">
        Discovery Program
       </a>
       . The plan was to visit both Phobos and Deimos, and launch projectiles at the satellites. The probe would collect the ejecta as it performed a slow flyby (~1 km/s).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These samples would be returned to Earth for study three years later.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The principal investigator was
       <a href="/wiki/Carle_M._Pieters" title="Carle M. Pieters">
        Carle M. Pieters
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">
        Brown University
       </a>
       . The total mission cost, including launch vehicle and operations was $247.7 million.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ultimately, the mission chosen to fly was
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/MESSENGER" title="MESSENGER">
         MESSENGER
        </a>
       </i>
       , a probe to the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2008, NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Research_Center" title="Glenn Research Center">
        Glenn Research Center
       </a>
       began studying a Phobos and Deimos
       <a href="/wiki/Sample-return_mission" title="Sample-return mission">
        sample-return mission
       </a>
       that would use solar electric propulsion. The study gave rise to the "Hall" mission concept, a
       <a href="/wiki/New_Frontiers_program" title="New Frontiers program">
        New Frontiers
       </a>
       -class mission currently under further study.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Also, the sample-return mission called
       <i>
        Gulliver
       </i>
       has been conceptualized and dedicated to Deimos,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britt_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-britt-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in which 1 kilogram (2.2 pounds) of material from Deimos would be returned to Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britt_34-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-britt-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another concept of sample-return mission from Phobos and Deimos is
       <i>
        OSIRIS-REx 2
       </i>
       , which would use heritage from the first
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/OSIRIS-REx" title="OSIRIS-REx">
         OSIRIS-REx
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In March 2014, a Discovery class mission was proposed to place an orbiter on Mars orbit by 2021 and study Phobos and Deimos. It is called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Phobos_And_Deimos_%26_Mars_Environment" title="Phobos And Deimos &amp; Mars Environment">
         Phobos And Deimos &amp; Mars Environment
        </a>
       </i>
       (PADME).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpsc_2014_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lpsc_2014-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reyes_2014_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Reyes_2014-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The human exploration of Deimos could serve as a catalyst for the human exploration of Mars. Recently, it was proposed that the sands of Deimos or Phobos could serve as a valuable material for
       <a href="/wiki/Aerobraking" title="Aerobraking">
        aerobraking
       </a>
       in the colonization of Mars
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arias16_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-arias16-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . Because the small delta-v budget of Phobos, a small amount of chemical fuel brought from Earth could be transformed in a very large amount of sand lifted from the surface of Phobos – from a permanent outpost, to a transfer orbit. This sand could be released in front of the spacecraft during the descent maneuver and then resulting in a densification of the atmosphere just in front of the spacecraft.
      </p>
      <p>
       <br/>
      </p>
