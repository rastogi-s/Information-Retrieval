      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         From the Earth to the Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="fr">
         <b>
          De la terre à la lune
         </b>
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is an 1865 novel by
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
        Jules Verne
       </a>
       . It tells the story of the Baltimore Gun Club, a post-
       <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
        American Civil War
       </a>
       society of weapons enthusiasts, and their attempts to build an enormous
       <a href="/wiki/Columbiad#In_fiction" title="Columbiad">
        Columbiad
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Space_gun" title="Space gun">
        space gun
       </a>
       and launch three people—the Gun Club's president, his Philadelphian armor-making rival, and a French poet—in a projectile with the goal of a
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">
        moon landing
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The story is also notable in that Verne attempted to do some rough calculations as to the requirements for the cannon and, considering the comparative lack of any data on the subject at the time, some of his figures are surprisingly close to reality. However, his scenario turned out to be impractical for safe manned space travel since a much longer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muzzle_(firearm)" title="Muzzle (firearm)">
        muzzle
       </a>
       would have been required to reach
       <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">
        escape velocity
       </a>
       while limiting
       <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">
        acceleration
       </a>
       to survivable limits for the passengers.
      </p>
      <p>
       The character of Michel Ardan, the French member of the party in the novel, was inspired by the real-life photographer
       <a href="/wiki/Nadar_(photographer)" title="Nadar (photographer)">
        Félix Nadar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>
       The story opens some time after the end of the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
        American Civil War
       </a>
       . The Gun Club, a society based in
       <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">
        Baltimore
       </a>
       and dedicated to the design of weapons of all kinds (especially cannons), comes together when Impey Barbicane, its president, calls them to support his latest idea. He's done some calculations, and believes that they could construct a cannon capable of shooting a projectile to the moon. After receiving the support of his companions, another meeting is held to decide the place from which the projectile will be fired, the dimensions and materials of both the cannon and the projectile, and which kind of powder they are to use.
      </p>
      <p>
       An old enemy of Barbicane, a Captain Nicholl of Philadelphia, designer of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plate_armor" title="Plate armor">
        plate armor
       </a>
       , declares that the enterprise is absurd and makes a series of bets with Barbicane, each of them of increasing amount, over the impossibility of such feat.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first obstacle, the money to construct the cannon (and against which Nicholl has bet 1,000 dollars), is raised from a number of countries in America and Europe. Notably, the US gives four million
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dollars" title="Dollars">
        dollars
       </a>
       , while England does not give a farthing, but in the end, nearly five and a half million dollars are raised, which ensures the financial feasibility of the project.
      </p>
      <p>
       Stone's Hill in
       <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">
        "Tampa Town"
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">
        Florida
       </a>
       is chosen as the site for the cannon's construction, predating
       <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">
        Kennedy Space Center
       </a>
       's placement in Florida by almost 100 years. Verne gives the exact position as 27°7' northern
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and 5°7' western
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       (of course relative to the meridian of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" title="Washington, DC">
        Washington
       </a>
       that is
       <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
        <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon&amp;params=27_7_0_N_82_9_0_W_">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_feasibility_of_a_space_cannon">
        Technical feasibility of a space cannon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technical feasibility of a space cannon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In his 1903 publication on space travel,
       <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">
        Konstantin Tsiolkovsky
       </a>
       refuted Verne's idea of using a cannon for space travel. He concluded that a gun would have to be impossibly long. The gun in the story would subject the payload to about 22,000
       <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">
        g
       </a>
       of acceleration (see
       <a href="/wiki/Space_gun#Acceleration" title="Space gun">
        formula
       </a>
       ). However, he was nevertheless inspired by the story and developed the theory of spaceflight.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Bull" title="Gerald Bull">
        Gerald Bull
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Project_HARP" title="Project HARP">
        Project HARP
       </a>
       proved after 1961 that a cannon can shoot a 180 kg (400 lb) projectile to an altitude of 180 kilometres (110 mi) and reach 32 percent of the needed
       <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">
        escape velocity
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Additionally, during the
       <a href="/wiki/Operation_Plumbbob" title="Operation Plumbbob">
        Plumbbob
       </a>
       nuclear test series, a 900 kg (2,000 lb) capping plate made of steel was blasted away and never found. It has been speculated that the plate entered outer space because its speed was estimated to be between two and six times the escape velocity,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brownlee_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brownlee-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but engineers believe it melted in the atmosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brownlee_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brownlee-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Technological_aspects_of_the_novel">
        Technological aspects of the novel

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <p>
       Jules Verne’s novel is a window to the knowledge of his time and his understanding of the technologies. It has several technical errors, which look unusual to the modern eyes, but reflect the knowledge of 1865-1870. Some of the issues are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The projectile is launched using a large gun. Of course, the largest issue is the resulting acceleration, which is well above the survivable limit for humans. The space capsule also performs a splashdown in the Pacific Ocean on the return trip, which also challenges the acceleration limits. Verne did not consider using a rocket as the main propulsion method although some small retro-rockets were carried onboard to be used to soften the landing on the Moon. This is somewhat understandable since
        <a href="/wiki/Black_powder_rocket_motor" title="Black powder rocket motor">
         black powder rockets
        </a>
        were rather small at that time and the possibility of using them for space propulsion was considered and described only some 40 years later by
        <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">
         Tsiolkovsky
        </a>
        . Liquid propellants were invented even later. These modern
        <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">
         propellants
        </a>
        offer much higher
        <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">
         specific impulse
        </a>
        than black powder. Verne has used a gun perhaps because he ignored that rockets could work in vacuum.
       </li>
       <li>
        The passengers en route to the Moon are described to be subjected to gravitation all the way to the “neutral point” between the Earth and the Moon. Actual passengers of a free-flight projectile would not feel any gravity anywhere along the journey.
       </li>
       <li>
        When the projectile is behind the Moon, the passengers measure the vacuum temperature while in the shadow of the moon. They "settle" the debate between
        <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">
         Joseph Fourier
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Claude_Pouillet" title="Claude Pouillet">
         Claude Pouillet
        </a>
        when the “actual” temperature is measured at -140 °C when a special thermometer is sent out of the capsule and retrieved by slightly opening the hatch. The accepted value today is -271 °C.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Aether_(classical_element)" title="Aether (classical element)">
         Aether
        </a>
        is explained to be the medium by which “heat and light is produced;” this was the standard model of the time. This model was later abandoned as the result of the measurement of the speed of light by
        <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment" title="Michelson–Morley experiment">
         Michelson and Morley
        </a>
        in 1887, which led later to the
        <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">
         theory of relativity
        </a>
        in 1904 by
        <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
         Albert Einstein
        </a>
        . This experiment and others disproved existence of aether.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       However, the novel also reports some state-of-the-art technologies and new knowledge of the time:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The projectile deviates and misses the Moon because it passes close to a large asteroid orbiting the Earth while en route to the Moon. Jules Verne makes a reference here to the “discovery” of second Moon by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Other_moons_of_Earth#Petit.27s_moon" title="Other moons of Earth">
         Frédéric Petit
        </a>
        in 1846. This theory was dismissed later.
       </li>
       <li>
        The propellant used to shoot the capsule is reported to be
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guncotton" title="Guncotton">
         guncotton
        </a>
        —an explosive discovered in 1846 and later made practical in 1865 and eventually developed into a
        <a href="/wiki/Smokeless_powder" title="Smokeless powder">
         smokeless powder
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The capsule is made of
        <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
         aluminium
        </a>
        , a metal first produced by
        <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_%C3%98rsted" title="Hans Christian Ørsted">
         Hans Christian Ørsted
        </a>
        in 1825 but later improved by two
        <a href="/wiki/Aluminium#history" title="Aluminium">
         Frenchmen
        </a>
        . This metal is common today but was very rare when the book was written. The modern low-cost
        <a href="/wiki/Hall%E2%80%93H%C3%A9roult_process" title="Hall–Héroult process">
         process
        </a>
        for producing aluminum was introduced in 1886, some 20 years after the novel was written.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
         Carbon dioxide
        </a>
        removal in the capsule's atmosphere is accomplished using
        <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">
         potassium hydroxide
        </a>
        while oxygen production uses the "Reiset and Regnaut machine" (see
        <a href="/wiki/Henri_Victor_Regnault" title="Henri Victor Regnault">
         Henri Victor Regnault
        </a>
        ).
        <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_toxicity" title="Oxygen toxicity">
         Oxygen euphoria
        </a>
        is even experienced along the trip when the oxygen production machine malfunctions.
       </li>
       <li>
        The "neutral point" is a simplified version of
        <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
         Lagrangian point
        </a>
        , calculated in 1772 by
        <a href="/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange" title="Joseph-Louis Lagrange">
         Joseph-Louis Lagrange
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_popular_culture">
        Influence on popular culture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influence on popular culture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The novel was adapted as the opera
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Le_voyage_dans_la_lune_(operetta)" title="Le voyage dans la lune (operetta)">
         Le voyage dans la lune
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1875, with music by
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Offenbach" title="Jacques Offenbach">
        Jacques Offenbach
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">
        H. G. Wells
       </a>
       ' 1901 novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_First_Men_in_the_Moon" title="The First Men in the Moon">
         The First Men in the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       (also relating to the first voyagers to the Moon) the protagonist, Mr. Bedford, mentions Verne's novel to his companion, Professor Cavor, who replies (in a possible dig at Verne) that he does not know what Bedford is referring to. Verne returned the dig later when he pointed out that he used
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gun_cotton" title="Gun cotton">
        gun cotton
       </a>
       to send his men to the moon, an actual substance. "Can Mr. Wells show me some 'cavourite'?", he asked archly.
      </p>
      <p>
       The novel (along with Wells'
       <i>
        The First Men in the Moon
       </i>
       ) inspired the first science fiction film,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_Trip_to_the_Moon" title="A Trip to the Moon">
         A Trip to the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       , made in 1902 by
       <a href="/wiki/Georges_M%C3%A9li%C3%A8s" title="Georges Méliès">
        Georges Méliès
       </a>
       . In 1958, another film adaptation of this story was released, titled
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon_(film)" title="From the Earth to the Moon (film)">
         From the Earth to the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       . It was one of the last films made under the
       <a href="/wiki/RKO_Pictures" title="RKO Pictures">
        RKO Pictures
       </a>
       banner. The story also became the basis for the very loose adaptation
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne%27s_Rocket_to_the_Moon" title="Jules Verne's Rocket to the Moon">
         Jules Verne's Rocket to the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       (1967), a caper-style British comedy starring
       <a href="/wiki/Burl_Ives" title="Burl Ives">
        Burl Ives
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Terry-Thomas" title="Terry-Thomas">
        Terry-Thomas
       </a>
       . The 1961 Czechoslovak film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Fabulous_Baron_Munchausen" title="The Fabulous Baron Munchausen">
         The Fabulous Baron Munchausen
        </a>
       </i>
       combines characters and plot elements from the Verne novel with those of the stories of
       <a href="/wiki/Baron_Munchausen" title="Baron Munchausen">
        Baron Munchausen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrano_de_Bergerac_(play)" title="Cyrano de Bergerac (play)">
        Cyrano de Bergerac
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1889 Verne wrote a second sequel to the novel,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Purchase_of_the_North_Pole" title="The Purchase of the North Pole">
         The Purchase of the North Pole
        </a>
       </i>
       , which has the Gun Club members (led by J. T. Maston) plan to use the "Columbiad" to alter the tilt of the earth to enable the mineral wealth of the Arctic region to be put within reach of exploitation.
      </p>
      <p>
       In March 1953, the Gilberton Company published a comic-book adaptation of
       <i>
        From the Earth to the Moon
       </i>
       as issue No. 105 in its
       <a href="/wiki/Classics_Illustrated" title="Classics Illustrated">
        Classics Illustrated
       </a>
       series. An unidentified scriptwriter combined Verne's
       <i>
        From the Earth to the Moon
       </i>
       with the sequel,
       <i>
        Around the Moon
       </i>
       . Gilberton art director
       <a href="/wiki/Alex_Blum" title="Alex Blum">
        Alex A. Blum
       </a>
       supplied both the cover painting and the 44 pages of interior art. The title went through twelve printings between 1953 and 1971.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During their return journey from the moon, the crew of
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       made reference to Jules Verne's book during a TV broadcast on July 23.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>

       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>

       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">
         sic
        </a>
       </i>
       ], took off from Florida and landed in the Pacific Ocean after completing a trip to the Moon. It seems appropriate to us to share with you some of the reflections of the crew as the modern-day Columbia completes its rendezvous with the planet Earth and the same Pacific Ocean tomorrow."
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III" title="Back to the Future Part III">

        </a>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_characters_in_the_Back_to_the_Future_films#Clara_Clayton" title="List of characters in the Back to the Future films">
        Clara Clayton

       <a href="/wiki/Emmett_Brown" title="Emmett Brown">

       <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">

      <p>
       The first incarnation of the roller coaster Space Mountain in
       <a href="/wiki/Disneyland_Paris" title="Disneyland Paris">
        Disneyland Paris
       </a>
       , named
       <i>
        Space Mountain: De la Terre à la Lune
       </i>
       , was based loosely on this novel. The attraction opened in 1995. The attraction's exterior was designed using a Verne era
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retro-futurism" title="Retro-futurism">
        retro-futuristic
       </a>
       influence. Elements of the ambiance include rivet and boiler plate effect and the "Columbiad," which recoils with a bang and produces smoke as each car passes, giving riders the perception of being shot into space. During 2005, the ride was refurbished and renamed
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Mountain:_Mission_2" title="Space Mountain: Mission 2">
         Space Mountain: Mission 2
        </a>
       </i>
       as part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Happiest_Celebration_on_Earth" title="Happiest Celebration on Earth">
        Happiest Celebration on Earth
       </a>
       . The ride no longer features elements of the original storyline from the novel.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1995 the BBC made a documentary about the creation of Space Mountain, called
       <i>
        Shoot For The Moon
       </i>
       . The 44-minute program followed Tim Delaney and his team in bringing the book
       <i>
        From the Earth to the Moon
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
        Jules Verne
       </a>
       to life. The program shows the development of the attraction, from conception to construction to testing the final attraction. The documentary, originally broadcast on
       <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">
        BBC Two
       </a>
       in the United Kingdom, was also aired on other channels in many countries.
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2013, a new film version of the story was announced, under the banner of Project Epicus
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Project_Epicus_website_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Project_Epicus_website-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       —a film project—with the full blessing of
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
        Jules Verne
       </a>
       ’s great-grandson Jean Verne and due for production in 2017.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jean_Verne_Project_Epicus_interview,_2013_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jean_Verne_Project_Epicus_interview,_2013-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RPF_Forum_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RPF_Forum-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
