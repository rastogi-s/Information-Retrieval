   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Japanese Lunar Exploration Program
   </h1>

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The (Japanese)
       <b>
        Lunar Exploration Program
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        月探査計画
       </b>
       ), is a program of robotic and human missions to the Moon undertaken by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency">
        Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency
       </a>
       (JAXA), and its division, the
       <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Space_and_Astronautical_Science" title="Institute of Space and Astronautical Science">
        Institute of Space and Astronautical Science
       </a>
       (ISAS). It is also one of the three major enterprises of the JAXA Space Exploration Center (JSPEC).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The main goal of the program is "to elucidate the origin and evolution of the Moon and utilize the Moon in the future".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LunarProgram_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LunarProgram-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first spacecraft of the program, the unmanned lunar orbiter
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
        SELENE
       </a>
       (Kaguya), was launched from
       <a href="/wiki/Tanegashima_Space_Center" title="Tanegashima Space Center">
        Tanegashima Space Center
       </a>
       on September 14, 2007, after being delayed several times.
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE-2" title="SELENE-2">
        SELENE-2
       </a>
       , Japan's first lunar lander and rover, is expected to be launched in 2017.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The program also includes a lunar sample return mission (SELENE-3), a mission to
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       to collect data for future manned expeditions (
       <a href="/wiki/MELOS" title="MELOS">
        MELOS
       </a>
       ), participation in the
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_sample_return_mission" title="Mars sample return mission">
        Mars international sample return mission
       </a>
       , and an advanced lander for future human missions to the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LunarProgram_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-LunarProgram-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The eventual goal is to participate in an international lunar outpost program, in which Japanese crews would stay on the lunar surface for a prolonged period of time and promote scientific research and environment utilization.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LunarProgram_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-LunarProgram-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h3>
       <span id="SELENE_.28Kaguya.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SELENE_(Kaguya)">
        SELENE (Kaguya)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: SELENE (Kaguya)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kaguya_mark.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="181" data-file-width="180" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c3/Kaguya_mark.jpg" width="180"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kaguya_mark.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
          SELENE
         </a>
         mission logo.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
        SELENE
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       SELENE (Selenological and Engineering Explorer), nicknamed Kaguya after a lunar princess in the ancient Japanese folklore
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_the_Bamboo_Cutter" title="The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter">
         The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter
        </a>
       </i>
       , is the second Japanese mission to the Moon. Launched in September 2007, it was "the largest lunar mission since the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mission featured three separate space craft, the main orbiter (Kaguya), the small relay satellite (Okina), and the VLBI satellite (Ouna).
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SELENE-2">
        SELENE-2
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: SELENE-2">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE-2" title="SELENE-2">
        SELENE-2
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       SELENE-2, also known as Kaguya-2, formerly known as SELENE-B, is the follow-on mission to Kaguya. SELENE-2 will consist from one large lander, which will have a small-sized rover, and if possible, will also have some penetrators inherited from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LUNAR-A" title="LUNAR-A">
        LUNAR-A
       </a>
       mission, and a small data relay satellite.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LunarExploration_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LunarExploration-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SELENE-3">
        SELENE-3
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: SELENE-3">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       SELENE-3 aims to return about 100 grams of samples from the Moon surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It will be launched around 2020. However, due to financial issues JAXA is currently facing, this mission may be merged into the option-3 of SELENE-X.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SELENE-X">
        SELENE-X
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: SELENE-X">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       SELENE-X will be launched in the late 2010s, in view of Japan’s participation in Humans Lunar Activities foreseen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SELENE-X_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SELENE-X-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The SELENE-X may perform either of the following demonstrations:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Option-1:Technology demonstration for building outposts such as the excavation for construction of Infrastructures.
       </li>
       <li>
        Option-2: Logistics capability demonstration for building common landers for both transportation and JAXA’s own robotic missions.
       </li>
       <li>
        Option-3: Highly sophisticated in-situ robotic lander, or returning samples of the surface soil to the Earth, including the development of high speed reentry capsules.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       There are other options under study, and will be determined after the international exploration strategy has been clarified.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SELENE-X_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-SELENE-X-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="MELOS">
        MELOS
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: MELOS">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/MELOS" title="MELOS">
        MELOS
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       MELOS (Mars Exploration with Lander-Orbiter Synergy) is a planned mission to Mars, consisting of an orbiter (MELOS-1), and a lander (MELOS-2). It would be the successor to the comet probe
       <a href="/wiki/Suisei_(spacecraft)" title="Suisei (spacecraft)">
        Suisei (PLANET-A)
       </a>
       the Mars probe
       <a href="/wiki/Nozomi_(spacecraft)" title="Nozomi (spacecraft)">
        Nozomi (PLANET-B)
       </a>
       , and the Venus probe
       <a href="/wiki/Akatsuki_(spacecraft)" title="Akatsuki (spacecraft)">
        Akatsuki (PLANET-C)
       </a>
       . MELOS-1 is planned to be launched in 2018, and will also have up to four small landers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MEPAG_2011_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MEPAG_2011-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MEPAG_2012_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MEPAG_2012-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hiten (MUSES-A)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Hiten" title="Hiten">
        Hiten
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Hiten, or MUSES-A (Mu Space Engineering Satellite-A) is a technology demonstration satellite built by ISAS, launched on January 24, 1990.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="LUNAR-A">
        LUNAR-A
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: LUNAR-A">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar-A" title="Lunar-A">
        Lunar-A
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       LUNAR-A was an ISAS mission consisting of a lunar space craft which would have carried two penetrators to the Moon, and deploy them at an altitude of 40 km on opposite sides of the lunar body. The penetrators were to have been braked by a small rocket at an altitude of 25 km, then free fall to the surface. They were designed to withstand a collision speed of 330 meters per second to deeply penetrate the lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       . Once the penetrators are deployed, the LUNAR-A spacecraft was mission-planned to maneuver to an orbital altitude of 200 km above the lunar surface. The craft was to have carried a monochromatic imaging camera with a resolution of 30 m. It was to be Japan's first large scale lunar probe. (Hiten was a technology demonstrator and only had a camera and a dust counter)
      </p>
      <p>
       Not only was LUNAR-A one of the first two missions of the original Lunar Exploration Program, it was supposed to be the first mission in the LUNAR series. However, the mission was delayed for years (the original launch date was in 1995), and from 2004, no launch date was set. The space craft was completed in 1997, but the development of the penetrators were severely delayed. The Project faced several other issues, including problems with the propllant and the thrusters. Worse, in 2006, JAXA decided to retire the
       <a href="/wiki/M-V" title="M-V">
        M-V
       </a>
       rocket, which the LUNAR-A planned to use. M-Ⅴ-2, the rocket which was to be used for LUNAR-A couldn't be used anymore, because many portions of it were reused for other launches. The M-Ⅴ-2 is now displayed in JAXA's
       <a href="/wiki/Sagamihara_Campus" title="Sagamihara Campus">
        Sagamihara Campus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In January 2007, JAXA cancelled the LUNAR-A mission, mostly due to the fact that the space craft had become old.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A follow-on mission known as LUNAR-B existed, but was merged into the SELENE series following LUNAR-A's cancellation. JAXA continued the research for the penetrators, and completed its development in October 2010, following a successful test fire. Russia's
       <a href="/wiki/Luna-Glob" title="Luna-Glob">
        Luna-Glob1
       </a>
       plans to use four penetrators provided by JAXA. JAXA is also considering to have the SELENE-2 carry penetrators.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LunarExploration_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-LunarExploration-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Britain's BNSC once stated that they were interested in acquiring several Japanese penetrators for their
       <a href="/wiki/MoonLITE" title="MoonLITE">
        MoonLITE
       </a>
       probe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SLIM">
        SLIM
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: SLIM">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Smart_Lander_for_Investigating_Moon" title="Smart Lander for Investigating Moon">
        Smart Lander for Investigating Moon
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       SLIM, short for
       <i>
        Smart Lander for Investigating Moon
       </i>
       , is a candidate for the SPRINT-C (Small scientific satellite Platform for Rapid INvestigation and Test-C) mission. The lander would be 120 kg (260 lb), and is proposed to be launched on an Epsilon advanced rocket from the Kagoshima Prefecture in late 2019.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_2015_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Times_2015-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The mission's main goal will be to perfect soft-landing technologies,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euan_2015_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Euan_2015-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       including navigation relative to the surface, and detection of obstacles such as rocks and holes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The demonstration mission is estimated to cost about ¥15 billion (US$125 million).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euan_2015_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Euan_2015-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
