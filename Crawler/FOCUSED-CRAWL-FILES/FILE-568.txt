   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunar plaque
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

         Replica of the
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
          Apollo 11
         </a>
         plaque, bearing the signatures of
         <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
          Neil Armstrong
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_(astronaut)" title="Michael Collins (astronaut)">
          Michael Collins
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
          Buzz Aldrin
         </a>
         , and
         <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">
          U.S. President
         </a>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" title="Richard M. Nixon">
          Richard M. Nixon
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Stainless steel
       <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_plaque" title="Commemorative plaque">
        commemorative plaques
       </a>
       measuring 9 by
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        7
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         5
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         8
        </sub>
       </span>
       inches (22.9 by 19.4 cm) were attached to the ladders on the descent stages of the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
        Apollo Lunar Modules
       </a>
       flown on lunar landing missions Apollo 11 through Apollo 17, to be left permanently on the lunar surface. The plaques were originally suggested and designed by NASA's head of technical services
       <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kinzler" title="Jack Kinzler">
        Jack Kinzler
       </a>
       , who oversaw their production.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moon_Flag_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moon_Flag-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       All of the plaques bear facsimiles of the participating astronauts' signatures. For this reason, an extra plaque had to be made for
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">
        Apollo 13
       </a>
       due to the late replacement of one crewmember. The first (
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       ) and last (
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        Apollo 17
       </a>
       ) plaques bear a facsimile of the signature of
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">
        Richard Nixon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">
        President of the United States
       </a>
       during the landings, along with references to the start and "completion" of "man's" "first" explorations of the Moon and expressions of peace for "all mankind". All, except the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        Apollo 12
       </a>
       plaque (which is also textured differently), bear pictures of the two hemispheres of Earth. Apollo 17's plaque bears a depiction of the lunar globe in addition to the Earth. The plaques used on missions 13 through 16 bear the call-sign of each mission's Lunar Module. All the plaques were left on the Moon, except the two for the aborted Apollo 13 which did not land on the Moon.
      </p>

       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
         Apollo 11
        </a>
        plaque inscription:
        <i>
         Here men from the planet Earth first set foot upon the Moon, July 1969
         <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">
          AD
         </a>
         .
        </i>
        The statement,
        <i>
         We came in peace for all mankind.
        </i>
        is derived from the 1958
        <a href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Act" title="National Aeronautics and Space Act">
         National Aeronautics and Space Act
        </a>
        's "declaration of policy and purpose":
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          The Congress hereby declares that it is the policy of the United States that activities in space should be devoted to peaceful purposes for the benefit of all mankind.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
           <a href="#cite_note-2">
            [2]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">
        Notes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_plaque&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The plaques are curved so as to fit around the landing leg and not hinder the astronauts from using the ladder. The plaques were attached directly to the ladder rungs, between the third and fourth rungs from the bottom.
       </li>
       <li>
        Two plaques were made for Apollo 13, because of the replacement of Command Module Pilot
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Mattingly" title="Thomas Mattingly">
         Thomas K. "Ken" Mattingly
        </a>
        with
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Swigert" title="John Swigert">
         John L. "Jack" Swigert
        </a>
        two days before launch. Mission Commander
        <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">
         James "Jim" Lovell
        </a>
        was to have placed the plaque bearing Swigert's name over the original already fastened to the LM
        <i>
         Aquarius
        </i>
        , when he descended the ladder to walk on the Moon. When the landing was aborted, Lovell saved this plaque to keep as a memento, and it remains in his possession. The original plaque bearing Mattingly's name was destroyed when
        <i>
         Aquarius
        </i>
        reentered the Earth's atmosphere.
       </li>
       <li>
        The lunar near-side map, with the six Apollo lunar landing sites marked on it, was added to the Apollo 17 plaque at the suggestion of astronaut
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gene_Cernan" title="Gene Cernan">
         Gene Cernan
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Reproductions of the plaques were given as mementos to foreign governments through various United States embassies after each flight.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/James_C._Humes" title="James C. Humes">
         James C. Humes
        </a>
        , a speech writer for President Nixon and four other presidents, is partly credited for authoring the text on the Apollo 11 lunar plaque.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <a href="/wiki/William_Safire" title="William Safire">
         William Safire
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Pat_Buchanan" title="Pat Buchanan">
         Pat Buchanan
        </a>
        also worked on drafting the plaque.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-safire_4-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-safire-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In 1989, William Safire in his capacity as a "word maven" wryly wrote that AD should have been placed before the year, not after:
        <i>
         "I had a hand in the first sign to be placed by earthlings on another celestial body, and it contains a glaring grammatical error."
        </i>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-safire_4-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-safire-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
