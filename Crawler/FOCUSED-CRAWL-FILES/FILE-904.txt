      <p>
       Pallene was discovered by the Cassini Imaging Team in 2004, during the
       <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
        Cassini–Huygens
       </a>
       mission.

        </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPorco_Baker_et_al._2005_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorco_Baker_et_al._2005-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was given the temporary designation
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/2004 S 2
        </span>
       </b>
       . In 2005, the name Pallene was provisionally approved by the IAU Division III Working Group for Planetary System Nomenclature,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8471_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8471-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was ratified at the IAU General Assembly in 2006. The name refers to
       <a href="/wiki/Pallene_(mythology)" title="Pallene (mythology)">
        Pallene
       </a>
       , one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Alkyonides" title="Alkyonides">
        Alkyonides
       </a>
       , the seven beautiful daughters of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_(mythology)" title="Giant (mythology)">
        giant
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyoneus" title="Alkyoneus">
        Alkyoneus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       After the discovery in 2004, it was realized that Pallene had been first photographed on August 23, 1981, by the space probe
       <i>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Voyager 2
        </span>
       </i>
       . It had appeared in a single photograph and had been provisionally named
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/1981 S 14
        </span>
       </b>
       and estimated to orbit 200,000 km from Saturn.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_6162_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_6162-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because it had not been visible in other images, it had not been possible to compute its orbit at the time, but recent comparisons have shown it to match Pallene's orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_characteristics">
        Orbital characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallene_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbital characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Pallene is visibly affected by a perturbing mean-longitude resonance with the much larger Enceladus, although this effect is not as large as Mimas's perturbations on
       <a href="/wiki/Methone_(moon)" title="Methone (moon)">
        Methone
       </a>
       . The perturbations cause Pallene's
       <a href="/wiki/Osculating_orbit" title="Osculating orbit">
        osculating orbital
       </a>
       elements to vary with an amplitude of about 4 km in semi-major axis, and 0.02° in longitude (corresponding to about 75 km). Eccentricity also changes on various timescales between 0.002 and 0.006, and inclination between about 0.178° and 0.184°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ring">
        Ring
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallene_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ring">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 2006, images taken in forward-scattered light by the
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       spacecraft enabled the Cassini Imaging Team to discover a faint dust ring around Saturn that shares Pallene's orbit, now named the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#Pallene_Ring" title="Rings of Saturn">
        Pallene Ring
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8759_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8759-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECICLOPS_2006,_''Moonmade_Rings''_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECICLOPS_2006,_''Moonmade_Rings''-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The ring has a radial extent of about 2,500 km. Its source is particles blasted off Pallene's surface by meteoroid impacts, which then form a diffuse ring around its orbital path.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Creating_New_Rings_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Creating_New_Rings-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHedman_et_al.,_2009_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHedman_et_al.,_2009-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallene_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       spacecraft, which studied Saturn and its moons until September, 2017, performed a fly-by of Pallene on 16 October 2010, and 14 September 2011 at a distance of 36,000 kilometeres (22,000 miles) and 44,000 kilometeres respectively.
      </p>
