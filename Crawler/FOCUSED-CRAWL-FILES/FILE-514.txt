   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Hadley–Apennine
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Hadley–Apennine
       </b>
       is a region on the
       <a href="/wiki/Near_side_of_the_Moon" title="Near side of the Moon">
        near side
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Earth's Moon
       </a>
       that served as the landing site for the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        American
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       mission, the fourth manned landing on the Moon and the first of the "
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Apollo_mission_types" title="List of Apollo mission types">
        J-missions
       </a>
       ", in July 1971.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpiapollo15_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lpiapollo15-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasma15_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasma15-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The site is located on the eastern edge of
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Imbrium" title="Mare Imbrium">
        Mare Imbrium
       </a>
       on a lava plain known as
       <a href="/wiki/Palus_Putredinis" title="Palus Putredinis">
        Palus Putredinis
       </a>
       . Hadley-Apennine is bordered by the
       <a href="/wiki/Montes_Apenninus" title="Montes Apenninus">
        Montes Apenninus
       </a>
       (often referred to as "Apennine Front"), a mountain range, and Hadley Rille, a meandering channel, on the east and west, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpi_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lpi-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a15ov_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-a15ov-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Data obtained from the composition of soil samples collected on Apollo 15 show that most (about 90%) of the samples from the Apennine Front are brown-
       <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">
        glass
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">
        breccias
       </a>
       , and approximately 60–70% obtained from the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare" title="Mare">
        mare
       </a>
       surface are
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       . Although the basalts seem to vary in their texture, their ages appear to be approximately the same. Most of the samples obtained on the Apennine Front are
       <a href="/wiki/KREEP" title="KREEP">
        KREEP
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">
        potassium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">
        rare-earth elements
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">
        phosphorus
       </a>
       ) materials,
       <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">
        anorthosites
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Recrystallization_(geology)" title="Recrystallization (geology)">
        recrystallized
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Norite" title="Norite">
        norite
       </a>
       , or recrystallized breccia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cameron_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cameron-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_geology">
        Geography and geology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadley%E2%80%93Apennine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography and geology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Location">
        Location
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadley%E2%80%93Apennine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_15_actual_traverses_and_sample_stations.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1839" data-file-width="1696" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Apollo_15_actual_traverses_and_sample_stations.jpg/220px-Apollo_15_actual_traverses_and_sample_stations.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Apollo_15_actual_traverses_and_sample_stations.jpg/330px-Apollo_15_actual_traverses_and_sample_stations.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Apollo_15_actual_traverses_and_sample_stations.jpg/440px-Apollo_15_actual_traverses_and_sample_stations.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_15_actual_traverses_and_sample_stations.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Orbital photo of Hadley-Apennine with Apollo 15 traverses labeled
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Hadley-Apennine is located west of the
       <a href="/wiki/Montes_Apenninus" title="Montes Apenninus">
        Montes Apenninus
       </a>
       and east of Hadley Rille. The Apennine mountains form a 15,000 foot (4,600 m)
       <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">
        escarpment
       </a>
       that rises higher above the Hadley plain than the
       <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">
        Himalayan
       </a>
       front above the plains of
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">
        Nepal
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasm_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasm-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hadley Rille (also referred to as Rima Hadley)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iaurima_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-iaurima-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is located to the west of the Apollo 15 landing site and was the subject of substantial investigation during that mission. The feature, named from nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Mons_Hadley" title="Mons Hadley">
        Mons Hadley
       </a>
       , is a channel that was likely formed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">
        volcanic
       </a>
       processes earlier in the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_geologic_timescale" title="Lunar geologic timescale">
        history of the Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpi_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-lpi-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_15_landing_site">
        Apollo 15 landing site
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadley%E2%80%93Apennine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apollo 15 landing site">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_15,_Lunar_surface" title="Apollo 15, Lunar surface">
        Apollo 15, Lunar surface
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The American
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       mission, the first of the J-series missions that featured both increased scientific capability and the use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Roving_Vehicle" title="Lunar Roving Vehicle">
        Lunar Roving Vehicle
       </a>
       , landed in an area of the Hadley-Apennine region to the west of the Apennine Front situated between the mountains
       <a href="/wiki/Mons_Hadley" title="Mons Hadley">
        Mons Hadley
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mons_Hadley_Delta" title="Mons Hadley Delta">
        Mons Hadley Delta
       </a>
       to the northeast and south, respectively. This landing site was selected with the objectives of exploring the Apennine Front, Hadley Rille, and other geologic features in the area.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-otm_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-otm-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Apollo 15 was the first mission in which landing sites were not restricted to equatorial areas. The Hadley-Apennine site was chosen with the specific objectives of sampling material from deeper within the Moon than had been obtained from the
       <a href="/wiki/Fra_Mauro_formation" title="Fra Mauro formation">
        Fra Mauro formation
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">
        Apollo 14
       </a>
       and investigating Hadley Rille, a sinuous rille possibly formed by volcanic activity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpi_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-lpi-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The site had been of interest to mission planners since early in the program. During the early stages of Apollo landing site planning,
       <a href="/wiki/Canceled_Apollo_missions#Follow-on_lunar_missions" title="Canceled Apollo missions">
        Apollo 19
       </a>
       (which was originally planned to be the fourth and penultimate J-mission of the initial exploration portion of the Apollo program) was tentatively set to land in the Hadley–Apennine region, albeit at a point south of the eventual Apollo 15 site and west of Hadley Rille—near the Carlos pit at the southwestern terminus of the rille.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An area known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Marius_Hills" title="Marius Hills">
        Marius Hills
       </a>
       was also considered for Apollo 15, but it was determined by mission planners that a landing on the edge of Mare Imbrium at the Hadley-Apennine site would be more scientifically fruitful than the Marius Hills alternative, and placing a
       <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">
        seismometer
       </a>
       at Hadley-Apennine, given the locations of seismic packages from previous Apollo expeditions, would create a more optimal configuration for seismic study.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpi_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-lpi-3">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apennine_Front">
        Apennine Front
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadley%E2%80%93Apennine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Apennine Front">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Montes_Apenninus" title="Montes Apenninus">
        Montes Apenninus
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Apennine mountains, which rise to a height of nearly 5 kilometres (3.1 mi)—farther above the mare plain than the Himalayan front above the Indian and Nepalese plains—are hypothesized to be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fault-block_mountain" title="Fault-block mountain">
        fault-block mountains
       </a>
       displaced upward and segmented by the impact that formed Mare Imbrium. The frontal
       <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">
        escarpment
       </a>
       of Mons Hadley Delta is believed to be an exposed segment of the pre-Imbrium impact lunar crust. This made the mountain one of the mission's primary objectives, as it would allow the astronauts to obtain samples of the lunar crust as it was before the creation of Mare Imbrium.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15psr_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-15psr-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The area of the Apennine mountains between Mons Hadley and Silver Spur (a mountain just southeast of Mons Hadley Delta), although lacking an official designation on maps and other official mission literature, was informally referred to as the "Swann Range" by Apollo 15 astronauts
       <a href="/wiki/David_Scott" title="David Scott">
        David Scott
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/James_Irwin" title="James Irwin">
        James Irwin
       </a>
       , after mission geology team leader Gordon Swann. One of the mountains in the Swann Range was informally referred to by the astronauts as "Big Rock Mountain", after the Apollo Program Director at the time of the mission,
       <a href="/wiki/Rocco_Petrone" title="Rocco Petrone">
        Rocco Petrone
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swannmtn_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-swannmtn-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A thick
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       layer on the lower slopes of the mountains and a thin cover of debris on the upper slopes are suggested by the relative absence of large boulders on the lower mountain flanks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15psr_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-15psr-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The majority of the samples collected by the Apollo 15 astronauts from the Apennine Front are, other than
       <a href="/wiki/KREEP" title="KREEP">
        KREEP
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">
        potassium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">
        rare-earth elements
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">
        phosphorus
       </a>
       ) materials, included
       <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">
        anorthosite
       </a>
       , and recrystallized norite and breccia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cameron_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-cameron-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Unknown before the Apollo 15 mission, there are parallel linear patterns on the faces of the mountains in the area. Although they appeared to be occurrences of
       <a href="/wiki/Fracture_(geology)" title="Fracture (geology)">
        fracturing
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">
        layering
       </a>
       appearing through the regolith, determining this is made difficult by the lighting circumstances during the mission. A dark band on
       <a href="/wiki/Mons_Hadley" title="Mons Hadley">
        Mount Hadley
       </a>
       , believed to be a marking left by lava after receding, was observed by the Apollo 15 crew.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15psr_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-15psr-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_15_Station_2_Rille,_Lunar_Rover,_Scott.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2353" data-file-width="2340" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Apollo_15_Station_2_Rille%2C_Lunar_Rover%2C_Scott.jpg/220px-Apollo_15_Station_2_Rille%2C_Lunar_Rover%2C_Scott.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Apollo_15_Station_2_Rille%2C_Lunar_Rover%2C_Scott.jpg/330px-Apollo_15_Station_2_Rille%2C_Lunar_Rover%2C_Scott.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Apollo_15_Station_2_Rille%2C_Lunar_Rover%2C_Scott.jpg/440px-Apollo_15_Station_2_Rille%2C_Lunar_Rover%2C_Scott.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_15_Station_2_Rille,_Lunar_Rover,_Scott.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Hadley Rille; taken by Apollo 15 astronaut James Irwin
        </div>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rima_Hadley">
        Rima Hadley
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadley%E2%80%93Apennine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rima Hadley">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Rima Hadley, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinuous" title="Sinuous">
        sinuous
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Rille" title="Rille">
        rille
       </a>
       located west of the Apollo 15 landing site, begins in an area of low
       <a href="/wiki/Dome_(geology)" title="Dome (geology)">
        domes
       </a>
       at an elongated crater,
       <a href="/wiki/Mons_Hadley#Nearby_craters" title="Mons Hadley">
        Běla
       </a>
       , and runs on to the North along the Apennine Mountain Range. Some research has suggested that both the rille and Běla are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_vent" title="Volcanic vent">
        volcanic vents
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
        lava
       </a>
       flows created the features. Another hypothesis suggests that the rille was originally an underground
       <a href="/wiki/Lava_tube" title="Lava tube">
        lava tube
       </a>
       , the roof of which collapsed, creating the current appearance of the rille. Several large boulders, some of which are as large as houses, are strewn about the feature's floor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-otm_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-otm-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hadleyrille_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hadleyrille-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a15base_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-a15base-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The elongated crater Běla, which forms the beginning of Rima Hadley, could have been formed by the collapse of a shallow
       <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">
        magma chamber
       </a>
       . Visual observation suggests that the same is the case with other lunar rilles with similar features at their beginnings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overmoon_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-overmoon-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Rima Hadley typically ranges in depth between 600 and 900 feet (180 and 270 m), but is approximately 1,200 feet (370 m) deep at the Apollo 15 landing site. The feature has a cumulative length of about 80 miles (130 km). The sides of the rille, at the Apollo 15 site, slope downwards at an angle of about 25 degrees. Before samples were returned from the Moon during the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       , several scientists believed that the feature and other similar features were formed by flowing water. This hypothesis has since been changed, however, to attribute the process of the feature's creation to volcanism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-otm_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-otm-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Rima Hadley is interpreted as one of the most defined sinuous rilles on the lunar surface. Outcrops of rock were observed by the Apollo 15 crew on both the near and far sides of the rille and were photographed and, in the case of outcrops on the near rim, were sampled.
       <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">
        Layering
       </a>
       in these outcrops is evident from the photos taken of them by the crew. Some of the strata observed have thicknesses of up to about 60 metres (200 ft) and appear to vary in
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       , or reflectivity, and texture. Large boulders near the bottom of the rille are believed to be blocks that have broken off of the outcrops above.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15psr_10-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-15psr-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="South_Cluster_and_North_Complex">
        South Cluster and North Complex
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadley%E2%80%93Apennine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: South Cluster and North Complex">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The South Cluster, a crater cluster located to the south of the Apollo 15 site near Mons Hadley Delta, is believed to have formed as a result of a
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_crater" title="Secondary crater">
        secondary impact
       </a>
       , or the impact of
       <a href="/wiki/Ejecta" title="Ejecta">
        ejecta
       </a>
       /debris from a larger impact elsewhere on the Moon, probably the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Autolycus_(crater)" title="Autolycus (crater)">
        Autolycus
       </a>
       about 100 mi (160 km) northwest of the cluster's location. This meant that the Apollo 15 astronauts were able to sample material from other parts of the lunar surface without traversing a great distance. The feature also allowed the astronauts to sample material that was originally located deep within the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-otm_8-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-otm-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The south rim of
       <a href="/wiki/Dune_(crater)" title="Dune (crater)">
        Dune
       </a>
       crater within the cluster was visited by the astronauts.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/North_Complex" title="North Complex">
        North Complex
       </a>
       is a collection of several landforms, including craters, that were thought to have been formed by volcanic activity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-otm_8-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-otm-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although the Apollo 15 astronauts were to visit this feature, they were unable to do so due to time constraints and time-consuming issues in obtaining
       <a href="/wiki/Core_sample" title="Core sample">
        core samples
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a15summ_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-a15summ-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
