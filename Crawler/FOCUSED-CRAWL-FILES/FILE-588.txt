      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Moon Lightweight Interior and Telecoms Experiment
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        MoonLITE
       </b>
       ), is a proposed
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        British
       </a>
       space mission to explore the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and develop techniques for future space exploration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-100107_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BBC-100107-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If funded, it will be built by a consortium of UK industry likely including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrey_Satellite_Technology_Ltd" title="Surrey Satellite Technology Ltd">
        Surrey Satellite Technology Ltd
       </a>
       , and it is planned to be launched into
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit" title="Lunar orbit">
        lunar orbit
       </a>
       in 2014.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
          <span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (May 2015)">
           needs update
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The mission concept emerged from a study run by the
       <a href="/wiki/Particle_Physics_and_Astronomy_Research_Council" title="Particle Physics and Astronomy Research Council">
        Particle Physics and Astronomy Research Council
       </a>
       (now the
       <a href="/wiki/Science_and_Technology_Facilities_Council" title="Science and Technology Facilities Council">
        Science and Technology Facilities Council
       </a>
       ) in 2006.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In December 2008, the
       <a href="/wiki/British_National_Space_Centre" title="British National Space Centre">
        British National Space Centre
       </a>
       (part of the
       <a href="/wiki/UK_Space_Agency" title="UK Space Agency">
        UK Space Agency
       </a>
       since April 2010) announced that the project was moving to Phase A study.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is a 12-month study of the mission system, the penetrators and the penetrator descent systems.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives">
        Objectives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=MoonLITE&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Objectives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The mission has both scientific and technological goals. The science goals are concerned with the interior structure, history and current state of the Moon. MoonLITE would deploy four one metre-long penetrators into the lunar surface in order to emplace a global network of seismometers, heat flow sensors and - possibly - volatile detectors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This would allow the internal structure of the Moon to be determined as well as exploring the frequency and origin of moonquakes. It would be the first space mission dedicated to studying the interior structure of the Moon since the experiments emplaced by the Apollo astronauts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-100107_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-BBC-100107-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Beyond the scientific value, the enhanced knowledge of the seismic environment would support engineering design and safety requirements for a future human lunar outpost.
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the technological goals is to demonstrate for the first time a lunar telecommunications network able to communicate with four stations on the lunar surface and relay data back to Earth. Such a comms network would be needed for future robotic and lunar explorers, especially for a human outpost located near the South Pole where line of sight communications would be limited. Another objective is to prove the feasibility of penetrators for deploying scientific payloads. Although extensive work has been carried out on the technique in the US and Japan, the only attempt to use penetrators - the
       <a href="/wiki/Deep_Space_2" title="Deep Space 2">
        Deep Space 2
       </a>
       vehicles aboard NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Polar_Lander" title="Mars Polar Lander">
        Mars Polar Lander
       </a>
       - was unsuccessful. Penetrators could be used at Mars, to deploy a seismic network; at
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       in order to measure the thickness of the ice sheet; and at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enceladus_(moon)" title="Enceladus (moon)">
        Enceladus
       </a>
       to investigate the fissures discovered by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Experimental tests of the penetrators have been carried out by the
       <a href="/wiki/Mullard_Space_Science_Laboratory" title="Mullard Space Science Laboratory">
        Mullard Space Science Laboratory
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/QinetiQ" title="QinetiQ">
        QinetiQ
       </a>
       company in 2008.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The use of low-cost satellite technology in planetary exploration is a further goal of the mission.
      </p>
