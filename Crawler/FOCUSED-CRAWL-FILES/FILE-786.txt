      <p>
       <b>
        Leda
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          LEE
         </span>
         -də
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Λήδα
       </span>
       ), also known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter XIII
        </span>
       </b>
       , is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_T._Kowal" title="Charles T. Kowal">
        Charles T. Kowal
       </a>
       at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Palomar_Observatory" title="Mount Palomar Observatory">
        Mount Palomar Observatory
       </a>
       on September 14, 1974, after three nights' worth of photographic plates had been taken (September 11 through 13; Leda appears on all of them).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kowal_1974_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kowal_1974-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_2702_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_2702-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was named after
       <a href="/wiki/Leda_(mythology)" title="Leda (mythology)">
        Leda
       </a>
       , who was a lover of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       , the Greek equivalent of Jupiter (who came to her in the form of a
       <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">
        swan
       </a>
       ). Kowal suggested the name and the IAU endorsed it in 1975.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_2846_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_2846-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Leda belongs to the
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_group" title="Himalia group">
        Himalia group
       </a>
       , five moons orbiting between 11 and 13 Gm from Jupiter at an inclination of about 27.5°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_2000_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson_2000-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The orbital elements given here are as of January 2000, but they are continuously changing due to
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       and planetary perturbations.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">
        In fiction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Leda_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In fiction">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The 1956 British film
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Fire_Maidens_from_Outer_Space" title="Fire Maidens from Outer Space">
          Fire Maidens from Outer Space
         </a>
        </i>
        was set on Jupiter's 13th moon, although this film was made before the discovery of Leda in 1974.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Brenda_Hiatt" title="Brenda Hiatt">
         Brenda Hiatt's
        </a>
        2013
        <a href="/wiki/Young_adult_fiction" title="Young adult fiction">
         teen
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_fiction_theme" title="Science fiction theme">
         science fiction themed
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Romance_novel" title="Romance novel">
         romance novel
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="external text" href="http://brendahiatt.com/brendas-books/teen-fiction/starstruck/" rel="nofollow">
         Starstruck
        </a>
        , Rigel impresses Marsha with a telescope that clearly sees Leda. Rigel is in turn impressed that Marsha knows so much about the obscure
        <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">
         moon of Jupiter
        </a>
        , to include that it was not even discovered until 1974.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Liu_Cixin" title="Liu Cixin">
         Liu Cixin's
        </a>
        2010 science fiction novel
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Death%27s_End" title="Death's End">
          Death's End
         </a>
        </i>
        , Jupiter XIII is consumed by a man-made blackhole, thus creating the first-ever stable man-made blackhole.
