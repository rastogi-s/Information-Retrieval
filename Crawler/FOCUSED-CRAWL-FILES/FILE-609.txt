   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunar lava tube
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Lunar lava tubes
       </b>
       are sub-surface tunnels on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       that are believed to have formed during basaltic lava flows. When the surface of a
       <a href="/wiki/Lava_tube" title="Lava tube">
        lava tube
       </a>
       cools, it forms a hardened lid that contains the ongoing
       <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
        lava
       </a>
       flow beneath the surface in a conduit-shaped passage. Once the flow of lava diminishes, the tunnel may become drained, forming a hollow void. Lunar lava tubes are formed on surfaces that have a slope that ranges in angle from 0.4–6.5°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tm3_3_289_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-tm3_3_289-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lunar lava tubes may be as wide as 500 metres (1,600 ft) before they become unstable against gravitational collapse. However, stable tubes may still be disrupted by seismic events or meteoroid bombardment.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tm3_4_412_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-tm3_4_412-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The existence of a lava tube is sometimes revealed by the presence of a "skylight", a place in which the roof of the tube has collapsed, leaving a circular hole.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pits2014_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pits2014-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired2011_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wired2011-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observational_evidence">
        Observational evidence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_lava_tube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observational evidence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_collapse_pits.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1174" data-file-width="540" height="478" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Lunar_collapse_pits.jpg/220px-Lunar_collapse_pits.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Lunar_collapse_pits.jpg/330px-Lunar_collapse_pits.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Lunar_collapse_pits.jpg/440px-Lunar_collapse_pits.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_collapse_pits.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Sinuous chain of collapse pits transitioning into a continuous uncollapsed segment of a lunar lava tube. The chain is about 50 km long.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       An area displaying a lava tube and
       <a href="/wiki/Rille" title="Rille">
        rilles
       </a>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Marius_Hills" title="Marius Hills">
        Marius Hills
       </a>
       region.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tm3_3_289_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-tm3_3_289-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2008, an opening to a lava tube in this area may have been discovered by the Japanese
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
        Kaguya
       </a>
       spacecraft.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ng20091026_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ng20091026-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The skylight was photographed in more detail in 2011 by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       , showing both the 65-meter pit and the floor of the cave about 36 meters below.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired2011_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-wired2011-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skylight1_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-skylight1-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Mons_Hadley" title="Mons Hadley">
        Hadley Rille
       </a>
       may have been a partly roofed lava channel, some parts of which have since collapsed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science172_3984_722_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-science172_3984_722-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There may also be lava tubes in the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Serenitatis" title="Mare Serenitatis">
        Mare Serenitatis
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coombs_hawke1992_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-coombs_hawke1992-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DailyNews_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DailyNews-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiscoverMag_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DiscoverMag-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UniverseToday_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UniverseToday-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       has now imaged over 200 pits that show the signature of being skylights into subsurface voids or caverns, ranging in diameter from about 16 feet (5 meters) to more than 2,950 feet (900 m),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-io9_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-io9-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although some of these are likely to be post-flow features rather than volcanic skylights.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wagner2014_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wagner2014-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-1" title="Chandrayaan-1">
        Chandrayaan-1
       </a>
       orbiter imaged a lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Rille" title="Rille">
        rille
       </a>
       , formed by an ancient lunar lava flow, with an uncollapsed segment indicating the likely presence of a lava tube near the lunar equator, measuring about 2 km (1.2 mi) in length and 360 m (1,180 ft) in width.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arya_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Arya-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siliconindia_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-siliconindia-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Gravitometric observations by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GRAIL" title="GRAIL">
        GRAIL
       </a>
       spacecraft suggest the presence of lunar lava tubes with widths of over one kilometer. Assuming a width-to-height ratio of 3:1, such a structure can remain stable with a ceiling that is 2 meters thick. Lava tubes at least 500 m underground can theoretically remain stable with widths of up to 5 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blair2017_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blair2017-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Several groups have proposed robotic missions to explore lunar and Martian lava tubes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pits2014_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-pits2014-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sites_for_human_habitats">
        Sites for human habitats
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_lava_tube&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sites for human habitats">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lunar lava tubes may potentially serve as enclosures for human habitats.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ng20091026_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ng20091026-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coombs_hawke1992_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-coombs_hawke1992-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oneill2009_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-oneill2009-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Tunnels larger than 300 metres (980 ft) in diameter may exist, lying under 40 metres (130 ft) or more of basalt with a stable temperature of −20 °C (−4 °F).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-york_et_al1992_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-york_et_al1992-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These natural tunnels provide protection from
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">
        cosmic ray
       </a>
       radiation,
       <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">
        meteorites
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometeorite" title="Micrometeorite">
        micrometeorites
       </a>
       , and ejecta from impacts. They are shielded from the variations in temperature at the lunar surface, which would provide a
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_habitability" title="Planetary habitability">
        stable environment for inhabitants
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baas33_1037_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-baas33_1037-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lunar lava tubes are typically found along the boundaries between lunar mares and highland regions. This would give ready access to elevated regions for communications, basaltic plains for landing sites and
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       harvesting, and underground mineral resources.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walden_et_al1998_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-walden_et_al1998-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
