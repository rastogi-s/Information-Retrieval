      <p>
       A
       <b>
        lunar regolith simulant
       </b>
       is a terrestrial material synthesized in order to approximate the chemical, mechanical, and engineering properties of, and the mineralogy and particle size distributions of, lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKayEtAl1_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-McKayEtAl1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lunar regolith simulants are used by researchers who wish to research the materials handling, excavation, transportation, and uses of lunar regolith. Samples of actual lunar regolith are too scarce, and too small, for such research.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="MLS-1">
        MLS-1
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_regolith_simulant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: MLS-1">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <b>
        MLS-1
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        Minnesota Lunar Simulant 1
       </b>
       ) is a lunar simulant that was developed at the University of Minnesota. The basaltic rock used in this simulant was mined from a quarry in Duluth, Minnesota. It contains
       <a href="/wiki/Plagioclase" title="Plagioclase">
        plagioclase
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">
        olivine
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">
        pyroxene
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">
        ilmenite
       </a>
       as some of its major minerals. The minerals and grain sizes resemble the chemistry of the Apollo 11
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        mare
       </a>
       material (specifically soil sample 10084).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="JSC-1">
        JSC-1
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_regolith_simulant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: JSC-1">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <b>
        JSC-1
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        Johnson Space Center Number One
       </b>
       ) is a lunar regolith simulant that was developed in 1994 under the auspices of
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center">
        Johnson Space Center
       </a>
       . Its developers intended it to approximate the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil" title="Lunar soil">
        lunar soil
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        maria
       </a>
       . Unlike MLS-1, it simulates a soil that is poor in titanium. It is a
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basaltic
       </a>
       ash with a high glass content.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKayEtAl1_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-McKayEtAl1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Toutanji et al. used JSC-1 to create a sulphur-based
       <a href="/wiki/Lunarcrete" title="Lunarcrete">
        Lunarcrete
       </a>
       simulant.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="JSC-1A">
        JSC-1A
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_regolith_simulant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: JSC-1A">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2005, NASA contracted with Orbital Technologies Corporation (ORBITEC) for a second batch of simulant in three grades:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        JSC-1AF, fine, 27 µm average size
       </li>
       <li>
        JSC-1A, a reproduction of JSC 1, less than 1 mm size
       </li>
       <li>
        JSC-1AC, coarse, a distribution of sizes &lt; 5 mm
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       NASA received 14 metric tons of JSC-1A, and one ton each of AF and AC in 2006. Another 15 tons of JSC-1A and 100 kg of JSC-1F were produced by ORBITEC for commercial sale.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An 8-ton sand box of commercial JSC‐1A is available for daily rental from the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_Solar_System_Exploration_Research_Virtual_Institute_(SSERVI)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NASA Solar System Exploration Research Virtual Institute (SSERVI) (page does not exist)">
        NASA Solar System Exploration Research Virtual Institute (SSERVI)
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       JSC-1A can
       <a href="/wiki/Geopolymer" title="Geopolymer">
        geopolymerize
       </a>
       in an alkaline solutions resulting in a hard, rock-like, material.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alexiadis2016_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Alexiadis2016-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Tests show that the maximum
       <a href="/wiki/Compressive_strength" title="Compressive strength">
        compressive
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Flexural_strength" title="Flexural strength">
        flexural strength
       </a>
       of the 'lunar' geopolymer is comparable to that of conventional cements.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alexiadis2016_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Alexiadis2016-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="FJS-1">
        FJS-1
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_regolith_simulant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: FJS-1">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <b>
        FJS-1
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        Fuji Japanese Simulant 1
       </b>
       ) was developed in Japan from Mount Fuji area basalts. The grain size and mineralogy of these basalts simulate well the samples from the Apollo 14 mission.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="FJS-2">
        FJS-2
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_regolith_simulant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: FJS-2">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       FJS-2 is similar to FJS-1 however
       <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">
        olivine
       </a>
       was added to change the composition slightly. FJS-2 is a better Apollo 14 simulant than FJS-1.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="FJS-3">
        FJS-3
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_regolith_simulant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: FJS-3">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       FJS-3 is composed of the root simulant, FJS-1, with added
       <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">
        olivine
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">
        ilmenite
       </a>
       . FJS-3 is a good simulant for Apollo 11 samples.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
