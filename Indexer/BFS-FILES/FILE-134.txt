       <b>
        Anisotropy
       </b>

       <span class="nowrap">

       is the property of being directionally dependent, which implies different properties in different directions, as opposed to
       <a href="/wiki/Isotropy" title="Isotropy">
        isotropy

       . It can be defined as a difference, when measured along different axes, in a material's
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_property" title="Physical property">

       <a href="/wiki/List_of_materials_properties#Mechanical_properties" title="List of materials properties">

       </a>

       </a>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fields_of_interest">
        Fields of interest
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fields of interest">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_graphics">
        Computer graphics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Computer graphics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the field of
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_graphics" title="Computer graphics">
        computer graphics
       </a>
       , an anisotropic surface changes in appearance as it rotates about its
       <a href="/wiki/Normal_(geometry)" title="Normal (geometry)">
        geometric normal
       </a>
       , as is the case with
       <a href="/wiki/Velvet" title="Velvet">
        velvet
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Anisotropic_filtering" title="Anisotropic filtering">
        Anisotropic filtering
       </a>
       (AF) is a method of enhancing the image quality of textures on surfaces that are far away and steeply angled with respect to the point of view. Older techniques, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Bilinear_filtering" title="Bilinear filtering">
        bilinear
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Trilinear_filtering" title="Trilinear filtering">
        trilinear filtering
       </a>
       , do not take into account the angle a surface is viewed from, which can result in
       <a href="/wiki/Aliasing" title="Aliasing">
        aliasing
       </a>
       or blurring of textures. By reducing detail in one direction more than another, these effects can be reduced.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">
        Chemistry
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemistry">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A chemical anisotropic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(chemistry)" title="Filter (chemistry)">
        filter
       </a>
       , as used to filter particles, is a filter with increasingly smaller interstitial spaces in the direction of filtration so that the
       <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Proximal_and_distal" title="Anatomical terms of location">
        proximal
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">
        regions
       </a>
       filter out larger particles and
       <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Proximal_and_distal" title="Anatomical terms of location">
        distal
       </a>
       regions increasingly remove smaller particles, resulting in greater flow-through and more efficient filtration.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance_spectroscopy" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy">
        NMR spectroscopy
       </a>
       , the orientation of nuclei with respect to the applied magnetic field determines their
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_shift" title="Chemical shift">
        chemical shift
       </a>
       . In this context, anisotropic systems refer to the electron distribution of molecules with abnormally high electron density, like the pi system of
       <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">
        benzene
       </a>
       . This abnormal electron density affects the applied magnetic field and causes the observed chemical shift to change.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence_spectroscopy" title="Fluorescence spectroscopy">
        fluorescence spectroscopy
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence_anisotropy" title="Fluorescence anisotropy">
        fluorescence anisotropy
       </a>
       , calculated from the
       <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">
        polarization
       </a>
       properties of fluorescence from samples excited with plane-polarized light, is used, e.g., to determine the shape of a macromolecule. Anisotropy measurements reveal the average angular displacement of the fluorophore that occurs between absorption and subsequent emission of a photon.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Real-world_imagery">
        Real-world imagery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Real-world imagery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Images of a gravity-bound or man-made environment are particularly anisotropic in the orientation domain, with more image structure located at orientations parallel with or orthogonal to the direction of gravity (vertical and horizontal).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physics">
        Physics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Physics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plasma-lamp_2.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1609" data-file-width="1589" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Plasma-lamp_2.jpg/300px-Plasma-lamp_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Plasma-lamp_2.jpg/450px-Plasma-lamp_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Plasma-lamp_2.jpg/600px-Plasma-lamp_2.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plasma-lamp_2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A
         <a href="/wiki/Plasma_globe" title="Plasma globe">
          plasma lamp
         </a>
         displaying the nature of
         <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
          plasmas
         </a>
         , in this case, the phenomenon of "filamentation"
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physicists" title="Physicists">
        Physicists
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">
        University of California, Berkeley
       </a>
       reported about their detection of the cosine anisotropy in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">
        cosmic microwave background radiation
       </a>
       in 1977. Their experiment demonstrated the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">
        Doppler shift
       </a>
       caused by the movement of the earth with respect to the
       <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">
        early Universe matter
       </a>
       , the source of the radiation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cosmic anisotropy has also been seen in the alignment of galaxies' rotation axes and polarisation angles of quasars.
      </p>
      <p>
       Physicists use the term anisotropy to describe direction-dependent properties of materials.
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_anisotropy" title="Magnetic anisotropy">
        Magnetic anisotropy
       </a>
       , for example, may occur in a
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       , so that its magnetic field is oriented in a preferred direction. Plasmas may also show "filamentation" (such as that seen in
       <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">
        lightning
       </a>
       or a
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_globe" title="Plasma globe">
        plasma globe
       </a>
       ) that is directional.
      </p>
      <p>
       An
       <i>
        anisotropic liquid
       </i>
       has the fluidity of a normal liquid, but has an average structural order relative to each other along the molecular axis, unlike water or
       <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">
        chloroform
       </a>
       , which contain no structural ordering of the molecules.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystals" title="Liquid crystals">
        Liquid crystals
       </a>
       are examples of anisotropic liquids.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some materials
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_conduction" title="Heat conduction">
        conduct heat
       </a>
       in a way that is isotropic, that is independent of spatial orientation around the heat source. Heat conduction is more commonly anisotropic, which implies that detailed geometric modeling of typically diverse materials being thermally managed is required. The materials used to transfer and reject heat from the heat source in
       <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">
        electronics
       </a>
       are often anisotropic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature8April2013_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nature8April2013-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Many
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">
        crystals
       </a>
       are anisotropic to
       <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
        light
       </a>
       ("optical anisotropy"), and exhibit properties such as
       <a href="/wiki/Birefringence" title="Birefringence">
        birefringence
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal_optics" title="Crystal optics">
        Crystal optics
       </a>
       describes light propagation in these media. An "axis of anisotropy" is defined as the axis along which isotropy is broken (or an axis of symmetry, such as normal to crystalline layers). Some materials can have multiple such
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_axis" title="Optical axis">
        optical axes
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geophysics_and_geology">
        Geophysics and geology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geophysics and geology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Seismic_anisotropy" title="Seismic anisotropy">
        Seismic anisotropy
       </a>
       is the variation of seismic wavespeed with direction. Seismic anisotropy is an indicator of long range order in a material, where features smaller than the seismic
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelength
       </a>
       (e.g., crystals, cracks, pores, layers or inclusions) have a dominant alignment. This alignment leads to a directional variation of
       <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">
        elasticity
       </a>
       wavespeed. Measuring the effects of anisotropy in seismic data can provide important information about processes and mineralogy in the Earth; indeed, significant seismic anisotropy has been detected in the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Inner_core" title="Inner core">
        inner core
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological" title="Geological">
        Geological
       </a>
       formations with distinct layers of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary" title="Sedimentary">
        sedimentary
       </a>
       material can exhibit electrical anisotropy;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">
        electrical conductivity
       </a>
       in one direction (e.g. parallel to a layer), is different from that in another (e.g. perpendicular to a layer). This property is used in the gas and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_exploration" title="Oil exploration">
        oil exploration
       </a>
       industry to identify
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">
        hydrocarbon
       </a>
       -bearing sands in sequences of
       <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">
        sand
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">
        shale
       </a>
       . Sand-bearing hydrocarbon assets have high
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistivity" title="Resistivity">
        resistivity
       </a>
       (low conductivity), whereas shales have lower resistivity.
       <a href="/wiki/Formation_evaluation" title="Formation evaluation">
        Formation evaluation
       </a>
       instruments measure this conductivity/resistivity and the results are used to help find oil and gas in wells.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_conductivity" title="Hydraulic conductivity">
        hydraulic conductivity
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">
        aquifers
       </a>
       is often anisotropic for the same reason. When calculating groundwater flow to
       <a href="/wiki/Drainage" title="Drainage">
        drains
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or to
       <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">
        wells
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the difference between horizontal and vertical permeability must be taken into account, otherwise the results may be subject to error.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most common rock-forming
       <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">
        minerals
       </a>
       are anisotropic, including
       <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">
        quartz
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">
        feldspar
       </a>
       . Anisotropy in minerals is most reliably seen in their
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_mineralogy" title="Optical mineralogy">
        optical properties
       </a>
       . An example of an isotropic mineral is
       <a href="/wiki/Garnet" title="Garnet">
        garnet
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_acoustics">
        Medical acoustics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Medical acoustics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Anisotropy is also a well-known property in medical ultrasound imaging describing a different resulting
       <a href="/wiki/Echogenicity" title="Echogenicity">
        echogenicity
       </a>
       of soft tissues, such as tendons, when the angle of the transducer is changed. Tendon fibers appear hyperechoic (bright) when the transducer is perpendicular to the tendon, but can appear hypoechoic (darker) when the transducer is angled obliquely. This can be a source of interpretation error for inexperienced practitioners.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Material_science_and_engineering">
        Material science and engineering
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Material science and engineering">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Anisotropy, in Material Science, is a material's directional dependence of a physical property. Most materials exhibit anisotropic behavior. An example would be the dependence of
       <a href="/wiki/Young%27s_modulus" title="Young's modulus">
        Young's modulus
       </a>
       on the direction of load.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Anisotropy in polycrystalline materials can also be due to certain
       <a href="/wiki/Texture_(crystalline)" title="Texture (crystalline)">
        texture
       </a>
       patterns often produced during manufacturing of the material. In the case of rolling, "stringers" of texture are produced in the direction of rolling, which can lead to vastly different properties in the rolling and transverse directions. Some materials, such as wood and fibre-reinforced
       <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">
        composites
       </a>
       are very anisotropic, being much stronger along the grain/fibre than across it. Metals and alloys tend to be more isotropic, though they can sometimes exhibit significant anisotropic behaviour. This is especially important in processes such as
       <a href="/wiki/Deep_drawing" title="Deep drawing">
        deep-drawing
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Wood is a naturally anisotropic (but often simplified to be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transversely_isotropic" title="Transversely isotropic">
        transversely isotropic
       </a>
       ) material. Its properties vary widely when measured with or against the growth grain. For example, wood's strength and hardness is different for the same sample measured in different orientations.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the Mechanics of Continuum Materials isotropy and anisotropy are rigorously described through the symmetry group of the constitutive relation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Microfabrication">
        Microfabrication
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Microfabrication">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Anisotropic etching techniques (such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_reactive_ion_etching" title="Deep reactive ion etching">
        deep reactive ion etching
       </a>
       ) are used in microfabrication processes to create well defined microscopic features with a high
       <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio" title="Aspect ratio">
        aspect ratio
       </a>
       . These features are commonly used in
       <a href="/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems" title="Microelectromechanical systems">
        MEMS
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microfluidic" title="Microfluidic">
        microfluidic
       </a>
       devices, where the anisotropy of the features is needed to impart desired optical, electrical, or physical properties to the device. Anisotropic etching can also refer to certain chemical etchants used to etch a certain material preferentially over certain crystallographic planes (e.g., KOH etching of
       <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">
        silicon
       </a>
       [100] produces pyramid-like structures)
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Neuroscience">
        Neuroscience
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Neuroscience">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffusion_tensor_imaging" title="Diffusion tensor imaging">
        Diffusion tensor imaging
       </a>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">
        MRI
       </a>
       technique that involves measuring the fractional anisotropy of the random motion (
       <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">
        Brownian motion
       </a>
       ) of water molecules in the brain. Water molecules located in
       <a href="/wiki/White_matter" title="White matter">
        fiber tracts
       </a>
       are more likely to be anisotropic, since they are restricted in their movement (they move more in the dimension parallel to the fiber tract rather than in the two dimensions orthogonal to it), whereas water molecules dispersed in the rest of the brain have less restricted movement and therefore display more isotropy. This difference in fractional anisotropy is exploited to create a map of the fiber tracts in the brains of the individual.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_radiative_transfer">
        Atmospheric radiative transfer
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anisotropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Atmospheric radiative transfer">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>

             {\displaystyle A(\Omega _{i},\Omega _{v})={\frac {\gamma (\Omega _{i},\Omega _{v})}{P(\Omega _{i})}}}
            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="A(\Omega _{i},\Omega _{v})={\frac {\gamma (\Omega _{i},\Omega _{v})}{P(\Omega _{i})}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ffefa34a1e1861761bdd2bb49159100ac276f00" style="vertical-align: -2.671ex; width:22.997ex; height:6.509ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       It is of interest because, with knowledge of the anisotropy function as defined, a measurement of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BRDF" title="BRDF">
        BRDF
       </a>
       from a single viewing direction (say,
       <span class="mwe-math-element">

       </span>
       ) yields a measure of the total scene reflectance (Planar Albedo) for that specific incident geometry (say,
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
