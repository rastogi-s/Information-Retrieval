   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Ray system
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        ray system
       </b>
       comprises radial streaks of fine
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ejecta" title="Ejecta">
         ejecta
        </a>
       </i>
       thrown out during the formation of an
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       , looking somewhat like many thin spokes coming from the hub of a wheel. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Line_(mathematics)#Ray" title="Line (mathematics)">
        rays
       </a>
       may extend for lengths up to several times the
       <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">
        diameter
       </a>
       of their originating crater, and are often accompanied by small secondary craters formed by larger chunks of ejecta. Ray systems have been identified on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Kamil_Crater" title="Kamil Crater">
        Kamil Crater
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       , and some
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moons
       </a>
       of the outer planets. Originally it was thought that they existed only on
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       or moons lacking an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" title="Celestial body atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       , but more recently they have been identified on
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       in infrared images taken from orbit by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/2001_Mars_Odyssey" title="2001 Mars Odyssey">
         2001 Mars Odyssey
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">
        '
       </span>
       s
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_Emission_Imaging_System" title="Thermal Emission Imaging System">
        thermal imager
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gratteri.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="427" data-file-width="500" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Gratteri.jpg/250px-Gratteri.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Gratteri.jpg/375px-Gratteri.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cd/Gratteri.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gratteri.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Gratteri crater, a rayed crater on
         <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
          Mars
         </a>
         that was imaged by
         <a href="/wiki/Thermal_Emission_Imaging_System" title="Thermal Emission Imaging System">
          THEMIS
         </a>
         at night. Image covers an area 32 km across.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Rays appear at visible, and in some cases infrared wavelengths, when ejecta are made of material with different reflectivity (i.e.,
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       ) or thermal properties from the surface on which they are deposited. Typically, visible rays have a higher albedo than the surrounding surface. More rarely an impact will excavate low albedo material, for example
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basaltic
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
        lava
       </a>
       deposits on the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       . Thermal rays, as seen on Mars, are especially apparent at night when slopes and shadows do not influence the infrared energy emitted by the Martian surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       The layering of rays across other surface features can be useful as an indicator of the relative age of the impact crater, because over time various processes obliterate the rays. On non-atmosphered bodies such as the Moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weathering" title="Space weathering">
        space weathering
       </a>
       from exposure to
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">
        cosmic rays
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">
        micrometeorites
       </a>
       causes a steady reduction of the differential between the ejecta's albedo and that of the underlying material. Micrometeorites in particular produce a glassy melt in the
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       that lowers the
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       . Rays can also become covered by
       <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
        lava
       </a>
       flows, or by other impact craters or ejecta.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_rays">
        Lunar rays
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ray_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lunar rays">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AS15-94-12836.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="909" data-file-width="900" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/AS15-94-12836.jpg/240px-AS15-94-12836.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/AS15-94-12836.jpg/360px-AS15-94-12836.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/AS15-94-12836.jpg/480px-AS15-94-12836.jpg 2x" width="240"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AS15-94-12836.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Asymmetrical ray system about the lunar crater
         <a href="/wiki/Proclus_(crater)" title="Proclus (crater)">
          Proclus
         </a>
         (
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">

        </div>
       </div>
      </div>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pierazzo_crater_Clementine_mosaic.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Pierazzo crater (Mosaic of
         <a href="/wiki/Clementine_(spacecraft)" title="Clementine (spacecraft)">
          Clementine
         </a>
         images)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The physical nature of lunar rays has historically been a subject of speculation. Early hypotheses suggested that they were deposits of salt from evaporated water. Later they were thought to be deposits of volcanic ash or streaks of dust. After the impact origin of craters became accepted,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Shoemaker" title="Eugene Shoemaker">
        Eugene Shoemaker
       </a>
       suggested during the 1960s that the rays were the result of fragmented ejecta material.
      </p>
      <p>
       Recent studies suggest that the relative brightness of a lunar ray system is not always a reliable indicator of the age of a ray system. Instead the albedo also depends on the portion of
       <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">
        iron oxide
       </a>
       (FeO). Low portions of FeO result in brighter materials, so such a ray system can retain its lighter appearance for longer periods. Thus the material composition needs to be factored into the albedo analysis to determine age.
      </p>
      <p>
       Among the lunar craters on the near side with pronounced ray systems are
       <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_(crater)" title="Aristarchus (crater)">
        Aristarchus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Copernicus_(lunar_crater)" title="Copernicus (lunar crater)">
        Copernicus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(lunar_crater)" title="Kepler (lunar crater)">
        Kepler
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Proclus_(crater)" title="Proclus (crater)">
        Proclus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_(crater)" title="Dionysius (crater)">
        Dionysius
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Censorinus_(crater)" title="Censorinus (crater)">
        Censorinus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Glushko_(crater)" title="Glushko (crater)">
        Glushko
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tycho_(crater)" title="Tycho (crater)">
        Tycho
       </a>
       . Similar ray systems also occur on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far_side_(Moon)" title="Far side (Moon)">
        far side
       </a>
       of the Moon, such as the rays radiating from the craters
       <a href="/wiki/Giordano_Bruno_(crater)" title="Giordano Bruno (crater)">
        Giordano Bruno
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Necho_(crater)" title="Necho (crater)">
        Necho
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ohm_(crater)" title="Ohm (crater)">
        Ohm
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jackson_(crater)" title="Jackson (crater)">
        Jackson
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/King_(crater)" title="King (crater)">
        King
       </a>
       , and the small but prominent
       <a href="/wiki/Pierazzo_(crater)" title="Pierazzo (crater)">
        Pierazzo
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/North_Ray_(crater)" title="North Ray (crater)">
        North Ray
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Ray_(crater)" title="South Ray (crater)">
        South Ray
       </a>
       craters, each with a clear ray system, were observed from the ground by the astronauts of
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">
        Apollo 16
       </a>
       in 1972.
      </p>
