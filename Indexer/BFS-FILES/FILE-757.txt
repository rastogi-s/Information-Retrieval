   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Peak of eternal light
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        peak of eternal light
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        PEL
       </b>
       ) is a point on an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_body" title="Astronomical body">
        astronomical body
       </a>
       within the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       that is always in
       <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
        sunlight
       </a>
       . Such a peak must have high
       <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">
        altitude
       </a>
       and be on a body with very small
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        axial tilt
       </a>
       . The existence of such peaks was first postulated by Beer and Mädler in 1837. The pair said of the lunar polar mountains, "...many of these peaks have (with the exception of eclipses caused by the Earth) eternal sunshine."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These polar peaks were later mentioned by
       <a href="/wiki/Camille_Flammarion" title="Camille Flammarion">
        Camille Flammarion
       </a>
       in 1879, who speculated that there may exist
       <i>
        pics de lumière éternelle
       </i>
       at the poles of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       PELs would be advantageous for
       <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">
        space exploration
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">
        colonization
       </a>
       due to the ability of an electrical device located there to receive
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
        solar power
       </a>
       regardless of the time of day or day of the year, and the relatively stable temperature range.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="On_the_Moon">
        On the Moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peak_of_eternal_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: On the Moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:418px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Southern_Pole_Peaks_of_Eternal_Light.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="236" data-file-width="421" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Lunar_Southern_Pole_Peaks_of_Eternal_Light.jpg/416px-Lunar_Southern_Pole_Peaks_of_Eternal_Light.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/99/Lunar_Southern_Pole_Peaks_of_Eternal_Light.jpg 1.5x" width="416"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Southern_Pole_Peaks_of_Eternal_Light.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Lunar South Pole, 4 peaks are identified which are illuminated more than 80% of the time
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        ESA
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/SMART-1" title="SMART-1">
        SMART-1
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Clementine_(spacecraft)" title="Clementine (spacecraft)">
        Clementine
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">
        JAXA
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
        SELENE
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecrafts
       </a>
       made maps of the lunar poles that have been used to identify sites receiving eternal light. The orbiting craft monitored the lighting at the poles and looked for seasonal variations as well as mapping the relief so that peaks of eternal light could be identified topographically.
      </p>
      <p>
       Peaks of eternal light on the Moon would not be perfectly "eternal", since sunlight would still be cut off occasionally by the Earth's shadow during a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       (which can last up to six hours) and by the shadows of other mountains and
       <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">
        plateaus
       </a>
       . The term "peak of eternal light" for the Moon is commonly used in the technical literature and newspaper articles as a popularization and is surprisingly applied even though the duration of illumination is not permanent, excluding the lunar eclipses. No peaks of eternal light have been positively identified on the moon, but many peaks have been detected that, via simulations based on imaging and laser and radar topography, appear to be illuminated for greater than 80% of a lunar year. Of course the concept of "eternity" in this case lasts only during the lifetime of the Sun (roughly 10 billion years) and will end when the Sun runs out of fuel and becomes a
       <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">
        red giant
       </a>
       , which will likely absorb and destroy the Moon, or else end when the Sun becomes a
       <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">
        white dwarf
       </a>
       and no longer produces enough light to directly illuminate the lunar surface.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_north_pole">
        Lunar north pole
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peak_of_eternal_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lunar north pole">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Based on images from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_mission" title="Clementine mission">
        Clementine lunar mission
       </a>
       , a team from
       <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">
        Johns Hopkins University
       </a>
       determined that four locations along the rim of the
       <a href="/wiki/Peary_(crater)" title="Peary (crater)">
        Peary
       </a>
       crater are candidates for peaks of eternal light. This crater lies near the north pole of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . Clementine's images were taken during the northern summer and were unable to confirm whether these four peaks would be shaded at any point during the winter. Further data from the
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
        SELENE
       </a>
       spaceprobe determined that one peak at Peary Crater receives sunlight for 89% of a lunar year, the highest level of illumination predicted for any peak of eternal light on the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_south_pole">
        Lunar south pole
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peak_of_eternal_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lunar south pole">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The lunar south pole is situated in a huge depression, leading to 16 km altitude differences over the region. Careful analysis of imagery and topographic conditions on the lunar South Pole by teams from NASA and the ESA revealed a small number of illuminated ridges within 15 km of the pole, each of them much like an island of no more than a few hundred meters across in an ocean of eternal darkness, where a lander could receive near-permanent lighting (for ~70–90% of the lunar winter, and likely all of the lunar summer).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Malapert Mountain region, on the rim of the
       <a href="/wiki/Malapert_(crater)" title="Malapert (crater)">
        Malapert
       </a>
       crater 122 km from the lunar south pole on the Earth-facing side, may also have high levels of illumination. One study estimates the Malapert Mountain region to receive less than full sunlight 11% of the time. Sunlight exposure varies by year due to the Moon's orbit being 1.5 degree off plane with the sun. 2005 estimates of sunlight coverage of the Malapert Mountain region found only six partially lit or unlit events that year: 0–159 hours of complete sunset per event and 41–199 hours of complete or partial sunset per event.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A later study using a combination of both Clementine imaging and SELENE topographical data estimated only 74% of full sunlight for the year 2020.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Icarus_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Icarus-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This study found that two points only ~8 km from each other along a straight ridge extending from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shackleton_Crater" title="Shackleton Crater">
        Shackleton Crater
       </a>
       at the Lunar South Pole are illuminated a combined ~94% of a lunar year. This is because both points cast shadows upon each other during different times of the lunar year, and only a few times of darkness occur when further peaks throw shadows over both of these points simultaneously.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="On_Mercury">
        On Mercury
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peak_of_eternal_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: On Mercury">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The existence of peaks of eternal light on
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       has also been theorized, but due to the lack of detailed mapping, no Mercurian peaks have been positively confirmed or ruled out as such. This may change when the data from the orbiter
       <a href="/wiki/MESSENGER" title="MESSENGER">
        MESSENGER
       </a>
       is fully analyzed. Such peaks would not even suffer the sporadic shadow of an eclipse, as Mercury has no known
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moons
       </a>
       .
      </p>
