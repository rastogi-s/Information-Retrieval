   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Space
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Space
       </b>
       is the boundless three-dimensional extent in which
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">
        objects
       </a>
       and events have relative position and direction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Physical space is often conceived in three
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear" title="Linear">
        linear
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">
        dimensions
       </a>
       , although modern
       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
        physicists
       </a>
       usually consider it, with
       <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">
        time
       </a>
       , to be part of a boundless four-dimensional
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuum_(theory)" title="Continuum (theory)">
        continuum
       </a>
       known as
       <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
        spacetime
       </a>
       . The concept of space is considered to be of fundamental importance to an understanding of the physical
       <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">
        universe
       </a>
       . However, disagreement continues between
       <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">
        philosophers
       </a>
       over whether it is itself an entity, a relationship between entities, or part of a
       <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_framework" title="Conceptual framework">
        conceptual framework
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Debates concerning the nature, essence and the mode of existence of space date back to antiquity; namely, to treatises like the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue)" title="Timaeus (dialogue)">
         Timaeus
        </a>
       </i>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
        Plato
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">
        Socrates
       </a>
       in his reflections on what the Greeks called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Kh%C3%B4ra" title="Khôra">
         khôra
        </a>
       </i>
       (i.e. "space"), or in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Physics_(Aristotle)" title="Physics (Aristotle)">
         Physics
        </a>
       </i>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       (Book IV, Delta) in the definition of
       <i>
        topos
       </i>
       (i.e. place), or in the later "geometrical conception of place" as "space
       <i>
        qua
       </i>
       extension" in the
       <i>
        Discourse on Place
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        Qawl fi al-Makan
       </i>
       ) of the 11th-century Arab polymath
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alhazen" title="Alhazen">
        Alhazen
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many of these classical philosophical questions were discussed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
        Renaissance
       </a>
       and then reformulated in the 17th century, particularly during the early development of
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">
        classical mechanics
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Isaac Newton
       </a>
       's view, space was absolute—in the sense that it existed permanently and independently of whether there was any matter in the space.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_philosopher" title="Natural philosopher">
        natural philosophers
       </a>
       , notably
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Leibniz">
        Gottfried Leibniz
       </a>
       , thought instead that space was in fact a collection of relations between objects, given by their
       <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">
        distance
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direction_(geometry)" title="Direction (geometry)">
        direction
       </a>
       from one another. In the 18th century, the philosopher and theologian
       <a href="/wiki/George_Berkeley" title="George Berkeley">
        George Berkeley
       </a>
       attempted to refute the "visibility of spatial depth" in his
       <i>
        Essay Towards a New Theory of Vision
       </i>
       . Later, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metaphysic" title="Metaphysic">
        metaphysician
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
        Immanuel Kant
       </a>
       said that the concepts of space and time are not empirical ones derived from experiences of the outside world—they are elements of an already given systematic framework that humans possess and use to structure all experiences. Kant referred to the experience of "space" in his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Critique_of_Pure_Reason" title="Critique of Pure Reason">
         Critique of Pure Reason
        </a>
       </i>
       as being a subjective "pure
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">
         a priori
        </a>
       </i>
       form of intuition".
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 19th and 20th centuries mathematicians began to examine geometries that are
       <a href="/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry" title="Non-Euclidean geometry">
        non-Euclidean
       </a>
       , in which space is conceived as
       <i>
        curved
       </i>
       , rather than
       <i>
        flat
       </i>
       . According to
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Albert Einstein
       </a>
       's theory of
       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
        general relativity
       </a>
       , space around
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_field" title="Gravitational field">
        gravitational fields
       </a>
       deviates from Euclidean space.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Experimental
       <a href="/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity" title="Tests of general relativity">
        tests of general relativity
       </a>
       have confirmed that non-Euclidean geometries provide a better model for the shape of space.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_of_space">
        Philosophy of space
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Leibniz_and_Newton">
        Leibniz and Newton
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gottfried_Wilhelm_von_Leibniz.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="316" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Gottfried_Wilhelm_von_Leibniz.jpg/150px-Gottfried_Wilhelm_von_Leibniz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Gottfried_Wilhelm_von_Leibniz.jpg/225px-Gottfried_Wilhelm_von_Leibniz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Gottfried_Wilhelm_von_Leibniz.jpg/300px-Gottfried_Wilhelm_von_Leibniz.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gottfried_Wilhelm_von_Leibniz.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Leibniz">
          Gottfried Leibniz
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the seventeenth century, the
       <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_space_and_time" title="Philosophy of space and time">
        philosophy of space and time
       </a>
       emerged as a central issue in
       <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">
        epistemology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">
        metaphysics
       </a>
       . At its heart,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Leibniz">
        Gottfried Leibniz
       </a>
       , the German philosopher-mathematician, and
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Isaac Newton
       </a>
       , the English physicist-mathematician, set out two opposing theories of what space is. Rather than being an entity that independently exists over and above other matter, Leibniz held that space is no more than the collection of spatial relations between objects in the world: "space is that which results from places taken together".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Unoccupied regions are those that
       <i>
        could
       </i>
       have objects in them, and thus spatial relations with other places. For Leibniz, then, space was an idealised
       <a href="/wiki/Abstraction" title="Abstraction">
        abstraction
       </a>
       from the relations between individual entities or their possible locations and therefore could not be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_probability_distribution" title="Continuous probability distribution">
        continuous
       </a>
       but must be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discrete_probability_distribution" title="Discrete probability distribution">
        discrete
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Space could be thought of in a similar way to the relations between family members. Although people in the family are related to one another, the relations do not exist independently of the people.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Leibniz argued that space could not exist independently of objects in the world because that implies a difference between two universes exactly alike except for the location of the material world in each universe. But since there would be no observational way of telling these universes apart then, according to the
       <a href="/wiki/Identity_of_indiscernibles" title="Identity of indiscernibles">
        identity of indiscernibles
       </a>
       , there would be no real difference between them. According to the
       <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_sufficient_reason" title="Principle of sufficient reason">
        principle of sufficient reason
       </a>
       , any theory of space that implied that there could be these two possible universes must therefore be wrong.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1074" data-file-width="780" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg/150px-GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg/225px-GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg/300px-GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
          Isaac Newton
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Newton took space to be more than relations between material objects and based his position on
       <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">
        observation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">
        experimentation
       </a>
       . For a
       <a href="/wiki/Relational_theory" title="Relational theory">
        relationist
       </a>
       there can be no real difference between
       <a href="/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference" title="Inertial frame of reference">
        inertial motion
       </a>
       , in which the object travels with constant
       <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">
        velocity
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Non-inertial_reference_frame" title="Non-inertial reference frame">
        non-inertial motion
       </a>
       , in which the velocity changes with time, since all spatial measurements are relative to other objects and their motions. But Newton argued that since non-inertial motion generates
       <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">
        forces
       </a>
       , it must be absolute.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He used the example of
       <a href="/wiki/Bucket_argument" title="Bucket argument">
        water in a spinning bucket
       </a>
       to demonstrate his argument.
       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
        Water
       </a>
       in a
       <a href="/wiki/Bucket" title="Bucket">
        bucket
       </a>
       is hung from a rope and set to spin, starts with a flat surface. After a while, as the bucket continues to spin, the surface of the water becomes concave. If the bucket's spinning is stopped then the surface of the water remains concave as it continues to spin. The concave surface is therefore apparently not the result of relative motion between the bucket and the water.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Instead, Newton argued, it must be a result of non-inertial motion relative to space itself. For several centuries the bucket argument was considered decisive in showing that space must exist independently of matter.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Kant">
        Kant
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Immanuel_Kant_(painted_portrait).jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1388" data-file-width="964" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Immanuel_Kant_%28painted_portrait%29.jpg/150px-Immanuel_Kant_%28painted_portrait%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Immanuel_Kant_%28painted_portrait%29.jpg/225px-Immanuel_Kant_%28painted_portrait%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Immanuel_Kant_%28painted_portrait%29.jpg/300px-Immanuel_Kant_%28painted_portrait%29.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Immanuel_Kant_(painted_portrait).jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
          Immanuel Kant
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the eighteenth century the German philosopher
       <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
        Immanuel Kant
       </a>
       developed a theory of
       <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">
        knowledge
       </a>
       in which knowledge about space can be both
       <i>
        a priori
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytic-synthetic_distinction" title="Analytic-synthetic distinction">
         synthetic
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to Kant, knowledge about space is
       <i>
        synthetic
       </i>
       , in that statements about space are not simply true by virtue of the meaning of the words in the statement. In his work, Kant rejected the view that space must be either a substance or relation. Instead he came to the conclusion that space and time are not discovered by humans to be objective features of the world, but imposed by us as part of a framework for organizing experience.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-Euclidean_geometry">
        Non-Euclidean geometry
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry" title="Non-Euclidean geometry">
        Non-Euclidean geometry
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sphere_closed_path.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="570" data-file-width="510" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Sphere_closed_path.svg/150px-Sphere_closed_path.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Sphere_closed_path.svg/225px-Sphere_closed_path.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Sphere_closed_path.svg/300px-Sphere_closed_path.svg.png 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sphere_closed_path.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Spherical_geometry" title="Spherical geometry">
          Spherical geometry
         </a>
         is similar to
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical_geometry" title="Elliptical geometry">
          elliptical geometry
         </a>
         . On a
         <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
          sphere
         </a>
         (the
         <a href="/wiki/Surface_(topology)" title="Surface (topology)">
          surface
         </a>
         of a
         <a href="/wiki/Ball_(mathematics)" title="Ball (mathematics)">
          ball
         </a>
         ) there are no
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parallel_line" title="Parallel line">
          parallel lines
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Euclid's
       <i>
        Elements
       </i>
       contained five postulates that form the basis for Euclidean geometry. One of these, the
       <a href="/wiki/Parallel_postulate" title="Parallel postulate">
        parallel postulate
       </a>
       , has been the subject of debate among mathematicians for many centuries. It states that on any
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">
        plane
       </a>
       on which there is a straight line
       <i>
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </i>
       and a point
       <i>
        P
       </i>
       not on
       <i>
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </i>
       , there is exactly one straight line
       <i>
        L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </i>
       on the plane that passes through the point
       <i>
        P
       </i>
       and is parallel to the straight line
       <i>
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </i>
       . Until the 19th century, few doubted the truth of the postulate; instead debate centered over whether it was necessary as an axiom, or whether it was a theory that could be derived from the other axioms.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Around 1830 though, the
       <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_Bolyai" title="János Bolyai">
        János Bolyai
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russian
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikolai_Ivanovich_Lobachevsky" title="Nikolai Ivanovich Lobachevsky">
        Nikolai Ivanovich Lobachevsky
       </a>
       separately published treatises on a type of geometry that does not include the parallel postulate, called
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry" title="Hyperbolic geometry">
        hyperbolic geometry
       </a>
       . In this geometry, an
       <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">
        infinite
       </a>
       number of parallel lines pass through the point
       <i>
        P
       </i>
       . Consequently, the sum of angles in a triangle is less than 180° and the ratio of a
       <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">
        circle
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Circumference" title="Circumference">
        circumference
       </a>
       to its
       <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">
        diameter
       </a>
       is greater than
       <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">
        pi
       </a>
       . In the 1850s,
       <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann" title="Bernhard Riemann">
        Bernhard Riemann
       </a>
       developed an equivalent theory of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical_geometry" title="Elliptical geometry">
        elliptical geometry
       </a>
       , in which no parallel lines pass through
       <i>
        P
       </i>
       . In this geometry, triangles have more than 180° and circles have a ratio of circumference-to-diameter that is less than
       <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">
        pi
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span id="Gauss_and_Poincar.C3.A9">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gauss_and_Poincaré">
        Gauss and Poincaré
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carl_Friedrich_Gauss.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1180" data-file-width="917" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss.jpg/150px-Carl_Friedrich_Gauss.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss.jpg/225px-Carl_Friedrich_Gauss.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss.jpg/300px-Carl_Friedrich_Gauss.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carl_Friedrich_Gauss.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">
          Carl Friedrich Gauss
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Young_Poincare.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="283" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Young_Poincare.jpg/150px-Young_Poincare.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Young_Poincare.jpg/225px-Young_Poincare.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/db/Young_Poincare.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Young_Poincare.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9" title="Henri Poincaré">
          Henri Poincaré
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Although there was a prevailing Kantian consensus at the time, once non-Euclidean geometries had been formalised, some began to wonder whether or not physical space is curved.
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">
        Carl Friedrich Gauss
       </a>
       , a German mathematician, was the first to consider an empirical investigation of the geometrical structure of space. He thought of making a test of the sum of the angles of an enormous stellar triangle, and there are reports that he actually carried out a test, on a small scale, by
       <a href="/wiki/Triangulation" title="Triangulation">
        triangulating
       </a>
       mountain tops in Germany.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9" title="Henri Poincaré">
        Henri Poincaré
       </a>
       , a French mathematician and physicist of the late 19th century, introduced an important insight in which he attempted to demonstrate the futility of any attempt to discover which geometry applies to space by experiment.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He considered the predicament that would face scientists if they were confined to the surface of an imaginary large sphere with particular properties, known as a
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere-world" title="Sphere-world">
        sphere-world
       </a>
       . In this world, the
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       is taken to vary in such a way that all objects expand and contract in similar proportions in different places on the sphere. With a suitable falloff in temperature, if the scientists try to use measuring rods to determine the sum of the angles in a triangle, they can be deceived into thinking that they inhabit a plane, rather than a spherical surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In fact, the scientists cannot in principle determine whether they inhabit a plane or sphere and, Poincaré argued, the same is true for the debate over whether real space is Euclidean or not. For him, which geometry was used to describe space was a matter of
       <a href="/wiki/Conventionalism" title="Conventionalism">
        convention
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since
       <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">
        Euclidean geometry
       </a>
       is simpler than non-Euclidean geometry, he assumed the former would always be used to describe the 'true' geometry of the world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Einstein">
        Einstein
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Albert_Einstein_Head.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4333" data-file-width="3250" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Albert_Einstein_Head.jpg/150px-Albert_Einstein_Head.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Albert_Einstein_Head.jpg/225px-Albert_Einstein_Head.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Albert_Einstein_Head.jpg/300px-Albert_Einstein_Head.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Albert_Einstein_Head.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
          Albert Einstein
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1905,
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Albert Einstein
       </a>
       published his
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_theory_of_relativity" title="Special theory of relativity">
        special theory of relativity
       </a>
       , which led to the concept that space and time can be viewed as a single construct known as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
         spacetime
        </a>
       </i>
       . In this theory, the
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
        speed of light
       </a>
       in a
       <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">
        vacuum
       </a>
       is the same for all observers—which has
       <a href="/wiki/Relativity_of_simultaneity" title="Relativity of simultaneity">
        the result
       </a>
       that two events that appear simultaneous to one particular observer will not be simultaneous to another observer if the observers are moving with respect to one another. Moreover, an observer will measure a moving clock to
       <a href="/wiki/Time_dilation" title="Time dilation">
        tick more slowly
       </a>
       than one that is stationary with respect to them; and objects are measured
       <a href="/wiki/Length_contraction" title="Length contraction">
        to be shortened
       </a>
       in the direction that they are moving with respect to the observer.
      </p>
      <p>
       Subsequently, Einstein worked on a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_theory_of_relativity" title="General theory of relativity">
        general theory of relativity
       </a>
       , which is a theory of how
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       interacts with spacetime. Instead of viewing gravity as a
       <a href="/wiki/Force_field_(physics)" title="Force field (physics)">
        force field
       </a>
       acting in spacetime, Einstein suggested that it modifies the geometric structure of spacetime itself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to the general theory, time
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation" title="Gravitational time dilation">
        goes more slowly
       </a>
       at places with lower gravitational potentials and rays of light bend in the presence of a gravitational field. Scientists have studied the behaviour of
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_pulsar" title="Binary pulsar">
        binary pulsars
       </a>
       , confirming the predictions of Einstein's theories, and non-Euclidean geometry is usually used to describe spacetime.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematics">
        Mathematics
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">
        Three-dimensional space
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Not to be confused with
       <a href="/wiki/Space_(mathematics)" title="Space (mathematics)">
        Space (mathematics)
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       In modern mathematics
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_space" title="Mathematical space">
        spaces
       </a>
       are defined as
       <a href="/wiki/Set_(mathematics)" title="Set (mathematics)">
        sets
       </a>
       with some added structure. They are frequently described as different types of
       <a href="/wiki/Manifold" title="Manifold">
        manifolds
       </a>
       , which are spaces that locally approximate to Euclidean space, and where the properties are defined largely on local connectedness of points that lie on the manifold. There are however, many diverse mathematical objects that are called spaces. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">
        vector spaces
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Function_space" title="Function space">
        function spaces
       </a>
       may have infinite numbers of independent dimensions and a notion of distance very different from Euclidean space, and
       <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">
        topological spaces
       </a>
       replace the concept of distance with a more abstract idea of nearness.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physics">
        Physics
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many of the laws of physics, such as the various
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inverse_square_law" title="Inverse square law">
        inverse square laws
       </a>
       , depend on dimension three.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In physics, our three-dimensional space is viewed as embedded in four-dimensional
       <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
        spacetime
       </a>
       , called
       <a href="/wiki/Minkowski_space" title="Minkowski space">
        Minkowski space
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
        special relativity
       </a>
       ). The idea behind space-time is that time is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperbolic-orthogonal" title="Hyperbolic-orthogonal">
        hyperbolic-orthogonal
       </a>
       to each of the three spatial dimensions.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_mechanics">
        Classical mechanics

      <p>
       Space is one of the few
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_quantity" title="Fundamental quantity">
        fundamental quantities
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
        physics
       </a>
       , meaning that it cannot be defined via other quantities because nothing more fundamental is known at the present. On the other hand, it can be related to other fundamental quantities. Thus, similar to other fundamental quantities (like
       <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">
        time
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        mass
       </a>
       ), space can be explored via
       <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">
        measurement
       </a>
       and experiment.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relativity">
        Relativity
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">
        Theory of relativity
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Before
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein" title="Einstein">
        Einstein
       </a>
       's work on relativistic physics, time and space were viewed as independent dimensions. Einstein's discoveries showed that due to relativity of motion our space and time can be mathematically combined into one object–
       <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
        spacetime
       </a>
       . It turns out that distances in
       <a href="/wiki/Spacetime#Space-like_interval" title="Spacetime">
        space
       </a>
       or in
       <a href="/wiki/Spacetime#Time-like_interval" title="Spacetime">
        time
       </a>
       separately are not invariant with respect to Lorentz coordinate transformations, but distances in Minkowski space-time along
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space-time_interval" title="Space-time interval">
        space-time intervals
       </a>
       are—which justifies the name.
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition, time and space dimensions should not be viewed as exactly equivalent in Minkowski space-time. One can freely move in space but not in time. Thus, time and space coordinates are treated differently both in
       <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
        special relativity
       </a>
       (where time is sometimes considered an
       <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_number" title="Imaginary number">
        imaginary
       </a>
       coordinate) and in
       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
        general relativity
       </a>
       (where different signs are assigned to time and space components of
       <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
        spacetime
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Metric_tensor" title="Metric tensor">
        metric
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Furthermore, in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein%27s_general_theory_of_relativity" title="Einstein's general theory of relativity">
        Einstein's general theory of relativity
       </a>
       , it is postulated that space-time is geometrically distorted-
       <i>
        curved
       </i>
       -near to gravitationally significant masses.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One consequence of this postulate, which follows from the equations of general relativity, is the prediction of moving ripples of space-time, called
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">
        gravitational waves
       </a>
       . While indirect evidence for these waves has been found (in the motions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Hulse%E2%80%93Taylor_binary" title="Hulse–Taylor binary">
        Hulse–Taylor binary
       </a>
       system, for example) experiments attempting to directly measure these waves are ongoing at the
       <a href="/wiki/LIGO" title="LIGO">
        LIGO
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Virgo_interferometer" title="Virgo interferometer">
        Virgo
       </a>
       collaborations. LIGO scientists reported the
       <a href="/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves" title="First observation of gravitational waves">
        first such direct observation of gravitational waves
       </a>
       on 14 September 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRL_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PRL-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmology">
        Cosmology
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe" title="Shape of the universe">
        Shape of the universe
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Relativity theory leads to the
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">
        cosmological
       </a>
       question of what shape the universe is, and where space came from. It appears that space was created in the
       <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">
        Big Bang
       </a>
       , 13.8 billion years ago
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and has been expanding ever since. The overall shape of space is not known, but space is known to be expanding very rapidly due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology)" title="Inflation (cosmology)">
        cosmic inflation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spatial_measurement">
        Spatial measurement
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">
        Measurement
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The measurement of
       <i>
        physical space
       </i>
       has long been important. Although earlier societies had developed measuring systems, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">
        International System of Units
       </a>
       , (SI), is now the most common system of units used in the measuring of space, and is almost universally used.
      </p>
      <p>
       Currently, the standard space interval, called a standard meter or simply
       <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">
        meter
       </a>
       , is defined as the
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
        distance traveled by light in a vacuum
       </a>
       during a time interval of exactly 1/299,792,458 of a second. This definition coupled with present definition of the
       <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">
        second
       </a>
       is based on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_theory_of_relativity" title="Special theory of relativity">
        special theory of relativity
       </a>
       in which the
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
        speed of light
       </a>
       plays the role of a fundamental constant of nature.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_space">
        Geographical space
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Spatial_analysis" title="Spatial analysis">
        Spatial analysis
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        Geography
       </a>
       is the branch of science concerned with identifying and describing the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , utilizing spatial awareness to try to understand why things exist in specific locations.
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        Cartography
       </a>
       is the mapping of spaces to allow better navigation, for visualization purposes and to act as a locational device.
       <a href="/wiki/Geostatistics" title="Geostatistics">
        Geostatistics
       </a>
       apply statistical concepts to collected spatial data to create an estimate for unobserved phenomena.
      </p>
      <p>
       Geographical space is often considered as land, and can have a relation to
       <a href="/wiki/Ownership" title="Ownership">
        ownership
       </a>
       usage (in which space is seen as
       <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">
        property
       </a>
       or territory). While some cultures assert the rights of the individual in terms of ownership, other cultures will identify with a communal approach to land ownership, while still other cultures such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginals" title="Australian Aboriginals">
        Australian Aboriginals
       </a>
       , rather than asserting ownership rights to land, invert the relationship and consider that they are in fact owned by the land.
       <a href="/wiki/Spatial_planning" title="Spatial planning">
        Spatial planning
       </a>
       is a method of regulating the use of space at land-level, with decisions made at regional, national and international levels. Space can also impact on human and cultural behavior, being an important factor in
       <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">
        architecture
       </a>
       , where it will impact on the design of buildings and structures, and on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farming" title="Farming">
        farming
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Ownership of space is not restricted to land. Ownership of
       <a href="/wiki/Airspace" title="Airspace">
        airspace
       </a>
       and of
       <a href="/wiki/International_waters" title="International waters">
        waters
       </a>
       is decided internationally. Other forms of ownership have been recently asserted to other spaces—for example to the
       <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
        radio
       </a>
       bands of the
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        electromagnetic spectrum
       </a>
       or to
       <a href="/wiki/Cyberspace" title="Cyberspace">
        cyberspace
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Public_space" title="Public space">
        Public space
       </a>
       is a term used to define areas of land as collectively owned by the community, and managed in their name by delegated bodies; such spaces are open to all, while
       <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">
        private property
       </a>
       is the land culturally owned by an individual or company, for their own use and pleasure.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Abstract_space" title="Abstract space">
        Abstract space
       </a>
       is a term used in
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        geography
       </a>
       to refer to a hypothetical space characterized by complete homogeneity. When modeling activity or behavior, it is a conceptual tool used to limit
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraneous_variables" title="Extraneous variables">
        extraneous variables
       </a>
       such as terrain.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_psychology">
        In psychology
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Psychologists first began to study the way space is perceived in the middle of the 19th century. Those now concerned with such studies regard it as a distinct branch of
       <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">
        psychology
       </a>
       . Psychologists analyzing the perception of space are concerned with how recognition of an object's physical appearance or its interactions are perceived, see, for example,
       <a href="/wiki/Visual_space" title="Visual space">
        visual space
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Other, more specialized topics studied include
       <a href="/wiki/Amodal_perception" title="Amodal perception">
        amodal perception
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Object_permanence" title="Object permanence">
        object permanence
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">
        perception
       </a>
       of surroundings is important due to its necessary relevance to survival, especially with regards to
       <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">
        hunting
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_preservation" title="Self preservation">
        self preservation
       </a>
       as well as simply one's idea of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_space" title="Personal space">
        personal space
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Several space-related
       <a href="/wiki/Phobia" title="Phobia">
        phobias
       </a>
       have been identified, including
       <a href="/wiki/Agoraphobia" title="Agoraphobia">
        agoraphobia
       </a>
       (the fear of open spaces),
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/astrophobia" title="wikt:astrophobia">
        astrophobia
       </a>
       (the fear of celestial space) and
       <a href="/wiki/Claustrophobia" title="Claustrophobia">
        claustrophobia
       </a>
       (the fear of enclosed spaces).
      </p>
      <p>
       The understanding of three-dimensional space in humans is thought to be learned during infancy using
       <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception#Unconscious_inference" title="Visual perception">
        unconscious inference
       </a>
       , and is closely related to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand-eye_coordination" title="Hand-eye coordination">
        hand-eye coordination
       </a>
       . The visual ability to perceive the world in three dimensions is called
       <a href="/wiki/Depth_perception" title="Depth perception">
        depth perception
       </a>
       .
      </p>
