      <p>
       <b>
        Supra-arcade downflows
       </b>
       (SADs) are sunward-traveling
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       voids that are sometimes observed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun’s
       </a>
       outer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_the_Sun" title="Atmosphere of the Sun">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       , during
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_physics" title="Solar physics">
        solar physics
       </a>
       , an “arcade” refers to a bundle of
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_loop" title="Coronal loop">
        coronal loops
       </a>
       , and the prefix “
       <a href="/wiki/Supra_(grammar)" title="Supra (grammar)">
        supra
       </a>
       ” indicates that the downflows appear above flare arcades. They were first described in 1999 using the Soft X-ray Telescope (SXT) on board the
       <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">
        Yohkoh
       </a>
       satellite.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       SADs are byproducts of the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       process that drives
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       , but their precise cause remains unknown.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">
        Observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Supra-arcade_downflows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Supra-arcade_downflows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       SADs are dark, finger-like plasma voids that are sometimes observed descending through the hot, dense
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       above bright
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_loop" title="Coronal loop">
        coronal loop
       </a>
       arcades during
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       . They were first reported for a flare and associated
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejection
       </a>
       that occurred on January 20, 1999 and was observed by the SXT onboard
       <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">
        Yohkoh
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       SADs are sometimes referred to as “
       <a href="/wiki/Tadpole" title="Tadpole">
        tadpoles
       </a>
       ” for their shape and have since been identified in many other events (e.g.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:3-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ). They tend to be most easily observed in the decay phases of long-duration
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        flares
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       when sufficient
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       has accumulated above the flare arcade to make SADs visible, but they do begin earlier during the rise phase.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition to the SAD voids, there are related structures known as supra-arcade downflowing loops (SADLs). SADLs are retracting (shrinking)
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_loop" title="Coronal loop">
        coronal loops
       </a>
       that form as the overlying
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       is reconfigured during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        flare
       </a>
       . SADs and SADLs are thought to be manifestations of the same process viewed from different angles, such that SADLs are observed if the viewer’s perspective is along the axis of the arcade (i.e. through the arch), while SADs are observed if the perspective is perpendicular to the arcade axis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:6-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_properties">
        Basic properties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Supra-arcade_downflows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Basic properties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       SADs typically begin 100–200
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megametre" title="Megametre">
        Mm
       </a>
       above the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       and descend 20—50
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megametre" title="Megametre">
        Mm
       </a>
       before dissipating near the top of the flare arcade after a few
       <a href="/wiki/Minute" title="Minute">
        minutes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sunward speeds generally fall between 50 and 500 km s
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but may occasionally approach 1000 km s
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_7-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As they fall, the downflows decelerate at rates of 0.1 to 2 km s
       <sup>
        −2
       </sup>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_7-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       SADs appear dark because they are considerably less dense than the surrounding
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while their temperatures (100,000 to 10,000,000
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        K
       </a>
       ) do not differ significantly from their surroundings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:5-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Their
       <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)#Area_and_volume" title="Cross section (geometry)">
        cross-sectional areas
       </a>
       range from a few million to 70 million km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_7-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (for comparison, the
       <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)#Area_and_volume" title="Cross section (geometry)">
        cross-sectional area
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       is 9.5 million km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation">
        Instrumentation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Supra-arcade_downflows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Instrumentation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       SADs are typically observed using soft
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-ray
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">
        Extreme Ultraviolet
       </a>
       (EUV)
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescopes
       </a>
       that cover a
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelength
       </a>
       range of roughly 10 to 1500
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angstrom" title="Angstrom">
        Angstroms (
       </a>
       Å) and are sensitive to the high-temperature (100,000 to 10,000,000
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        K
       </a>
       )
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        coronal
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       through which the downflows move. These emissions are blocked by
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">
        Earth’s atmosphere
       </a>
       , so observations are made using
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_observatory" title="Space observatory">
        space observatories
       </a>
       . The first detection was made by the Soft X-ray Telescope (SXT) onboard
       <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">
        Yohkoh
       </a>
       (1991—2001).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Observations soon followed from the
       <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
        Transition Region and Coronal Explorer
       </a>
       (TRACE, 1998–2010), an
       <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">
        EUV
       </a>
       imaging satellite, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectroscopic
       </a>
       SUMER instrument on board the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
       </a>
       (SOHO, 1995—2016).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:3-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       More recently, studies on SADs have used data from the X-Ray Telescope (XRT) onboard
       <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">
        Hinode
       </a>
       (2006—present) and the Atmospheric Imaging Assembly (AIA) on board the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
        Solar Dynamics Observatory
       </a>
       (SDO, 2010—present).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:5-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition to EUV and X-ray instruments, SADs may also be seen by
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum#Visible_radiation_(light)" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        white light
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Coronagraph" title="Coronagraph">
        coronagraphs
       </a>
       such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Large_Angle_and_Spectrometric_Coronagraph" title="Large Angle and Spectrometric Coronagraph">
        Large Angle and Spectrometric Coronagraph
       </a>
       (LASCO) onboard
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       though these observations are less common.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">
        Causes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Supra-arcade_downflows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Causes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       SADs are widely accepted to be byproducts of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       , the physical process that drives
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       by releasing energy stored in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun#Magnetic_field" title="Sun">
        Sun’s magnetic field
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        Reconnection
       </a>
       reconfigures the local
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       surrounding the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        flare
       </a>
       site from a higher-energy (non-potential,
       <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_stress_tensor" title="Maxwell stress tensor">
        stressed
       </a>
       ) state to a lower-energy (
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_potential" title="Magnetic potential">
        potential
       </a>
       ) state. This process is facilitated by the development of a
       <a href="/wiki/Current_sheet" title="Current sheet">
        current sheet
       </a>
       , often preceded by or in tandem with a
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejection
       </a>
       . As the field is being reconfigured, newly formed
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field#Magnetic_field_lines" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field lines
       </a>
       are swept away from the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        reconnection
       </a>
       site, producing outflows both toward and away from the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        solar surface
       </a>
       , respectively referred to as downflows and upflows. SADs are believed to be related to
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        reconnection
       </a>
       downflows that perturb the hot, dense
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       that collects above
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        flare
       </a>
       arcades,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:3-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but precisely how SADs form is uncertain and is an area of active research.
      </p>
      <p>
       SADs were first interpreted as
       <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">
        cross sections
       </a>
       of magnetic
       <a href="/wiki/Flux_tube" title="Flux tube">
        flux tubes
       </a>
       , which comprise
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_loop" title="Coronal loop">
        coronal loops
       </a>
       , that retract down due to
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tension_force" title="Magnetic tension force">
        magnetic tension
       </a>
       after being formed at the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        reconnection
       </a>
       site.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_7-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This interpretation was later revised to suggest that SADs are instead
       <a href="/wiki/Wake" title="Wake">
        wakes
       </a>
       behind much smaller retracting
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_loop" title="Coronal loop">
        loops
       </a>
       (SADLs),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:6-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       rather than cross sections of the
       <a href="/wiki/Flux_tube" title="Flux tube">
        flux tubes
       </a>
       themselves. Another possibility, also related to
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        reconnection
       </a>
       outflows, is that SADs arise from an instability, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh%E2%80%93Taylor_instability" title="Rayleigh–Taylor instability">
        Rayleigh-Taylor instability
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or a combination of the tearing mode and
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin%E2%80%93Helmholtz_instability" title="Kelvin–Helmholtz instability">
        Kelvin-Helmholtz
       </a>
       instabilities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
