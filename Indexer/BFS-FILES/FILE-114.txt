      <p>
       <b>
        John Fiske
       </b>
       (March 30, 1842 – July 4, 1901) was an
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        American
       </a>
       philosopher and historian.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
        Biography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Fiske_(philosopher)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       John Fiske was born
       <b>
        Edmund Fiske Green
       </b>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">
        Hartford
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">
        Connecticut
       </a>
       , March 30, 1842. He was the only child of Edmund Brewster Green, of
       <a href="/wiki/Smyrna,_Delaware" title="Smyrna, Delaware">
        Smyrna, Delaware
       </a>
       , and Mary Fiske Bound, of
       <a href="/wiki/Middletown,_Connecticut" title="Middletown, Connecticut">
        Middletown, Connecticut
       </a>
       . His father was editor of newspapers in Hartford,
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
        New York City
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       , where he died in 1852, and his widow married Edwin W. Stoughton, of New York, in 1855.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appletons_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-appletons-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On the second marriage of his mother, Edmund Fiske Green assumed the name of his maternal great-grandfather, John Fiske.
      </p>
      <p>
       As a child, Fiske exhibited remarkable precocity. He lived at Middletown during childhood, until he entered
       <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
        Harvard
       </a>
       . He graduated from Harvard College in 1863 and from
       <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">
        Harvard Law School
       </a>
       in 1865. He had already admitted to the Suffolk bar in 1864, but never practised law. His career as author began in 1861, with an article on “Mr. Buckle's Fallacies” published in the
       <i>
        National Quarterly Review
       </i>
       . After that, he was a frequent contributor to American and British periodicals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appletons_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-appletons-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From 1869 to 1871, he was university lecturer on philosophy at Harvard, in 1870 instructor in history there, and assistant librarian 1872-1879. On resigning the latter position in 1879, he was elected a member of the board of overseers, and at the expiration of the six-years' term was re-elected in 1885. Beginning in 1881, he lectured annually on American history at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_University" title="Washington University">
        Washington University
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">
        St. Louis, Missouri
       </a>
       , and beginning in 1884 held a professorship of American history at that institution, but continued to make his home in
       <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">
        Cambridge, Massachusetts
       </a>
       . He lectured on American history at
       <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">
        University College London
       </a>
       in 1879, and at the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution" title="Royal Institution">
        Royal Institution
       </a>
       of Great Britain in 1880. He gave many hundreds of lectures, chiefly upon American history, in the principal cities of the United States and Great Britain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appletons_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-appletons-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Fiske was elected a member of the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Antiquarian_Society" title="American Antiquarian Society">
        American Antiquarian Society
       </a>
       in 1884.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The largest part of his life was devoted to the study of history, but at an early age inquiries into the nature of human progress led him to a careful study of the doctrine of evolution, and it was through the popularization of
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">
        Charles Darwin
       </a>
       's work that he first became known to the public.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appletons_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-appletons-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He applied himself to the philosophical interpretation of Darwin's work and produced many books and essays on this subject. His philosophy was influenced by
       <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Spencer" title="Herbert Spencer">
        Herbert Spencer
       </a>
       's views on
       <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">
        evolution
       </a>
       . In a letter from Charles Darwin to John Fiske, dated from 1874, the naturalist remarks: "I never in my life read so lucid an expositor (and therefore thinker) as you are."
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cartoon_of_Philosopher_John_Fiske.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="976" data-file-width="761" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Cartoon_of_Philosopher_John_Fiske.jpg/200px-Cartoon_of_Philosopher_John_Fiske.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Cartoon_of_Philosopher_John_Fiske.jpg/300px-Cartoon_of_Philosopher_John_Fiske.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Cartoon_of_Philosopher_John_Fiske.jpg/400px-Cartoon_of_Philosopher_John_Fiske.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cartoon_of_Philosopher_John_Fiske.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         "Professor John Fiske flies the evolution kite in America." –
         <i>
          Daily Graphic,
         </i>
         September 12, 1874.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Nineteenth-century enthusiasm for brain size as a simple measure of human performance, championed by scientists including Darwin's cousin
       <a href="/wiki/Francis_Galton" title="Francis Galton">
        Francis Galton
       </a>
       and the French neurologist
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Broca" title="Paul Broca">
        Paul Broca
       </a>
       , led Fiske to believe in the racial superiority of the "
       <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">
        Anglo-Saxon
       </a>
       race". Fiske's beliefs on race did not preclude his commitment to
       <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">
        abolitionist
       </a>
       causes. Indeed, so anti-slavery was he that twenty-three years after the cessation of the American Civil War, he declared the North's victory complete "despite the feeble wails" of "unteachable bigots."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In his book "The Destiny of Man" (1884), he devotes a whole chapter to the "End of the working of natural selection upon man", describing it as "a fact of unparalleled grandeur." In his view, "the action of natural selection upon Man has
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/..." title="...">
        ...
       </a>
       been essentially diminished through the operation of social conditions."
      </p>
      <p>
       In books such as
       <i>
        Outlines of Cosmic Philosophy
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Book_Number" title="International Standard Book Number">
        ISBN
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0-384-15780-7" title="Special:BookSources/0-384-15780-7">
        0-384-15780-7
       </a>
       ), Fiske aimed to show that "in reality there has never been any conflict between religion and science, nor is any reconciliation called for where harmony has always existed." On page 364, he demonstrates his sensitivity to Christianity as a religion:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "We arrive at a deeper reason than has hitherto been disclosed for the difference between our position with reference to
        <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
         Christianity
        </a>
        , and that which has been assumed by
        <a href="/wiki/Political_radicalism" title="Political radicalism">
         Radicalism
        </a>
        and by
        <a href="/wiki/Positivism" title="Positivism">
         Positivism
        </a>
        . It is not merely that we refuse to attack Christianity because we recognize its necessary adaptation to a certain stage of culture, not yet passed by the average minds of the community ; it is that we still regard Christianity as, in the deepest sense, our own
        <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">
         religion
        </a>
        ."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Fiske was a popular lecturer on these topics in his early career. Later he turned to historical writings, publishing books such as
       <i>
        The Discovery of America
       </i>
       (1892). In addition, he edited, with
       <a href="/wiki/James_Grant_Wilson" title="James Grant Wilson">
        James Grant Wilson
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Appletons%27_Cyclop%C3%A6dia_of_American_Biography" title="Appletons' Cyclopædia of American Biography">
         Appletons' Cyclopædia of American Biography
        </a>
       </i>
       (1887). He died, worn out by overwork, at
       <a href="/wiki/Gloucester,_Massachusetts" title="Gloucester, Massachusetts">
        Gloucester
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">
        Massachusetts
       </a>
       , July 4, 1901.
      </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_John_Fiske_Monument.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1214" data-file-width="778" height="296" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/The_John_Fiske_Monument.jpg/190px-The_John_Fiske_Monument.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/The_John_Fiske_Monument.jpg/285px-The_John_Fiske_Monument.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/The_John_Fiske_Monument.jpg/380px-The_John_Fiske_Monument.jpg 2x" width="190"/>

       <i>
        The New Nation,
       </i>
       1962, the historian Merrill Jensen called Fiske's work "a book of vast influence but of no value as either history or example." A few sentences farther in Jensen's Preface, he stated, "Andrew C. McLaughlin, an impeccably conservative historian of the Constitution who wrote a far better book on the same period, said that Fiske's book was 'altogether without scientific standing, because it is little more than a remarkably skillful adaptation of a very few secondary authorities showing almost no evidence of first hand acquaintance with the sources.'"
