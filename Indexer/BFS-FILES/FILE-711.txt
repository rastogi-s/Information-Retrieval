      <p>
       The
       <b>
        solar dynamo
       </b>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">
        physical process
       </a>
       that generates the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       . A dynamo, essentially a naturally occurring electric generator in the Sun's interior, produces
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">
        electric currents
       </a>
       and a magnetic field, following the laws of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amp%C3%A8re%27s_law" title="Ampère's law">
        Ampère
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Faraday%27s_law_of_induction" title="Faraday's law of induction">
        Faraday
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ohm%27s_law" title="Ohm's law">
        Ohm
       </a>
       , as well as the laws of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrodynamics" title="Hydrodynamics">
        hydrodynamics
       </a>
       , which together form the laws of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">
        magnetohydrodynamics
       </a>
       . The detailed mechanism of the solar dynamo is not known and is the subject of current research.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">
        Mechanism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mechanism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">
        dynamo
       </a>
       converts
       <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">
        kinetic energy
       </a>
       into electric-magnetic energy. An electrically conducting fluid with shear or more complicated motion, such as turbulence, can temporarily amplify a magnetic field through
       <a href="/wiki/Lenz%27s_law" title="Lenz's law">
        Lenz's law
       </a>
       : fluid motion relative to a magnetic field induces electric currents in the fluid that distort the initial field. If the fluid motion is sufficiently complicated, it can sustain its own magnetic field, with advective fluid amplification essentially balancing diffusive or ohmic decay. Such systems are called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MHD_dynamo" title="MHD dynamo">
        self-sustaining dynamos
       </a>
       . The Sun is a self-sustaining dynamo that converts convective motion and differential rotation within the Sun to electric-magnetic energy.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_cycle">
        Solar cycle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Solar cycle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        Solar cycle
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The most prominent time variation of the solar magnetic field is related to the quasi-periodic 11-year
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       , characterized by an increasing and decreasing number and size of
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspots
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doi10.1146/annurev-astro-081913-040012_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-doi10.1146/annurev-astro-081913-040012-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zirker2002-119_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Zirker2002-119-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sunspots are visible as dark patches on the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       and correspond to concentrations of magnetic field. At a typical
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">
        solar minimum
       </a>
       , few or no sunspots are visible. Those that do appear are at high solar latitudes. As the solar cycle progresses towards its
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">
        maximum
       </a>
       , sunspots tend to form closer to the solar equator, following
       <a href="/wiki/Sp%C3%B6rer%27s_law" title="Spörer's law">
        Spörer's law
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The 11-year sunspot cycle is half of a 22-year
       <a href="/wiki/Babcock_Model" title="Babcock Model">
        Babcock
       </a>
       –Leighton solar dynamo cycle, which corresponds to an oscillatory exchange of energy between
       <a href="/wiki/Toroidal_and_poloidal" title="Toroidal and poloidal">
        toroidal and poloidal
       </a>
       solar magnetic fields. At
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">
        solar-cycle maximum
       </a>
       , the external poloidal dipolar magnetic field is near its dynamo-cycle minimum strength, but an internal
       <a href="/wiki/Toroidal_and_poloidal" title="Toroidal and poloidal">
        toroidal
       </a>
       quadrupolar field, generated through differential rotation within the
       <a href="/wiki/Tachocline" title="Tachocline">
        tachocline
       </a>
       , is near its maximum strength. At this point in the dynamo cycle, buoyant upwelling within the
       <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">
        convection zone
       </a>
       forces emergence of the toroidal magnetic field through the photosphere, giving rise to pairs of sunspots, roughly aligned east–west with opposite magnetic polarities. The magnetic polarity of sunspot pairs alternates every solar cycle, a phenomenon known as the Hale cycle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solarcycle_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-solarcycle-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the solar cycle’s declining phase, energy shifts from the internal toroidal magnetic field to the external poloidal field, and sunspots diminish in number. At solar minimum, the toroidal field is, correspondingly, at minimum strength, sunspots are relatively rare and the poloidal field is at maximum strength. During the next cycle, differential rotation converts magnetic energy back from the poloidal to the toroidal field, with a polarity that is opposite to the previous cycle. The process carries on continuously, and in an idealized, simplified scenario, each 11-year sunspot cycle corresponds to a change in the polarity of the Sun's large-scale magnetic field.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solarcycle_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-solarcycle-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
