      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Man in the Moon
       </b>
       refers to any of several
       <a href="/wiki/Pareidolia" title="Pareidolia">
        pareidolic images
       </a>
       of a human face, head or body that certain traditions recognise in the disc of the
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       . The images are composed of the dark areas of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        lunar
        <i>
         maria
        </i>
       </a>
       , or "seas" and the lighter highlands of the lunar surface.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_and_occurrence_globally">
        Examples and occurrence globally
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Man_in_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples and occurrence globally">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One older European tradition sees a figure of a man carrying a wide burden on his back.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       He is sometimes seen as accompanied by a small dog. Various cultures recognise other examples of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_pareidolia" title="Lunar pareidolia">
        lunar pareidolia
       </a>
       , such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_rabbit" title="Moon rabbit">
        Moon rabbit
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the Northern Hemisphere, a common Western perception of the face has it that the figure's eyes are
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Imbrium" title="Mare Imbrium">
        Mare Imbrium
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Serenitatis" title="Mare Serenitatis">
        Mare Serenitatis
       </a>
       , its nose is
       <a href="/wiki/Sinus_Aestuum" title="Sinus Aestuum">
        Sinus Aestuum
       </a>
       , and its open mouth is
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Nubium" title="Mare Nubium">
        Mare Nubium
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Cognitum" title="Mare Cognitum">
        Mare Cognitum
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       This particular human face can also be seen in tropical regions on both sides of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       . However, the moon orientation associated with the face is observed less frequently—and eventually not at all—as one moves towards the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Pole
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Conventionalized illustrations of the Man in the Moon seen in Western art often show a very simple face in the full moon, or a human profile in the crescent moon, corresponding to no actual markings.
      </p>
      <p>
       "The Man in the Moon" can also refer to a mythological character said to live on or in the moon, but who is not necessarily represented by the markings on the face of the moon. An example is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yue-Laou" title="Yue-Laou">
        Yue-Laou
       </a>
       , from
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        Chinese
       </a>
       tradition.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_stories">
        Origin stories
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Man_in_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin stories">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are various explanations for how the Man in the Moon came to be.
      </p>
      <p>
       A longstanding European tradition holds that the man was banished to the moon for some crime. Christian lore commonly held that he is the man caught gathering sticks on the Sabbath and sentenced by God to death by stoning in the book of Numbers XV.32-36.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some Germanic cultures thought he was a man caught stealing from a neighbor's hedgerow to repair his own. There is a Roman legend that he is a sheep-thief.
      </p>
      <p>
       One medieval Christian tradition claims him to be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cain" title="Cain">
        Cain
       </a>
       , the Wanderer, forever doomed to circle the Earth.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dante" title="Dante">
        Dante
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Divine_Comedy#Inferno" title="The Divine Comedy">
         Inferno
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inferno126_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Inferno126-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       alludes to this:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <div class="poem">
        <p>
         For now doth Cain with fork of thorns confine
         <br/>
         On either hemisphere, touching the wave
         <br/>
         Beneath the towers of
         <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">
          Seville
         </a>
         . Yesternight
         <br/>
         The moon was round.
        </p>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       This is mentioned again in his
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Divine_Comedy#Paradiso" title="The Divine Comedy">
         Paradise
        </a>
       </i>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paradise_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Paradise-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <div class="poem">
        <p>
         But tell, I
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pray_thee" title="Pray thee">
          pray thee
         </a>
         , whence the gloomy spots
         <br/>
         Upon this body, which below on earth
         <br/>
         Give rise to talk of Cain in fabling quaint?
        </p>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       There is also a
       <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">
        Talmudic
       </a>
       tradition that the image of
       <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">
        Jacob
       </a>
       is engraved on the moon,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although no such mention appears in the
       <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">
        Torah
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/John_Lyly" title="John Lyly">
        John Lyly
       </a>
       says in the prologue to his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Endymion_(play)" title="Endymion (play)">
         Endymion
        </a>
       </i>
       (1591),
       <i>
        "There liveth none under the sunne, that knows what to make of the man in the moone."
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">
        Norse mythology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1ni" title="Máni">
        Máni
       </a>
       is the male personification of the moon who crosses the sky in a horse-drawn carriage. He is continually pursued by the Great Wolf
       <a href="/wiki/Hati_Hr%C3%B3%C3%B0vitnisson" title="Hati Hróðvitnisson">
        Hati
       </a>
       who catches him at
       <a href="/wiki/Ragnar%C3%B6k" title="Ragnarök">
        Ragnarök
       </a>
       . The name
       <i>
        Máni
       </i>
       simply means "Moon".
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">
        Chinese mythology
       </a>
       , the goddess
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e" title="Chang'e">
        Chang'e
       </a>
       is stranded upon the moon after foolishly consuming a double dose of an
       <a href="/wiki/Elixir_of_life" title="Elixir of life">
        immortality potion
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       She is accompanied by a small group of
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_rabbit" title="Moon rabbit">
        moon rabbits
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Haida_mythology" title="Haida mythology">
        Haida mythology
       </a>
       , the figure represents a boy gathering sticks. The boy's father had told him the moon's light would brighten the night, allowing the chore to be completed. Not wanting to gather sticks, the boy complained and ridiculed the moon. As punishment for his disrespect, the boy was taken from earth and trapped on the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There is a story in African mythology that the man on the moon is a king trying to retrieve the moon from the sky for his only son.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions">
        Traditions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Man_in_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Traditions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There is a traditional belief that the Man in the Moon enjoyed drinking, especially claret. An old
       <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">
        ballad
       </a>
       runs (original spelling):
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <div class="poem">
        <p>
         Our man in the moon drinks clarret,
         <br/>
         With powder-beef, turnep, and carret.
         <br/>
         If he doth so, why should not you
         <br/>
         Drink until the sky looks blew?
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
          <a href="#cite_note-9">
           [9]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </p>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       In the English Middle Ages and renaissance, the moon was held to be the god of drunkards, and at least three London taverns were named "The Man in the Moone".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The man in the moon is named in an early dated English
       <a href="/wiki/Nursery_rhyme" title="Nursery rhyme">
        nursery rhyme
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <div class="poem">
        <p>
         The man in the moon came tumbling down
         <br/>
         And asked his way to
         <a href="/wiki/Norwich" title="Norwich">
          Norwich
         </a>
         ;
         <br/>
         He went by the south and burnt his mouth
         <br/>
         With supping cold pease porridge.
        </p>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_explanation">
        Scientific explanation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Man_in_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Scientific explanation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Man in the Moon is made up of various lunar maria (which ones depends on the pareidolic image seen). These vast, flat spots on the moon are called "maria" or "seas" because, for a long time, astronomers believed they were large bodies of water. They are large areas formed by lava that covered up old craters and then cooled, becoming smooth,
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       rock.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The near side of the moon, containing these maria that make up the man, is always facing earth. This is due to a
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal locking
       </a>
       or synchronous orbit. Thought to have occurred because of the gravitational forces partially caused by the moon's oblong shape, its rotation has slowed to the point where it rotates exactly once on each trip around the earth. This causes the near side of the moon to always turn its face toward earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
