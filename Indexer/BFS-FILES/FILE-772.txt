      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Lunar basalt 70017
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_rock" title="Moon rock">
        moon rock
       </a>
       gathered in 1972 by astronauts
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Cernan" title="Eugene Cernan">
        Eugene Cernan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">
        Harrison Schmitt
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        Apollo 17
       </a>
       mission near their
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
        Apollo lunar lander
       </a>
       from the valley of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taurus-Littrow" title="Taurus-Littrow">
        Taurus-Littrow
       </a>
       on the moon and divided into 1.1 gram pieces.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_basalt_70017&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17_lunar_sample_display" title="Apollo 17 lunar sample display">
        Apollo 17 lunar sample display
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Lunar basalt 70017 is a moon
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       that was last collected by astronauts Eugene Cernan and Harrison Schmitt on the last manned moon landing, Apollo 17. After gathering the basalt rock from the surface of the moon the two astronauts made a speech referring to "the children of the world". They wished that parts of the rock be given out to various institutions around the world as a goodwill gesture. In 1973 President Nixon gifted 1.1 gram pieces of the lunar basalt 70017 that were encased in a small
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucite" title="Lucite">
        Lucite
       </a>
       ball and mounted on a wooden plaque display. They were given worldwide to all countries and all 50 United States and US territories and possessions. Some countries were not even aware they were given the Apollo displays. Some of the displays with the moon rocks have been stolen, while others have been lost in inventory. NASA still has about 80 percent of the original rock.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goodwillgesture_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-goodwillgesture-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rockcollector_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-rockcollector-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earth_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Earth-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_basalt_70017&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Apollo 17 Lunar Sample Information Catalog, 1973, describes it as a "holocrystalline, equigranular basalt containing some poikilitic plagioclase". Its total weight before being broken up was 2957 grams. The sliced off 1.1 gram moon chip segments from lunar basalt 70017 consists of 30 percent
       <a href="/wiki/Plagioclase" title="Plagioclase">
        plagioclase
       </a>
       , 59 percent
       <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">
        pyroxene
       </a>
       , 10 percent
       <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">
        ilmenite
       </a>
       and 1 percent
       <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">
        olivine
       </a>
       . The Apollo 17 "Lunar basalt 70017" is described by technicians as a coarse-grained high-Ti basalt. It has been described as moon soil with a crystallization age around 3.7 billion years and an exposure age of about 220 million years. All surfaces of the blocky angular shaped stone are rough and jagged.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
