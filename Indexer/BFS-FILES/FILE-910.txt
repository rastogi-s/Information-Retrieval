      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         The BMJ
        </b>
       </i>
       is a weekly
       <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">
        peer-reviewed
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Healthcare_journal" title="Healthcare journal">
        medical journal
       </a>
       . It is one of the world's oldest general medical journals. Originally called the
       <i>
        <b>
         British Medical Journal
        </b>
       </i>
       , the title was officially shortened to
       <i>
        BMJ
       </i>
       in 1988, and then changed to
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       in 2014. The journal is published by the global knowledge provider BMJ, a
       <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">
        wholly owned subsidiary
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Medical_Association" title="British Medical Association">
        British Medical Association
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Editor-in-chief" title="Editor-in-chief">
        editor in chief
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Fiona_Godlee" title="Fiona Godlee">
        Fiona Godlee
       </a>
       , who was appointed in February 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">
        Impact
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Impact">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the 2016
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Journal_Citation_Reports" title="Journal Citation Reports">
         Journal Citation Reports
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        The BMJ'
       </i>
       s
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">
        impact factor
       </a>
       was 19.697 in 2015,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ranking it fourth among general medical journals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2012-journal_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-2012-journal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The journal began publishing on 3 October 1840 as the
       <i>
        <b>
         Provincial Medical and Surgical Journal
        </b>
       </i>
       and quickly attracted the attention of physicians around the world through its publication of high-impact original research articles and unique case reports.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MoM_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MoM-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        BMJ'
       </i>
       s first editors were
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P._Hennis_Green" title="P. Hennis Green">
        P. Hennis Green
       </a>
       , lecturer on the diseases of children at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunterian_School_of_Medicine" title="Hunterian School of Medicine">
        Hunterian School of Medicine
       </a>
       , who also was its founder and Robert Streeten of
       <a href="/wiki/Worcester" title="Worcester">
        Worcester
       </a>
       , a member of the PMSA council.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:208px;">

      <p>
       The first issue of the
       <i>
        British Medical Journal
       </i>
       was 16 pages long and contained three simple woodcut illustrations. The longest items were the editors' introductory editorial and a report of the Provincial Medical and Surgical Association's Eastern Branch. Other pages included a condensed version of
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Warburton" title="Henry Warburton">
        Henry Warburton
       </a>
       's medical reform bill, book reviews, clinical papers, and case notes. There were ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        2
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </span>
       columns of advertisements. Inclusive of stamp duty it cost 7d, a price which remained until 1844. In their main article, Green and Streeten noted that they had "received as many advertisements (in proportion to the quantity of letter press) for our first number, as the most popular Medical Journal, (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Lancet" title="The Lancet">
         The Lancet
        </a>
       </i>
       ) after seventeen years of existence."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MoM_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-MoM-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In their introductory editorial and later statements, Green and Streeten defined "the main objects of promotion of which the
       <i>
        Provincial Medical and Surgical Journal
       </i>
       is established". Summarised, there were two clear main objectives: the advancement of the profession, especially in the provinces and the dissemination of medical knowledge. Green and Streeten also expressed interest in promoting public well-being as well as maintaining 'medical practitioners, as a class in that rank of society which, by their intellectual acquirements, by their general moral character, and by the importance of the duties entrusted to them, they are justly entitled to hold'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MoM_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-MoM-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       published the first centrally randomised controlled trial.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The journal also carried the seminal papers on the causal effects of smoking on health
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and lung cancer and other causes of death in relation to smoking.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For a long time, the journal's sole competitor was
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Lancet" title="The Lancet">
         The Lancet
        </a>
       </i>
       , also based in the UK, but with increasing globalisation,
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       has faced tough competition from other medical journals, particularly
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_England_Journal_of_Medicine" title="The New England Journal of Medicine">
         The New England Journal of Medicine
        </a>
       </i>
       and the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Medical_Association" title="Journal of the American Medical Association">
         Journal of the American Medical Association
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Journal_content">
        Journal content
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Journal content">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       is an advocate of
       <a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_medicine" title="Evidence-based medicine">
        evidence-based medicine
       </a>
       . It publishes research as well as clinical reviews, recent medical advances, editorial perspectives, among others.
      </p>
      <p>
       The journal releases a number of "theme issues" every year, when it publishes research and review articles pertaining to the theme addressed. Some of the popular theme issues in recent
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
          <span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2011)">
           when?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       years include "Health in Africa", "
       <a href="/wiki/Chronic_care_management" title="Chronic care management">
        Management of Chronic Diseases
       </a>
       ", and "Global Voices on the AIDS Catastrophe".
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A special "Christmas Edition" is published annually on the Friday before Christmas. This edition is known for research articles which apply a serious academic approach to investigating less serious medical questions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tis_11-0">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The results are often humorous and widely reported by the mainstream media.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tis_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tis-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Editions">
        Editions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Editions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       is principally an online journal, and it is only the website which carries the full text content of every article. However, a number of print editions are produced, targeting different groups of readers with selections of content, some of it abridged, and different advertising.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The print editions are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        General Practice (weekly) for
        <a href="/wiki/General_practitioner" title="General practitioner">
         general practitioners
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        Clinical Research (weekly) for hospital doctors
       </li>
       <li>
        Academic (monthly) for institutions, researchers and medical academics
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In addition, a number of local editions of The BMJ are published in translation. There is also
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Student_BMJ" title="Student BMJ">
         Student BMJ
        </a>
       </i>
       , an online resource for medical students, junior doctors and those applying to medical school, which also publishes three print editions a year.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Functioning_of_the_journal">
        Functioning of the journal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Functioning of the journal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       has an
       <a href="/wiki/Open_peer_review" title="Open peer review">
        open peer review
       </a>
       system, wherein authors are told who reviewed their manuscript. About half the original articles are rejected after review in-house.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Manuscripts chosen for peer review are first reviewed by external experts, who comment on the importance and suitability for publication, before the final decision on a manuscript is made by the editorial ("hanging") committee. The acceptance rate is less than 7% for original research articles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="BMJ_website_and_access_policies">
        <i>
         BMJ
        </i>
        website and access policies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: BMJ website and access policies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       went fully online in 1995 and has archived all its issues on the
       <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">
        web
       </a>
       . In addition to the print content, supporting material for original research articles, additional news stories, and electronic letters to the editors are its principal attractions.
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       website has the policy of publishing most e-letters to the journal, called Rapid Responses,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and is shaped like a fully moderated Internet forum. As of January 2013
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       there had been 88 500 rapid responses posted on the BMJ website.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJ_RR_Jan2013_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BMJ_RR_Jan2013-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Comments are screened for libellous and obscene content, however potential contributors are warned that once published, they will not have the right to remove or edit their response.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMJ_RR_Jan2013_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-BMJ_RR_Jan2013-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From 1999, all content of
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       was freely available online; however, in 2006 this changed to a subscription model. Original research articles continue to be available freely, but from January 2006, all other 'added value' contents, including clinical reviews and editorials, require a subscription.
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       allows complete free access for visitors from economically disadvantaged countries as part of the
       <a href="/wiki/HINARI" title="HINARI">
        HINARI
       </a>
       initiative.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 14 October 2008,
       <i>
        The BMJ
       </i>
       announced it would become an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_access_(publishing)" title="Open access (publishing)">
        open access
       </a>
       journal. This only refers to their research articles. To view other articles, a subscription is required.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suber-OA_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Suber-OA-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
