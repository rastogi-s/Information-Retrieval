      <p>
       <b>
        Transition Region and Coronal Explorer
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        TRACE
       </b>
       ) was a
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Heliophysics" title="Heliophysics">
        heliophysics
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_observatory" title="Solar observatory">
        solar observatory
       </a>
       designed to investigate the connections between fine-scale magnetic fields and the associated plasma structures on the Sun by providing high resolution images and observation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       , the transition region, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       . A main focus of the TRACE instrument is the fine structure of
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_loop" title="Coronal loop">
        coronal loops
       </a>
       low in the solar atmosphere. TRACE is the fourth spacecraft in the
       <a href="/wiki/Small_Explorer_program" title="Small Explorer program">
        Small Explorer program
       </a>
       , launched on April 2, 1998, and obtained its last science image in 2010.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       was built by NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">
        Goddard Space Flight Center
       </a>
       . Its telescope was constructed by a consortium led by
       <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">
        Lockheed Martin
       </a>
       's Advanced Technology Center. The optics were designed and built to a state-of-the-art surface finish by the
       <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory">
        Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory
       </a>
       (SAO). The telescope has a 30 cm (12 in) aperture and 1024×1024
       <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
        CCD
       </a>
       detector giving an 8.5
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_minute" title="Arc minute">
        arc minute
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">
        field of view
       </a>
       . The telescope is designed to take
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlated" title="Correlated">
        correlated
       </a>
       images in a range of wavelengths from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">
        visible light
       </a>
       through the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyman_alpha_line" title="Lyman alpha line">
        Lyman alpha line
       </a>
       to far
       <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
        ultraviolet
       </a>
       . The different wavelength
       <a href="/wiki/Passband" title="Passband">
        passbands
       </a>
       correspond to
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       emission temperatures from 4,000 to 4,000,000
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        K
       </a>
       . The optics use a special multilayer technique to focus the difficult-to-reflect
       <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">
        EUV
       </a>
       light; the technique was first used for solar imaging in the late 1980s and 1990s, notably by the
       <a href="/wiki/MSSTA" title="MSSTA">
        MSSTA
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/NIXT" title="NIXT">
        NIXT
       </a>
       sounding rocket payloads.
      </p>
