      <p>
       A
       <b>
        G-type main-sequence star
       </b>
       (Spectral type: G-V), often (and imprecisely) called a
       <b>
        yellow dwarf
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        G dwarf star
       </b>
       , is a
       <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">
        main-sequence
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       (luminosity class V) of
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">
        spectral type
       </a>
       G. Such a star has about 0.84 to 1.15
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">
        solar masses
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">
        surface temperature
       </a>
       of between 5,300 and 6,000
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        K
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hh_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hh-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup>
        , Tables VII, VIII.
       </sup>
       Like other main-sequence stars, a G-type main-sequence star is converting the
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
        element
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       in its core by means of
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">
        nuclear fusion
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valpo_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-valpo-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , the star to which the Earth is gravitationally bound in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       and the object with the largest
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent magnitude
       </a>
       , is an example of a G-type main-sequence star (G2V type). Each second, the Sun fuses approximately 600 million
       <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">
        tons
       </a>
       of hydrogen to helium,
       <a href="/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence" title="Mass–energy equivalence">
        converting
       </a>
       about 4 million tons of
       <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">
        matter
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
        energy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Besides the Sun, other well-known examples of G-type main-sequence stars include
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_Centauri_A" title="Alpha Centauri A">
        Alpha Centauri A
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tau_Ceti" title="Tau Ceti">
        Tau Ceti
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/51_Pegasi" title="51 Pegasi">
        51 Pegasi
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        yellow dwarf
       </i>
       is a misnomer, because G-type stars actually range in color from white, for more luminous types like the Sun, to only very slightly yellow for the less massive and luminous G-type main-sequence stars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vend_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-vend-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Sun is in fact white, and its spectrum peaks in blue and green light, but it can often appear yellow, orange or red through
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's atmosphere due to atmospheric
       <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">
        Rayleigh scattering
       </a>
       , especially at sunrise and sunset.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition, although the term "dwarf" is used to contrast yellow main-sequence stars from
       <a href="/wiki/Giant_star" title="Giant star">
        giant stars
       </a>
       , yellow dwarfs like the Sun outshine 90% of the stars in the
       <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">
        Milky Way
       </a>
       (which are largely much dimmer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orange_dwarf" title="Orange dwarf">
        orange dwarfs
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">
        red dwarfs
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">
        white dwarfs
       </a>
       , the last being a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_remnant" title="Stellar remnant">
        stellar remnant
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       A G-type main-sequence star will fuse hydrogen for approximately 10 billion years, until it is exhausted at the center of the star. When this happens, the star expands to many times its previous size and becomes a
       <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">
        red giant
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Aldebaran" title="Aldebaran">
        Aldebaran
       </a>
       (or Alpha Tauri).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Eventually the red giant sheds its outer layers of gas, which become a
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">
        planetary nebula
       </a>
       , while the core rapidly cools and contracts into a compact, dense white dwarf.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valpo_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-valpo-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spectral_standard_stars">
        Spectral standard stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=G-type_main-sequence_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spectral standard stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The revised Yerkes Atlas system (Johnson &amp; Morgan 1953)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       listed 11 G-type
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_star" title="Dwarf star">
        dwarf
       </a>
       spectral standard stars; however, not all of these have survived to this day as standards.
      </p>
      <p>
       The "anchor points" of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MK_spectral_classification" title="MK spectral classification">
        MK spectral classification
       </a>
       system among the G-type
       <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">
        main-sequence
       </a>
       dwarf stars, i.e. those standard stars that have remained unchanged over years, are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_CVn" title="Beta CVn">
        beta CVn
       </a>
       (G0V), the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       (G2V),
       <a href="/wiki/Kappa1_Ceti" title="Kappa1 Ceti">
        Kappa1 Ceti
       </a>
       (G5V),
       <a href="/wiki/61_Ursae_Majoris" title="61 Ursae Majoris">
        61 Ursae Majoris
       </a>
       (G8V).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrison_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Garrison-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other primary MK standard stars include HD 115043 (G1V) and
       <a href="/wiki/16_Cygni" title="16 Cygni">
        16 Cygni
       </a>
       B (G3V).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keenan89_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Keenan89-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The choices of G4 and G6 dwarf standards have changed slightly over the years among expert classifiers, but often-used examples include
       <a href="/wiki/70_Virginis" title="70 Virginis">
        70 Virginis
       </a>
       (G4V) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/82_Eridani" title="82 Eridani">
        82 Eridani
       </a>
       (G8V). There are not yet any generally agreed upon G7V and G9V standards.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Planets">
        Planets
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=G-type_main-sequence_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Planets">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some of the nearest G-type stars known to have planets include the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/61_Virginis" title="61 Virginis">
        61 Virginis
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/HD_102365" title="HD 102365">
        HD 102365
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/HD_147513" title="HD 147513">
        HD 147513
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/47_Ursae_Majoris" title="47 Ursae Majoris">
        47 Ursae Majoris
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mu_Arae" title="Mu Arae">
        Mu Arae
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tau_Ceti" title="Tau Ceti">
        Tau Ceti
       </a>
       and possibly
       <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Centauri" title="Alpha Centauri">
        Alpha Centauri
       </a>
       .
      </p>
