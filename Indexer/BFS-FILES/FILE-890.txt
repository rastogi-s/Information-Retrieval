      <p>
       <b>
        Soissons
       </b>
       (
       <small>
        French pronunciation: ​
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">
         [swasɔ̃]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Communes_of_France" title="Communes of France">
        commune
       </a>
       in the northern French
       <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">
        department
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Aisne" title="Aisne">
        Aisne
       </a>
       , in the
       <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_France" title="Regions of France">
        region
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Hauts-de-France" title="Hauts-de-France">
        Hauts-de-France
       </a>
       . Located on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aisne_River" title="Aisne River">
        Aisne River
       </a>
       , about 100 kilometres (62 mi) northeast of
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       , it is one of the most ancient towns of France, and is probably the ancient capital of the
       <a href="/wiki/Suessiones" title="Suessiones">
        Suessiones
       </a>
       . Soissons is also the see of an ancient Roman Catholic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Soissons" title="Diocese of Soissons">
        diocese
       </a>
       , whose establishment dates from about 300.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soissons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Soissons enters
       <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">
        written history
       </a>
       under its
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic
       </a>
       name (as later borrowed in
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       ),
       <b>
        Noviodunum
       </b>
       , meaning "new hillfort". At
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Roman
       </a>
       contact, it was a town of the
       <a href="/wiki/Suessiones" title="Suessiones">
        Suessiones
       </a>
       , mentioned by
       <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
        Julius Caesar
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        B. G.
       </i>
       ii. 12). Caesar (
       <i>
        B.C.
       </i>
       57), after leaving the Axona (modern
       <a href="/wiki/Aisne" title="Aisne">
        Aisne
       </a>
       ), entered the territory of the Suessiones, and making one day's long march, reached Noviodunum, which was surrounded by a high wall and a broad ditch. The place surrendered to Caesar.
      </p>
      <p>
       From 457 to 486, under
       <a href="/wiki/Aegidius" title="Aegidius">
        Aegidius
       </a>
       and his son
       <a href="/wiki/Syagrius" title="Syagrius">
        Syagrius
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Noviodunum" title="Noviodunum">
        Noviodunum
       </a>
       was the capital of the "
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Soissons" title="Kingdom of Soissons">
        Kingdom of Soissons
       </a>
       ", until it fell to the
       <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">
        Frankish
       </a>
       king
       <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">
        Clovis I
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Soissons_(486)" title="Battle of Soissons (486)">
        Battle of Soissons
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Part of the Frankish territory of
       <a href="/wiki/Neustria" title="Neustria">
        Neustria
       </a>
       , the Soissons region, and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbey_of_St._Medard,_Soissons" title="Abbey of St. Medard, Soissons">
        Abbey of Saint-Médard
       </a>
       , built in the 8th century, played an important political part during the rule of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merovingian" title="Merovingian">
        Merovingian
       </a>
       kings (A.D. 447-751). After the death of
       <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">
        Clovis I
       </a>
       in 511, Soissons was made the capital of one of the four kingdoms into which his states were divided. Eventually, the kingdom of Soissons disappeared in 613 when the
       <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">
        Frankish
       </a>
       lands were amalgamated under
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clotaire_II" title="Clotaire II">
        Clotaire II
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 744 the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synod_of_Soissons" title="Synod of Soissons">
        Synod of Soissons
       </a>
       met at the instigation of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pippin_III" title="Pippin III">
        Pippin III
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface" title="Saint Boniface">
        Saint Boniface
       </a>
       , the Pope's missionary to pagan Germany, secured the condemnation of the Frankish bishop
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adalbert_(mystic)" title="Adalbert (mystic)">
        Adalbert
       </a>
       and the Irish missionary Clement.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years' War">
        Hundred Years' War
       </a>
       , French forces committed a notorious massacre of English archers stationed at the town's garrison, in which many of the French townsfolk were themselves raped and killed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The massacre of French citizens by French soldiers shocked Europe; Henry V of England, noting that the town of Soissons was dedicated to the saints
       <a href="/wiki/Crispin_and_Crispinian" title="Crispin and Crispinian">
        Crispin and Crispinian
       </a>
       , claimed to avenge the honour of the saints when he met the French forces at the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Agincourt" title="Battle of Agincourt">
        Battle of Agincourt
       </a>
       on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Crispin%27s_Day" title="St Crispin's Day">
        St Crispin's Day
       </a>
       1415.
      </p>
      <p>
       Between June 1728 and July 1729 it hosted the
       <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Soissons" title="Congress of Soissons">
        Congress of Soissons
       </a>
       an attempt to resolve a long-standing series of disputes between
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">
        Great Britain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       which had spilled over into the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1727)" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1727)">
        Anglo-Spanish War of 1727
       </a>
       . The Congress was largely successful and led to the signing of a
       <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Seville_(1729)" title="Treaty of Seville (1729)">
        peace treaty
       </a>
       between them.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
        First World War
       </a>
       , the city came under heavy bombardment. There was mutiny after the disastrous
       <i>
        Chemin des Dames
       </i>
       offensive. A statue erected with images of French soldiers killed in action in 1917 is behind the St Peter's Church, next to the Soissons Courthouse.
      </p>

        Solar eclipse of August 11, 1999
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sights">
        Sights
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soissons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sights">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Today, Soissons is a commercial and manufacturing centre with the 12th century
       <a href="/wiki/Soissons_Cathedral" title="Soissons Cathedral">
        Soissons Cathedral
       </a>
       and the ruins of
       <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_St._Jean_des_Vignes" title="Abbey of St. Jean des Vignes">
        St. Jean des Vignes Abbey
       </a>
       as two of its most important historical buildings. The nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Espace_Pierres_Folles" title="Espace Pierres Folles">
        Espace Pierres Folles
       </a>
       contains a museum, geological trail, and
       <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">
        botanical garden
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       The Cathédrale Saint-Gervais-et-Saint-Protais de Soissons is constructed in the style of
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">
        Gothic architecture
       </a>
       . The building of the south transept was begun about 1177, and the lowest courses of the choir in 1182. The choir with its original three-storey elevation and extremely tall clerestory was completed in 1211. This was earlier than
       <a href="/wiki/Chartres" title="Chartres">
        Chartres
       </a>
       , on which the design was supposed to have been based. Work then continued into the nave until the late 13th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Abbey_of_Notre_Dame">
        Abbey of Notre Dame
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soissons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Abbey of Notre Dame">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The former abbey of Notre Dame, former royal abbey, founded in the Merovingian era, famous for its rich treasure of relics, including the "shoe of the Virgin." The abbey was prestigious abbesses like Gisèle, sister of Charlemagne, or Catherine de Bourbon, aunt of Henry IV.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Saint-M.C3.A9dard_Abbey">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saint-Médard_Abbey">
        Saint-Médard Abbey
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soissons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Saint-Médard Abbey">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbey_of_St._Medard,_Soissons" title="Abbey of St. Medard, Soissons">
        Saint-Médard Abbey
       </a>
       was a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">
        Benedictine
       </a>
       monastery of Soissons whose foundation went back to the sixth century. Today, only the crypt remains.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="City_Hall">
        City Hall
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soissons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: City Hall">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The city hall built by architect Jean-François Advyné, between 1772 and 1775, at the request of the Intendant Pelletier Mortefontaine on the site of the old castle counts of Soissons. The town hall settled there in 1833.
      </p>
      <p>
       Arsenal: contemporary art exhibitions. UK Monument (1914-1918)
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="La_passerelle_des_Anglais_Bridge">
        La passerelle des Anglais Bridge
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soissons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: La passerelle des Anglais Bridge">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Gateway Anglais Bridge is a concrete casson built cantilevered from an abutment against-weight with an isostatic central beam of 20.50 m in length. The floor has a width of 3.50 m between railings. The original bridge was destroyed in 1914. It was rebuilt by British soldiers, and logically took the name of the English bridge. Again destroyed during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       , the bridge was rebuilt in 1950 as a footbridge. The covered market, built in 1908 by architect Albert-Désiré Guilbert (1866-1949).
      </p>
