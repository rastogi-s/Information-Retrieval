      <p>
       <b>
        Sir Patrick Alfred Caldwell-Moore
       </b>
       <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;">
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">
         CBE
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Honorary Fellow of the Royal Society">
         HonFRS
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Astronomical Society">
         FRAS
        </a>
       </span>
       (4 March 1923 – 9 December 2012)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       was an English
       <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">
        amateur astronomer
       </a>
       who attained prominent status in that field as a writer, researcher, radio commentator and television presenter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcprofile_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bbcprofile-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcbiog_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bbcbiog-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore was President of the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Astronomical_Association" title="British Astronomical Association">
        British Astronomical Association
       </a>
       , co-founder and president of the
       <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Popular_Astronomy" title="Society for Popular Astronomy">
        Society for Popular Astronomy
       </a>
       , author of over seventy books on astronomy, and presenter of the world's longest-running television series with the same original presenter,
       <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
        BBC
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sky_at_Night" title="The Sky at Night">
         The Sky at Night
        </a>
       </i>
       . He became known as a specialist in
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       observation and for creating the
       <a href="/wiki/Caldwell_catalogue" title="Caldwell catalogue">
        Caldwell catalogue
       </a>
       . Idiosyncrasies such as his rapid diction and
       <a href="/wiki/Monocle" title="Monocle">
        monocle
       </a>
       made him a popular and instantly recognisable figure on British television.
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore was also a self-taught
       <a href="/wiki/Xylophone" title="Xylophone">
        xylophonist
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Glockenspiel" title="Glockenspiel">
        glockenspiel
       </a>
       player and
       <a href="/wiki/Pianist" title="Pianist">
        pianist
       </a>
       , as well as an accomplished composer. He was a former amateur cricketer, golfer and chess player. In addition to many
       <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">
        popular science
       </a>
       books, he wrote numerous works of fiction. He was an opponent of
       <a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">
        fox hunting
       </a>
       , an outspoken critic of the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
        European Union
       </a>
       , supporter of the
       <a href="/wiki/UK_Independence_Party" title="UK Independence Party">
        UK Independence Party
       </a>
       and served as chairman of the short-lived anti-immigration
       <a href="/wiki/United_Country_Party_(United_Kingdom)" title="United Country Party (United Kingdom)">
        United Country Party
       </a>
       . He served in the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">
        Royal Air Force
       </a>
       during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       ; he said his fiancée died during the war and he subsequently never married or had children.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
        Early life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Moore was born in
       <a href="/wiki/Pinner" title="Pinner">
        Pinner
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">
        Middlesex
       </a>
       on 4 March 1923
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_1_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_1-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to Capt. Charles Trachsel Caldwell-Moore
       <a href="/wiki/Military_Cross" title="Military Cross">
        MC
       </a>
       (died 1947)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_4_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_4-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Gertrude (née White) (died 1981).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_4_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_4-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His family moved to
       <a href="/wiki/Bognor_Regis" title="Bognor Regis">
        Bognor Regis
       </a>
       , and subsequently to
       <a href="/wiki/East_Grinstead" title="East Grinstead">
        East Grinstead
       </a>
       where he spent his childhood. His youth was marked by heart problems, which left him in poor health and he was educated at home by private tutors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_1_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_1-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He developed an interest in astronomy at the age of six
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and joined the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Astronomical_Association" title="British Astronomical Association">
        British Astronomical Association
       </a>
       at the age of eleven.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_3_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_3-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was invited to run a small observatory in East Grinstead at the age of 14, after his mentor – who ran the observatory – was killed in a road accident.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_12_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_12-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the age of 16 he began wearing a
       <a href="/wiki/Monocle" title="Monocle">
        monocle
       </a>
       after an
       <a href="/wiki/Ophthalmology" title="Ophthalmology">
        oculist
       </a>
       told him his right eye was weaker than his left.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Three years later, he began wearing a full set of
       <a href="/wiki/Dentures" title="Dentures">
        dentures
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       , Moore joined the
       <a href="/wiki/Home_Guard_(United_Kingdom)" title="Home Guard (United Kingdom)">
        Home Guard
       </a>
       in East Grinstead where his father had been elected
       <a href="/wiki/Platoon" title="Platoon">
        platoon
       </a>
       commander.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite recounting in his autobiography that he had lied about his age to join the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">
        Royal Air Force
       </a>
       in 1940 at age 16,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       records show that he enlisted in the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force_Volunteer_Reserve" title="Royal Air Force Volunteer Reserve">
        Royal Air Force Volunteer Reserve
       </a>
       in December 1941 at age 18 and was not called up for service until July 1942 as an
       <a href="/wiki/Aircraftman" title="Aircraftman">
        Aircraftman
       </a>
       , 2nd Class.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After basic training at various RAF bases in England, he went to Canada under the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Commonwealth_Air_Training_Plan" title="British Commonwealth Air Training Plan">
        British Commonwealth Air Training Plan
       </a>
       and completed training at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Moncton_International_Airport" title="Greater Moncton International Airport">
        RAF Moncton
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">
        New Brunswick
       </a>
       as a navigator and pilot.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In his biography, he claims to have met
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Albert Einstein
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">
        Orville Wright
       </a>
       while on leave in New York.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Returning to England in June 1944, he was commissioned as a
       <a href="/wiki/Pilot_officer" title="Pilot officer">
        pilot officer
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was posted to RAF
       <a href="/wiki/Millom" title="Millom">
        Millom
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">
        Cumbria
       </a>
       , where he was a navigator in the crew of a
       <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Wellington" title="Vickers Wellington">
        Vickers Wellington
       </a>
       bomber, engaged in
       <a href="/wiki/Maritime_patrol" title="Maritime patrol">
        maritime patrolling
       </a>
       and bombing missions to mainland Europe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After the end of hostilities, Moore became an
       <a href="/wiki/Adjutant#Britain_and_the_Commonwealth" title="Adjutant">
        adjutant
       </a>
       and then an Area Meteorological Officer, finally demobilised in October 1945 with the rank of
       <a href="/wiki/Flying_officer" title="Flying officer">
        flying officer
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The war had a significant influence on Moore's life – he said his only romance ended when his fiancée Lorna, a nurse, was killed in London in 1943 by a bomb which struck her ambulance. Moore subsequently remarked that he never married because "there was no one else for me ... second best is no good for me ... I would have liked a wife and family, but it was not to be."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In his autobiography he said that after sixty years he still thought about her, and because of her death "if I saw the entire German nation sinking into the sea, I could be relied upon to help push it down."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_5_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_5-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In May 2012 he said to the
       <i>
        Radio Times
       </i>
       magazine, "We must take care. There may be another war. The Germans will try again, given another chance". He also said, in the same interview, that "The only good Kraut is a dead Kraut."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, some doubt has arisen over Moore's account, as researchers have been unable to identify Lorna, despite the meticulous records kept of wartime civilian casualties.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore said he was "exceptionally close" to his mother Gertrude,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_4_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_4-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a talented artist who shared his
       <a href="/wiki/Selsey" title="Selsey">
        Selsey
       </a>
       home, which was decorated with her paintings of "bogeys" – little friendly aliens – that she produced and sent out annually as the Moores' Christmas cards.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore wrote the foreword for his mother's 1974 book,
       <i>
        Mrs Moore In Space
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Career_in_astronomy">
        Career in astronomy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career in astronomy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       After the war, Moore rejected a grant to study at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">
        University of Cambridge
       </a>
       , citing a wish to "stand on my own two feet".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_3_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_3-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He wrote his first book,
       <i>
        Guide to the Moon
       </i>
       (later retitled
       <i>
        Patrick Moore on the Moon
       </i>
       ) in 1952 and it was published a year later.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_3_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_3-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He wrote it on a 1908 Woodstock typewriter, which he used for every book he published.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_5_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_5-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was a teacher in
       <a href="/wiki/Woking" title="Woking">
        Woking
       </a>
       and at
       <a href="/wiki/Holmewood_House_School" title="Holmewood House School">
        Holmewood House School
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Langton_Green" title="Langton Green">
        Langton Green
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       from 1945 to 1953.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KSC270712_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-KSC270712-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His second book was a translation of a work of French astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_Vaucouleurs" title="Gérard de Vaucouleurs">
        Gérard de Vaucouleurs
       </a>
       (Moore spoke fluent French).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After his second original science book,
       <i>
        Guide to the Planets
       </i>
       , he penned his first work of fiction,
       <i>
        The Master of the Moon
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the first of numerous
       <a href="/wiki/Young_adult_fiction" title="Young adult fiction">
        young adult fiction
       </a>
       space adventure books (including the late 1970s series the
       <a href="/wiki/Scott_Saunders_Space_Adventure" title="Scott Saunders Space Adventure">
        Scott Saunders Space Adventure
       </a>
       ); he wrote a more adult novel and a
       <a href="/wiki/Farce" title="Farce">
        farce
       </a>
       titled
       <i>
        Ancient Lights
       </i>
       , though he did not wish either to be published.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       While teaching at Holmewood he set up a 12½ inch
       <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">
        reflector telescope
       </a>
       at his home, which he kept into his old age.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_12_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_12-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He developed a particular interest in the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side of the Moon
       </a>
       , a small part of which is visible from Earth as a result of the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       ; the Moon was his specialist subject throughout his life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_12_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_12-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He claimed to have discovered and named the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Orientale" title="Mare Orientale">
        Mare Orientale
       </a>
       (Eastern Sea) in 1946,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_14_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_14-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       along with
       <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Percy_Wilkins" title="Hugh Percy Wilkins">
        Hugh Percy Wilkins
       </a>
       , though he later conceded that German astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Julius_Heinrich_Franz" title="Julius Heinrich Franz">
        Julius Heinrich Franz
       </a>
       should be credited with the discovery.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The feature had been observed several times since telescopic observations began. Moore described the short-lived glowing areas on the lunar surface, and gave them the name
       <a href="/wiki/Transient_lunar_phenomenon" title="Transient lunar phenomenon">
        transient lunar phenomena
       </a>
       in 1968.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_14_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_14-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       His first television appearance was in a debate about the existence of
       <a href="/wiki/Flying_saucer" title="Flying saucer">
        flying saucers
       </a>
       following a spate of reported
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_reported_UFO_sightings" title="List of reported UFO sightings">
        sightings
       </a>
       in the 1950s; Moore argued against
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugh_Dowding,_1st_Baron_Dowding" title="Hugh Dowding, 1st Baron Dowding">
        Lord Dowding
       </a>
       and other UFO proponents.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was invited to present a live astronomy programme and said the greatest difficulty was finding an appropriate theme tune; the opening of
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">
        Jean Sibelius
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pell%C3%A9as_et_M%C3%A9lisande_(Sibelius)" title="Pelléas et Mélisande (Sibelius)">
         Pelléas et Mélisande
        </a>
       </i>
       was chosen and used throughout the programme's existence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_23_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_23-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The programme was originally named
       <i>
        Star Map
       </i>
       before
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sky_at_Night" title="The Sky at Night">
         The Sky at Night
        </a>
       </i>
       was chosen in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Radio_Times" title="Radio Times">
         Radio Times
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_23_35-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_23-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 24 April 1957, at 10:30 pm, Moore presented the first episode about the
       <a href="/wiki/Comet_Arend%E2%80%93Roland" title="Comet Arend–Roland">
        Comet Arend–Roland
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_23_35-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_23-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The programme was pitched to casual viewers up to professional astronomers, in a format which remained consistent from its inception.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore presented every monthly episode except for one in July 2004 when he suffered a near-fatal bout of
       <a href="/wiki/Foodborne_illness" title="Foodborne illness">
        food poisoning
       </a>
       caused by eating a contaminated goose egg and was replaced for that episode by
       <a href="/wiki/Chris_Lintott" title="Chris Lintott">
        Chris Lintott
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore appears in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">
         Guinness World Records
        </a>
       </i>
       book as the world's longest-serving TV presenter having presented the programme since 1957. From 2004 to 2012, the programme was broadcast from Moore's home, when
       <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">
        arthritis
       </a>
       prevented him from travelling to the studios. Over the years he received many lucrative offers to take his programme onto other networks, but rejected them because he held a '
       <a href="/wiki/Gentlemen%27s_agreement" title="Gentlemen's agreement">
        gentlemen's agreement
       </a>
       ' with the BBC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A highlight of the series in 1959 was when the Russians allowed Moore to be the first Westerner to see the photographic results of the
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_3" title="Luna 3">
        Luna 3
       </a>
       probe, and to show them live on air.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Less successful was the transmission of the
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_4" title="Luna 4">
        Luna 4
       </a>
       probe, which ran into technical difficulties and around this time Moore famously swallowed a large fly; both episodes were live and Moore had to continue regardless.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was invited to visit the
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet Union
       </a>
       , where he met
       <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">
        Yuri Gagarin
       </a>
       , the first man to journey into
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">
        outer space
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_41_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_41-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For the fiftieth episode of
       <i>
        The Sky at Night
       </i>
       , in September 1961, Moore's attempt to be the first to broadcast a live direct telescopic view of a planet resulted in another unintended 'comedy episode', as cloud obscured the sky.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

         </div>

        </div>

      <p>
       In 1965, he was appointed director of the newly constructed
       <a href="/wiki/Armagh_Planetarium" title="Armagh Planetarium">
        Armagh Planetarium
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">
        Northern Ireland
       </a>
       , a post he held until 1968.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His stay outside England was short partly because of the beginning of
       <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">
        The Troubles
       </a>
       , a dispute Moore wanted no involvement in.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was appointed
       <a href="/wiki/County_Armagh" title="County Armagh">
        Armagh County
       </a>
       secretary of the
       <a href="/wiki/Scouting" title="Scouting">
        Scout
       </a>
       movement, but resigned after being informed that Catholics could not be admitted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In developing the Planetarium, Moore travelled to Japan to secure a
       <a href="/wiki/Planetarium_projector" title="Planetarium projector">
        Goto Mars projector
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He helped with the redevelopment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Birr,_County_Offaly#Birr_Telescope_-_The_'Leviathan_of_Parsonstown'" title="Birr, County Offaly">
        Birr Telescope
       </a>
       in the Republic of Ireland.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was a key figure in the development of the
       <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Museum_of_Astronomy" title="Herschel Museum of Astronomy">
        Herschel Museum of Astronomy
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">
        Bath
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In June 1968 he returned to England, settling in Selsey after resigning his post in Armagh.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During the NASA Apollo programme, presenting on the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">
        Apollo 8
       </a>
       mission, he said that "this is one of the great moments of human history", only to have his broadcast interrupted by the children's programme
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Jackanory" title="Jackanory">
         Jackanory
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_67_50-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_67-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was a presenter for the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_9" title="Apollo 9">
        Apollo 9
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_10" title="Apollo 10">
        Apollo 10
       </a>
       missions, and a commenter, with
       <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Michelmore" title="Cliff Michelmore">
        Cliff Michelmore
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/James_Burke_(science_historian)" title="James Burke (science historian)">
        James Burke
       </a>
       , for
       <a href="/wiki/British_television_Apollo_11_coverage" title="British television Apollo 11 coverage">
        BBC television's coverage of the Moon landing
       </a>
       missions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_67_50-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_67-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore could not remember his words at the "
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Eagle has landed
       </a>
       " moment, and the BBC has lost the tapes of the broadcast.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A homemade recording reveals that the studio team was very quiet during the landing sequence, leaving the NASA commentary clear of interruptions. Some 14 seconds after "contact" Burke says "They've touched". At 36 seconds he says "Eagle has landed". Between 53 and 62 seconds he explains the upcoming stay/no-stay decision and NASA announces the T1 stay at 90 seconds after contact. At 100 seconds the recorded sequence ends. Thus any real-time comment Moore made was not broadcast live and the recording ends before Burke polls the studio team for comment and reaction. Moore participated in TV coverage of
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo missions
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        12
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        17
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="quotebox pullquote" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:35%; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
       <div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
        <div>
         "Patrick was the last of a lost generation, a true gentleman, the most generous in nature that I ever knew, and an inspiration to thousands in his personal life, and to millions through his 50 years of unique broadcasting. It's no exaggeration to say that Patrick, in his tireless and ebullient communication of the magic of astronomy, inspired every British astronomer, amateur and professional, for half a century. There will never be another Patrick Moore. But we were lucky enough to get one."
        </div>
       </div>
       <cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">
        —
        <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">
         Brian May
        </a>
        , speaking shortly after Moore's death
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_53-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-BBC-53">
          [53]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </cite>
      </div>
      <p>
       He was elected a member of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       in 1966;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
        <a href="#cite_note-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
        <a href="#cite_note-55">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
       having twice edited the Union's General Assembly newsletters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
        <a href="#cite_note-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He attempted to establish an International Union of Amateur Astronomers, which failed due to lack of interest.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
        <a href="#cite_note-57">
         [57]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During the 1970s and 80s, he reported on the
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">
        Voyager
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_program" title="Pioneer program">
        Pioneer programs
       </a>
       , often from
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       headquarters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
        <a href="#cite_note-58">
         [58]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At this time he became increasingly annoyed by
       <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theory" title="Conspiracy theory">
        conspiracy theorists
       </a>
       and reporters who asked him questions such as "Why waste money on space research when there is so much to be done here?". He said that when asked these type of questions "I know that I'm dealing with an idiot."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
        <a href="#cite_note-59">
         [59]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another question that annoyed him was "what is the difference between
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">
        astrology
       </a>
       ?"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
        <a href="#cite_note-60">
         [60]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite this he made a point of responding to all letters delivered to his house, and sent a variety of standard replies to letters asking basic questions, as well as those from conspiracy theorists, proponents of hunting and '
       <a href="/wiki/Crank_(person)" title="Crank (person)">
        cranks
       </a>
       '.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
        <a href="#cite_note-61">
         [61]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite his fame, his telephone number was always listed in the telephone directory and he was happy to show members of the public his observatory.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
        <a href="#cite_note-62">
         [62]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He compiled the
       <a href="/wiki/Caldwell_catalogue" title="Caldwell catalogue">
        Caldwell catalogue
       </a>
       of astronomical objects and in 1982,
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2602_Moore" title="2602 Moore">
        2602 Moore
       </a>
       was named in his honour.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
        <a href="#cite_note-63">
         [63]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In February 1986 he presented a special episode of
       <i>
        The Sky at Night
       </i>
       on the approach of
       <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley's Comet">
        Halley's Comet
       </a>
       , though he later said the BBC's better-funded
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horizon_(BBC_TV_series)" title="Horizon (BBC TV series)">
         Horizon
        </a>
       </i>
       team "made a complete hash of the programme."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
        <a href="#cite_note-64">
         [64]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In January 1998, a tornado destroyed part of Moore's garden observatory; it was subsequently rebuilt.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
        <a href="#cite_note-65">
         [65]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore campaigned unsuccessfully against the closure of the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich" title="Royal Observatory, Greenwich">
        Royal Observatory, Greenwich
       </a>
       in 1998.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
        <a href="#cite_note-66">
         [66]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Among Moore's favourite episodes of
       <i>
        The Sky at Night
       </i>
       were those that dealt with
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipses
       </a>
       , and he said "there is nothing in nature to match the glory of a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        total eclipse of the Sun
       </a>
       ."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
        <a href="#cite_note-67">
         [67]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore was a BBC presenter for the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_August_11,_1999" title="Solar eclipse of August 11, 1999">
        total eclipse in England in 1999
       </a>
       , though the view he and his team had from
       <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">
        Cornwall
       </a>
       was obscured by cloud.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
        <a href="#cite_note-68">
         [68]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore was the patron of the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Downs_Planetarium_%26_Science_Centre" title="South Downs Planetarium &amp; Science Centre">
        South Downs Planetarium &amp; Science Centre
       </a>
       , which opened in 2001.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
        <a href="#cite_note-69">
         [69]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        </a>

       On 1 April 2007, a 50th anniversary semi-spoof edition of the programme was broadcast on
       <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">
        BBC One
       </a>
       , with Moore depicted as a
       <a href="/wiki/Time_Lord" title="Time Lord">

       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">

       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
        <a href="#cite_note-70">
         [70]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was reported in January 2012 that because of arthritis and the effects of an old spinal injury he was no longer able to operate a telescope. However, he was still able to present
       <i>
        The Sky at Night
       </i>
       from his home.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
        <a href="#cite_note-71">
         [71]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He died aged 89 at 12:25 p.m. on 9 December 2012 at his home in Selsey, West Sussex.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
        <a href="#cite_note-72">
         [72]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 9 December 2014 it was reported that the
       <a href="/wiki/Science_Museum,_London" title="Science Museum, London">
        Science Museum, London
       </a>

       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sky_at_Night" title="The Sky at Night">
         The Sky at Night
        </a>

       scripts, and about 70 of his observation books, over more than 60 years, and manuscripts for astronomy and fiction books, and a 12.5 inch
       <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">
        reflecting telescope
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
        <a href="#cite_note-73">
         [73]

       </sup>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Activism_and_political_beliefs">
        Activism and political beliefs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Activism and political beliefs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Moore was known for his conservative political views. In the 1970s, he was chairman of the anti-immigration
       <a href="/wiki/United_Country_Party_(United_Kingdom)" title="United Country Party (United Kingdom)">
        United Country Party
       </a>
       , a position he held until the party was absorbed by the
       <a href="/wiki/New_Britain_Party" title="New Britain Party">
        New Britain Party
       </a>
       in 1980. He campaigned for the politician
       <a href="/wiki/United_Country_Party_(United_Kingdom)" title="United Country Party (United Kingdom)">
        Edmund Iremonger
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1979" title="United Kingdom general election, 1979">
        1979 general election
       </a>
       , as they agreed the French and Germans were not to be trusted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_167_74-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_167-74">
         [74]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Iremonger and Moore gave up political campaigning after deciding they were
       <a href="/wiki/Thatcherism" title="Thatcherism">
        Thatcherites
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_167_74-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_167-74">
         [74]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore campaigned on behalf of Douglas Denny (
       <a href="/wiki/UK_Independence_Party" title="UK Independence Party">
        UKIP
       </a>
       ) for the
       <a href="/wiki/Chichester_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Chichester (UK Parliament constituency)">
        Chichester constituency
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">
        2001
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_168_75-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_168-75">
         [75]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Euroscepticism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Euroscepticism in the United Kingdom">
        Eurosceptic
       </a>
       , he was a supporter and patron of the UK Independence Party.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
        <a href="#cite_note-76">
         [76]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore briefly supported the
       <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">
        Liberal Party
       </a>
       in the 1950s, though condemned the
       <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">
        Liberal Democrats
       </a>
       , saying he believed they could alter their position radically and that they "would happily join up with the
       <a href="/wiki/British_National_Party" title="British National Party">
        BNP
       </a>
       or the
       <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Workers_Party_(UK)" title="Socialist Workers Party (UK)">
        Socialist Workers Party
       </a>
       ... if [by doing so] they could win a few extra votes."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_168_75-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_168-75">
         [75]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He admired the
       <a href="/wiki/Official_Monster_Raving_Loony_Party" title="Official Monster Raving Loony Party">
        Official Monster Raving Loony Party
       </a>
       and was briefly their financial adviser.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_169_77-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_169-77">
         [77]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He wrote in his autobiography that
       <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">
        Liechtenstein
       </a>
       – a
       <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">
        constitutional monarchy
       </a>
       headed by a prince – had the best political system in the world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
        <a href="#cite_note-78">
         [78]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore was a critic of the
       <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">
        Iraq War
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">
        <a href="#cite_note-79">
         [79]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and said "the world was a safer place when
       <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">
        Ronald Reagan
       </a>
       was in the White House".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">
        <a href="#cite_note-80">
         [80]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Proudly declaring himself to be English (rather than British) with "not the slightest wish to integrate with anybody",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_169_77-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_169-77">
         [77]
        </a>
       </sup>
       he stated his admiration for controversial MP
       <a href="/wiki/Enoch_Powell" title="Enoch Powell">
        Enoch Powell
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81">
        <a href="#cite_note-81">
         [81]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore devoted an entire chapter ("The Weak Arm of the Law") of his autobiography to denouncing modern British society, particularly "motorist-hunting" policemen, sentencing policy, the
       <a href="/wiki/Race_Relations_Act_1976" title="Race Relations Act 1976">
        Race Relations Act
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sex_Discrimination_Act_1975" title="Sex Discrimination Act 1975">
        Sex Discrimination Act
       </a>
       and the "
       <a href="/wiki/Thought_Police" title="Thought Police">
        Thought Police
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">
        Politically Correct Brigade
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82">
        <a href="#cite_note-82">
         [82]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He wrote that "homosexuals are mainly responsible for the spreading of
       <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">
        AIDS
       </a>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">
        Garden of Eden
       </a>
       is home of
       <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">
        Adam and Eve
       </a>
       , not
       <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Steve" title="Adam and Steve">
        Adam and Steve
       </a>
       )".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83">
        <a href="#cite_note-83">
         [83]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In an interview with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Radio_Times" title="Radio Times">
         Radio Times
        </a>
       </i>
       , he said the BBC was being "ruined by women", commenting that: "The trouble is that the BBC now is run by women and it shows: soap operas, cooking, quizzes,
       <a href="/wiki/Kitchen_sink_realism" title="Kitchen sink realism">
        kitchen-sink plays
       </a>
       . You wouldn't have had that in the golden days." In response, a BBC spokeswoman described Moore as being one of TV's best-loved figures and remarked that his "forthright" views were "what we all love about him".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84">
        <a href="#cite_note-84">
         [84]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During his June 2002 appearance on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Room_101_(TV_series)" title="Room 101 (TV series)">
         Room 101
        </a>
       </i>
       he banished female newsreaders into Room 101.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85">
        <a href="#cite_note-85">
         [85]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        "I may be accused of being a dinosaur, but I would remind you that dinosaurs ruled the Earth for a very long time."
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Moore responds to those who criticise his
         <a href="/wiki/Right-wing_politics" title="Right-wing politics">
          right-wing
         </a>
         beliefs.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86">
          <a href="#cite_note-86">
           [86]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Moore cited his opposition to
       <a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">
        fox hunting
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Blood_sport" title="Blood sport">
        blood sports
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">
        capital punishment
       </a>
       to rebut claims that he had ultra right-wing views.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_169_77-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_169-77">
         [77]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87">
        <a href="#cite_note-87">
         [87]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Though not a vegetarian, he held "a deep contempt for people who go out to kill merely to amuse themselves."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88">
        <a href="#cite_note-88">
         [88]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was an animal lover, supporting many animal welfare charities (particularly
       <a href="/wiki/Cats_Protection" title="Cats Protection">
        Cats Protection
       </a>
       ). He had a particular affinity for cats and stated that "a catless house is a soulless house".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89">
        <a href="#cite_note-89">
         [89]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore was opposed to astronomy being taught in schools. In an interview he said:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        You see, anyone who is interested in astronomy will gravitate to it, as I did. If you start teaching it as a school subject, it's going to be taught badly, like everything else these days, and enthusiasm is going to be killed."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90">
         <a href="#cite_note-90">
          [90]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_interests_and_popular_culture">
        Other interests and popular culture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other interests and popular culture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because of his long-running television career and eccentric demeanour, Moore was widely recognised and became a popular public figure. In 1976 it was used to good effect for an
       <a href="/wiki/April_Fools%27_Day" title="April Fools' Day">
        April Fools' Day
       </a>
       spoof on
       <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">
        BBC Radio 2
       </a>
       , when Moore announced a once-in-a-lifetime astronomical event that meant that if listeners could jump at that exact moment, 9.47 a.m. they would experience a temporary sensation of
       <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">
        weightlessness
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_252_91-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_252-91">
         [91]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The BBC received many telephone calls from listeners alleging they experienced the sensation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_252_91-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_252-91">
         [91]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was a key figure in the establishment of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/International_Birdman" title="International Birdman">
         International Birdman
        </a>
       </i>
       event in
       <a href="/wiki/Bognor_Regis" title="Bognor Regis">
        Bognor Regis
       </a>
       , which was initially held in Selsey.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92">
        <a href="#cite_note-92">
         [92]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore appeared in other television and radio shows, including
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Just_a_Minute" title="Just a Minute">
         Just a Minute
        </a>
       </i>
       and, from 1992 until 1998, playing the role of
       <a href="/wiki/GamesMaster" title="GamesMaster">
        GamesMaster
       </a>
       in the television show of the same name: a character who professed to know everything there is to know about video gaming.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93">
        <a href="#cite_note-93">
         [93]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He would issue video game challenges and answered questions on cheats and tips. The show's host,
       <a href="/wiki/Dominik_Diamond" title="Dominik Diamond">
        Dominik Diamond
       </a>
       , claimed that Moore did not understand anything that he said on the show, yet managed to record his contributions in single takes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94">
        <a href="#cite_note-94">
         [94]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore was a keen amateur actor, appearing in local plays.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95">
        <a href="#cite_note-95">
         [95]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He appeared in self-parodying roles, in several episodes of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Goodies_(TV_series)" title="The Goodies (TV series)">
         The Goodies
        </a>
       </i>
       and on the
       <a href="/wiki/Morecambe_and_Wise" title="Morecambe and Wise">
        Morecambe and Wise
       </a>
       show, and broadcast with
       <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Horne" title="Kenneth Horne">
        Kenneth Horne
       </a>
       only a few days before Horne's death.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96">
        <a href="#cite_note-96">
         [96]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He had a minor role in the fourth radio series of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy" title="The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy">
         The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy
        </a>
       </i>
       , and a lead role in the
       <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">
        Radio 1
       </a>
       sci-fi BBC/20th Century Fox radio play,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_UK" title="Independence Day UK">
         Independence Day UK
        </a>
       </i>
       in which amongst other things, Moore fills in as a navigator. He appeared in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/It%27s_a_Knockout" title="It's a Knockout">
         It's a Celebrity Knockout
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Blankety_Blank" title="Blankety Blank">
         Blankety Blank
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Face_the_Music_(TV_series)" title="Face the Music (TV series)">
         Face the Music
        </a>
       </i>
       . He appeared on television at least once in a film prop
       <a href="/wiki/Space_suit" title="Space suit">
        space suit
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He expressed appreciation for the
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       television series
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">
         Doctor Who
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">
         Star Trek
        </a>
       </i>
       , but stated that he had stopped watching when "they went
       <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">
        PC
       </a>
       - making women commanders, that kind of thing".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97">
        <a href="#cite_note-97">
         [97]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite this he subsequently made a
       <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">
        cameo appearance
       </a>
       in the
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       episode "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Eleventh_Hour_(Doctor_Who)" title="The Eleventh Hour (Doctor Who)">
        The Eleventh Hour
       </a>
       " in 2010, which was
       <a href="/wiki/Matt_Smith_(actor)" title="Matt Smith (actor)">
        Matt Smith
       </a>
       's debut as the
       <a href="/wiki/Eleventh_Doctor" title="Eleventh Doctor">
        Eleventh Doctor
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98">
        <a href="#cite_note-98">
         [98]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the 1960s, Moore had been approached by the
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Story_editor" title="Story editor">
        story editor
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Davis_(screenwriter)" title="Gerry Davis (screenwriter)">
        Gerry Davis
       </a>
       to act as a scientific advisor on the series to help with the accuracy of stories, a position ultimately taken by
       <a href="/wiki/Kit_Pedler" title="Kit Pedler">
        Kit Pedler
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burk/Smith_99-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Burk/Smith-99">
         [99]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A keen amateur chess player, Moore carried a pocket set and was vice president of Sussex Junior Chess Association.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100">
        <a href="#cite_note-100">
         [100]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2003, he presented Sussex Junior
       <a href="/wiki/David_Howell_(chess_player)" title="David Howell (chess player)">
        David Howell
       </a>
       with the best young chess player award on
       <a href="/wiki/Carlton_Television" title="Carlton Television">
        Carlton Television
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Britain's Brilliant Prodigies
       </i>
       show. Moore had represented Sussex in his youth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page_41_41-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-page_41-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore was an enthusiastic amateur cricketer, playing for the Selsey Cricket Club well into his seventies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101">
        <a href="#cite_note-101">
         [101]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He played for the
       <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Taverners" title="Lord's Taverners">
        Lord's Taverners
       </a>
       , a cricketing charity team, as a bowler with an unorthodox action. Though an accomplished
       <a href="/wiki/Leg_spin" title="Leg spin">
        leg spin
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Bowling_(cricket)" title="Bowling (cricket)">
        bowler
       </a>
       , he was a
       <a href="/wiki/Batting_order_(cricket)" title="Batting order (cricket)">
        number 11 batsman
       </a>
       and a poor
       <a href="/wiki/Fielding_(cricket)" title="Fielding (cricket)">
        fielder
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102">
        <a href="#cite_note-102">
         [102]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The jacket notes to his book "Suns, Myths and Men" (1968) said his hobbies included "chess, which he plays with a peculiar leg-spin, and cricket." He played golf, and won a Pro-Am competition in
       <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">
        Southampton
       </a>
       in 1975.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103">
        <a href="#cite_note-103">
         [103]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Until forced to give up because of
       <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">
        arthritis
       </a>
       , Moore was a keen
       <a href="/wiki/Pianist" title="Pianist">
        pianist
       </a>
       and accomplished
       <a href="/wiki/Xylophone" title="Xylophone">
        xylophone
       </a>
       player, having first played the instrument at the age of 13.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104">
        <a href="#cite_note-104">
         [104]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He composed a substantial corpus of works, including two
       <a href="/wiki/Operetta" title="Operetta">
        operettas
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105">
        <a href="#cite_note-105">
         [105]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore had a ballet,
       <i>
        Lyra's Dream
       </i>
       , written to his music. He performed at a
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Command_Performance" title="Royal Command Performance">
        Royal Command Performance
       </a>
       , and performed a duet with
       <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Glennie" title="Evelyn Glennie">
        Evelyn Glennie
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106">
        <a href="#cite_note-106">
         [106]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1998, as
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Have_I_Got_News_for_You_episodes#Series_15" title="List of Have I Got News for You episodes">
        a guest
       </a>
       on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Have_I_Got_News_for_You" title="Have I Got News for You">
         Have I Got News for You
        </a>
        ,
       </i>
       he accompanied the show's closing theme tune on the xylophone and as a pianist, he once accompanied
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Albert Einstein
       </a>
       playing
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Le_cygne" title="Le cygne">
         The Swan
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">
        Camille Saint-Saëns
       </a>
       on the violin (no recording was made).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107">
        <a href="#cite_note-107">
         [107]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1981 he performed a solo xylophone rendition of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols" title="Sex Pistols">
        Sex Pistols
       </a>
       ' "
       <a href="/wiki/Anarchy_in_the_U.K." title="Anarchy in the U.K.">
        Anarchy in the U.K.
       </a>
       " in a
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Variety_Performance" title="Royal Variety Performance">
        Royal Variety Performance
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108">
        <a href="#cite_note-108">
         [108]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He did not enjoy most popular music: when played ten modern
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">
        rock
       </a>
       songs by such artists as
       <a href="/wiki/Hawkwind" title="Hawkwind">
        Hawkwind
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Muse_(band)" title="Muse (band)">
        Muse
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">
        Pink Floyd
       </a>
       , in a 2009 interview with journalist
       <a href="/wiki/Joel_McIver" title="Joel McIver">
        Joel McIver
       </a>
       , he explained, "To my ear, all these songs are universally awful."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109">
        <a href="#cite_note-109">
         [109]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Before encountering health problems he was an extensive traveller, and had visited all seven continents, including Antarctica; he said his favourite two countries were
       <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">
        Iceland
       </a>
       and Norway.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110">
        <a href="#cite_note-110">
         [110]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 7 March 2006 he was hospitalised and fitted with a
       <a href="/wiki/Artificial_cardiac_pacemaker" title="Artificial cardiac pacemaker">
        pacemaker
       </a>
       because of a cardiac abnormality.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111">
        <a href="#cite_note-111">
         [111]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He was a friend of
       <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">
        Queen
       </a>
       guitarist and astrophysicist
       <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">
        Brian May
       </a>
       , who was an occasional guest on
       <i>
        The Sky at Night
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112">
        <a href="#cite_note-112">
         [112]
        </a>
       </sup>
       May bought Moore's Selsey home in 2008, leasing it back to him for a
       <a href="/wiki/Peppercorn_(legal)" title="Peppercorn (legal)">
        peppercorn rent
       </a>
       the same day to provide financial security.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113">
        <a href="#cite_note-113">
         [113]
        </a>
       </sup>
       May, Moore and
       <a href="/wiki/Chris_Lintott" title="Chris Lintott">
        Chris Lintott
       </a>
       co-wrote a book
       <i>
        Bang! The Complete History of the Universe
       </i>
       . In February 2011, Moore completed (with Robin Rees and Iain Nicolson) his comprehensive
       <i>
        Patrick Moore's Data Book of Astronomy
       </i>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Press" title="Cambridge University Press">
        Cambridge University Press
       </a>
       . In 1986 he was identified as the co-author of a book published in 1954 called
       <i>
        Flying Saucer from Mars
       </i>
       , attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Cedric_Allingham" title="Cedric Allingham">
        Cedric Allingham
       </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allen_114-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-allen-114">
         [114]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moore never admitted his involvement. He once joined the
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_flat_Earth_societies" title="Modern flat Earth societies">
        Flat Earth Society
       </a>
       as an ironic joke.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115">
        <a href="#cite_note-115">
         [115]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore believed himself to be the only person to have met the first aviator,
       <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">
        Orville Wright
       </a>
       , the first man in space,
       <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">
        Yuri Gagarin
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>
       .

       <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hollander" title="Tom Hollander">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Honours_and_appointments">
        Honours and appointments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Honours and appointments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1945, Moore was elected a
       <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Astronomical Society">
        Fellow of the Royal Astronomical Society
       </a>
       (FRAS), and in 1977 he was awarded the society's
       <a href="/wiki/Jackson-Gwilt_Medal" title="Jackson-Gwilt Medal">
        Jackson-Gwilt Medal
       </a>
       . In 1968, he was appointed an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire">
        Officer of the Order of the British Empire
       </a>
       (OBE) and promoted to a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire">
        Commander of the same Order
       </a>
       (CBE) in 1988. In 1999 he became the Honorary President of the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=East_Sussex_Astronomical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="East Sussex Astronomical Society (page does not exist)">
        East Sussex Astronomical Society
       </a>
       , a position he held until his death. Moore was
       <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">
        knighted
       </a>
       for "services to the popularisation of science and to broadcasting" in 2001.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118">
        <a href="#cite_note-118">
         [118]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2001, he was appointed an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Honorary Fellow of the Royal Society">
        Honorary Fellow of the Royal Society
       </a>
       (HonFRS), the only amateur astronomer ever to achieve the distinction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119">
        <a href="#cite_note-119">
         [119]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In June 2002, he was appointed as the Honorary Vice-President of the
       <a href="/wiki/Society_for_the_History_of_Astronomy" title="Society for the History of Astronomy">
        Society for the History of Astronomy
       </a>
       . Also in 2002,
       <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
        Buzz Aldrin
       </a>
       presented him with a
       <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">
        British Academy of Film and Television Arts
       </a>
       (BAFTA) award for services to television.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120">
        <a href="#cite_note-120">
         [120]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was patron of
       <a href="/wiki/Torquay_Boys%27_Grammar_School" title="Torquay Boys' Grammar School">
        Torquay Boys' Grammar School
       </a>
       in south Devon. Moore had a long association with the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leicester" title="University of Leicester">
        University of Leicester
       </a>
       and its Department of Physics and Astronomy, and was awarded an Honorary
       <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Science" title="Doctor of Science">
        Doctor of Science
       </a>
       (HonDSc) degree in 1996 and a Distinguished Honorary Fellowship in 2008, the highest award that the university can bestow.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bull1_121-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bull1-121">
         [121]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2009, after saving
       <a href="/wiki/Airdrie_Public_Observatory" title="Airdrie Public Observatory">
        Airdrie Public Observatory
       </a>
       from closure in 2002, Moore accepted the position of Honorary President of
       <a href="/wiki/Airdrie_Public_Observatory#Airdrie_Astronomical_Association" title="Airdrie Public Observatory">
        Airdrie Astronomical Association
       </a>
       , a position which he held until his death.
      </p>

      <p>
       Moore wrote many popular books. The website
       <i>
        Astronomy Now Online
       </i>
       estimates that he published over a thousand books on popular science, including all known editions and reprints.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122">
        <a href="#cite_note-122">
         [122]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From 1962 to 2011 he also edited the long running
       <i>
        Yearbook of Astronomy
       </i>
       , published annually; and was editor on many other science books in that period. He also wrote science fiction novels for children; and, under the pen name R T Fishall,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123">
        <a href="#cite_note-123">
         [123]
        </a>
       </sup>
