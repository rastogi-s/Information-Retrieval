      <p>
       A
       <b>
        pinhole camera
       </b>
       is a simple camera without a lens but with a tiny
       <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">
        aperture
       </a>
       , a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pinhole_(optics)" title="Pinhole (optics)">
         pinhole
        </a>
       </i>
       – effectively a light-proof box with a small hole in one side. Light from a scene passes through the aperture and projects an inverted image on the opposite side of the box, which is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_obscura" title="Camera obscura">
        camera obscura
       </a>
       effect.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinhole_camera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Camera_obscura">
        Camera obscura
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinhole_camera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Camera obscura">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The camera obscura or
       <b>
        pinhole image
       </b>
       is a natural optical phenomenon. Early known descriptions are found in the Chinese
       <a href="/wiki/Mozi_(book)" title="Mozi (book)">
        Mozi
       </a>
       writings (circa 500 BC) and the Aristotelian
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Problems_(Aristotle)" title="Problems (Aristotle)">
         Problems
        </a>
       </i>
       (circa 300 BC – 600 AD). A practical demonstration of the pinhole effect from 700 AD is still in existence at the Virupaksha Temple in Hampi, India. Starting with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alhazen" title="Alhazen">
        Alhazen
       </a>
       (965–1039), the effect was used in dark rooms, mostly to study the nature of light and to safely watch
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_della_Porta" title="Giambattista della Porta">
        Giambattista della Porta
       </a>
       wrote in 1558 in his
       <a href="/wiki/Magia_Naturalis" title="Magia Naturalis">
        Magia Naturalis
       </a>
       about using a convex mirror to project the image onto paper and to use this as a drawing aid. However, about the same time the use of a lens instead of a pinhole was introduced. In the 17th century the camera obscura with a lens became a popular drawing aid that was further developed into a mobile device, first in a little tent and later in a box. The
       <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">
        photographic
       </a>
       camera, as developed early in the 19th century, was basically an adaptation of the box-type camera obscura.
      </p>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camera_Obscura" title="Camera Obscura">
        Camera Obscura
       </a>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_pinhole_photography">
        Early pinhole photography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinhole_camera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early pinhole photography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The first known description of pinhole photography is found in the 1856 book
       <i>
        The Stereoscope
       </i>
       by Scottish inventor
       <a href="/wiki/David_Brewster" title="David Brewster">
        David Brewster
       </a>
       , including the description of the idea as "a camera without lenses, and with only a pin-hole". One older use of the term "pin-hole" in the context of optics was found in James Ferguson's 1764 book
       <i>
        Lectures on select subjects in mechanics, hydrostatics, pneumatics, and optics
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sir
       <a href="/wiki/William_Crookes" title="William Crookes">
        William Crookes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/William_de_Wiveleslie_Abney" title="William de Wiveleslie Abney">
        William de Wiveleslie Abney
       </a>
       were other early photographers to try the pinhole technique.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_integral_photography_experiments">
        Film and integral photography experiments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinhole_camera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Film and integral photography experiments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       According to inventor
       <a href="/wiki/William_Kennedy_Dickson" title="William Kennedy Dickson">
        William Kennedy Dickson
       </a>
       , the first experiments directed at moving pictures by
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">
        Thomas Edison
       </a>
       and his researchers took place around 1887 and involved "microscopic pin-point photographs, placed on a cylindrical shell". The size of the cylinder corresponded with their
       <a href="/wiki/Phonograph" title="Phonograph">
        phonograph
       </a>
       cylinder as they wanted to combine the moving images with sound recordings. Problems arose in recording clear pictures "with phenomenal speed" and the "coarseness" of the photographic emulsion when the pictures were enlarged. The microscopic pin-point photographs were soon abandoned.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1893 the
       <a href="/wiki/Kinetoscope" title="Kinetoscope">
        Kinetoscope
       </a>
       was finally introduced with moving pictures on celluloid film strips. The camera that recorded the images, dubbed
       <i>
        Kinetograph
       </i>
       , was fitted with a lens.
      </p>
      <p>
       Eugène Estanave experimented with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integral_photography" title="Integral photography">
        integral photography
       </a>
       , exhibiting a result in 1925 and publishing his findings in
       <i>
        La Nature
       </i>
       . After 1930 he chose to continue his experiments with pinholes replacing the lenticular screen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timby_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Timby-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinhole_camera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The image of a pinhole camera may be projected onto a translucent screen for real-time viewing (used for safe observation of solar eclipses) or to trace the image on paper. But it is more often used without a translucent screen for pinhole photography with
       <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">
        photographic film
       </a>
       or photographic paper placed on the surface opposite to the pinhole aperture.
      </p>
      <p>
       A common use of pinhole photography is to capture the movement of the sun over a long period of time. This type of photography is called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_exposure_photography#Solargraphy" title="Long exposure photography">
        solargraphy
       </a>
       . Pinhole photography is used for artistic reasons, but also for educational purposes to let pupils learn about, and experiment with, the basics of photography.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pinhole cameras with CCDs (
       <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
        charge-coupled devices
       </a>
       ) are sometimes used for
       <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">
        surveillance
       </a>
       because they are difficult to detect.
      </p>
      <p>
       Related cameras, image forming devices, or developments from it include Franke's widefield pinhole camera, the
       <a href="/wiki/Pinspeck_camera" title="Pinspeck camera">
        pinspeck camera
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Pinhead_mirror" title="Pinhead mirror">
        pinhead mirror
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_of_pinhole_camera_photography">
        Characteristics of pinhole camera photography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinhole_camera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Characteristics of pinhole camera photography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Pinhole photographs have nearly infinite depth of field, everything appears
        <i>
         in focus
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        As there's no lens distortion, wide angle images remain absolutely rectilinear.
       </li>
       <li>
        Exposure times are usually long, resulting in motion blur around moving objects and the absence of objects that moved too fast.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Other special features can be built into pinhole cameras such as the ability to take double images by using multiple pinholes, or the ability to take pictures in
       <a href="/wiki/Cylindrical_perspective" title="Cylindrical perspective">
        cylindrical
       </a>
       or
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spherical_perspective&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spherical perspective (page does not exist)">
        spherical perspective
       </a>
       by curving the film plane.
      </p>

      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PinholeCameraAndRelatedSupplies.jpg">
         <img alt="A pinhole camera made from an oatmeal container, wrapped in opaque plastic to prevent light leaks; a box of photographic paper; tongs and dishes for developing film; bottles of film developing chemicals" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1680" data-file-width="2592" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7d/PinholeCameraAndRelatedSupplies.jpg/220px-PinholeCameraAndRelatedSupplies.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7d/PinholeCameraAndRelatedSupplies.jpg/330px-PinholeCameraAndRelatedSupplies.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7d/PinholeCameraAndRelatedSupplies.jpg/440px-PinholeCameraAndRelatedSupplies.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PinholeCameraAndRelatedSupplies.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A home-made pinhole camera (on the left), wrapped in black plastic to prevent light leaks, and related developing supplies
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Pinhole cameras can be handmade by the photographer for a particular purpose. In its simplest form, the photographic pinhole camera can consist of a light-tight box with a pinhole in one end, and a piece of film or photographic paper wedged or taped into the other end. A flap of cardboard with a tape hinge can be used as a shutter. The pinhole may be punched or drilled using a sewing needle or small diameter bit through a piece of tinfoil or thin aluminum or brass sheet. This piece is then taped to the inside of the light-tight box behind a hole cut through the box. A cylindrical oatmeal container may be made into a pinhole camera.
      </p>
      <p>
       The interior of an effective pinhole camera is black to avoid any reflection of the entering light onto the photographic material or viewing screen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Pinhole cameras can be constructed with a sliding film holder or back so the distance between the film and the pinhole can be adjusted. This allows the
       <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_view" title="Angle of view">
        angle of view
       </a>
       of the camera to be changed and also the effective
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-stop" title="F-stop">
        f-stop
       </a>
       ratio of the camera. Moving the film closer to the pinhole will result in a wide angle field of view and a shorter exposure time. Moving the film farther away from the pinhole will result in a telephoto or narrow angle view and a longer exposure time.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pinhole cameras can also be constructed by replacing the lens assembly in a conventional camera with a pinhole. In particular, compact 35 mm cameras whose lens and focusing assembly have been damaged can be reused as pinhole cameras—maintaining the use of the shutter and film winding mechanisms. As a result of the enormous increase in
       <a href="/wiki/F-number" title="F-number">
        f-number
       </a>
       while maintaining the same exposure time, one must use a fast film in direct sunshine.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pinholes (homemade or commercial) can be used in place of the lens on an SLR. Use with a digital SLR allows metering and composition by trial and error, and is effectively free, so is a popular way to try pinhole photography.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCW_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PCW-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Unusual materials have been used to construct pinhole cameras, e.g., a Chinese roast duck
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       by Martin Cheung.
      </p>
      <div style="clear:both;">
      </div>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Selection_of_pinhole_size">
        Selection of pinhole size
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinhole_camera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Selection of pinhole size">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Up to a certain point, the smaller the hole, the sharper the image, but the dimmer the projected image. Optimally, the size of the aperture should be 1/100 or less of the distance between it and the projected image.
      </p>
      <p>
       Within limits, a smaller pinhole (with a thinner surface that the hole goes through) will result in sharper image
       <a href="/wiki/Image_resolution" title="Image resolution">
        resolution
       </a>
       because the projected
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_confusion" title="Circle of confusion">
        circle of confusion
       </a>
       at the image plane is practically the same size as the pinhole. An extremely small hole, however, can produce significant
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">
        diffraction
       </a>
       effects and a less clear image due to the wave properties of light.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hecht_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hecht-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additionally,
       <a href="/wiki/Vignetting" title="Vignetting">
        vignetting
       </a>
       occurs as the diameter of the hole approaches the thickness of the material in which it is punched, because the sides of the hole obstruct the light entering at anything other than 90 degrees.
      </p>
      <p>
       The best pinhole is perfectly round (since irregularities cause higher-order diffraction effects), and in an extremely thin piece of material. Industrially produced pinholes benefit from
       <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">
        laser
       </a>
       etching, but a hobbyist can still produce pinholes of sufficiently high quality for photographic work.
      </p>
      <p>
       One method is to start with a sheet of
       <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">
        brass
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shim_(engineering)" title="Shim (engineering)">
        shim
       </a>
       or metal reclaimed from an aluminium drinks can or
       <a href="/wiki/Tin_foil" title="Tin foil">
        tin foil
       </a>
       /
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum_foil" title="Aluminum foil">
        aluminum foil
       </a>
       , use fine sand paper to reduce the thickness of the centre of the material to the minimum, before carefully creating a pinhole with a suitably sized needle.
      </p>
      <p>
       A method of calculating the optimal pinhole diameter was first attempted by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jozef_Maximili%C3%A1n_Petzval" title="Jozef Maximilián Petzval">
        Jozef Petzval
       </a>
       . The crispest image is obtained using a pinhole size determined by the formula
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
          <math alttext="{\displaystyle d=2{\sqrt {f\lambda }}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
           <semantics>
            <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
             <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
              <mi>
               d
              </mi>
              <mo>
               =
              </mo>
              <mn>
               2
              </mn>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <msqrt>
                <mi>
                 f
                </mi>
                <mi>
                 λ
                 <!-- λ -->
                </mi>
               </msqrt>
              </mrow>
             </mstyle>
            </mrow>
            <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
             {\displaystyle d=2{\sqrt {f\lambda }}}
            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="{\displaystyle d=2{\sqrt {f\lambda }}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c66aac939b3cca4f3513cd3a5787adfc0948ed5" style="vertical-align: -1.171ex; width:10.434ex; height:3.509ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       where
       <i>
        d
       </i>
       is pinhole diameter,
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       is focal length (distance from pinhole to image plane) and
       <i>
        λ
       </i>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelength
       </a>
       of light.
      </p>
      <p>
       For standard black-and-white film, a wavelength of light corresponding to yellow-green (550
       <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">
        nm
       </a>
       ) should yield optimum results. For a pinhole-to-film distance of 1 inch (25 mm), this works out to a pinhole 0.236 mm in diameter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For 5 cm, the appropriate diameter is 0.332 mm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">
        depth of field
       </a>
       is basically
       <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">
        infinite
       </a>
       , but this does not mean that no optical blurring occurs. The infinite depth of field means that image blur depends not on object distance, but on other factors, such as the distance from the aperture to the
       <a href="/wiki/Film_plane" title="Film plane">
        film plane
       </a>
       , the aperture size, the wavelength(s) of the light source, and motion of the subject or canvas.
      </p>

      <p>
       In the 1970s, Young measured the resolution limit of the pinhole camera as a function of pinhole diameter
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and later published a tutorial in The Physics Teacher.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Partly to enable a variety of diameters and focal lengths, he defined two normalized variables: pinhole radius divided by resolution limit, and focal length divided by the quantity
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       /λ, where
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       is the radius of the pinhole and λ is the wavelength of the light, typically about 550 nm. His results are plotted in the figure.
      </p>
      <p>
       To the left, the pinhole is large, and geometric optics applies; the resolution limit is about 1.5 times the radius of the pinhole. (Spurious resolution is also seen in the geometric-optics limit.) To the right, the pinhole is small, and
       <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_diffraction" title="Fraunhofer diffraction">
        Fraunhofer diffraction
       </a>
       applies; the resolution limit is given by the far-field diffraction formula shown in the graph and now increases as the pinhole is made smaller. In the region of near-field diffraction (or
       <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_diffraction" title="Fresnel diffraction">
        Fresnel diffraction
       </a>
       ), the pinhole focuses the light slightly, and the resolution limit is minimized when the focal length
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       (the distance between the pinhole and the film plane) is given by
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       =
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       /λ. At this focal length, the pinhole focuses the light slightly, and the resolution limit is about 2/3 of the radius of the pinhole. The pinhole in this case is equivalent to a Fresnel zone plate with a single zone. The value
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       /λ is in a sense the natural focal length of the pinhole.
      </p>
      <p>
       The relation
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       =
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       /
       <i>
        λ
       </i>
       yields an optimum pinhole diameter d = 2
       <span class="nowrap">
        √
        <span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;">
         <i>
          fλ
         </i>
        </span>
       </span>
       , so the experimental value differs slightly from the estimate of Petzval, above.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Calculating_the_f-number_.26_required_exposure">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Calculating_the_f-number_&amp;_required_exposure">
        Calculating the f-number &amp; required exposure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinhole_camera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Calculating the f-number &amp; required exposure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pinhole_hydrant_neg_pos.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4332" data-file-width="3000" height="318" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Pinhole_hydrant_neg_pos.jpg/220px-Pinhole_hydrant_neg_pos.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Pinhole_hydrant_neg_pos.jpg/330px-Pinhole_hydrant_neg_pos.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Pinhole_hydrant_neg_pos.jpg/440px-Pinhole_hydrant_neg_pos.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pinhole_hydrant_neg_pos.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A
         <a href="/wiki/Fire_hydrant" title="Fire hydrant">
          fire hydrant
         </a>
         photographed by a pinhole camera made from a shoe box, exposed on photographic paper (top). The length of the exposure was 40 seconds. There is noticeable flaring in the bottom-right corner of the image, likely due to extraneous light entering the camera box.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The f-number of the camera may be calculated by dividing the distance from the pinhole to the imaging plane (the
       <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">
        focal length
       </a>
       ) by the diameter of the pinhole. For example, a camera with a 0.5 mm diameter pinhole, and a 50 mm focal length would have an f-number of 50/0.5, or 100 (
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       /100 in conventional notation).
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to the large f-number of a pinhole camera, exposures will often encounter
       <a href="/wiki/Reciprocity_(photography)" title="Reciprocity (photography)">
        reciprocity
       </a>
       failure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Once exposure time has exceeded about 1 second for film or 30 seconds for paper, one must compensate for the breakdown in linear response of the film/paper to intensity of illumination by using longer exposures.
      </p>
      <p>
       Exposures projected on to modern light-sensitive photographic film can typically range from five seconds up to as much as several hours, with smaller pinholes requiring longer exposures to produce the same size image. Because a pinhole camera requires a lengthy exposure, its
       <a href="/wiki/Shutter_(photography)" title="Shutter (photography)">
        shutter
       </a>
       may be manually operated, as with a flap made of opaque material to cover and uncover the pinhole.
      </p>

       </span>

        </span>

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

       A non-focusing coded-aperture optical system may be thought of as multiple pinhole cameras in conjunction. By adding pinholes, light throughput and thus sensitivity are increased. However, multiple images are formed, usually requiring computer
       <a href="/wiki/Deconvolution" title="Deconvolution">
        deconvolution
       </a>
       .
      </p>
