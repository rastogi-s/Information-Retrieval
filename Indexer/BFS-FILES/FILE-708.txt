    Bow shocks in astrophysics
   </h1>

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Bow_shock_(aerodynamics)" title="Bow shock (aerodynamics)">
        Bow shocks
       </a>
       form the boundary between a
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       and an ambient magnetized medium. This occurs when the magnetic field of an astrophysical object interacts with the nearby flowing ambient
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       . For example, when the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       , flowing with a relative speed of order 400 km/s, encounters the magnetic field of Earth, a bow shape boundary forms. For Earth and other magnetized planets, it is the boundary at which the speed of the stellar wind abruptly drops as a result of its approach to the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetopause" title="Magnetopause">
        magnetopause
       </a>
       . For stars, this boundary is typically the edge of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrosphere" title="Astrosphere">
        astrosphere
       </a>
       , where the
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">
        stellar wind
       </a>
       meets the
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">
        interstellar medium
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>

       <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">
        shock wave
       </a>
       is that the bulk velocity of the
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma

      <p>
       A common complication in astrophysics is the presence of a magnetic field. For instance, the charged particles making up the solar wind follow spiral paths along magnetic field lines. The velocity of each particle as it gyrates around a field line can be treated similarly to a thermal velocity in an ordinary gas, and in an ordinary gas, the mean thermal velocity is roughly the speed of sound. At the bow shock, the bulk forward velocity of the wind (which is the component of the velocity parallel to the field lines about which the particles gyrate) drops below the speed at which the particles are gyrating.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Around_the_Earth">
        Around the Earth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Around the Earth">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The best-studied example of a bow shock is that occurring where the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        Sun's wind
       </a>
       encounters
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's magnetopause, although bow shocks occur around all magnetized planets, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter#Magnetosphere" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn#Magnetosphere" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Earth's bow shock is about 17 kilometres (11 mi) thick
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and located about 90,000 kilometres (56,000 mi) from the planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Around_the_Sun">
        Around the Sun
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Around the Sun">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere#Bow_shock" title="Heliosphere">
        Heliosphere § Bow shock
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       For several decades, the solar wind has been thought to form a bow shock at the edge of the
       <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">
        heliosphere
       </a>
       , where it collides with the surrounding interstellar medium. Moving away from the Sun, the point where the solar wind flow becomes subsonic is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Termination_shock" title="Termination shock">
        termination shock
       </a>
       , the point where the interstellar medium and solar wind pressures balance is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">
        heliopause
       </a>
       , and the point where the flow of the interstellar medium becomes subsonic would be the bow shock. This solar bow shock was thought to lie at a distance around 230 AU
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       from the Sun – more than twice the distance of the termination shock as encountered by the Voyager spacecraft. However, data obtained in 2012 from NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer" title="Interstellar Boundary Explorer">
        Interstellar Boundary Explorer
       </a>
       (IBEX) indicates the lack of any solar bow shock.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bow_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bow-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Along with corroborating results from the
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">
        Voyager spacecraft
       </a>
       , these findings have motivated some theoretical refinements; current thinking is that formation of a bow shock is prevented, at least in the galactic region through which the Sun is passing, by a combination of the strength of the local interstellar magnetic-field and of the relative velocity of the heliosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Around_other_stars">
        Around other stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Around other stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Around_massive_stars">
        Around massive stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Around massive stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       If a massive star is a
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_kinematics#Runaway_stars" title="Stellar kinematics">
        runaway star
       </a>
       , it can form an
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       bow-shock that is detectable in 24 μm and sometimes in 8μm of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">
        spitzer space telescope
       </a>
       or the W3/W4-channels of
       <a href="/wiki/Wide-field_Infrared_Survey_Explorer" title="Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer">
        WISE
       </a>
       . In 2016 Kobulnicky et al. did create the largest spitzer/WISE bow-shock catalog to date with 709 bow-shock candidates.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To get a larger bow-shock catalog the
       <a href="/wiki/Citizen_science" title="Citizen science">
        citizen science
       </a>
       project
       <a href="/wiki/The_Milky_Way_Project" title="The Milky Way Project">
        Milky Way Project
       </a>
       aims to map infrared bow-shocks in the galactic plane. This larger catalog will help to understand the
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">
        stellar wind
       </a>
       of massive stars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_draping_effect">
        Magnetic draping effect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Magnetic draping effect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A similar effect, known as the magnetic draping effect, occurs when a super-Alfvenic plasma flow impacts an unmagnetized object such as what happens when the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       reaches the ionosphere of
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the flow deflects around the object draping the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       along the wake flow.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The condition for the flow to be super-Alfvenic means that the relative velocity between the flow and object,
       <span class="mwe-math-element">

        </span>

       </span>
       is the draped magnetic field near the object, and
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

       </span>
       is the relative speed between the plasma and the object. Magnetic draping has been detected around planets, moons, solar coronal mass ejections, and galaxies.
