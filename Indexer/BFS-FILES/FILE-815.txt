      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Moon illusion
       </b>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_illusion" title="Optical illusion">
        optical illusion
       </a>
       which causes the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       to appear larger near the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       than it does higher up in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">
        sky
       </a>
       . It has been known since ancient times and recorded by various cultures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wade_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wade-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross_Plug_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ross_Plug-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The explanation of this illusion is still debated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross_Plug_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ross_Plug-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hershenson_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hershenson-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proof_of_illusion">
        Proof of illusion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_illusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Proof of illusion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A fact, stretching back at least to
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       in the 4th century B.C., holds that the Moon appears larger near the horizon due to a real
       <a href="/wiki/Magnification" title="Magnification">
        magnification
       </a>
       effect caused by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
        Earth's atmosphere
       </a>
       , he legitimated a fact that this is an illusion perceived through the human eye. Everybody has legitimated this through our eyes in the low horizon. Human Illusions are only perceived through our senses when they are on the brink, to make us clear that we can not sense everything. The angle that the full Moon
       <a href="/wiki/Subtended_angle" title="Subtended angle">
        subtends
       </a>
       at an observer's eye can be measured directly with a
       <a href="/wiki/Theodolite" title="Theodolite">
        theodolite
       </a>
       to show that it remains constant as the Moon rises or sinks in the sky (discounting the very small variations due to the physical effects mentioned). Photographs of the Moon at different elevations also show that its size remains the same. A theodolite or camera changes the illusion of the human eye to another illusion. Aristotle looked at the human illusion appearance, not manipulation through perfect machines
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lynch_and_Livingstone_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lynch_and_Livingstone-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Human eyes connected to the brain can measure waves of light, sometimes it will make a bloom, especially certain colours witch the horizon can make an filter, then the brain will process it and give you the feedback of illusion. An equipment will give another illusion of symmetrical perfection perhaps. A simple way of demonstrating that the effect is an illusion is to hold a small pebble (say, 0.33 inches or 8.4 millimetres wide) at arm's length (25 inches or 640 millimetres) with one eye closed, positioning the pebble so that it covers (eclipses) the full Moon when high in the night sky. Then, when the seemingly very large Moon is on the horizon, the same pebble will also cover it, revealing that there has been no change in the size of the Moon, because the pebble will cover the bloom. The same results looking through a Theodolite with an solar filter.
      </p>
      <p>
       Between
       <i>
        different
       </i>
       full moons, the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        angular diameter
       </a>
       can vary from 29.43
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcminute" title="Arcminute">
        arcminutes
       </a>
       at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
        apogee
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Supermoon" title="Supermoon">
        33.5 arcminutes
       </a>
       at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
        perigee
       </a>
       —an increase of around 14% in apparent diameter or 30% in apparent area.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is because of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(astronomy)" title="Eccentricity (astronomy)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        Moon's orbit
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_explanations">
        Possible explanations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_illusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Possible explanations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The size of a viewed object can be measured objectively either as an
       <i>
        angular size
       </i>
       (the visual angle that it subtends at the eye, corresponding to the proportion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Visual_field" title="Visual field">
        visual field
       </a>
       that it occupies), or as
       <i>
        physical size
       </i>
       (its real size measured in, say, metres). Perceived size is only loosely related to these concepts, however. For example, if two identical, familiar objects are placed at distances of five and ten meters, respectively, then the more distant object subtends approximately half the visual angle of the nearer object, but we normally perceive that as the same size (a phenomenon referred to as
       <i>
        size constancy
       </i>
       ), not as half the size. Conversely, if the more distant object did subtend the same angle as the nearer object then we would normally perceive it to be twice as big.
      </p>
      <p>
       One question concerning the Moon illusion, therefore, is whether the horizon moon appears larger because its perceived angular size seems greater, or because its perceived physical size seems greater, or some combination of both. There is currently no consensus on this point. Most recent research on the Moon illusion has been conducted by psychologists specializing in human perception. The 1989 book
       <i>
        The Moon Illusion
       </i>
       , edited by Hershenson, offers about 24 chapters written by various illusion researchers reaching different conclusions. After reviewing the many different explanations in their 2002 book
       <i>
        The Mystery of the Moon Illusion
       </i>
       , Ross and Plug conclude "No single theory has emerged victorious".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They argue that the size of the illusion is variable, but is usually an apparent increase in diameter of about 50 percent. The most important factor is the sight of the terrain, but there is a small contribution from other factors such as the angle of regard, posture and oculomotor
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2017)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       commands.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Refraction_and_distance">
        Refraction and distance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_illusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Refraction and distance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       attempted to explain the Moon illusion through
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric" title="Atmospheric">
        atmospheric
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">
        refraction
       </a>
       in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">
         Almagest
        </a>
       </i>
       , and later (in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy#Optics" title="Ptolemy">
         Optics
        </a>
       </i>
       ) as an optical illusion due to
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_illusion#Apparent_distance_hypothesis" title="Moon illusion">
        apparent distance
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or the difficulty of looking upwards, although interpretations of the account in the
       <i>
        Optics
       </i>
       are disputed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Similarly
       <a href="/wiki/Cleomedes" title="Cleomedes">
        Cleomedes
       </a>
       (about 200 A.D.), in his book on astronomy, ascribed the illusion both to refraction and to changes in apparent distance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross_Plug_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ross_Plug-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Optics" title="Book of Optics">
         Book of Optics
        </a>
       </i>
       (1011–1022 A.D.),
       <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Haytham" title="Ibn al-Haytham">
        Ibn al-Haytham
       </a>
       (Alhazen) repeated refraction as an explanation, but also proposed a more detailed explanation based on intervening objects and apparent distance.
      </p>
      <p>
       Through additional works (by
       <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">
        Roger Bacon
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Pecham" title="John Pecham">
        John Pecham
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Witelo" title="Witelo">
        Witelo
       </a>
       , and others) based on Ibn al-Haytham's explanation, the Moon illusion came to be accepted as a psychological phenomenon in the 17th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hershenson1989_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hershenson1989-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apparent_distance_hypothesis">
        Apparent distance hypothesis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_illusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Apparent distance hypothesis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GoldenMedows.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="919" data-file-width="1225" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/GoldenMedows.jpg/220px-GoldenMedows.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/GoldenMedows.jpg/330px-GoldenMedows.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/GoldenMedows.jpg/440px-GoldenMedows.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GoldenMedows.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Clouds near the horizon are typically farther away from the viewer, while those high in the sky are closer, giving the impression of a flat, or gently curved, sky surface
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       An apparent distance theory evidently was first clearly described by
       <a href="/wiki/Cleomedes" title="Cleomedes">
        Cleomedes
       </a>
       around 200 A.D. The theory proposes that the horizon moon looks larger than the zenith moon because it looks farther away. Ibn al-Haytham was more specific. His argument was that judging the distance of an object depends on there being an uninterrupted sequence of intervening bodies between the object and the observer; however, since there are no intervening objects between the Earth and the Moon, the perceived distance is too short and the Moon appears smaller than on the horizon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross_Plug_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ross_Plug-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When we see objects such as clouds, birds, and aeroplanes in the sky, those near the horizon are typically farther away from us than those overhead. This may result in the perception of the sky itself as a comparatively flat or only gently curving surface in which objects moving towards the horizon always recede away from us.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1813,
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">
        Schopenhauer
       </a>
       wrote about this, that the moon illusion is "purely intellectual or cerebral and not optical or sensuous."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The brain takes the sense data that is given to it from the eye and it apprehends a large moon because "our intuitively perceiving understanding regards everything that is seen in a horizontal direction as being more distant and therefore as being larger than objects that are seen in a vertical direction."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The brain is accustomed to seeing terrestrially–sized objects in a horizontal direction and also as they are affected by atmospheric perspective, according to Schopenhauer.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_size_illusion.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="615" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Moon_size_illusion.png/220px-Moon_size_illusion.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Moon_size_illusion.png/330px-Moon_size_illusion.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Moon_size_illusion.png/440px-Moon_size_illusion.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_size_illusion.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A diagram of the moon seen against a cloud of the same size, at different heights in the sky. When the moon is high, the clouds it is against are closer to the viewer and appear larger. When the moon is low in the sky, the same clouds are further away and appear smaller, giving the illusion of a larger moon.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       If we perceive the Moon to be in the general vicinity of the other things we see in the sky, we would expect it to also recede as it approaches the horizon, which should result in a smaller
       <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">
        retinal
       </a>
       image. But since its retinal image is approximately the same size whether it is near the horizon or not, our brains, attempting to compensate for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_(visual)" title="Perspective (visual)">
        perspective
       </a>
       , assume that a low Moon must be physically larger.
      </p>
      <p>
       Extensive experiments in 1962 by Kaufman and Rock showed that a crucial causative factor in the illusion is a change in the pattern of cues to distance, comparable to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ponzo_illusion" title="Ponzo illusion">
        Ponzo illusion
       </a>
       . The horizon moon is perceived to be at the end of a stretch of terrain receding into the distance, accompanied by distant trees, buildings and so forth, all of which indicate that it must be a long way away, while these cues are absent from the zenith moon. Experiments by many other researchers have found the same result; namely, when pictorial cues to a great distance are subtracted from the vista of the large-looking horizon moon it looks smaller. When pictorial cues to an increased distance are added into the vista of the zenith moon, it appears larger.
      </p>
      <p>
       A potential problem for the apparent distance theory has been that very few people (perhaps about 5%) perceive the horizon moon as being both larger and farther away. Indeed, most people (perhaps 90%) say the horizon moon looks both larger and
       <i>
        closer
       </i>
       than the zenith moon (Boring, 1962; Hershenson, 1982; McCready, 1965, 1986; Restle, 1970). Most of the rest say it looks larger and about the same distance away as the zenith moon, with a few people reporting no Moon illusion at all. However, the response that the horizon moon appears larger, but not closer than the zenith moon could be because the viewer's logic confounds his or her perception; because the viewer knows that the moon can't possibly be
       <i>
        physically
       </i>
       farther away, he or she is not consciously aware of the perception. This is reinforced by the idea that we do not consciously perceive distance and size, as spatial awareness is a subconscious, retino cortical cognition. Nevertheless, the apparent distance explanation is the one most often found in textbooks.
      </p>
      <p>
       Those advocating the apparent distance hypothesis might counter that in the Ponzo illusion, people do not perceive one line to actually be farther away than another either, or that everything on the horizon is perceived as farther away, out of the habit that this is true for every object ever seen there (average-altitude clouds are about 100 times farther away at the horizon than when overhead).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_size_hypothesis">
        Relative size hypothesis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_illusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relative size hypothesis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ebbinghaus_Illusion.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="155" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Ebbinghaus_Illusion.svg/220px-Ebbinghaus_Illusion.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Ebbinghaus_Illusion.svg/330px-Ebbinghaus_Illusion.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Ebbinghaus_Illusion.svg/440px-Ebbinghaus_Illusion.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ebbinghaus_Illusion.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Ebbinghaus_illusion" title="Ebbinghaus illusion">
          Ebbinghaus illusion
         </a>
         . The lower central circle surrounded by small circles might represent the horizon moon accompanied by objects of smaller visual extent, while the upper central circle represents the zenith moon surrounded by expanses of sky of larger visual extent. Although both central circles are actually the same size, the lower one looks larger to many people.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Historically, the best-known alternative to the "apparent distance" theory has been a "relative size" theory. This states that the perceived size of an object depends not only on its retinal size, but also on the size of objects in its immediate visual environment. In the case of the Moon illusion, objects in the vicinity of the horizon moon (that is, objects on or near the horizon) exhibit a fine detail that makes the Moon appear larger, while the zenith moon is surrounded by large expanses of empty sky that make it appear smaller.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The effect is illustrated by the classic
       <a href="/wiki/Ebbinghaus_illusion" title="Ebbinghaus illusion">
        Ebbinghaus illusion
       </a>
       , where a circle appears larger when surrounded by smaller circles, than it does when surrounded by larger circles.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Angle_of_regard_hypothesis">
        Angle of regard hypothesis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_illusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Angle of regard hypothesis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       According to the "angle of regard" hypothesis, the Moon illusion is produced by changes in the position of the eyes in the head accompanying changes in the angle of elevation of the Moon. Though once popular, this explanation no longer has much support.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hershenson_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hershenson-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Looking through one's legs at the horizon moon does reduce the illusion noticeably, but this may be because the image on the retina is inverted. Raising the eyes or tilting the head when in an upright posture gives only a very small reduction in the illusion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross_Plug_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ross_Plug-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
