      <p>
       A
       <b>
        wet moon
       </b>
       (also called a
       <b>
        Cheshire moon
       </b>
       ) is the visual
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_event" title="Celestial event">
        phenomenon
       </a>
       when the "horns" of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        crescent Moon
       </a>
       point up at an angle, away from the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       , so that the
       <a href="/wiki/Crescent" title="Crescent">
        crescent
       </a>
       takes on the appearance of a
       <a href="/wiki/Bowl" title="Bowl">
        bowl
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Smile" title="Smile">
        smile
       </a>
       . A wet moon occurs when the crescent Moon is low above the horizon and at a point more or less directly above the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's (invisible)
       <a href="/wiki/Position_of_the_Sun" title="Position of the Sun">
        position
       </a>
       below the horizon. This in turn is determined by the positions of the Moon and Earth in their respective orbits, the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclinations
       </a>
       of these orbits relative to one another and to Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">
        celestial equator
       </a>
       , and the observer's
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       on Earth. Wet moons occur routinely in the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">
        tropics
       </a>
       (where the Sun and Moon rise and set nearly vertically), but rarely in the
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_regions_of_Earth" title="Polar regions of Earth">
        polar regions
       </a>
       (where the Sun and Moon rise and set at a glancing angle or not at all).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The terms
       <i>
        wet moon
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        dry moon
       </i>
       originate from
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_religion" title="Hawaiian religion">
        Hawaiian mythology
       </a>
       , where it was thought that the Moon appeared as a bowl that would fill up with rainwater. The period when this is most common, January 20 to February 18, corresponds with Kaelo the Water Bearer in Hawaiian astrology and makes the Moon known as the "dripping wet moon". As summer comes, the crescent shape shifts, pouring out the water and causing the summer rains. After the "bowl" empties, it dries out and rights itself, creating the "dry moon". However, others say that this is a wet moon because it permitted the water to pour out.
      </p>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        Cheshire moon
       </i>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Allusion" title="Allusion">
        reference
       </a>
       to the smile of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cheshire_Cat" title="Cheshire Cat">
        Cheshire Cat
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">
        Lewis Carroll
       </a>
       's novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">
         Alice's Adventures in Wonderland
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">
        Explanation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Explanation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_precession" title="Lunar precession">
        Lunar precession
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nodal_precession" title="Nodal precession">
        Nodal precession
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The annual path that the Sun appears to follow against the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        background
       </a>
       of relatively
       <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">
        fixed stars
       </a>
       is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       . Since the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        Moon's orbit
       </a>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclined
       </a>
       5.14° to the ecliptic, the Moon will always remain within about 5° north or south of the ecliptic. For half of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_month" title="Sidereal month">
        sidereal month
       </a>
       (with respect to the stars), the Moon is either north or south of the ecliptic. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_nodes" title="Orbital nodes">
        two points
       </a>
       where the Moon's orbit
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Intersection_(Euclidian_geometry)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Intersection (Euclidian geometry) (page does not exist)">
        intersects
       </a>
       that of Earth are called the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">
        lunar nodes
       </a>
       ; at the ascending node, the Moon moves north of the ecliptic, while at the descending node, the
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       moves south. Eclipses occur at these points, hence the name
       <i>
        ecliptic
       </i>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Nodal_precession" title="Nodal precession">
        nodes precess
       </a>
       around the ecliptic axis at the rate of one circuit every 18.6 years. The ecliptic makes the steepest angle to the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinoxes
       </a>
       . Since the crescent Moon appears near the Sun, the crescent would appear to lie on its back when low above the horizon around the equinoxes.
      </p>
