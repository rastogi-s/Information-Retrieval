   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Photic retinopathy
       </b>
       , also known as
       <b>
        foveomacular retinitis
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        solar retinopathy
       </b>
       , is damage to the
       <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">
        eye
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">
        retina
       </a>
       , particularly the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macula" title="Macula">
        macula
       </a>
       , from prolonged exposure to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">
        solar radiation
       </a>
       or other bright light, e.g., lasers or arc welders. The term includes solar, laser, and welder's retinopathy and is synonymous with retinal phototoxicity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It usually occurs due to
       <a href="/wiki/Sungazing" title="Sungazing">
        staring at the sun
       </a>
       , watching a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       , or viewing an
       <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
        ultraviolet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Illuminant_D65" title="Illuminant D65">
        Illuminant D65
       </a>
       , or other bright light.
       <sup>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_retinopathy" title="Solar retinopathy">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">
        Signs and symptoms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photic_retinopathy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Long-term reduced
        <a href="/wiki/Visual_acuity" title="Visual acuity">
         eyesight
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sefic_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Sefic-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kallmark_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Kallmark-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Central or paracentral
        <a href="/wiki/Scotoma" title="Scotoma">
         scotoma
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sefic_2-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Sefic-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kallmark_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Kallmark-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vision_loss" title="Vision loss">
        Vision loss
       </a>
       due to solar retinopathy is typically reversible,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen2_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chen2-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       lasting for as short as one month
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sefic_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sefic-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to over one year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kallmark_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kallmark-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The fundus changes are variable and usually bilateral, mild cases often show no alteration and moderate to severe cases show a
       <a href="/wiki/Fovea_centralis" title="Fovea centralis">
        foveal
       </a>
       yellow spot on the first days after exposure. After a few days it is replaced by a reddish dot often surrounded by pigment.
      </p>
      <p>
       Permanent holes and lesions are possible; prognosis worsens with dilated pupils or prolonged exposure.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">
        Pathophysiology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photic_retinopathy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although it is frequently claimed that the retina is burned by looking at the sun, retinal damage appears to occur primarily due to photochemical injury rather than thermal injury. The temperature rise from looking at the sun with a 3-mm pupil only causes a 4 °C increase in temperature, insufficient to photocoagulate. The energy is still phototoxic: since light promotes oxidation, chemical reactions occur in the exposed tissues with unbonded oxygen molecules.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It also appears that central serous retinopathy can be a result of a depression in a treated solar damaged eye.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen2_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chen2-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The duration of exposure necessary to cause injury varies with the intensity of light, and also affects the possibility and length of recovery
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">
        Diagnosis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photic_retinopathy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A person with photic retinopathy may notice an impairment in their vision, for example a spot that does not go away after a reasonable recovery time, or blurring. They may also have eye pain or headaches. Vision impairment is usually in both eyes, but
       <i>
        can
       </i>
       be in just one. Impairment of a person with 20/20 vision usually ends up being about 20/40 or 20/60, but can be better or far worse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retinalp_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-retinalp-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A doctor examining an eye with retinopathy may be able to see no signs at all, or a slight
       <a href="/wiki/Macular_edema" title="Macular edema">
        macular edema
       </a>
       , which is a sort of blister on or under the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macula" title="Macula">
        macula
       </a>
       , an oval colored spot normally visible to an eye doctor on each person's
       <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">
        retina
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       But while even that
       <a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">
        edema
       </a>
       goes away, within a few days the patient will generally develop a discoloration of the retina at the injured point, often yellow or white, turning red over the next few weeks.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">
        Treatment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photic_retinopathy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treatment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Photic retinopathy generally goes away on its own over time, but there is no specific treatment known to be reliable for speeding recovery. One path sometimes attempted, which has unclear results, is to treat the initial macular edema with
       <a href="/wiki/Corticosteroid" title="Corticosteroid">
        corticosteroids
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retinalp_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-retinalp-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prognosis">
        Prognosis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photic_retinopathy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prognosis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Generally speaking, people diagnosed with photic retinopathy recover visual acuity completely within two months,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retinalp_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-retinalp-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       though more severe cases may take longer, or not see complete recovery at all.
      </p>
