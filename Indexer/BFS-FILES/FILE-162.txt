      <p>
       A total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       occurred on March 20, 2015. A solar eclipse occurs when the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       passes between
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , thereby totally or partly obscuring the image of the Sun for a viewer on Earth. A total solar eclipse occurs when the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_diameter" title="Apparent diameter">
        apparent diameter
       </a>
       is larger than the Sun's, blocking all direct sunlight, turning day into darkness. Totality occurs in a narrow path across Earth's surface, with a partial solar eclipse visible over a surrounding region thousands of kilometres wide.
      </p>
      <p>
       It had a
       <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_of_eclipse" title="Magnitude of eclipse">
        magnitude
       </a>
       of 1.045. The longest duration of totality was 2 minutes and 47 seconds off the coast of the
       <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">
        Faroe Islands
       </a>
       . It will be the last total solar eclipse visible in Europe until
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_August_12,_2026" title="Solar eclipse of August 12, 2026">
        the eclipse of August 12, 2026
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The track of totality passed across the North Atlantic and into the Arctic Ocean. The only populated places from which the totality could be seen were the
       <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">
        Faroe Islands
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">
        Svalbard
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Event">
        Event
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_March_20,_2015&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Event">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The solar eclipse began at 08:30GMT in North West Europe and moved towards the northeast but still in northern Europe. It was most visible from the North Atlantic and Arctic Oceans,
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">
        Iceland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">
        Ireland
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">
        Faroe Islands
       </a>
       , northern
       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
        Norway
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Murmansk_Oblast" title="Murmansk Oblast">
        Murmansk Oblast
       </a>
       . The shadow began its pass off the south coast of Greenland. It then moved to the northeast, passing between Iceland and the United Kingdom before moving over the Faroe Islands and the northernmost islands of Norway. The shadow of the eclipse was visible in varying degrees all over Europe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, London experienced an 85% partial solar eclipse while points north of the Faroe Islands in the Norwegian Sea saw a complete solar eclipse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Three
       <a href="/wiki/Air_charter" title="Air charter">
        chartered airliners
       </a>
       flew above the clouds, giving passengers a slightly prolonged view.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The eclipse was observed at radio frequencies at the
       <a href="/wiki/Mets%C3%A4hovi_Radio_Observatory" title="Metsähovi Radio Observatory">
        Metsähovi Radio Observatory
       </a>
       , Finland, where a partial eclipse was seen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metsahovi_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-metsahovi-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The eclipse was also observed by meteorological satellite Meteosat-10.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eumetsatcasestudy_7-0">

        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meteosatvideo_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-meteosatvideo-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">
        Impact
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_March_20,_2015&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <div style="width:52px">
          <img alt="Ambox current red.svg" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="360" height="34" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" width="42"/>
         </div>
        </td>
        <td class="mbox-text">
         <div class="mbox-text-span">

           updated
          </b>

      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
        European Union
       </a>
       has a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
        solar power
       </a>
       output of about 90
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigawatt" title="Gigawatt">
        gigawatts
       </a>
       and production could have been temporarily decreased by up to 34 GW of that dependent on the clarity of the sky. In actuality the dip was less than expected, with a 13 GW drop in Germany happening due to overcast skies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This was the first time that an eclipse had a significant impact on the power system, and the electricity sector took measures to mitigate the impact. The power
       <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">
        gradient
       </a>
       (change in power) may be −400 MW/minute and +700 MW/minute. Places in
       <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
        Netherlands
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
        Denmark
       </a>
       may be 80% obscured.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eclImp_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-eclImp-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Temperature may decrease by 3 °C, and
       <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
        wind power
       </a>
       may decrease as winds are reduced by 0.7 m/s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSwind_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RSwind-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Coincidence_of_events">
        Coincidence of events
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_March_20,_2015&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coincidence of events">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In addition to the eclipse, 20 March 2015 was also the day of the March
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinox
       </a>
       (also known as the spring or
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox#Names" title="Equinox">
        vernal equinox
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
        Northern Hemisphere
       </a>
       ). In addition, six
       <a href="/wiki/Supermoon" title="Supermoon">
        supermoons
       </a>
       were expected for 2015. The supermoon on 20 March 2015 was the third of the year; however, it was a
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       (near side facing away from the sun), and only its shadow was visible.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_Significance">
        Religious Significance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_March_20,_2015&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religious Significance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_Moon_Prophecy" title="Blood Moon Prophecy">
        Blood Moon Prophecy
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Proponents of the Blood Moon Prophecy, such as Bob O'Dell
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       also pointed out that 20 March 2015 was also a significant day on the Jewish and Biblical calendar. That evening was the onset of the Hebrew month of
       <a href="/wiki/Nisan" title="Nisan">
        Nisan
       </a>
       , the first month in the Biblical calendar year. Furthermore, the path of the total eclipse over the North Pole
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       was a highly symbolic location infusing the day with both great natural significance and profound religious meaning according to O'Dell. Due to the significance of the eclipse, a global prayer event in Jerusalem was organized that day.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Eclipse_visibility">
        Eclipse visibility

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        <a href="#cite_note-18">

        </a>

       Ireland,

        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Portugal, France,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Germany,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Poland,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">

      <h3>
       <span id="Solar_eclipses_2015.E2.80.932018">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_eclipses_2015–2018">
        Solar eclipses 2015–2018
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_March_20,_2015&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Solar eclipses 2015–2018">

         ]
        </span>

       Each member in a
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_cycle#Eclipse_cycles" title="Eclipse cycle">
        semester series
       </a>
       of solar eclipses repeats approximately every 177 days and 4 hours (a semester) at alternating
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">
        nodes
       </a>
       of the Moon's orbit.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saros_series">
        Saros series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_March_20,_2015&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Saros series">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       It is a part of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Saros_120" title="Solar Saros 120">
        Saros cycle 120
       </a>
       , repeating every 18 years, 11 days, containing 71 events. The series started with partial solar eclipse on May 27, 933 AD, and reached an annular eclipse on August 11, 1059. It was a hybrid event for 3 dates: May 8, 1510, through May 29, 1546, and total eclipses from June 8, 1564, through March 30, 2033. The series ends at member 71 as a partial eclipse on July 7, 2195. The longest duration of totality was 2 minutes, 50 seconds on March 9, 1997.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Metonic_series">
        Metonic series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         ]
        </span>

       <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">
        metonic series
       </a>
       repeats eclipses every 19 years (6939.69 days), lasting about 5 cycles. Eclipses occur in nearly the same calendar date. In addition, the octon subseries repeats 1/5 of that or every 3.8 years (1387.94 days).
