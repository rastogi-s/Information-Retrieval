      <p>
       <b>
        Differential rotation
       </b>
       is seen when different parts of a rotating object move with different
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">
        angular velocities
       </a>
       (rates of
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">
        rotation
       </a>
       ) at different latitudes and/or depths of the body and/or in time. This indicates that the object is not solid. In fluid objects, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">
        accretion disks
       </a>
       , this leads to
       <a href="/wiki/Shearing_(physics)" title="Shearing (physics)">
        shearing
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">
        Galaxies
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">
        protostars
       </a>
       usually show differential rotation; examples in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       include the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Around the year 1610,
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
        Galileo Galilei
       </a>
       observed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">
        sunspots
       </a>
       and calculated the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_rotation" title="Solar rotation">
        rotation of the Sun
       </a>
       . In 1630,
       <a href="/wiki/Christoph_Scheiner" title="Christoph Scheiner">
        Christoph Scheiner
       </a>
       reported that the Sun had different rotational periods at the poles and at the equator, in good agreement with modern values.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_cause_of_differential_rotation">
        The cause of differential rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Differential_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The cause of differential rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because of the pre-stellar accretion phase, and the conservation of
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       , rotation is induced. Differential rotation is caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
        convection
       </a>
       in stars which is a movement of mass, due to steep temperature gradients from the core outwards. This mass carries a portion of the star’s angular momentum, thus redistributing the angular velocity, possibly even far enough out for the star to lose angular velocity in
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">
        stellar winds
       </a>
       . Differential rotation thus depends on temperature differences in adjacent regions.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_differential_rotation">
        Measuring differential rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Differential_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measuring differential rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are many ways to measure and calculate differential rotation in stars to see if different latitudes have different angular velocities. The most obvious being tracking spots on the stellar surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       By doing
       <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">
        helioseismological
       </a>
       measurements of solar "p-modes" it is possible to deduce the differential rotation. The Sun has very many acoustic modes that oscillate in the interior simultaneously, and the inversion of their frequencies can yield the rotation of the solar interior. This varies with both depth and (especially) latitude.
      </p>
      <p>
       The broadened shapes of absorption lines in the optical spectrum depend on v
       <sub>
        rot
       </sub>
       sin(i), where
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       is the angle between the line of sight and the rotation axis, permitting the study of the rotational velocity’s line-of-sight component v
       <sub>
        rot
       </sub>
       . This is calculated from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_transforms" title="Fourier transforms">
        Fourier transforms
       </a>
       of the line shapes, using equation (2) below for v
       <sub>
        rot
       </sub>
       at the equator and poles. See also plot 2. Solar differential rotation is also seen in magnetograms, images showing the strength and location of solar magnetic fields.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_differential_rotation">
        Effects of differential rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Differential_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effects of differential rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Gradients in angular rotation caused by angular momentum redistribution within the convective layers of a star are expected to be a main driver for generating the large-scale magnetic field, through magneto-hydrodynamical (dynamo) mechanisms in the outer envelopes. The interface between these two regions is where angular rotation gradients are strongest and thus where dynamo processes are expected to be most efficient.
      </p>
      <p>
       The inner differential rotation is one part of the mixing processes in stars, mixing the materials and the heat/energy of the stars.
      </p>
      <p>
       Differential rotation affects stellar optical absorption-
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Line_spectra" title="Line spectra">
        line spectra
       </a>
       through
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_broadening" title="Doppler broadening">
        line broadening
       </a>
       caused by lines being differently
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler-shifted" title="Doppler-shifted">
        Doppler-shifted
       </a>
       across the stellar surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       Solar differential rotation causes shear at the so-called tachocline. This is a region where rotation changes from differential in the convection zone to nearly solid-body rotation in the interior, at 0.71 solar radii from the center.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_rotation_of_the_Sun">
        Differential rotation of the Sun
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Differential_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Differential rotation of the Sun">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tachocline.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="314" data-file-width="297" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Tachocline.gif/200px-Tachocline.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/Tachocline.gif 1.5x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tachocline.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Internal rotation in the Sun, showing differential rotation in the outer convective region and almost uniform rotation in the central radiative region.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_rotation" title="Solar rotation">
        Solar rotation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       On the Sun, the study of oscillations revealed that rotation is roughly constant within the whole radiative interior and variable with radius and latitude within the convective envelope. The Sun has an equatorial rotation speed of ~2 km/s; its differential rotation implies that the angular velocity decreases with increased latitude. The poles make one rotation every 34.3 days and the equator every 25.05 days, as measured relative to distant stars (sidereal rotation).
      </p>
      <p>
       The highly turbulent nature of solar convection and anisotropies induced by rotation complicate the dynamics of modeling. Molecular dissipation scales on the Sun are at least six orders of magnitude smaller than the depth of the convective envelope. A direct numerical simulation of solar convection would have to resolve this entire range of scales in each of the three dimensions. Consequently, all solar differential rotation models must involve some approximations regarding momentum and heat transport by turbulent motions that are not explicitly computed. Thus, modeling approaches can be classified as either mean-field models or large-eddy simulations according to the approximations.
      </p>
