      <p>
       A
       <b>
        crown
       </b>
       is a traditional
       <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">
        symbolic
       </a>
       form of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Headwear" title="Headwear">
        headwear
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Hat" title="Hat">
        hat
       </a>
       , worn by a
       <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">
        monarch
       </a>
       or by a
       <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">
        deity
       </a>
       , for whom the crown traditionally represents
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_power" title="Political power">
        power
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(political)" title="Legitimacy (political)">
        legitimacy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Victory" title="Victory">
        victory
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_triumph" title="Roman triumph">
        triumph
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honor" title="Honor">
        honor
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Glory_(religion)" title="Glory (religion)">
        glory
       </a>
       , as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Immortality" title="Crown of Immortality">
        immortality
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Righteousness" title="Righteousness">
        righteousness
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Resurrection" title="Resurrection">
        resurrection
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">
        art
       </a>
       , the crown may be shown being offered to those on Earth by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angels" title="Angels">
        angels
       </a>
       . Apart from the traditional form,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="&quot;traditional form&quot; is not defined in the previous two sentences -- are our readers supposed to interpret &quot;traditional&quot; as meaning &quot;in popular culture&quot;? Because that's INCREDIBLY problematic. (September 2017)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       crowns also may be in the form of a
       <a href="/wiki/Wreath_(attire)" title="Wreath (attire)">
        wreath
       </a>
       and be made of flowers,
       <a href="/wiki/Civic_Crown" title="Civic Crown">
        oak leaves
       </a>
       , or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_of_Thorns" title="Crown of Thorns">
        thorns
       </a>
       and be worn by others, representing what the
       <a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">
        coronation
       </a>
       part aims to symbolize with the specific crown. In religious art, a crown of
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Immortality" title="Crown of Immortality">
        stars
       </a>
       is used similarly to a
       <a href="/wiki/Halo_(religious_iconography)" title="Halo (religious iconography)">
        halo
       </a>
       . Crowns worn by rulers often contain
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_jewels" title="Crown jewels">
        jewels
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="As_an_emblem">
        As an emblem
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_(headgear)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: As an emblem">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A crown is often an
       <a href="/wiki/Emblem" title="Emblem">
        emblem
       </a>
       of the monarchy, a monarch's government, or items endorsed by it. The word itself is used, particularly in Commonwealth countries, as an abstract name for the monarchy itself, as distinct from the individual who inhabits it (see
       <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">
        The Crown
       </a>
       ). A specific type of crown (or
       <a href="/wiki/Coronet" title="Coronet">
        coronet
       </a>
       for lower ranks of peerage) is employed in
       <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">
        heraldry
       </a>
       under strict rules. Indeed, some monarchies never had a physical crown, just a heraldic representation, as in the constitutional kingdom of Belgium, where no coronation ever took place; the royal installation is done by a solemn oath in parliament, wearing a military uniform: the King is not acknowledged as by divine right, but assumes the only hereditary public office in the service of the law; so he in turn will swear in all members of "his" federal government.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Costume" title="Costume">
         Costume
        </a>
        headgear imitating a monarch's crown is also called a crown. Such costume crowns may be worn by
        <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">
         actors
        </a>
        portraying a monarch, people at costume parties, or ritual "monarchs" such as the king of a
        <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">
         Carnival
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Krewe" title="Krewe">
         krewe
        </a>
        , or the person who found the trinket in a
        <a href="/wiki/King_cake" title="King cake">
         king cake
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <b>
         nuptial crown
        </b>
        , sometimes called a
        <b>
         coronal
        </b>
        , worn by a bride, and sometimes the bridegroom, at her wedding is found in many European cultures since ancient times. In the present day, it is most common in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" title="Eastern Orthodox">
         Eastern Orthodox
        </a>
        cultures. The Eastern Orthodox marriage service has a section called the crowning, wherein the bride and groom are crowned as "king" and "queen" of their future household. In Greek weddings, the crowns are
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diadem_(personal_wear)" title="Diadem (personal wear)">
         diadems
        </a>
        usually made of white flowers, synthetic or real, often adorned with
        <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">
         silver
        </a>
        or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mother_of_pearl" title="Mother of pearl">
         mother of pearl
        </a>
        . They are placed on the heads of the newlyweds and are held together by a ribbon of white
        <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">
         silk
        </a>
        . They are then kept by the couple as a reminder of their special day. In Slavic weddings, the crowns are usually made of ornate metal, designed to resemble an imperial crown, and are held above the newlyweds heads by their best men. A parish usually owns one set to use for all the couples that are married there since these are much more expensive than Greek-style crowns. This was common in Catholic countries in the past.
       </li>
       <li>
        Crowns are also often used as symbols of religious status or veneration, by divinities (or their representation such as a statue) or by their representatives, e.g. the
        <a href="/wiki/Black_Crown" title="Black Crown">
         Black Crown
        </a>
        of the Karmapa Lama, sometimes used a model for wider use by devotees.
       </li>
       <li>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_thorns" title="Crown of thorns">
         Crown of thorns
        </a>
        according to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Bible" title="The Bible">
         the Bible
        </a>
        , was placed on the head of
        <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
         Jesus
        </a>
        before his
        <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion" title="Crucifixion">
         crucifixion
        </a>
        and has become a common symbol of martyrdom.
       </li>
       <li>
        According to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">
         Roman Catholic
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Sacred_tradition" title="Sacred tradition">
         tradition
        </a>
        , the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">
         Blessed Virgin Mary
        </a>
        was crowned as
        <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Heaven" title="Queen of Heaven">
         Queen of Heaven
        </a>
        after her
        <a href="/wiki/Assumption_of_Mary" title="Assumption of Mary">
         assumption
        </a>
        into
        <a href="/wiki/Heaven#In_Roman_Catholicism" title="Heaven">
         heaven
        </a>
        . She is often depicted wearing a crown, and statues of her in churches and
        <a href="/wiki/Shrines_to_the_Virgin_Mary" title="Shrines to the Virgin Mary">
         shrines
        </a>
        are
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_crowning" title="May crowning">
         ceremonially crowned
        </a>
        during May.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Immortality" title="Crown of Immortality">
         Crown of Immortality
        </a>
        is also common in historical symbolism.
       </li>
       <li>
        The heraldic symbol of
        <a href="/wiki/Three_Crowns" title="Three Crowns">
         Three Crowns
        </a>
        , referring to the three evangelical
        <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Magi" title="Biblical Magi">
         Magi (wise men)
        </a>
        , traditionally called kings, is believed thus to have become the symbol of the Swedish kingdom, but it also fits the historical (personal, dynastic)
        <a href="/wiki/Kalmar_Union" title="Kalmar Union">
         Kalmar Union
        </a>
        (1397–1520) between the three kingdoms of Denmark, Sweden, and Norway.
       </li>
       <li>
        Dancers of certain traditional
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thai_dance" title="Thai dance">
         Thai dances
        </a>
        often wear crowns (
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chada_and_mongkut" title="Chada and mongkut">
          mongkut
         </a>
        </i>
        ) on their head. These are inspired in the crowns worn by deities and
        <a href="/wiki/Great_Crown_of_Victory" title="Great Crown of Victory">
         by kings
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
         India
        </a>
        , crowns are known as
        <i>
         mukuta
        </i>
        (Sanskrit for "crest"), and have been used in India since ancient times and are described adorning Hindu gods or kings.
       </li>
       <li>
        In pre-Hispanic
        <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
         Philippines
        </a>
        , a crown-like
        <a href="/wiki/Diadem" title="Diadem">
         diadems
        </a>
        or
        <i>
         Putong
        </i>
        is wore by
        <a href="/wiki/Maharlika" title="Maharlika">
         elite individuals
        </a>
        and also
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Mythology" title="Philippine Mythology">
         deities
        </a>
        , include array of golden ornaments.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
        Terminology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_(headgear)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Three distinct categories of crowns exist in those
       <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">
        monarchies
       </a>
       that use crowns or state regalia.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Coronation_crown" title="Coronation crown">
          Coronation
         </a>
        </b>
        : worn by monarchs when being
        <a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">
         crowned
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/State_crown" title="State crown">
          State
         </a>
        </b>
        : worn by monarchs on other state occasions.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Consort_crown" title="Consort crown">
          Consort crowns
         </a>
        </b>
        : worn by
        <a href="/wiki/Queen_consort" title="Queen consort">
         queens consort
        </a>
        , signifying rank granted as a
        <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">
         constitutional
        </a>
        courtesy
        <a href="/wiki/Protocol_(diplomacy)" title="Protocol (diplomacy)">
         protocol
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Crowns or similar headgear, as worn by
       <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">
        nobility
       </a>
       and other high-ranking people below the ruler, is in English often called a
       <a href="/wiki/Coronet" title="Coronet">
        coronet
       </a>
       ; However, in many languages, this distinction is not made and the same word is used for both types of headgear (e.g., French
       <i>
        couronne
       </i>
       , German
       <i>
        Krone
       </i>
       , Dutch
       <i>
        kroon
       </i>
       ). In some of these languages the term "rank crown" (rangkroon, etc.) refers to the way these crowns may be ranked according to hierarchical status.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kroon_van_Nederland.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="392" data-file-width="553" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Kroon_van_Nederland.jpg/220px-Kroon_van_Nederland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Kroon_van_Nederland.jpg/330px-Kroon_van_Nederland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Kroon_van_Nederland.jpg/440px-Kroon_van_Nederland.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kroon_van_Nederland.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Crown of the Kingdom of the Netherlands
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">
        Classical antiquity
       </a>
       , the crown (
       <i>
        corona
       </i>
       ) that was sometimes awarded to people other than rulers, such as triumphal
       <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">
        military
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General" title="General">
        generals
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sportsperson" title="Sportsperson">
        athletes
       </a>
       , was actually a
       <a href="/wiki/Wreath_(attire)" title="Wreath (attire)">
        wreath
       </a>
       or chaplet, or ribbon-like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diadem_(personal_wear)" title="Diadem (personal wear)">
        diadem
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       The precursor to the crown was the browband called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diadem_(personal_wear)" title="Diadem (personal wear)">
        diadem
       </a>
       , which had been worn by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Achaemenid" title="Achaemenid">
        Achaemenid Persian emperors
       </a>
       , was adopted by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">
        Constantine I
       </a>
       , and was worn by all subsequent rulers of the later Roman Empire.
      </p>
      <p>
       Numerous crowns of various forms were used in antiquity, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Hedjet" title="Hedjet">
        Hedjet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Deshret" title="Deshret">
        Deshret
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Pschent" title="Pschent">
        Pschent
       </a>
       double crown, and
       <a href="/wiki/Khepresh" title="Khepresh">
        Khepresh
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">
        Pharaonic Egypt
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        corona radiata
       </i>
       , the "
       <a href="/wiki/Radiant_crown" title="Radiant crown">
        radiant crown
       </a>
       " known best on the
       <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">
        Statue of Liberty
       </a>
       , and perhaps worn by the
       <a href="/wiki/Helios" title="Helios">
        Helios
       </a>
       that was the
       <a href="/wiki/Colossus_of_Rhodes" title="Colossus of Rhodes">
        Colossus of Rhodes
       </a>
       , was worn by Roman emperors as part of the cult of
       <a href="/wiki/Sol_Invictus" title="Sol Invictus">
        Sol Invictus
       </a>
       prior to the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       's conversion to Christianity. It was referred to as "the chaplet studded with sunbeams” by
       <a href="/wiki/Lucian" title="Lucian">
        Lucian
       </a>
       , about 180 AD.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Perhaps the oldest extant Christian crown in Europe is the
       <a href="/wiki/Iron_Crown_of_Lombardy" title="Iron Crown of Lombardy">
        Iron Crown of Lombardy
       </a>
       , of Roman and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longobard" title="Longobard">
        Longobard
       </a>
       age, later again used to crown modern Kings of Napoleonic and Austrian Italy, and to represent united Italy after 1860.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the Christian tradition of European cultures, where ecclesiastical sanction authenticates monarchic power, when a new monarch assumes the throne in a coronation ceremony, the crown is placed on the new monarch's head by a religious official. Some, though not all early
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">
        Holy Roman Emperors
       </a>
       travelled to Rome at some point in their careers to be crowned by the pope.
       <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">
        Napoleon
       </a>
       , according to legend, surprised
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pius_VII" title="Pius VII">
        Pius VII
       </a>
       when he reached out and crowned himself, although in reality this order of ceremony had been pre-arranged (see
       <a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">
        coronation
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Today, only the
       <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">
        British Monarchy
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Tonga" title="Monarchy of Tonga">
        Tongan Monarchy
       </a>
       continue this tradition as the only remaining anointed and crowned monarchs, though many monarchies retain a crown as a national symbol in heraldry. The
       <a href="/wiki/French_Crown_Jewels" title="French Crown Jewels">
        French Crown Jewels
       </a>
       were sold in 1885 on the orders of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_French_Republic" title="Third French Republic">
        Third French Republic
       </a>
       , with only a token number, with their precious stones replaced by glass, held on to for historic reasons and displayed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">
        Louvre
       </a>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Crown_Jewels" title="Spanish Crown Jewels">
        Spanish Crown Jewels
       </a>
       were destroyed in a major fire in the 18th century while the so-called "
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_Crown_Jewels" title="Irish Crown Jewels">
        Irish Crown Jewels
       </a>
       " (actually merely the British Sovereign's insignia of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Most_Illustrious_Order_of_St_Patrick" title="Most Illustrious Order of St Patrick">
        Most Illustrious Order of St Patrick
       </a>
       ) were stolen from
       <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Castle" title="Dublin Castle">
        Dublin Castle
       </a>
       in 1907, just before the investiture of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernard_Edward_Barnaby_FitzPatrick,_2nd_Baron_Castletown" title="Bernard Edward Barnaby FitzPatrick, 2nd Baron Castletown">
        Bernard Edward Barnaby FitzPatrick, 2nd Baron Castletown
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Special headgear to designate rulers dates back to pre-history, and is found in many separate civilizations around the globe. Commonly, rare and precious materials are incorporated into the crown, but that is only essential for the notion of crown jewels.
       <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">
        Gold
       </a>
       and precious
       <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">
        jewels
       </a>
       are common in western and oriental crowns. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
        Native American
       </a>
       civilizations of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">
        Pre-Columbian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">
        New World
       </a>
       , rare
       <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">
        feathers
       </a>
       , such as that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Quetzal" title="Quetzal">
        quetzal
       </a>
       , often decorated crowns; so too in Polynesia (e.g. Hawaii).
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">
         Coronation
        </a>
        is often combined with other rituals, such as enthronement (the throne is as much a symbol of monarchy as the crown) and anointing (again religious sanction, the only defining act in the Biblical tradition of Israel).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In other cultures, no crown is used in the equivalent of coronation, but the head may still be otherwise symbolically adorned, as a royal
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tikka_(forehead_mark)" title="Tikka (forehead mark)">
        tikka
       </a>
       in the Hindu tradition of India.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Numismatics">
        Numismatics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_(headgear)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Numismatics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because one or more crowns, alone or as part of a more elaborate design, often appear on coins, several monetary denominations came to be known as 'a crown' (see
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_(British_coin)" title="Crown (British coin)">
        Crown (British coin)
       </a>
       ) or the equivalent word in the local language, such as
       <i>
        krone
       </i>
       . This persists in the case of the national currencies of the Scandinavian countries and the Czech Republic. The generic term "crown sized" is frequently used for any coin roughly the size of an American
       <a href="/wiki/Dollar_coin_(United_States)" title="Dollar coin (United States)">
        silver dollar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
