   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Thermal conductivity
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

     <div class="mw-parser-output">

      <p>
       Thermal
       <b>
        conductivity
       </b>
       (often denoted
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        λ
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        κ
       </i>
       ) is the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_materials_properties" title="List of materials properties">
        property
       </a>
       of a material to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_conduction" title="Heat conduction">
        conduct heat
       </a>
       . It is evaluated primarily in terms of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_conduction#Fourier's_law" title="Heat conduction">
        Fourier's Law
       </a>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_conduction" title="Heat conduction">
        heat conduction
       </a>
       . In general, thermal conductivity is a
       <a href="/wiki/Tensor" title="Tensor">
        tensor
       </a>
       property, expressing the
       <a href="/wiki/Anisotropy" title="Anisotropy">
        anisotropy
       </a>
       of the property.
      </p>
      <p>
       Heat transfer occurs at a lower rate in materials of low thermal conductivity than in materials of high thermal conductivity. Correspondingly, materials of high thermal conductivity are widely used in
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_sink" title="Heat sink">
        heat sink
       </a>
       applications and materials of low thermal conductivity are used as
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">
        thermal insulation
       </a>
       . The thermal conductivity of a material may depend on temperature. The reciprocal of thermal conductivity is called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_resistivity" title="Thermal resistivity">
        thermal resistivity
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Units_of_thermal_conductivity">
        Units of thermal conductivity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Units of thermal conductivity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">
        International System of Units
       </a>
       (SI), thermal conductivity is measured in watts per meter-kelvin (
       <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
        W
       </a>
       /(
       <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">
        m
       </a>
       ⋅
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        K
       </a>
       )). The
       <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">
        dimension
       </a>
       of thermal conductivity is M
       <sup>
        1
       </sup>
       L
       <sup>
        1
       </sup>
       T
       <sup>
        −3
       </sup>
       Θ
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       , expressed in terms of the dimensions mass (M), length (L), time (T), and temperature (Θ). In
       <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">
        Imperial units
       </a>
       , thermal conductivity is measured in
       <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">
        BTU
       </a>
       /(
       <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">
        hr
       </a>
       ⋅
       <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">
        ft
       </a>
       ⋅
       <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">
        °F
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other units which are closely related to the thermal conductivity are in common use in the construction and textile industries. The construction industry makes use of units such as the
       <a href="/wiki/R-value_(insulation)" title="R-value (insulation)">
        R-value
       </a>
       (resistance) and the
       <a href="/wiki/R-value_(insulation)#U-factor" title="R-value (insulation)">
        U-value
       </a>
       (transmittance). Although related to the thermal conductivity of a material used in an insulation product, R- and U-values are dependent on the thickness of the product.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [note 2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Likewise the textile industry has several units including the
       <a href="/wiki/Tog_(unit)" title="Tog (unit)">
        tog
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_Comfort" title="Thermal Comfort">
        clo
       </a>
       which express thermal resistance of a material in a way analogous to the R-values used in the construction industry.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">
        Measurement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measurement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity_measurement" title="Thermal conductivity measurement">
        Thermal conductivity measurement
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       There are a number of ways to measure thermal conductivity. Each of these is suitable for a limited range of materials, depending on the thermal properties and the medium temperature. There is a distinction between steady-state and transient techniques.
      </p>
      <p>
       In general, steady-state techniques are useful when the temperature of the material does not change with time. This makes the signal analysis straightforward (steady state implies constant signals). The disadvantage is that a well-engineered experimental setup is usually needed. The Divided Bar (various types) is the most common device used for consolidated rock solids.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_values">
        Experimental values
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Experimental values">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:552px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thermal_conductivity.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="234" data-file-width="512" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Thermal_conductivity.svg/550px-Thermal_conductivity.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Thermal_conductivity.svg/825px-Thermal_conductivity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Thermal_conductivity.svg/1100px-Thermal_conductivity.svg.png 2x" width="550"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thermal_conductivity.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Experimental values of thermal conductivity.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_thermal_conductivities" title="List of thermal conductivities">
        List of thermal conductivities
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Thermal conductivity is important in material science, research, electronics,
       <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">
        building insulation
       </a>
       and related fields, especially where high operating temperatures are achieved. Several materials are shown in the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_thermal_conductivities" title="List of thermal conductivities">
        list of thermal conductivities
       </a>
       . These should be considered approximate due to the uncertainties related to material definitions.
      </p>
      <p>
       High energy generation rates within
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_cooling" title="Computer cooling">
        electronics
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Turbine_blade#Cooling" title="Turbine blade">
        turbines
       </a>
       require the use of materials with high thermal conductivity such as
       <a href="/wiki/Copper_in_heat_exchangers" title="Copper in heat exchangers">
        copper
       </a>
       <i>
        (see:
        <a href="/wiki/Copper_in_heat_exchangers" title="Copper in heat exchangers">
         Copper in heat exchangers
        </a>
        )
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
        aluminium
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">
        silver
       </a>
       . On the other hand, materials with low thermal conductance, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">
        polystyrene
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">
        alumina
       </a>
       , are used in building
       <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">
        construction
       </a>
       or in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Furnaces" title="Furnaces">
        furnaces
       </a>
       in an effort to slow the flow of heat, i.e. for insulation purposes.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">
        Definitions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Definitions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The reciprocal of thermal conductivity is
       <i>
        thermal resistivity
       </i>
       , usually expressed in kelvin-meters per watt (K⋅m⋅W
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       ). For a given thickness of a material, that particular construction's
       <i>
        thermal resistance
       </i>
       and the reciprocal property,
       <i>
        thermal conductance
       </i>
       , can be calculated. Unfortunately, there are differing definitions for these terms.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thermal conductivity,
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       , often depends on temperature. Therefore, the definitions listed below make sense when the thermal conductivity is temperature independent. Otherwise an representative mean value has to be considered; for more, see the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_Conductivity#Equations" title="Thermal Conductivity">
        equations
       </a>
       section below.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conductance">
        Conductance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conductance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       For general scientific use,
       <i>
        thermal conductance
       </i>
       is the quantity of heat that passes in unit time through a plate of
       <i>
        particular area and thickness
       </i>
       when its opposite faces differ in temperature by one kelvin. For a plate of thermal conductivity
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       , area
       <i>
        A
       </i>
       and thickness
       <i>
        L
       </i>
       , the conductance calculated is
       <i>
        kA
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        L
       </i>
       , measured in W⋅K
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       (equivalent to: W/°C). ASTM C168-15, however, defines thermal conductance as "time rate of steady state heat flow through a unit area of a material or construction induced by a unit temperature difference between the body surfaces" and defines the units as W/(m
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ⋅K) (Btu/(h⋅ft
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ⋅°F))
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The thermal conductance of that particular construction is the inverse of the thermal resistance. Thermal conductivity and conductance are
       <a href="/wiki/Analogy" title="Analogy">
        analogous
       </a>
       to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">
        electrical conductivity
       </a>
       (A⋅m
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       ⋅V
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       ) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductance" title="Electrical conductance">
        electrical conductance
       </a>
       (A⋅V
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       There is also a measure known as
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer_coefficient" title="Heat transfer coefficient">
        heat transfer coefficient
       </a>
       : the quantity of heat that passes in unit time through a
       <i>
        unit area
       </i>
       of a plate of particular thickness when its opposite faces differ in temperature by one kelvin. The reciprocal is
       <i>
        thermal insulance
       </i>
       . In summary:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        thermal conductance =
        <i>
         kA
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         L
        </i>
        , measured in W⋅K
        <sup>
         −1
        </sup>
        or in ASTM C168-15 as W/(m
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        ⋅K)
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <ul>
         <li>
          thermal resistance =
          <i>
           L
          </i>
          /(
          <i>
           kA
          </i>
          ), measured in K⋅W
          <sup>
           −1
          </sup>
          (equivalent to: °C/W)
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        heat transfer coefficient =
        <i>
         k
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         L
        </i>
        , measured in W⋅K
        <sup>
         −1
        </sup>
        ⋅m
        <sup>
         −2
        </sup>
        <ul>
         <li>
          thermal insulance =
          <i>
           L
          </i>
          /
          <i>
           k
          </i>
          , measured in K⋅m
          <sup>
           2
          </sup>
          ⋅W
          <sup>
           −1
          </sup>
          .
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The heat transfer coefficient is also known as
       <i>
        thermal admittance
       </i>
       in the sense that the material may be seen as admitting heat to flow.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Resistance">
        Resistance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Resistance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_resistance" title="Thermal resistance">
        Thermal resistance
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Thermal resistance is the ability of a material to resist the flow of heat.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thermal resistance is the reciprocal of thermal conductance, i.e., lowering its value will raise the heat conduction and vice versa.
      </p>
      <p>
       When thermal resistances occur in
       <a href="/wiki/Series_and_parallel_circuits" title="Series and parallel circuits">
        series
       </a>
       , they are
       <i>
        additive
       </i>
       . Thus, when heat flows consecutively through two components each with a resistance of 3 °C/W, the total resistance is 3 °C/W + 3 °C/W = 6 °C/W.
      </p>
      <p>
       A common engineering design problem involves the selection of an appropriate sized
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_sink" title="Heat sink">
        heat sink
       </a>
       for a given heat source. Working in units of thermal resistance greatly simplifies the design calculation. The following formula can be used to estimate the performance:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
          <math alttext="{\displaystyle R_{hs}={\frac {\Delta T}{P_{th}}}-R_{s}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

              </msub>

              </mo>

                 </mi>

                 </mi>
                </mrow>
                <msub>
                 <mi>
                  P
                 </mi>
                 <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
                  <mi>
                   t
                  </mi>
                  <mi>

                  </mi>
                 </mrow>
                </msub>
               </mfrac>
              </mrow>
              <mo>

               <!-- − -->
              </mo>
              <msub>
               <mi>
                R
               </mi>
               <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">

            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>

        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       where:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         R
        </i>
        <sub>
         hs
        </sub>
        is the maximum thermal resistance of the heat sink to ambient, in °C/W (equivalent to K/W)
       </li>
       <li>
        Δ
        <i>
         T
        </i>
        is the required temperature difference (temperature drop), in °C
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         P
        </i>
        <sub>
         th
        </sub>
        is the thermal power (heat flow), in watts
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         R
        </i>
        <sub>
         s
        </sub>
        is the thermal resistance of the heat source, in °C/W
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For example, if a component produces 100 W of heat, and has a thermal resistance of 0.5 °C/W, what is the maximum thermal resistance of the heat sink? Suppose the maximum temperature is 125 °C, and the ambient temperature is 25 °C; then Δ
       <i>
        T
       </i>
       is 100 °C. The heat sink's thermal resistance to ambient must then be 0.5 °C/W or less (total resistance component and heat sink is then 1.0 °C/W).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transmittance">
        Transmittance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transmittance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A third term,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Thermal_transmittance" title="Thermal transmittance">
         thermal transmittance
        </a>
       </i>
       , sub way the thermal conductance of a structure along with heat transfer due to
       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
        convection
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       . It is measured in the same units as thermal conductance and is sometimes known as the
       <i>
        composite thermal conductance
       </i>
       . The term
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U-value" title="U-value">
         U-value
        </a>
       </i>
       is often used.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Admittance">
        Admittance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Admittance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The thermal admittance of a material, such as a building fabric, is a measure of the ability of a material to transfer heat in the presence of a temperature difference on opposite sides of the material. Thermal admittance is measured in the same units as a heat transfer coefficient, power (watts) per unit area (square meters) per temperature change (kelvins). Thermal admittance of a building fabric affects a building's thermal response to variation in outside temperature.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Co-efficient of thermal conductivity: The co-efficient of thermal conductivity of the material of a substance is numerically equal to the quantity of heat that conducts in one second normally through a slab of unit length and unit area, the difference of temperature between its end faces being one degree.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influencing_factors">
        Influencing factors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Influencing factors">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_temperature_on_thermal_conductivity">
        Effect of temperature on thermal conductivity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Effect of temperature on thermal conductivity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The effect of temperature on thermal conductivity is different for metals and nonmetals. In metals, heat conductivity is primarily due to free electrons. Following the
       <a href="/wiki/Wiedemann%E2%80%93Franz_law" title="Wiedemann–Franz law">
        Wiedemann–Franz law
       </a>
       , thermal conductivity of metals is approximately proportional to the absolute temperature (in
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        kelvins
       </a>
       ) times electrical conductivity. In pure metals the electrical conductivity decreases with increasing temperature and thus the product of the two, the thermal conductivity, stays approximately constant. However, as temperatures approach absolute zero, the thermal conductivity decreases sharply.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ozisik_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ozisik-6">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In alloys the change in electrical conductivity is usually smaller and thus thermal conductivity increases with temperature, often proportionally to temperature. Many pure metals have a peak thermal conductivity between 2 K and 10 K.
      </p>
      <p>
       On the other hand, heat conductivity in nonmetals is mainly due to lattice vibrations (
       <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">
        phonons
       </a>
       ). Except for high quality crystals at low temperatures, the phonon mean free path is not reduced significantly at higher temperatures. Thus, the thermal conductivity of nonmetals is approximately constant at high temperatures. At low temperatures well below the
       <a href="/wiki/Debye_model#Debye_temperature_table" title="Debye model">
        Debye temperature
       </a>
       , thermal conductivity decreases, as does the heat capacity, due to
       <a href="/wiki/Carrier_scattering" title="Carrier scattering">
        carrier scattering
       </a>
       from defects at very low temperatures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ozisik_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ozisik-6">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_phase">
        Chemical phase
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chemical phase">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When a material undergoes a phase change from solid to liquid or from liquid to gas the thermal conductivity may change. An example of this would be the change in thermal conductivity that occurs when ice (thermal conductivity of 2.18 W/(m⋅K) at 0 °C) melts to form liquid water (thermal conductivity of 0.56 W/(m⋅K) at 0 °C).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_anisotropy">
        Thermal anisotropy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Thermal anisotropy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some substances, such as non-
       <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">
        cubic
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">
        crystals
       </a>
       , can exhibit different thermal conductivities along different crystal axes, due to differences in
       <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">
        phonon
       </a>
       coupling along a given crystal axis.
       <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">
        Sapphire
       </a>
       is a notable example of variable thermal conductivity based on orientation and temperature, with 35 W/(m⋅K) along the C-axis and 32 W/(m⋅K) along the A-axis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">
        Wood
       </a>
       generally conducts better along the grain than across it. Other examples of materials where the thermal conductivity varies with direction are metals that have undergone
       <a href="/wiki/Cold-formed_steel" title="Cold-formed steel">
        heavy cold pressing
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lamination" title="Lamination">
        laminated
       </a>
       materials, cables, the materials used for the
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_thermal_protection_system" title="Space Shuttle thermal protection system">
        Space Shuttle thermal protection system
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Fiber-reinforced_composite" title="Fiber-reinforced composite">
        fiber-reinforced composite
       </a>
       structures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When anisotropy is present, the direction of heat flow may not be exactly the same as the direction of the thermal gradient.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_conductivity">
        Electrical conductivity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Electrical conductivity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In metals, thermal conductivity approximately tracks electrical conductivity according to the
       <a href="/wiki/Wiedemann%E2%80%93Franz_law" title="Wiedemann–Franz law">
        Wiedemann–Franz law
       </a>
       , as freely moving
       <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">
        valence electrons
       </a>
       transfer not only electric current but also heat energy. However, the general correlation between electrical and thermal conductance does not hold for other materials, due to the increased importance of
       <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">
        phonon
       </a>
       carriers for heat in non-metals. Highly electrically conductive
       <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">
        silver
       </a>
       is less thermally conductive than
       <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">
        diamond
       </a>
       , which is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_insulator" title="Electrical insulator">
        electrical insulator
       </a>
       , but due to its orderly array of atoms it is conductive of heat via phonons.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_field">
        Magnetic field
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Magnetic field">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The influence of magnetic fields on thermal conductivity is known as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Righi-Leduc_effect" title="Righi-Leduc effect">
        Righi-Leduc effect
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Convection">
        Convection
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Convection">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coloured_ceramic_thermal_barrier_coating_on_exhaust_component.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="293" data-file-width="450" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Coloured_ceramic_thermal_barrier_coating_on_exhaust_component.jpg/220px-Coloured_ceramic_thermal_barrier_coating_on_exhaust_component.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Coloured_ceramic_thermal_barrier_coating_on_exhaust_component.jpg/330px-Coloured_ceramic_thermal_barrier_coating_on_exhaust_component.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Coloured_ceramic_thermal_barrier_coating_on_exhaust_component.jpg/440px-Coloured_ceramic_thermal_barrier_coating_on_exhaust_component.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coloured_ceramic_thermal_barrier_coating_on_exhaust_component.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Exhaust system components with ceramic coatings having a low thermal conductivity reduce heating of nearby sensitive components
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Air and other gases are generally good insulators, in the absence of convection. Therefore, many insulating materials function simply by having a large number of gas-filled pockets which prevent large-scale convection. Examples of these include expanded and extruded
       <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">
        polystyrene
       </a>
       (popularly referred to as "styrofoam") and silica
       <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">
        aerogel
       </a>
       , as well as warm clothes. Natural, biological insulators such as fur and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feathers" title="Feathers">
        feathers
       </a>
       achieve similar effects by dramatically inhibiting convection of air or water near an animal's skin.
      </p>
      <p>
       Light gases, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       typically have high thermal conductivity. Dense gases such as
       <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">
        xenon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dichlorodifluoromethane" title="Dichlorodifluoromethane">
        dichlorodifluoromethane
       </a>
       have low thermal conductivity. An exception,

       </a>

       </a>

       </a>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_origins">
        Physical origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Physical origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       At the atomic level, there are no simple, correct expressions for thermal conductivity. Atomically, the thermal conductivity of a system is determined by how atoms composing the system interact. There are two different approaches for calculating the thermal conductivity of a system.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The first approach employs the
        <a href="/wiki/Green%E2%80%93Kubo_relations" title="Green–Kubo relations">
         Green–Kubo relations
        </a>
        . Although this employs analytic expressions, which, in principle, can be solved, calculating the thermal conductivity of a dense fluid or solid using this relation requires the use of molecular dynamics computer
        <a class="external text" href="https://archive.is/20050616001931/http://rsc.anu.edu.au/~evans/evansmorrissbook.htm" rel="nofollow">
         simulation
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The second approach is based on the relaxation time approach. Due to the anharmonicity within the crystal potential, the phonons in the system are known to scatter. There are three main mechanisms for scattering:
        <ul>
         <li>
          Boundary scattering, a phonon hitting the boundary of a system;
         </li>
         <li>
          Mass defect scattering, a phonon hitting an impurity within the system and scattering;
         </li>
         <li>
          Phonon-phonon scattering, a phonon breaking into two lower energy phonons or a phonon colliding with another phonon and merging into one higher-energy phonon.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lattice_waves">
        Lattice waves
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conductivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Lattice waves">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Heat transport in both amorphous and crystalline
       <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">
        dielectric
       </a>
       solids is by way of elastic vibrations of the lattice (
       <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">
        phonons
       </a>

These predictions were confirmed by the experiments of Chang and Jones on commercial glasses and glass ceramics, where the mean free paths were limited by "internal boundary scattering" to length scales of 10
       <sup>
        −2
       </sup>
       cm to 10
       <sup>
        −3
       </sup>
       cm.

        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">

        </a>

      </p>

       Regarding the dependence of wave velocity on wavelength or frequency (
       <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_dispersion" title="Acoustic dispersion">
        dispersion
       </a>
       ), low-frequency phonons of long wavelength will be limited in relaxation length by elastic
       <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">
        Rayleigh scattering
       </a>

For higher frequencies, the power of the frequency will decrease until at highest frequencies scattering is almost frequency independent. Similar arguments were subsequently generalized to many glass forming substances using

       <i>
        λ
       </i>
       <sub>
        L
       </sub>

      </p>

        <a href="#cite_note-Banduru-19">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>

       The heat capacity per particle
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       and the cross section
       <i>
        σ
       </i>
       both are temperature independent so the temperature dependence of
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       is determined by the
       <i>
        T
       </i>
       dependence of
       <i>
        v
       </i>
       . For a monatomic gas, with atomic mass
       <i>
        M
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        v
       </i>
       is given by
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

       This expression also shows why gases with a low mass (hydrogen, helium) have a high thermal conductivity.

       For
       <b>
        metals at low temperatures
       </b>
       the heat is carried mainly by the free electrons. In this case the mean velocity is the Fermi velocity which is temperature independent. The mean free path is determined by the impurities and the crystal imperfections which are temperature independent as well. So the only temperature-dependent quantity is the heat capacity
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       , which, in this case, is proportional to
       <i>
        T
       </i>
       . So
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

              </mo>

                k
               </mi>

                <mn>
                 0
                </mn>
               </mrow>
              </msub>
              <mspace width="thinmathspace">
              </mspace>
              <mi>
               T
              </mi>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mtext>
                (metal at low temperature)
               </mtext>
              </mrow>
             </mstyle>
            </mrow>
            <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
             {\displaystyle k=k_{0}\,T{\text{     (metal at low temperature)}}}
            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="{\displaystyle k=k_{0}\,T{\text{     (metal at low temperature)}}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22d7c4cb84808d55161a72e0443882c8e8be0e61" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:36.38ex; height:2.843ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       with
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       <sub>
        0
       </sub>
       a constant. For pure metals such as copper, silver, etc.
       <i>
        l
       </i>
       is large, so the thermal conductivity is high. At higher temperatures the mean free path is limited by the phonons, so the thermal conductivity tends to decrease with temperature. In alloys the density of the impurities is very high, so
       <i>
        l
       </i>
       and, consequently
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       , are small. Therefore, alloys, such as stainless steel, can be used for thermal insulation.
      </p>
