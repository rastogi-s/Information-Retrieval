      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Bulgarian Academy of Sciences
       </b>
       (abbreviated
       <b>
        BAS
       </b>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">
        Bulgarian
       </a>
       : Българска академия на науките,
       <i>
        Balgarska akademiya na naukite
       </i>
       , abbreviated
       <i>
        БАН
       </i>
       ) is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Academy" title="National Academy">
        National Academy
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">
        Bulgaria
       </a>
       , established in 1869. The Academy, located in
       <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">
        Sofia
       </a>
       , is autonomous and has a Society of Academicians, Correspondent Members and Foreign Members. It publishes and circulates different scientific works, encyclopedias, dictionaries and journals, and runs its own publishing house.
      </p>
      <p>
       Stefan Vodenicharov has been president of the BAS since 2012. Its budget in 2009 was 84 million leva, or 42.7 million euro. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Space_Agency" title="Bulgarian Space Agency">
        Bulgarian Space Agency
       </a>
       , part of the BAS, has a budget of 1 million euro.
      </p>

      <p>
       As Bulgaria was part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman Empire
       </a>
       , Bulgarian émigrés founded the
       <i>
        Bulgarian Literary Society
       </i>
       on 26 September 1869, in

        – President

      <p>
       The following year, the Literary Society began issuing the
       <i>
        Periodical Journal
       </i>
       , its official publication, and in 1871 elected its first honorary member -
       <a href="/wiki/Gavril_Krastevich" title="Gavril Krastevich">
        Gavril Krastevich
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1878, shortly after Bulgaria's liberation from Ottoman rule, the General Assembly voted to move the seat of the Society from Brăila to
       <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">
        Sofia
       </a>
       , and on 1 March 1893 the BLS moved into its own building, right next to where the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(Bulgaria)" title="National Assembly (Bulgaria)">
        Bulgarian Parliament
       </a>
       is seated. The BLS headquarters were completed in 1892. The building was designed by architect Hermann Mayer
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mihaylova_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-mihaylova-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was expanded during the 1920s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ivanov_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ivanov-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Bulgarian Literary Society adopted its present-day name in 1911, and Ivan Geshov became the Academy's first president. The BAS became a member of the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Union_of_Slavonic_Academies_and_Scientific_Communities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Union of Slavonic Academies and Scientific Communities (page does not exist)">
        Union of Slavonic Academies and Scientific Communities
       </a>
       in 1913, and was accepted as member of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Council_of_Scientific_Unions" title="International Council of Scientific Unions">
        International Council of Scientific Unions
       </a>
       in 1931.
      </p>

       </i>
       . Each consists of independent scientific institutes, laboratories and other sections.
