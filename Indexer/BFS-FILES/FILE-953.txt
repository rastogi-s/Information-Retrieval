      <p>
       A
       <b>
        spectral line
       </b>
       is a dark or bright line in an otherwise uniform and
       <a href="/wiki/Continuous_spectrum" title="Continuous spectrum">
        continuous spectrum
       </a>
       , resulting from emission or absorption of light in a narrow frequency range, compared with the nearby frequencies. Spectral lines are often used to identify
       <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
        atoms
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
        molecules
       </a>
       . These "fingerprints" can be compared to the previously collected "fingerprints" of atoms and molecules,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RothmanGordon2013_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RothmanGordon2013-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and are thus used to identify the atomic and molecular components of stars and planets which would otherwise be impossible.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_line_spectra">
        Types of line spectra
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of line spectra">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Simple_spectroscope.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3185" data-file-width="2000" height="478" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Simple_spectroscope.jpg/300px-Simple_spectroscope.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Simple_spectroscope.jpg/450px-Simple_spectroscope.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Simple_spectroscope.jpg/600px-Simple_spectroscope.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Simple_spectroscope.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Continuous spectrum of an
         <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">
          incandescent lamp
         </a>
         (mid) and discrete spectrum lines of a
         <a href="/wiki/Compact_fluorescent_lamp" title="Compact fluorescent lamp">
          fluorescent lamp
         </a>
         (bottom)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Spectral lines are the result of interaction between a
       <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">
        quantum system
       </a>
       (usually
       <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
        atoms
       </a>
       , but sometimes
       <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
        molecules
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">
        atomic nuclei
       </a>
       ) and a single
       <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
        photon
       </a>
       . When a photon has about the right amount of energy to allow a change in the energy state of the system (in the case of an atom this is usually an
       <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">
        electron
       </a>
       changing
       <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">
        orbitals
       </a>
       ), the photon is absorbed. Then it will be spontaneously re-emitted, either in the same frequency as the original or in a cascade, where the sum of the energies of the photons emitted will be equal to the energy of the one absorbed (assuming the system returns to its original state).
      </p>
      <p>
       A spectral line may be observed either as an
       <b>
        emission line
       </b>
       or an
       <b>
        absorption line
       </b>
       . Which type of line is observed depends on the type of material and its temperature relative to another emission source. An absorption line is produced when photons from a hot, broad spectrum source pass through a cold material. The intensity of light, over a narrow frequency range, is reduced due to absorption by the material and re-emission in random directions. By contrast, a bright, emission line is produced when photons from a hot material are detected in the presence of a broad spectrum from a cold source. The intensity of light, over a narrow frequency range, is increased due to emission by the material.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spectral lines are highly atom-specific, and can be used to identify the chemical composition of any medium capable of letting light pass through it (typically
       <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
        gas
       </a>
       is used). Several elements were discovered by spectroscopic means, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">
        thallium
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">
        cerium
       </a>
       . Spectral lines also depend on the physical conditions of the gas, so they are widely used to determine the chemical composition of
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       and other celestial bodies that cannot be analyzed by other means, as well as their physical conditions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mechanisms other than atom-photon interaction can produce spectral lines. Depending on the exact physical interaction (with molecules, single particles, etc.), the frequency of the involved photons will vary widely, and lines can be observed across the
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        electromagnetic spectrum
       </a>
       , from
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">
        radio waves
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">
        gamma rays
       </a>
       .
      </p>

        Nomenclature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

      <p>
       Strong spectral lines in the
       <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">
        visible
       </a>
       part of the spectrum often have a unique
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_line" title="Fraunhofer line">
        Fraunhofer line
       </a>
       designation, such as
       <b>
        K
       </b>
       for a line at 393.366 nm emerging from singly ionized
       <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">
        Ca
       </a>
       <sup>
        +
       </sup>
       , though some of the Fraunhofer "lines" are blends of multiple lines from several different
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">
        species
       </a>
       . In other cases the lines are designated according to the level of
       <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
        ionization
       </a>
       by adding a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_numeral" title="Roman numeral">
        Roman numeral
       </a>
       to the designation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
        chemical element
       </a>
       , so that Ca
       <sup>
        +
       </sup>
       also has the designation
       <b>
        Ca II
       </b>
       . Neutral atoms are denoted with the roman number I, singly ionized atoms with II, and so on, so that for example Fe IX (IX, roman 9) represents eight times ionized
       <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
        iron
       </a>
       . More detailed designations usually include the line
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelength
       </a>
       and may include a
       <a href="/wiki/Multiplet" title="Multiplet">
        multiplet
       </a>
       number (for atomic lines) or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_spectra_or_band_spectra" title="Molecular spectra or band spectra">
        band designation
       </a>
       (for molecular lines). Many spectral lines of atomic
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       also have designations within their respective
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_spectral_series" title="Hydrogen spectral series">
        series
       </a>
       , such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Lyman_series" title="Lyman series">
        Lyman series
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Balmer_series" title="Balmer series">
        Balmer series
       </a>
       . Originally all spectral lines were classified into series of Principle series,
       <a href="/wiki/Sharp_series" title="Sharp series">
        Sharp series
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_series" title="Diffuse series">
        Diffuse series
       </a>
       . These series exist across atoms of all elements and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rydberg-Ritz_combination_principle" title="Rydberg-Ritz combination principle">
        Rydberg-Ritz combination principle
       </a>
       is a formula that predicts the pattern of lines to be found in all atoms of the elements. For this reason, the NIST spectral line database contains a column for Ritz calculated lines. These series were later associated with suborbitals.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Line_broadening_and_shift">
        Line broadening and shift
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Line broadening and shift">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A spectral line extends over a range of frequencies, not a single frequency (i.e., it has a nonzero linewidth). In addition, its center may be shifted from its nominal central wavelength. There are several reasons for this broadening and shift. These reasons may be divided into two general categories – broadening due to local conditions and broadening due to extended conditions. Broadening due to local conditions is due to effects which hold in a small region around the emitting element, usually small enough to assure
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Local thermodynamic equilibrium">
        local thermodynamic equilibrium
       </a>
       . Broadening due to extended conditions may result from changes to the spectral distribution of the radiation as it traverses its path to the observer. It also may result from the combining of radiation from a number of regions which are far from each other.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Broadening_due_to_local_effects">
        Broadening due to local effects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Broadening due to local effects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_broadening">
        Natural broadening
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural broadening">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">
        uncertainty principle
       </a>
       relates the lifetime of an excited state (due to
       <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_emission" title="Spontaneous emission">
        spontaneous radiative decay
       </a>
       or the
       <a href="/wiki/Auger_effect" title="Auger effect">
        Auger process
       </a>
       ) with the uncertainty of its energy. A short lifetime will have a large energy uncertainty and a broad emission. This broadening effect results in an unshifted
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorentzian_function" title="Lorentzian function">
        Lorentzian profile
       </a>
       . The natural broadening can be experimentally altered only to the extent that decay rates can be artificially suppressed or enhanced.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_Doppler_broadening">
        Thermal Doppler broadening
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Thermal Doppler broadening">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_broadening" title="Doppler broadening">
        Doppler broadening
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The atoms in a gas which are emitting radiation will have a distribution of velocities. Each photon emitted will be "red"- or "blue"-shifted by the
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">
        Doppler effect
       </a>
       depending on the velocity of the atom relative to the observer. The higher the temperature of the gas, the wider the distribution of velocities in the gas. Since the spectral line is a combination of all of the emitted radiation, the higher the temperature of the gas, the broader the spectral line emitted from that gas. This broadening effect is described by a
       <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_function" title="Gaussian function">
        Gaussian profile
       </a>
       and there is no associated shift.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pressure_broadening">
        Pressure broadening
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pressure broadening">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The presence of nearby particles will affect the radiation emitted by an individual particle. There are two limiting cases by which this occurs:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Impact pressure broadening
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         collisional broadening
        </i>
        : The collision of other particles with the emitting particle interrupts the emission process, and by shortening the characteristic time for the process, increases the uncertainty in the energy emitted (as occurs in natural broadening).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The duration of the collision is much shorter than the lifetime of the emission process. This effect depends on both the
        <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
         density
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
         temperature
        </a>
        of the gas. The broadening effect is described by a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorentzian_function" title="Lorentzian function">
         Lorentzian profile
        </a>
        and there may be an associated shift.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Quasistatic pressure broadening
        </i>
        : The presence of other particles shifts the energy levels in the emitting particle,
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
           <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2015)">
            clarification needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        thereby altering the frequency of the emitted radiation. The duration of the influence is much longer than the lifetime of the emission process. This effect depends on the
        <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
         density
        </a>
        of the gas, but is rather insensitive to
        <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
         temperature
        </a>
        . The form of the line profile is determined by the functional form of the perturbing force with respect to distance from the perturbing particle. There may also be a shift in the line center. The general expression for the lineshape resulting from quasistatic pressure broadening is a 4-parameter generalization of the Gaussian distribution known as a
        <a href="/wiki/Stable_distribution" title="Stable distribution">
         stable distribution
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Inhomogeneous_broadening">
        Inhomogeneous broadening
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Inhomogeneous broadening">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inhomogeneous_broadening" title="Inhomogeneous broadening">
         Inhomogeneous broadening
        </a>
       </i>
       is a general term for broadening because some emitting particles are in a different local environment from others, and therefore emit at a different frequency. This term is used especially for solids, where surfaces, grain boundaries, and stoichiometry variations can create a variety of local environments for a given atom to occupy. In liquids, the effects of inhomogeneous broadening is sometimes reduced by a process called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Motional_narrowing" title="Motional narrowing">
         motional narrowing
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Broadening_due_to_non-local_effects">
        Broadening due to non-local effects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Broadening due to non-local effects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Certain types of broadening are the result of conditions over a large region of space rather than simply upon conditions that are local to the emitting particle.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Opacity_broadening">
        Opacity broadening
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Opacity broadening">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Electromagnetic radiation emitted at a particular point in space can be reabsorbed as it travels through space. This absorption depends on wavelength. The line is broadened because the photons at the line center have a greater reabsorption probability than the photons at the line wings. Indeed, the reabsorption near the line center may be so great as to cause a
       <b>
        self reversal
       </b>
       in which the intensity at the center of the line is less than in the wings. This process is also sometimes called
       <b>
        self-absorption
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Macroscopic_Doppler_broadening">
        Macroscopic Doppler broadening
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Macroscopic Doppler broadening">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Radiation emitted by a moving source is subject to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">
        Doppler shift
       </a>
       due to a finite line-of-sight velocity projection. If different parts of the emitting body have different velocities (along the line of sight), the resulting line will be broadened, with the line width proportional to the width of the velocity distribution. For example, radiation emitted from a distant rotating body, such as a
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       , will be broadened due to the line-of-sight variations in velocity on opposite sides of the star. The greater the rate of rotation, the broader the line. Another example is an imploding
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       shell in a
       <a href="/wiki/Z-pinch" title="Z-pinch">
        Z-pinch
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radiative_broadening">
        Radiative broadening
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Radiative broadening">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Radiative broadening of the spectral absorption profile occurs because the on-resonance absorption in the center of the profile is saturated at much lower intensities than the off-resonant wings.Therefore, as intensity rises, absorption in the wings rises faster than absorption in the center, leading to a broadening of the profile. Radiative broadening occurs even at very low light intensities.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Combined_effects">
        Combined effects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Combined effects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Each of these mechanisms can act in isolation or in combination with others. Assuming each effect is independent, the observed line profile is a convolution of the line profiles of each mechanism. For example, a combination of the thermal Doppler broadening and the impact pressure broadening yields a
       <a href="/wiki/Voigt_profile" title="Voigt profile">
        Voigt profile
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       However, the different line broadening mechanisms are not always independent. For example, the collisional effects and the motional Doppler shifts can act in a coherent manner, resulting under some conditions even in a collisional
       <i>
        narrowing
       </i>
       , known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Dicke_effect" title="Dicke effect">
        Dicke effect
       </a>
       .
      </p>
