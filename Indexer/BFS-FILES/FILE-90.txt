      <p>
       <b>
        Lydia
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">
        Assyrian
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        Luddu
       </i>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Λυδία
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        Lydía
       </i>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">
         Lidya
        </span>
       </i>
       ) was an
       <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">
        Iron Age
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">
        kingdom
       </a>
       of western
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">
        Asia Minor
       </a>
       located generally east of ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">
        Ionia
       </a>
       in the modern western
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkish
       </a>
       provinces of
       <a href="/wiki/U%C5%9Fak_Province" title="Uşak Province">
        Uşak
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Manisa_Province" title="Manisa Province">
        Manisa
       </a>
       and inland
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Izmir_Province" title="Izmir Province">
        İzmir
       </a>
       . Its population spoke an
       <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_languages" title="Anatolian languages">
        Anatolian language
       </a>
       known as
       <a href="/wiki/Lydian_language" title="Lydian language">
        Lydian
       </a>
       . Its capital was
       <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">
        Sardis
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhodes_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-rhodes-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Kingdom of Lydia existed from about 1200
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_the_Common_Era" title="Before the Common Era">
        BCE
       </a>
       to 546 BCE. At its greatest extent during the 7th century BCE, it covered all of western
       <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">
        Anatolia
       </a>
       . In 546 BCE, it became a province of the
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
        Achaemenid Persian Empire
       </a>
       , known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Lydia_(satrapy)" title="Lydia (satrapy)">
        satrapy of Lydia
       </a>
       or
       <i>
        Sparda
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Persian" title="Old Persian">
        Old Persian
       </a>
       . In 133 BCE, it became part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">
        Roman
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Asia_(Roman_province)" title="Asia (Roman province)">
        province of Asia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">
        Coins
       </a>
       are said to have been invented in Lydia around the 7th century BCE.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Defining_Lydia">
        Defining Lydia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Defining Lydia">
         edit

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endonym" title="Endonym">
        endonym
       </a>
       <i>
        Śfard
       </i>
       (the name the Lydians called themselves) survives in bilingual and trilingual stone-carved notices of the
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
        Achaemenid Empire
       </a>
       : the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satrapy" title="Satrapy">
        satrapy
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        Sparda
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Persian" title="Old Persian">
        Old Persian
       </a>
       ),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">
        Aramaic
       </a>
       <i>
        Saparda
       </i>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">
        Babylonian
       </a>
       <i>
        Sapardu
       </i>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elamitic" title="Elamitic">
        Elamitic
       </a>
       <i>
        Išbarda
       </i>
       , Hebrew
       <i>
        סְפָרַד
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These in the Greek tradition are associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">
        Sardis
       </a>
       , the capital city of King
       <a href="/wiki/Gyges_of_Lydia" title="Gyges of Lydia">
        Gyges
       </a>
       , constructed during the 7th century BCE.
      </p>
      <p>
       The region of the Lydian kingdom was during the 15th-14th centuries part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Arzawa" title="Arzawa">
        Arzawa
       </a>
       kingdom. However, the Lydian language is not usually categorized as part of the Luwic subgroup, as are the other nearby Anatolian languages
       <a href="/wiki/Luwian_language" title="Luwian language">
        Luwian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Carian_language" title="Carian language">
        Carian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Lycian_language" title="Lycian language">
        Lycian
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An Etruscan/Lydian association has long been a subject of conjecture. The Greek historian
       <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
        Herodotus
       </a>
       stated that the Etruscans came from Lydia, repeated in
       <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">
        Virgil
       </a>
       's epic poem the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">
         Aeneid
        </a>
       </i>
       , and Etruscan-like language was found on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lemnos_stele" title="Lemnos stele">
        Lemnos stele
       </a>
       from the Aegean Sea island of Lemnos. However, the decipherment of
       <a href="/wiki/Lydian_language" title="Lydian language">
        Lydian
       </a>
       and its classification as an Anatolian language mean that Etruscan and Lydian were not even part of the same language family. Nevertheless, a recent genetic study of likely Etruscan descendants in
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">
        Tuscany
       </a>
       found strong similarities with individuals in western Anatolia, indicating that Etruscans may have lived in or near the region at one time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
        Geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The boundaries of historical Lydia varied across the centuries. It was bounded first by
       <a href="/wiki/Mysia" title="Mysia">
        Mysia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Caria" title="Caria">
        Caria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">
        Phrygia
       </a>
       and coastal
       <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">
        Ionia
       </a>
       . Later, the military power of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alyattes_II" title="Alyattes II">
        Alyattes II
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">
        Croesus
       </a>
       expanded Lydia, which, with its capital at
       <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">
        Sardis
       </a>
       , controlled all Asia Minor west of the River Halys, except
       <a href="/wiki/Lycia" title="Lycia">
        Lycia
       </a>
       .
       <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">
        Lydia never again shrank back into its original dimensions.
       </span>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       After the Persian conquest the River
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maeander" title="Maeander">
        Maeander
       </a>
       was regarded as its southern boundary, and during imperial Roman times Lydia comprised the country between Mysia and Caria on the one side and Phrygia and the
       <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">
        Aegean Sea
       </a>
       on the other.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language">
        Language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Lydian_language" title="Lydian language">
        Lydian language
       </a>
       was an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European_language" title="Indo-European language">
        Indo-European language
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_languages" title="Anatolian languages">
        Anatolian language family
       </a>
       , related to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luwian" title="Luwian">
        Luwian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hittite_language" title="Hittite language">
        Hittite
       </a>
       . Due to its fragmentary attestation, the meanings of many words are unknown but much of the grammar has been determined. Similar to other Anatolian languages, it featured extensive use of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefix_(linguistics)" title="Prefix (linguistics)">
        prefixes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">
        grammatical particles
       </a>
       to chain clauses together.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lydian had also undergone extensive
       <a href="/wiki/Syncope_(phonology)" title="Syncope (phonology)">
        syncope
       </a>
       , leading to numerous consonant clusters atypical of Indo-European languages. Lydian finally became
       <a href="/wiki/Extinct_language" title="Extinct language">
        extinct
       </a>
       during the 1st century BCE.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history:_Maeonia_and_Lydia">
        Early history: Maeonia and Lydia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early history: Maeonia and Lydia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Lydia developed after the decline of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hittite_Empire" title="Hittite Empire">
        Hittite Empire
       </a>
       in the 12th century BCE. In Hittite times, the name for the region had been
       <a href="/wiki/Arzawa" title="Arzawa">
        Arzawa
       </a>
       . According to Greek source, the original name of the Lydian kingdom was
       <i>
        Maionia
       </i>
       (Μαιονία), or
       <i>
        Maeonia
       </i>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">
        Homer
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">
         Iliad
        </a>
       </i>
       ii. 865; v. 43, xi. 431) refers to the inhabitants of Lydia as
       <i>
        Maiones
       </i>
       (Μαίονες).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Homer describes their capital not as Sardis but as
       <i>
        Hyde
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        Iliad
       </i>
       xx. 385); Hyde may have been the name of the district in which Sardis was located.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later,
       <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
        Herodotus
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Histories_(Herodotus)" title="Histories (Herodotus)">
         Histories
        </a>
       </i>
       i. 7) adds that the "Meiones" were renamed Lydians after their king
       <a href="/wiki/Lydus" title="Lydus">
        Lydus
       </a>
       (Λυδός), son of
       <a href="/wiki/Atys_(King_of_the_Maeonians)" title="Atys (King of the Maeonians)">
        Atys
       </a>
       , during the mythical epoch that preceded the Heracleid dynasty. This
       <a href="/wiki/Etiology" title="Etiology">
        etiological
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">
        eponym
       </a>
       served to account for the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ethnic name
       <i>
        Lydoi
       </i>
       (Λυδοί). The
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       term for Lydians,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ludim" title="Ludim">
         Lûḏîm
        </a>
       </i>
       (לודים), as found in the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jeremiah" title="Book of Jeremiah">
        Book of Jeremiah
       </a>
       (46.9), has been similarly considered, beginning with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavius_Josephus" title="Flavius Josephus">
        Flavius Josephus
       </a>
       , to be derived from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lud_son_of_Shem" title="Lud son of Shem">
        Lud son of Shem
       </a>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       however,
       <a href="/wiki/Hippolytus_of_Rome" title="Hippolytus of Rome">
        Hippolytus of Rome
       </a>
       (234
       <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">
        CE
       </a>
       ) offered an alternative opinion that the Lydians were descended from Ludim, son of
       <a href="/wiki/Mizraim" title="Mizraim">
        Mizraim
       </a>
       . During Biblical times, the Lydian warriors were famous archers. Some Maeones still existed during historical times in the upland interior along the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermus_(river)" title="Hermus (river)">
        River Hermus
       </a>
       , where a town named Maeonia existed, according to
       <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">
        Pliny the Elder
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Natural History
       </i>
       book v:30) and
       <a href="/wiki/Hierocles_(author_of_Synecdemus)" title="Hierocles (author of Synecdemus)">
        Hierocles
       </a>
       (author of Synecdemus).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Greek_mythology">
        In Greek mythology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In Greek mythology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Lydian mythology is virtually unknown, and their literature and rituals lost due to the absence of any monuments or archaeological finds with extensive inscriptions; therefore, myths involving Lydia are mainly from
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For the Greeks,
       <a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">
        Tantalus
       </a>
       was a primordial ruler of mythic Lydia, and
       <a href="/wiki/Niobe" title="Niobe">
        Niobe
       </a>
       his proud daughter; her husband
       <a href="/wiki/Amphion_and_Zethus" title="Amphion and Zethus">
        Amphion
       </a>
       associated Lydia with
       <a href="/wiki/Thebes,_Greece" title="Thebes, Greece">
        Thebes
       </a>
       in Greece, and through
       <a href="/wiki/Pelops" title="Pelops">
        Pelops
       </a>
       the line of Tantalus was part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Founding_myth" title="Founding myth">
        founding myths
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Mycenae" title="Mycenae">
        Mycenae
       </a>
       's second dynasty. (In reference to the myth of
       <a href="/wiki/Bellerophon" title="Bellerophon">
        Bellerophon
       </a>
       , Karl Kerenyi remarked, in
       <i>
        The Heroes of The Greeks
       </i>
       1959, p. 83. "As
       <a href="/wiki/Lycia" title="Lycia">
        Lykia
       </a>
       was thus connected with
       <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">
        Crete
       </a>
       , and as the person of
       <a href="/wiki/Pelops" title="Pelops">
        Pelops
       </a>
       , the hero of Olympia, connected Lydia with the Peloponnesos, so Bellerophontes connected another Asian country, or rather two, Lykia and
       <a href="/wiki/Caria" title="Caria">
        Karia
       </a>
       , with the kingdom of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argolid" title="Argolid">
        Argos
       </a>
       ".)
      </p>
      <p>
       In Greek myth, Lydia had also adopted the double-axe symbol, that also appears in the Mycenaean civilization, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Labrys" title="Labrys">
         labrys
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Omphale" title="Omphale">
        Omphale
       </a>
       , daughter of the river Iardanos, was a ruler of Lydia, whom
       <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">
        Heracles
       </a>
       was required to serve for a time. His adventures in Lydia are the adventures of a Greek hero in a peripheral and foreign land: during his stay, Heracles enslaved the Itones; killed Syleus, who forced passers-by to hoe his vineyard; slew the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpent_(mythology)" title="Serpent (mythology)">
        serpent
       </a>
       of the river Sangarios (which appears in the heavens as the constellation
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ophiucus" title="Ophiucus">
        Ophiucus
       </a>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and captured the simian tricksters, the
       <a href="/wiki/Cercopes" title="Cercopes">
        Cercopes
       </a>
       . Accounts tell of at least one son born to Omphale and Heracles:
       <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">
        Diodorus Siculus
       </a>
       (4.31.8) and
       <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">
        Ovid
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Heroides
       </i>
       9.54) mention a son Lamos, while pseudo-Apollodorus (
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bibliotheke" title="Bibliotheke">
         Bibliotheke
        </a>
       </i>
       2.7.8) gives the name Agelaus, and
       <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">
        Pausanias
       </a>
       (2.21.3) names Tyrsenus son of Heracles by "the Lydian woman."
      </p>
      <p>
       All three heroic ancestors indicate a Lydian dynasty claiming Heracles as their ancestor. Herodotus (1.7) refers to a Heraclid dynasty of kings who ruled Lydia, yet were perhaps not descended from Omphale. He also mentions (1.94) the recurring legend that the
       <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_civilization" title="Etruscan civilization">
        Etruscan civilization
       </a>
       was founded by colonists from Lydia led by
       <a href="/wiki/Tyrrhenus" title="Tyrrhenus">
        Tyrrhenus
       </a>
       , brother of Lydus. However,
       <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_of_Halicarnassus" title="Dionysius of Halicarnassus">
        Dionysius of Halicarnassus
       </a>
       was skeptical of this story, indicating that the
       <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_language" title="Etruscan language">
        Etruscan language
       </a>
       and customs were known to be totally dissimilar to those of the Lydians. Later chronographers also ignored Herodotus's statement that
       <a href="/wiki/Agron_of_Lydia" title="Agron of Lydia">
        Agron
       </a>
       was the first to be a king, and included
       <a href="/wiki/Alcaeus_(mythology)" title="Alcaeus (mythology)">
        Alcaeus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Belus_(Assyrian)" title="Belus (Assyrian)">
        Belus
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ninus" title="Ninus">
        Ninus
       </a>
       in their list of kings of Lydia. Strabo (5.2.2) makes Atys, father of Lydus and Tyrrhenus, to be a descendant of Heracles and Omphale. All other accounts name Atys, Lydus, and Tyrrhenus as being among the pre-Heraclid kings of Lydia. The gold deposits in the river
       <a href="/wiki/Pactolus" title="Pactolus">
        Pactolus
       </a>
       that were the source of the proverbial wealth of
       <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">
        Croesus
       </a>
       (Lydia's last king) were said to have been left there when the legendary king
       <a href="/wiki/Midas" title="Midas">
        Midas
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">
        Phrygia
       </a>
       washed away the "Midas touch" in its waters. In Euripides' tragedy
       <i>
        The Bacchae
       </i>
       , Dionysus, while he is maintaining his human disguise, declares his country to be Lydia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="First_coinage">
        First coinage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: First coinage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BMC_06.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="202" data-file-width="500" height="89" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/BMC_06.jpg/220px-BMC_06.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/BMC_06.jpg/330px-BMC_06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/BMC_06.jpg/440px-BMC_06.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BMC_06.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Early 6th century BCE Lydian electrum coin (one-third stater denomination)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       According to
       <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
        Herodotus
       </a>
       , the Lydians were the first people to use gold and silver
       <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">
        coins
       </a>
       and the first to establish retail shops in permanent locations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is not known, however, whether Herodotus meant that the Lydians were the first to use coins of pure gold and pure silver or the first precious metal coins in general. Despite this ambiguity, this statement of Herodotus is one of the pieces of evidence most often cited on behalf of the argument that Lydians invented coinage, at least in the West, even though the first coins were neither gold nor silver but an alloy of the two called
       <a href="/wiki/Electrum" title="Electrum">
        electrum
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The dating of these first stamped coins is one of the most frequently debated topics of ancient numismatics,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with dates ranging from 700 BCE to 550 BCE, but the most common opinion is that they were minted at or near the beginning of the reign of King Alyattes (sometimes referred to incorrectly as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alyattes_II" title="Alyattes II">
        Alyattes II
       </a>
       ), who ruled Lydia c. 610-550 BCE.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first coins were made of
       <a href="/wiki/Electrum" title="Electrum">
        electrum
       </a>
       , an
       <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">
        alloy
       </a>
       of gold and silver that occurs naturally but that was further debased by the Lydians with added silver and copper.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The largest of these coins are commonly referred to as a 1/3
       <a href="/wiki/Stater" title="Stater">
        stater
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        trite
       </i>
       ) denomination, weighing around 4.7 grams, though no full staters of this type have ever been found, and the 1/3 stater probably should be referred to more correctly as a stater, after a type of a transversely held scale, the weights used in such a scale (from ancient Greek ίστημι=to stand), which also means "standard."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These coins were stamped with a lion's head adorned with what is likely a sunburst, which was the king's symbol.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The most prolific mint for early electrum coins was Sardis which produced large quantities of the lion head thirds, sixths and twelfths along with lion paw fractions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To complement the largest denomination, fractions were made, including a
       <i>
        hekte
       </i>
       (sixth),
       <i>
        hemihekte
       </i>
       (twelfth), and so forth down to a 96th, with the 1/96 stater weighing only about 0.15 grams. There is disagreement, however, over whether the fractions below the twelfth are actually Lydian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It took some time before ancient coins were used for commerce and trade. Even the smallest-denomination electrum coins, perhaps worth about a day's subsistence, would have been too valuable for buying a loaf of bread.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Horse_head,_rough_incuse.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="339" data-file-width="600" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Horse_head%2C_rough_incuse.jpg/220px-Horse_head%2C_rough_incuse.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Horse_head%2C_rough_incuse.jpg/330px-Horse_head%2C_rough_incuse.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Horse_head%2C_rough_incuse.jpg/440px-Horse_head%2C_rough_incuse.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Horse_head,_rough_incuse.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Ionia, Uncertain city 600-550 BCE, Hemiobol. Horse head, rough incuse
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The first coins to be used for retailing on a large-scale basis were likely small silver fractions, Hemiobol,
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_coinage" title="Ancient Greek coinage">
        Ancient Greek coinage
       </a>
       minted by the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionians" title="Ionians">
        Ionian Greeks
       </a>
       in the late sixth century BCE.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Alyattes' son was Croesus, who became associated with great wealth. Sardis was renowned as a beautiful city. Around 550 BCE, near the beginning of his reign, Croesus paid for the construction of the
       <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Artemis" title="Temple of Artemis">
        temple of Artemis
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">
        Ephesus
       </a>
       , which became one of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seven_Wonders_of_the_ancient_world" title="Seven Wonders of the ancient world">
        Seven Wonders of the ancient world
       </a>
       . Croesus was defeated in battle by
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">
        Cyrus II
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
        of Persia
       </a>
       in 546 BCE, with the Lydian kingdom losing its autonomy and becoming a Persian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satrapy" title="Satrapy">
        satrapy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Autochthonous_dynasties">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>

      </h3>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Lydia_ancient_times-en.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>

        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Lydia was ruled by three dynasties:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Atyads (1300 BCE or earlier) – Heraclids (Tylonids) (to 687 BCE)
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <p>
       According to
       <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
        Herodotus
       </a>
       E, the Heraclids ruled for 22 generations during the period from 1185 BCE, lasting for 505 years). Alyattes was the king of Lydia in 776 BCE.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The last king of this dynasty was Myrsilos or Candaules.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Candaules" title="Candaules">
         Candaules
        </a>
        - After ruling for seventeen years, he was assassinated by his former friend Gyges, who succeeded him on the throne of Lydia.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Mermnads
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Gyges_of_Lydia" title="Gyges of Lydia">
         Gyges
        </a>
        , called
        <i>
         Gugu of Luddu
        </i>
        in Assyrian inscriptions (687-652 BCE or 690-657 BCE) - Once established on the throne, Gyges devoted himself to consolidating his kingdom and making it a military power. The capital was relocated from Hyde to Sardis. Barbarian
        <a href="/wiki/Cimmerians" title="Cimmerians">
         Cimmerians
        </a>
        sacked many Lydian cities, except for Sardis. Gyges was the son of Dascylus, who, when recalled from banishment in Cappadocia by the Lydian king Myrsilos—called Candaules "the Dog-strangler" (a title of the Lydian Hermes) by the Greeks—sent his son back to Lydia instead of himself. Gyges turned to Egypt, sending his faithful Carian troops along with Ionian mercenaries to assist Psammetichus in ending Assyrian domination. Some Bible scholars believe that Gyges of Lydia was the Biblical character
        <a href="/wiki/Gog_and_Magog" title="Gog and Magog">
         Gog
        </a>
        , ruler of Magog, who is mentioned in the Book of Ezekiel and the
        <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">
         Book of Revelation
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Ardys_II" title="Ardys II">
         Ardys II
        </a>
        (652-621 BCE).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Sadyattes" title="Sadyattes">
         Sadyattes
        </a>
        (621-609 BCE) or (624-610 BCE) - Herodotus wrote (in his Inquiries) that he fought with
        <a href="/wiki/Cyaxares" title="Cyaxares">
         Cyaxares
        </a>
        , the descendant of Deioces, and with the
        <a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">
         Medes
        </a>
        , drove out the
        <a href="/wiki/Cimmerians" title="Cimmerians">
         Cimmerians
        </a>
        from Asia, captured
        <a href="/wiki/Smyrna" title="Smyrna">
         Smyrna
        </a>
        , which had been founded by colonists from Colophon, and invaded the city-states
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clazomenae" title="Clazomenae">
         Clazomenae
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">
         Miletus
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alyattes_II" title="Alyattes II">
         Alyattes II
        </a>
        (609 or 619-560 BCE) - one of the greatest kings of Lydia. When
        <a href="/wiki/Cyaxares" title="Cyaxares">
         Cyaxares
        </a>
        attacked Lydia, the kings of
        <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">
         Cilicia
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">
         Babylon
        </a>
        intervened and negotiated a peace in 585 BCE, whereby the River
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halys_River" title="Halys River">
         Halys
        </a>
        was established as the Medes' frontier with Lydia. Herodotus writes:
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "On the refusal of Alyattes to give up his supplicants when
        <a href="/wiki/Cyaxares" title="Cyaxares">
         Cyaxares
        </a>
        sent to demand them of him, war broke out between the Lydians and the Medes, and continued for five years, with various success. In the course of it the Medes gained many victories over the Lydians, and the Lydians also gained many victories over the Medes."
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Eclipse" title="Battle of the Eclipse">
        Battle of the Eclipse
       </a>
       was the final battle in a five year
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       war between Alyattes II of Lydia and
       <a href="/wiki/Cyaxares" title="Cyaxares">
        Cyaxares
       </a>
       of the Medes. It took place on May 28, 585 BCE, and ended abruptly due to a total solar eclipse.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">
         Croesus
        </a>
        (560-546 BCE) - the expression "rich as Croesus" refers to this king. The Lydian Empire ended when Croesus attacked the Persian Empire of
        <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">
         Cyrus II
        </a>
        and was defeated in 546 BCE.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Persian_Empire">
        Persian Empire
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Persian Empire">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lydia_(satrapy)" title="Lydia (satrapy)">
        Lydia (satrapy)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 547 BCE, the Lydian king
       <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">
        Croesus
       </a>
       besieged and captured the Persian city of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pteria_(Turkey)" title="Pteria (Turkey)">
        Pteria
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia" title="Cappadocia">
        Cappadocia
       </a>
       and enslaved its inhabitants. The Persian king
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyrus_The_Great" title="Cyrus The Great">
        Cyrus The Great
       </a>
       marched with his army against the Lydians. The
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pteria" title="Battle of Pteria">
        Battle of Pteria
       </a>
       resulted in a stalemate, forcing the Lydians to retreat to their capital city of Sardis. Some months later the Persian and Lydian kings met at the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thymbra" title="Battle of Thymbra">
        Battle of Thymbra
       </a>
       . Cyrus won and captured the capital city of Sardis by 546 BC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lydia became a province (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satrapy" title="Satrapy">
        satrapy
       </a>
       ) of the Persian Empire.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_Empire">
        Hellenistic Empire
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hellenistic Empire">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Lydia remained a satrapy after Persia's conquest by the Macedonian king
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
        Alexander III
       </a>
       (the Great) of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macedon" title="Macedon">
        Macedon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       When Alexander's empire ended after his death, Lydia was possessed by the major Asian diadoch dynasty, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucids" title="Seleucids">
        Seleucids
       </a>
       , and when it was unable to maintain its territory in Asia Minor, Lydia was acquired by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attalid" title="Attalid">
        Attalid
       </a>
       dynasty of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pergamum" title="Pergamum">
        Pergamum
       </a>
       . Its last king avoided the spoils and ravage of a Roman war of conquest by leaving the realm by testament to the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_province_of_Asia">

      <p>
       When the Romans entered the capital Sardis in 133 BCE, Lydia, as the other western parts of the Attalid legacy, became part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Asia_(Roman_province)" title="Asia (Roman province)">
        province of Asia
       </a>
       , a very rich
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">
        Roman province
       </a>
       , worthy of a governor with the high rank of
       <a href="/wiki/Proconsul" title="Proconsul">
        proconsul
       </a>
       . The whole west of Asia Minor had
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">
        Jewish
       </a>
       colonies very early, and Christianity was also soon present there.
       <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">
        Acts of the Apostles
       </a>
       16:14-15 mentions the baptism of a merchant woman called "Lydia" from
       <a href="/wiki/Thyatira" title="Thyatira">
        Thyatira
       </a>
       , known as
       <a href="/wiki/Lydia_of_Thyatira" title="Lydia of Thyatira">
        Lydia of Thyatira
       </a>
       , in what had once been the satrapy of Lydia. Christianity spread rapidly during the 3rd century
       <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">
        CE
       </a>
       , based on the nearby Exarchate of Ephesus.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_province_of_Lydia">
        Roman province of Lydia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Roman province of Lydia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Under the
       <a href="/wiki/Tetrarchy" title="Tetrarchy">
        tetrarchy
       </a>
       reform of Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">
        Diocletian
       </a>
       in 296 CE, Lydia was revived as the name of a separate Roman province, much smaller than the former satrapy, with its capital at Sardis.
      </p>
      <p>
       Together with the provinces of
       <a href="/wiki/Caria" title="Caria">
        Caria
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellespontus_(province)" title="Hellespontus (province)">
        Hellespontus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lycia" title="Lycia">
        Lycia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pamphylia" title="Pamphylia">
        Pamphylia
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Phrygia_prima&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Phrygia prima (page does not exist)">
        Phrygia prima
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Phrygia_secunda&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Phrygia secunda (page does not exist)">
        Phrygia secunda
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pisidia" title="Pisidia">
        Pisidia
       </a>
       (all in modern Turkey) and the Insulae (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionian_islands" title="Ionian islands">
        Ionian islands
       </a>
       , mostly in modern Greece), it formed the
       <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">
        diocese
       </a>
       (under a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vicarius" title="Vicarius">
         vicarius
        </a>
       </i>
       ) of
       <a href="/wiki/Asia_(Roman_province)" title="Asia (Roman province)">
        Asiana
       </a>
       , which was part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture" title="Praetorian prefecture">
        praetorian prefecture
       </a>
       of Oriens, together with the dioceses
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pontiana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pontiana (page does not exist)">
        Pontiana
       </a>
       (most of the rest of Asia Minor), Oriens proper (mainly Syria), Aegyptus (Egypt) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thraciae" title="Thraciae">
        Thraciae
       </a>
       (on the Balkans, roughly Bulgaria).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Byzantine_.28and_Crusader.29_age">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Byzantine_(and_Crusader)_age">
        Byzantine (and Crusader) age
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Byzantine (and Crusader) age">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Under the Byzantine emperor Heraclius (610-641), Lydia became part of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatolikon" title="Anatolikon">
        Anatolikon
       </a>
       , one of the original
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_(Byzantine_administrative_unit)" title="Theme (Byzantine administrative unit)">
         themata
        </a>
       </i>
       , and later of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thrakesion" title="Thrakesion">
        Thrakesion
       </a>
       . Although the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turks" title="Seljuk Turks">
        Seljuk Turks
       </a>
       conquered most of the rest of Anatolia, forming the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Ikonion" title="Sultanate of Ikonion">
        Sultanate of Ikonion
       </a>
       (Konya), Lydia remained part of the Byzantine Empire. During the occupation of Constantinople in the
       <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">
        Fourth Crusade
       </a>
       , Lydia continued to be a part of the Byzantine Orthodox 'Greek Empire' based at
       <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0znik" title="İznik">
        Nicaea
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Under_Turkish_rule">
        Under Turkish rule
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Under Turkish rule">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Lydia was captured finally by Turkish
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatolian_Turkish_Beyliks" title="Anatolian Turkish Beyliks">
         beyliks
        </a>
       </i>
       , which were all absorbed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman
       </a>
       state in 1390. The area became part of the Ottoman
       <a href="/wiki/Aidin_Vilayet" title="Aidin Vilayet">
        Aidin Vilayet
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Province" title="Province">
         province
        </a>
       </i>
       ), and is now in the modern republic of
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkey
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">
        Christianity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Christianity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lydia had numerous Christian communities and, after Christianity became the official religion of the Roman Empire in the 4th century, Lydia became one of the provinces of the diocese of Asia in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarchate_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarchate of Constantinople">
        Patriarchate of Constantinople
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The ecclesiastical province of Lydia had a metropolitan diocese at
       <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">
        Sardis
       </a>
       and suffragan dioceses for
       <a href="/wiki/Ala%C5%9Fehir" title="Alaşehir">
        Philadelphia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thyatira" title="Thyatira">
        Thyatira
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tripolis_(Phrygia)" title="Tripolis (Phrygia)">
        Tripolis
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Settae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Settae (page does not exist)">
        Settae
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gordus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gordus (page does not exist)">
        Gordus
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tralles" title="Tralles">
        Tralles
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silandus" title="Silandus">
        Silandus
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maeonia_(city)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maeonia (city) (page does not exist)">
        Maeonia
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonos_Hierum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Apollonos Hierum (page does not exist)">
        Apollonos Hierum
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mostene" title="Mostene">
        Mostene
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollonias" title="Apollonias">
        Apollonias
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attalia" title="Attalia">
        Attalia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hyrcania" title="Hyrcania">
        Hyrcania
       </a>
       , Bage,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Balandus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Balandus (page does not exist)">
        Balandus
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hermocapella&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hermocapella (page does not exist)">
        Hermocapella
       </a>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hierocaesarea" title="Hierocaesarea">

        Dalda
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stratonicia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stratonicia (page does not exist)">
        Stratonicia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cerasa" title="Cerasa">
        Cerasa
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jabala" title="Jabala">
        Gabala

       <a href="/wiki/Satala" title="Satala">

        Hellenopolis

       </sup>
      </p>

              History
             </div>
            </th>
           </tr>
           <tr>
            <td class="navbox-list navbox-odd" colspan="2" style="width:100%;padding:0px">
             <div style="padding:0em 0.25em">
              As found in the
              <i>
               <a href="/wiki/Notitia_Dignitatum" title="Notitia Dignitatum">
                Notitia Dignitatum
               </a>
              </i>
              . Provincial administration reformed and
              <a href="/wiki/Roman_diocese" title="Roman diocese">
               dioceses
              </a>
              established by
              <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">
               Diocletian
              </a>
              ,
              <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">
               c.
              </a>
              293. Permanent
              <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture" title="Praetorian prefecture">
               praetorian prefectures
              </a>
              established after the death of
              <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">
               Constantine I
              </a>
              . Empire permanently partitioned after 395. Exarchates of
              <a href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Ravenna" title="Exarchate of Ravenna">
               Ravenna
              </a>
              and
              <a href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Africa" title="Exarchate of Africa">
               Africa
              </a>
              established after 584. After massive territorial losses in the 7th century, the remaining provinces were superseded by the
              <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_system" title="Theme system">
               theme system
              </a>
              in c. 640–660, although in
              <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">
               Asia Minor
              </a>
              and parts of Greece they survived under the themes until the early 9th century.
