      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Solar Dynamics Observatory
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        SDO
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       mission which has been observing the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       since 2010.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTRS_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NTRS-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Launched on February 11, 2010, the observatory is part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Living_With_a_Star" title="Living With a Star">
        Living With a Star
       </a>
       (LWS) program.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The goal of the LWS program is to develop the scientific understanding necessary to effectively address those aspects of the connected
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       –
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       system directly affecting life and society. The goal of the SDO is to understand the influence of the Sun on the Earth and near-Earth space by studying the solar atmosphere on small scales of space and time and in many wavelengths simultaneously. SDO has been investigating how the
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_magnetic_field" title="Stellar magnetic field">
        Sun's magnetic field
       </a>
       is generated and structured, how this stored magnetic energy is converted and released into the
       <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">
        heliosphere
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geospace" title="Geospace">
        geospace
       </a>
       in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       , energetic particles, and variations in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">
        solar irradiance
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The SDO spacecraft was developed at NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">
        Goddard Space Flight Center
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greenbelt,_Maryland" title="Greenbelt, Maryland">
        Greenbelt, Maryland
       </a>
       , and launched on February 11, 2010, from
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">
        Cape Canaveral Air Force Station
       </a>
       . The primary mission is scheduled to last five years and three months, with expendables expected to last for ten years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guide-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some consider SDO to be a follow-on mission to the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
       </a>
       (SOHO).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       SDO is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-axis_stabilized_spacecraft" title="3-axis stabilized spacecraft">
        3-axis stabilized spacecraft
       </a>
       , with two solar arrays, and two high-gain antennas, in an inclined
       <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit" title="Geosynchronous orbit">
        geosynchronous orbit
       </a>
       around Earth. The spacecraft includes three instruments: the Extreme Ultraviolet Variability Experiment (EVE) built in partnership with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_at_Boulder" title="University of Colorado at Boulder">
        University of Colorado at Boulder
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Atmospheric_and_Space_Physics" title="Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics">
        Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics
       </a>
       (LASP), the Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager (HMI) built in partnership with
       <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">
        Stanford University
       </a>
       , and the Atmospheric Imaging Assembly (AIA) built in partnership with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Solar_%26_Astrophysics_Laboratory" title="Lockheed Martin Solar &amp; Astrophysics Laboratory">
        Lockheed Martin Solar &amp; Astrophysics Laboratory
       </a>
       . Data which is collected by the craft is made available as soon as possible, after it is received.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Helioseismic_and_Magnetic_Imager_.28HMI.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Helioseismic_and_Magnetic_Imager_(HMI)">
        Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager (HMI)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Dynamics_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager (HMI)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">
        Helioseismic
       </a>
       and Magnetic Imager (HMI), led from
       <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">
        Stanford University
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Stanford,_California" title="Stanford, California">
        Stanford, California
       </a>
       , studies solar variability and characterizes the Sun's interior and the various components of magnetic activity. HMI produces data to determine the interior sources and mechanisms of solar variability and how the physical processes inside the Sun are related to surface magnetic field and activity. It also produces data to enable estimates of the coronal magnetic field for studies of variability in the extended solar atmosphere. HMI observations will enable establishing the relationships between the internal dynamics and magnetic activity in order to understand solar variability and its effects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       HMI will take high-resolution measurements of the longitudinal and vector magnetic field over the entire visible solar disk thus extending the capabilities of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO
       </a>
       's MDI instrument.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Extreme_Ultraviolet_Variability_Experiment_.28EVE.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_Ultraviolet_Variability_Experiment_(EVE)">
        Extreme Ultraviolet Variability Experiment (EVE)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Dynamics_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Extreme Ultraviolet Variability Experiment (EVE)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Extreme Ultraviolet Variability Experiment (EVE) measures the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">
        extreme ultraviolet
       </a>
       irradiance with improved
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_resolution" title="Spectral resolution">
        spectral resolution
       </a>
       , "temporal cadence", accuracy, and precision over preceding measurements made by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TIMED_(Thermosphere_Ionosphere_Mesosphere_Energetics_and_Dynamics)" title="TIMED (Thermosphere Ionosphere Mesosphere Energetics and Dynamics)">
        TIMED
       </a>
       SEE,
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SORCE" title="SORCE">
        SORCE
       </a>
       XPS. The instrument incorporates physics-based models in order to further scientific understanding of the relationship between solar EUV variations and magnetic variation changes in the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EVE_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EVE-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Sun's output of energetic extreme ultraviolet photons is primarily what heats the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's upper atmosphere and creates the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
        ionosphere
       </a>
       . Solar EUV radiation output undergoes constant changes, both moment to moment and over the Sun's 11-year
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       , and these changes are important to understand because they have a significant impact on
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_physics" title="Atmospheric physics">
        atmospheric heating
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">
        satellite drag
       </a>
       , and communications system degradation, including disruption of the
       <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">
        Global Positioning System
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EVEimprovements_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EVEimprovements-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The EVE instrument package was built by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_at_Boulder" title="University of Colorado at Boulder">
        University of Colorado at Boulder
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Atmospheric_and_Space_Physics" title="Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics">
        Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics
       </a>
       , with Dr. Tom Woods as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principal_Investigator" title="Principal Investigator">
        Principal Investigator
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guide-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was delivered to
       <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">
        Goddard Space Flight Center
       </a>
       on September 7, 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The instrument provides improvements of up to 70 percent in spectral resolution measurements in the wavelengths below 30 nm, and a 30 percent improvement in "time cadence" by taking measurements every 10 seconds over a 100 percent
       <a href="/wiki/Duty_cycle" title="Duty cycle">
        duty cycle
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EVEimprovements_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-EVEimprovements-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Atmospheric_Imaging_Assembly_.28AIA.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_Imaging_Assembly_(AIA)">
        Atmospheric Imaging Assembly (AIA)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Dynamics_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Atmospheric Imaging Assembly (AIA)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Atmospheric Imaging Assembly (AIA), led from the
       <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Solar_and_Astrophysics_Laboratory" title="Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory">
        Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory
       </a>
       (LMSAL), provides continuous full-disk observations of the solar
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       in seven
       <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">
        extreme ultraviolet
       </a>
       (EUV) channels, spanning a temperature range from approximately 20,000 Kelvin to in excess of 20 million Kelvin. The 12-second cadence of the image stream with 4096 by 4096 pixel images at 0.6 arcsec/pixel provides unprecedented views of the various phenomena that occur within the evolving solar outer atmosphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       The AIA science investigation is led by LMSAL, which also operates the instrument and – jointly with Stanford University – runs the Joint Science Operations Center from which all of the data are served to the worldwide scientific community, as well as the general public. LMSAL designed the overall instrumentation and led its development and integration. The four telescopes providing the individual light feeds for the instrument were designed and built at the
       <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory">
        Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory
       </a>
       (SAO).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since beginning its operational phase on 2010/05/01, AIA has operated successfully, with unprecedented EUV image quality.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Communications">
        Communications
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Dynamics_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Communications">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       SDO down-links science data (
       <a href="/wiki/K_band_(IEEE)" title="K band (IEEE)">
        K band
       </a>
       ) from its two onboard
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-gain_antenna" title="High-gain antenna">
        high-gain antennas
       </a>
       , and telemetry (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-band" title="S-band">
        S-band
       </a>
       ) from its two onboard
       <a href="/wiki/Omnidirectional_antenna" title="Omnidirectional antenna">
        omnidirectional antennas
       </a>
       . The ground station consists of two dedicated (redundant) 18-meter radio antennas in
       <a href="/wiki/White_Sands_Missile_Range" title="White Sands Missile Range">
        White Sands Missile Range
       </a>
       , New Mexico, constructed specifically for SDO. Mission controllers operate the spacecraft remotely from the Mission Operations Center at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center. The combined data rate is about 130 Mbit/s (150 Mbit/s with overhead, or 300 Msymbols/s with rate 1/2
       <a href="/wiki/Convolutional_code" title="Convolutional code">
        convolutional encoding
       </a>
       ), and the craft generates approximately 1.5 terabytes of data per day (equivalent to downloading around 500,000 songs).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guide-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Dynamics_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       After launch, the spacecraft was placed into an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       around the Earth with an initial
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
        perigee
       </a>
       of about 2,500 kilometres (1,600 mi). SDO then underwent a series of orbit-raising maneuvers which adjusted its orbit until the spacecraft reached its planned
       <a href="/wiki/Circular_orbit" title="Circular orbit">
        circular
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit" title="Geosynchronous orbit">
        geosynchronous orbit
       </a>
       at an altitude of 35,789 kilometres (22,238 mi), at 102° W longitude, inclined at 28.5°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sun_dog_phenomenon">
        Sun dog phenomenon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Dynamics_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sun dog phenomenon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Moments after launch, SDO's Atlas V rocket flew past a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_dogs" title="Sun dogs">
        sun dog
       </a>
       hanging suspended in the blue Florida sky and when the rocket penetrated the cirrus cloud, shock waves rippled through the cloud and destroyed the alignment of the crystals of the sun dog making a visible rippling effect in the sky.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Camilla">
        Camilla
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Dynamics_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Camilla">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <b>
        Camilla Corona
       </b>
       is a rubber chicken (similar to a children's toy), and is the mission mascot for
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO). It is part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Education_and_public_outreach" title="Education and public outreach">
        Education and public outreach
       </a>
       team and assists with various functions to help educate the public, mainly children, about the SDO mission, facts about the Sun and
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">
        space weather
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Camilla also assists in cross-informing the public about other NASA missions and space related projects. Camilla Corona SDO uses
       <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">
        social media
       </a>
       to interact with fans.
      </p>
