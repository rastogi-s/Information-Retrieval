   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Moreton wave
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:98px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MoretonWaveAnimation200612.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="96" data-file-width="96" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f6/MoretonWaveAnimation200612.gif" width="96"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         Animation of a Moreton wave which occurred on December 6, 2006
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        Moreton wave
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Moreton-Ramsey wave
       </b>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromospheric
       </a>
       signature of a large-scale solar
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        coronal
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">
        shock wave
       </a>
       . Described as a kind of
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">
        tsunami
       </a>
       ",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tphill09_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-tphill09-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       they are generated by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">
        solar flares
       </a>
       . They are named for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">
        American
       </a>
       astronomer
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gail_Moreton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gail Moreton (page does not exist)">
        Gail Moreton
       </a>
       , an observer at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Solar_and_Astrophysics_Laboratory_(LMSAL)" title="Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory (LMSAL)">
        Lockheed Solar Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">
        Burbank
       </a>
       , and Harry E. Ramsey, an observer who spotted them in 1959 at The Sacramento Peak Observatory.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He discovered them in
       <a href="/wiki/Time-lapse_photography" title="Time-lapse photography">
        time-lapse photography
       </a>
       of the chromosphere in the light of the
       <a href="/wiki/Balmer_series" title="Balmer series">
        Balmer alpha transition
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There were few follow-up studies for decades. Then the 1995 launch of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
       </a>
       led to observation of coronal waves, which cause Moreton waves. Moreton waves were a research topic again. (SOHO's
       <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet_Imaging_Telescope" title="Extreme ultraviolet Imaging Telescope">
        EIT
       </a>
       instrument discovered another, different wave type called "EIT waves".)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The reality of Moreton waves (a.k.a. fast-mode
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">
        MHD
       </a>
       waves) has also been confirmed by the two
       <a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">
        STEREO
       </a>
       spacecraft. They observed a 100,000-km-high wave of hot plasma and magnetism, moving at 250 km/s, in conjunction with a big
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejection
       </a>
       in February 2009.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STEREO_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-STEREO-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pho111909_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pho111909-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_tsunami.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1261" data-file-width="1261" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Solar_tsunami.jpg/150px-Solar_tsunami.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Solar_tsunami.jpg/225px-Solar_tsunami.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Solar_tsunami.jpg/300px-Solar_tsunami.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_tsunami.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Solar Tsunami
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Moreton measured the waves propagating at a
       <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">
        speed
       </a>
       of 500–1500 km/s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STEREO_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-STEREO-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yutaka_Uchida&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yutaka Uchida (page does not exist)">
        Yutaka Uchida
       </a>
       interpreted Moreton waves as MHD fast mode shock waves propagating in the corona.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He links them to
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Type_II_radio_bursts&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Type II radio bursts (page does not exist)">
        type II radio bursts
       </a>
       , which are radio-wave discharges created when coronal mass ejections accelerate shocks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moreton waves can be observed primarily in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%CE%B1" title="Hα">
        Hα
       </a>
       band.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Narukage,_et_al._10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Narukage,_et_al.-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
