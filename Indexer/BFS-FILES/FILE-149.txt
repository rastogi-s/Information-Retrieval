      <p>
       A
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Transit_(astronomy)" title="Transit (astronomy)">
         transit
        </a>
        of Mercury
       </b>
       across the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       takes place when the
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       passes directly between the Sun and a superior planet, becoming visible against (and hence obscuring a small portion of) the solar disk. During a transit, Mercury appears as a tiny black dot moving across the disk of the Sun.
      </p>
      <p>
       Transits of Mercury with respect to Earth are much more frequent than
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">
        transits of Venus
       </a>
       , with about 13 or 14 per century, in part because Mercury is closer to the Sun and orbits it more rapidly.
      </p>
      <p>
       Transits of Mercury occur in May or November. The last four transits occurred on November 15, 1999; May 7, 2003; November 8, 2006; and May 9, 2016. The next will occur on November 11, 2019, and then on November 13, 2032. A typical transit lasts several hours.
      </p>
      <p>
       On June 3, 2014, the
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_rover" title="Mars rover">
        Mars rover
       </a>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Curiosity_(rover)" title="Curiosity (rover)">
         Curiosity
        </a>
       </i>
       observed the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       transiting the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , marking the first time a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_transit" title="Planetary transit">
        planetary transit
       </a>
       has been observed from a celestial body besides
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140610_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140610-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       More generally, transits can also occur for Venus
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and were investigated in the context of searching for the hypothetical
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_planet" title="Inner planet">
        inner planet
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(hypothetical_planet)" title="Vulcan (hypothetical planet)">
        Vulcan
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_observations">
        Scientific observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_of_Mercury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scientific observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The most common observation to be made at a transit is to record the times when the disk of Mercury appears to be in contact with the limb of the Sun. Those contacts are traditionally referred to as the 1st, 2nd, 3rd and 4th contacts – with the 2nd and 3rd contacts occurring when the disk of Mercury is fully on the disk of the sun. As a general rule, 1st and 4th contacts cannot be accurately detected, while 2nd and 3rd contacts are readily visible within the constraints of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Drop_effect" title="Black Drop effect">
        Black Drop effect
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Irradiation_illusion" title="Irradiation illusion">
        irradiation
       </a>
       , atmospheric conditions, and the quality of the optics being used.
      </p>
      <p>
       Observed contact times for transits between 1677 and 1881 are given in S Newcomb's analysis of transits of Mercury.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newcomb_S_1882,_page_367_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Newcomb_S_1882,_page_367-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Observed 2nd and 3rd contacts times for transits between 1677 and 1973 are given in Royal Greenwich Observatory Bulletin No.181, 359-420 (1975).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples of the scientific investigations based on transits of Mercury are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Investigations of the variability of the Earth's rotation and of the tidal acceleration of the Moon.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Measuring the mass of Venus from secular variations in Mercury's orbit.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newcomb_S_1882,_page_367_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Newcomb_S_1882,_page_367-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Looking for long term variations in the solar radius.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Investigating the black drop effect, including calling into question the purported discovery of the atmosphere of Venus during the 1761 transit.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Assessing the likely drop in light level in an exoplanet transit.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Images of the November 15, 1999 transit from the Transition Region and Coronal explorer (TRACE) satellite were on Astronomy Picture of the Day (APOD) on November 19
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Three APOD's featured the May 9, 2016 transit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_of_transits">
        Occurrence of transits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_of_Mercury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occurrence of transits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Transits of Mercury can only occur when the Earth is aligned with a node of Mercury's orbit. Currently that alignment occurs within a few days of May 8 (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descending_node" title="Descending node">
        descending node
       </a>
       ) and November 10 (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascending_node" title="Ascending node">
        ascending node
       </a>
       ), with the angular diameter of Mercury being about 12" for May transits, and 10" for November transits. The average date for a transit increases over centuries as a result of the longitude of the nodes of Mercury's orbit increasing by about 1.1 deg per century.
      </p>
      <p>
       Transits of Mercury occur on a regular basis. As explained in 1882 by Newcomb,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the interval between passages of Mercury through the ascending node of its orbit is 87.969 days, and the interval between the Earth's passage through that same longitude is 365.254 days. From the ratio of these values, it can be easily shown that Mercury will make an almost integral number of revolutions about the Sun over intervals of 4, 6, 7, 13, 33, 46, 171 and 217 years.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1894 Crommelin
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       noted that at these intervals, the successive paths of Mercury relative to the Sun are consistently displaced northwards or southwards. He noted the displacements as:
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Displacements at subsequent transits
       </b>
      </p>

      <p>
       Comparing these displacements with the solar diameter (about 31.7’ for May, and 32.4’ for November, transits) the following may be deduced about the interval between transits:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        For May transits, intervals of 6 and 7 years are not possible. For November transits, an interval of 6 years is possible but rare (the last such pair was 1993 and 1999), while an interval of 7 years is to be expected.
       </li>
       <li>
        An interval of 13 years is to be expected for both May and November transits.
       </li>
       <li>
        An interval of 20 years is possible but rare for a May transit, but is to be expected for November transits.
       </li>
       <li>
        An interval of 33 years is to be expected for both May and November transits.
       </li>
       <li>
        A transit having a similar path across the sun will occur 46 (&amp; 171) years later – for both November and May transits.
       </li>
       <li>
        A transit having an almost identical path across the Sun will occur 217 years later – for both November and May transits.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Transits that occur 46 years apart can be grouped into a series. For November transits each series includes about 20 transits over 874 years, with the path of Mercury across the Sun passing further north than for the previous transit. For May transits each series includes about 10 transits over 414 years, with the path of Mercury across the Sun passing further south than for the previous transit. Some authors
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Espenak_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Espenak-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       have allocated a series number to transits on the basis of this 46-year grouping.
      </p>
      <p>
       Similarly transits that occur 217 years apart can be grouped into a series. For November transits each series would include about 135 transits over 30,000 years. For May transits each series would include about 110 transits over 24,000 years. For both the May and November series, the path of Mercury across the Sun passes further north than for the previous transit. Series numbers have not been traditionally allocated on the basis of the 217 year grouping.
      </p>
      <p>
       Predictions of transits of Mercury covering many years are available at NASA,

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Partial_transits_of_Mercury">
        Partial transits of Mercury
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_of_Mercury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Partial transits of Mercury">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Transit_of_Mercury_November_15_1999_path_across_sun.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="504" data-file-width="529" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Transit_of_Mercury_November_15_1999_path_across_sun.png/220px-Transit_of_Mercury_November_15_1999_path_across_sun.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Transit_of_Mercury_November_15_1999_path_across_sun.png/330px-Transit_of_Mercury_November_15_1999_path_across_sun.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Transit_of_Mercury_November_15_1999_path_across_sun.png/440px-Transit_of_Mercury_November_15_1999_path_across_sun.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Transit_of_Mercury_November_15_1999_path_across_sun.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         November 15, 1999 simulated transit of Mercury across the Sun.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Sometimes Mercury appears to only graze the Sun during a transit. There are two possible scenarios:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Firstly, it is possible for a transit to occur where, at mid-transit, the disk of Mercury has fully entered the disk of the Sun as seen from some parts of the world, while as seen from other parts of the world the disk of Mercury has only partially entered the disk of the Sun. The transit of November 15, 1999 was such a transit,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
         <a href="#cite_note-23">
          [23]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
         <a href="#cite_note-24">
          [24]
         </a>
        </sup>
        with the transit being a full transit for most of the world, but only a partial transit for Australia, New Zealand, and Antarctica. The previous such transit was on October 28, 743 and the next will be on May 11, 2391. While these events are very rare, two such transits will occur within 2½ years on December 6149 and June 6152.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fourmilab.ch_22-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-fourmilab.ch-22">
          [22]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Secondly, it is possible for a transit to occur where, at mid-transit, the disk of Mercury has partially entered the disk of the Sun as seen from some parts of the world, while as seen from other parts of the world Mercury completely misses the Sun. Such a transit last occurred on May 11, 1937 where a partial transit occurred in southern Africa and southern Asia and no transit was visible from Europe and northern Asia. The previous such transit was on October 21, 1342 and the next will be on May 13, 2608.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The possibility where, at mid-transit, Mercury is seen to be fully on the solar disk from some parts of the world, and completely miss the Sun as seen from other parts of the world cannot occur.
      </p>
      <h2>

      <p>

        Johannes Kepler
       </a>
