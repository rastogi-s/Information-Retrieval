      <p>
       <b>
        Magnetic pressure
       </b>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">
        energy density
       </a>
       associated with a
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       . Any magnetic field has an associated magnetic pressure contained by the boundary conditions on the field. It is identical to any other physical
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
        pressure
       </a>
       except that it is carried by the magnetic field rather than (in the case of a
       <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
        gas
       </a>
       ) by the
       <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">
        kinetic energy
       </a>
       of gas molecules. A gradient in field strength causes a force due to the magnetic
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient" title="Pressure gradient">
        pressure gradient
       </a>
       called the
       <b>
        magnetic pressure force
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The magnetic pressure force is readily observed in an unsupported loop of
       <a href="/wiki/Wire" title="Wire">
        wire
       </a>
       . If an
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">
        electric current
       </a>
       passes through the loop, the wire serves as an
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">
        electromagnet
       </a>
       , such that the magnetic field strength inside the loop is much greater than the field strength just outside the loop. This gradient in field strength gives rise to a magnetic pressure force that tends to stretch the wire uniformly outward. If enough current travels through the wire, the loop of wire will form a
       <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">
        circle
       </a>
       . At even higher currents, the magnetic pressure can create
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_stress" title="Tensile stress">
        tensile stress
       </a>
       that exceeds the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">
        tensile strength
       </a>
       of the wire, causing it to fracture, or even explosively fragment. Thus, management of magnetic pressure is a significant challenge in the design of ultrastrong electromagnets.
      </p>

      <p>
       Where
       <i>
        Y
       </i>
       is the internal inductance of the coil, defined by the distribution of current.
       <i>
        Y
       </i>
       is 0 for high frequency currents carried mostly by the outer surface of the conductor, and 0.25 for DC currents distributed evenly throughout the conductor. See
       <a href="/wiki/Inductance" title="Inductance">
        inductance
       </a>
       for more information.
      </p>
      <p>
       Interplay between magnetic pressure and ordinary gas pressure is important to
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">
        magnetohydrodynamics
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_physics" title="Plasma physics">
        plasma physics
       </a>
       . Magnetic pressure can also be used to propel
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Projectiles" title="Projectiles">
        projectiles
       </a>
       ; this is the operating principle of a
       <a href="/wiki/Railgun" title="Railgun">
        railgun
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>

       <a href="/wiki/Force-free_magnetic_field" title="Force-free magnetic field">
        force-free
       </a>
       because there is no

      </p>
