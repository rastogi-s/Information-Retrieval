      <p>
       <b>
        Shadow bands
       </b>
       are thin, wavy lines of alternating light and dark that can be seen moving and undulating in parallel on plain-coloured surfaces immediately before and after a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_solar_eclipse" title="Total solar eclipse">
        total solar eclipse
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are caused by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scintillation_(astronomy)" title="Scintillation (astronomy)">
        refraction by Earth's atmospheric turbulence
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-codona_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-codona-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of the solar crescent as it thins to a narrow slit, which increasingly
       <a href="/wiki/Collimated_light" title="Collimated light">
        collimates the light
       </a>
       reaching Earth in the minute just before and after
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse#Terminology_for_central_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        totality
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-codona_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-codona-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The shadows' detailed structure is due to random patterns of fine air
       <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
        turbulence
       </a>
       that refract the collimated sunlight arriving from the narrow eclipse crescent.
      </p>
      <p>
       The bands' rapid sliding motion is due to shifting air currents combined with the angular motion of the sun projecting through higher altitudes. The degree of collimation in the light gradually increases as the crescent thins, until the solar disk is completely covered and the eclipse is total.
      </p>
      <p>
       Stars twinkle for the same reason. They are so far from Earth that they appear as point sources of light easily disturbed by Earth's atmospheric turbulence which acts like lenses and prisms diverting the light's path. Viewed toward the collimated light of a star, the shadows bands from atmospheric refraction pass over the eye.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadow_bands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In the 9th century CE, shadow bands during a total solar eclipse were described for the first time – in the
        <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lusp%C3%A1" title="Völuspá">
         Völuspá
        </a>
        , part of the old Icelandic
        <a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">
         poetic edda
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In 1820,
        <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Goldschmidt" title="Hermann Goldschmidt">
         Hermann Goldschmidt
        </a>
        of Germany noted shadow bands visible just before and after totality at some eclipses.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In 1842,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_B._Airy" title="George B. Airy">
         George B. Airy
        </a>
        , the English astronomer royal, saw his first total eclipse of the sun. He recalled shadow bands as one of the highlights: "As the totality approached, a strange fluctuation of light was seen upon the walls and the ground, so striking that in some places children ran after it and tried to catch it with their hands."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In 2008, British astrophysicist Stuart Eves speculated that shadow bands might be an effect of
        <a href="/wiki/Infrasound" title="Infrasound">
         infrasound
        </a>
        , which involves the shadow of the moon travelling at supersonic speed and inducing an atmospheric shock wave. However, astronomy professor Barrie Jones, an expert on shadow bands,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
        stated, "The [accepted] theory works; there's no need to seek an alternative."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
