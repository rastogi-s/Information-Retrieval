       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">

         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>

       </a>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_bodies" title="Celestial bodies">
        celestial bodies

       in a gravitational system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">

       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syzygy_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The word is often used in reference to the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , and either the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       or a
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       , where the latter is in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_conjunction" title="Astronomical conjunction">
        conjunction
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_opposition" title="Astronomical opposition">
        opposition
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        Solar
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipses
       </a>
       occur at times of syzygy, as do
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">
        transits
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occultations
       </a>
       . The term is often applied when the Sun and Moon are in conjunction (
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       ) or opposition (
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The word
       <i>
        syzygy
       </i>
       is often used to describe interesting configurations of astronomical objects in general. For example, one such case occurred on March 21, 1894, around 23:00
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GMT" title="GMT">
        GMT
       </a>
       , when
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       transited the Sun as would have been seen from Venus, and Mercury and
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       both simultaneously transited the Sun as seen from
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . It is also used to describe situations when all the planets are on the same side of the Sun although they are
       <i>
        not necessarily in a straight line
       </i>
       , such as on March 10, 1982.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-1982_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-1982-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On June 3, 2014, the
       <a href="/wiki/Curiosity_(rover)" title="Curiosity (rover)">
        <i>
         Curiosity
        </i>
        rover
       </a>
       on Mars observed the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Mercury_from_Mars" title="Transit of Mercury from Mars">
        Mercury transiting the Sun
       </a>
       , marking the first time a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_transit" title="Planetary transit">
        planetary transit
       </a>
       has been observed from a celestial body besides
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140610_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140610-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Occultations.2C_transits.2C_and_eclipses">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Occultations,_transits,_and_eclipses">
        Occultations, transits, and eclipses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syzygy_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occultations, transits, and eclipses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Syzygy sometimes results in an
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occultation
       </a>
       , transit, or
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipse
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        An
        <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
         occultation
        </a>
        occurs when an apparently larger body passes in front of an apparently smaller one.
       </li>
       <li>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Transit_(astronomy)" title="Transit (astronomy)">
         transit
        </a>
        occurs when a smaller body passes in front of a larger one.
        <ul>
         <li>
          In the combined case where the smaller body regularly transits the larger, an occultation is also termed a secondary eclipse.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        An
        <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
         eclipse
        </a>
        occurs when a body totally or partially disappears from view, either by an occultation, as with a
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
         solar eclipse
        </a>
        , or by passing into the shadow of another body, as with a
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
         lunar eclipse
        </a>
        (thus both are listed on
        <a class="external text" href="http://eclipse.gsfc.nasa.gov/eclipse.html" rel="nofollow">
         NASA's eclipse page
        </a>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Transits and occultations of the Sun by Earth's Moon are called
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       regardless of whether the Sun is completely or partially covered. By extension, transits of the Sun by a
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       of a planet may also be called eclipses, as with the transits of Phobos and Deimos shown on
       <a class="external text" href="http://photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/target/phobos" rel="nofollow">
        NASA's JPL photojournal
       </a>
       , as may the passage of a satellite into the planet's shadow, as with this
       <a class="external text" href="http://photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/catalog/PIA03612" rel="nofollow">
        eclipse of Phobos
       </a>
       . The term
       <i>
        eclipse
       </i>
       is also used more generally for bodies passing in front of one another. For example,
       <a class="external text" href="http://apod.nasa.gov/apod/ap070316.html" rel="nofollow">
        a NASA Astronomy Picture of the Day
       </a>
       refers to the Moon eclipsing and occulting Saturn interchangeably.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Einstein_ring">
        Einstein ring
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syzygy_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Einstein ring">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As electromagnetic rays are somewhat bent by gravitation, when they pass by a heavy mass. One says the mass acts as a gravitational lens. If the light source, the diffracting mass and the observer stand in a line, one sees an
       <a href="/wiki/Einstein_ring" title="Einstein ring">
        Einstein ring
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_variation">
        Tidal variation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syzygy_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tidal variation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_range" title="Tidal range">
        Tidal range
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Syzygy causes the bimonthly phenomena of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_tide" title="Spring tide">
        spring
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neap_tide" title="Neap tide">
        neap
       </a>
       tides. At the new and full moon, the Sun and Moon are in syzygy. Their
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       act to reinforce each other, and the ocean both rises higher and falls lower than the average. Conversely, at the first and third quarter, the Sun and Moon are at right angles, their tidal forces counteract each other, and the tidal range is smaller than average.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Tidal variation can also be measured in the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_tide" title="Earth tide">
        earth's crust
       </a>
       , and this
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_triggering_of_earthquakes" title="Tidal triggering of earthquakes">
        may affect the frequency of earthquakes
       </a>
       .
      </p>
