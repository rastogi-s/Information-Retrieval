   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunar distance (astronomy)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>

    </div>

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       This article is about the average distance from the Earth to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . For its other use in navigation, see
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(navigation)" title="Lunar distance (navigation)">
        Lunar distance (navigation)
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_perigee_apogee.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="485" data-file-width="800" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Lunar_perigee_apogee.png/320px-Lunar_perigee_apogee.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Lunar_perigee_apogee.png/480px-Lunar_perigee_apogee.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Lunar_perigee_apogee.png/640px-Lunar_perigee_apogee.png 2x" width="320"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_perigee_apogee.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A lunar distance,
         <span class="nowrap">
          <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
           7008384402000000000♠
          </span>
          384
          <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
           402
          </span>
          km
         </span>
         , is the Moon's average distance to Earth. The actual distance varies over the course of
         <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
          its orbit
         </a>
         . The image compares the Moon's
         <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
          apparent size
         </a>
         when it is
         <a href="/wiki/Supermoon" title="Supermoon">
          closest
         </a>
         and farthest from Earth.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Lunar distance
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        LD
       </b>
       or
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
         <math alttext="{\textstyle \Delta _{\oplus L}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
          <semantics>
           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mstyle displaystyle="false" scriptlevel="0">
             <msub>
              <mi mathvariant="normal">
               Δ
               <!-- Δ -->
              </mi>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mo>
                ⊕
                <!-- ⊕ -->
               </mo>
               <mi>
                L
               </mi>
              </mrow>
             </msub>
            </mstyle>
           </mrow>
           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
            {\textstyle \Delta _{\oplus L}}
           </annotation>
          </semantics>
         </math>
        </span>
        <img alt="{\textstyle \Delta _{\oplus L}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc4cbdbca7d4fd84bbc4572852e77a36679bc84e" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:4.566ex; height:2.509ex;"/>
       </span>
       ), also called
       <b>
        Earth–Moon distance
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        Earth–Moon characteristic distance
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        distance to the Moon
       </b>
       , is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_of_measure" title="Unit of measure">
        unit of measure
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       . It is the average distance from the center of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       to the center of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . More technically, it is the mean
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">
        semi-major axis
       </a>
       of the geocentric
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        lunar orbit
       </a>
       . It may also refer to the time-averaged distance between the centers of the Earth and the Moon, or less commonly, the instantaneous Earth–Moon distance. The lunar distance is approximately a quarter of a million miles (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7008400000000000000♠
        </span>
        400
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         000
        </span>
        km
       </span>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groten_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Groten-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The mean semi-major axis has a value of 384,402 km (238,856 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Apache_Point_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Apache_Point-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The time-averaged distance between Earth and Moon centers is 385,000.6 km (239,228.3 mi). The actual distance varies over the course of the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        orbit of the Moon
       </a>
       , from 356,500 km (221,500 mi) at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
        perigee
       </a>
       to 406,700 km (252,700 mi) at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
        apogee
       </a>
       , resulting in a differential range of 50,200 km (31,200 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millimeter_challenge_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-millimeter_challenge-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lunar distance is commonly used to express the distance to
       <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">
        near-Earth object
       </a>
       encounters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEO_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NEO-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lunar distance is also an important astronomical datum; the precision of this measurement to a few parts in a trillion has useful implications for testing gravitational theories such as
       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
        general relativity
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and for refining other astronomical values such as
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_mass" title="Earth mass">
        Earth mass
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonbounce_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonbounce-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">
        Earth radius
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fischer_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fischer-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Earth's rotation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LLR_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LLR-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The measurement is also useful in characterizing the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_radius" title="Lunar radius">
        lunar radius
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">
        mass of the Sun
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        distance to the Sun
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Millimeter-
       <a href="/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision" title="Accuracy and precision">
        precision
       </a>
       measurements of the lunar distance are made by measuring the time taken for light to travel between
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LIDAR" title="LIDAR">
        LIDAR
       </a>
       stations on the Earth and
       <a href="/wiki/Retroreflector" title="Retroreflector">
        retroreflectors
       </a>
       placed on the Moon. The Moon is spiraling away from the Earth at an average rate of 3.8 cm (1.5 in) per year, as detected by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Laser_Ranging_Experiment" title="Lunar Laser Ranging Experiment">
        Lunar Laser Ranging Experiment
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By coincidence, the diameter of
       <a href="/wiki/Corner_reflector" title="Corner reflector">
        corner cubes
       </a>
       in retroreflectors on the Moon is also
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6998380000000000000♠
        </span>
        3.8 cm
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variation">
        Variation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The instantaneous lunar distance is constantly changing. In fact the true distance between the Moon and Earth can change as quickly as
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001750000000000000♠
        </span>
        75 meters per second
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millimeter_challenge_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-millimeter_challenge-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or more than
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7006100000000000000♠
        </span>
        1000 km
       </span>
       in just 6 hours, due to its non-circular orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zuluaga_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Zuluaga-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are other effects that also influence the lunar distance. Some factors are described in this section.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Perturbations_and_eccentricity">
        Perturbations and eccentricity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Perturbations and eccentricity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The distance to the Moon can be measured to an accuracy of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6997200000000000000♠
        </span>
        2 mm
       </span>
       over a 1-hour sampling period,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APOLLO_Analysis_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-APOLLO_Analysis-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which results in an overall uncertainty of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6998200000000000000♠
        </span>
        2–3 cm
       </span>
       for the average distance. However, due to
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        its elliptical orbit
       </a>
       with varying eccentricity, the instantaneous distance varies with monthly periodicity. Furthermore, the distance is perturbed by the gravitational effects of various astronomical bodies - most significantly the Sun and less so by Jupiter. Other forces responsible for minute perturbations are other planets in the solar system, asteroids, tidal forces, and relativistic effects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-integration_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-integration-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The effect of
       <a href="/wiki/Yarkovsky_effect" title="Yarkovsky effect">
        radiation pressure
       </a>
       from the sun contributes an amount of ±
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6997360000000000000♠
        </span>
        3.6 mm
       </span>
       to the lunar distance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APOLLO_Analysis_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-APOLLO_Analysis-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the instantaneous uncertainty is sub-millimeter, the measured lunar distance can change by more than
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7007210000000000000♠
        </span>
        21
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         000
        </span>
        km
       </span>
       from the mean value throughout a typical month. These perturbations are well understood
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DE431_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DE431-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the lunar distance can be accurately modeled over thousands of years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-integration_19-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-integration-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_dissipation">
        Tidal dissipation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tidal dissipation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Through the action of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_forces" title="Tidal forces">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       is slowly being transferred from Earth's rotation to the Moon's orbit. The result is that Earth's rate of spin is imperceptibly decreasing (at a rate of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7000229999999999999♠
        </span>
        2.3 milliseconds/century
       </span>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the lunar orbit is gradually expanding. The current rate of recession is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7000380500000000000♠
        </span>
        3.805
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.004 cm per year
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DE431_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-DE431-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, it is believed that this rate has recently increased, as a rate of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6991120417206325883♠
        </span>
        3.8 cm/year
       </span>
       would imply that the Moon is only 1.5 billion years old, whereas scientific consensus assumes an age of ~4 billion years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Precambrian_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Precambrian-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also believed that this anomalously high rate of recession may continue to accelerate.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lunar_Orbit_Evolution_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lunar_Orbit_Evolution-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is predicted that the lunar distance will continue to increase until the Earth and Moon become
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       . This would occur when the duration of the lunar orbital period equals the rotational period of Earth. The two bodies will then be at equilibrium, and no further rotational energy will be exchanged. Models predict that 50 billion years would be required to achieve this configuration,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tidal_lock_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-tidal_lock-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which is significantly longer than the
       <a href="/wiki/Red_giant#The_Sun_as_a_red_giant" title="Red giant">
        expected lifetime of the solar system
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_history">
        Orbital history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Orbital history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The average lunar distance is increasing, which implies that the Moon was closer in the past. There is geological evidence that the average lunar distance was about 52
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">
        <var>
         R
        </var>
        <sub>
         ⊕
        </sub>
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">
        Precambrian Era
       </a>
       ; 2,500 million years
       <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">
        BP
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Precambrian_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Precambrian-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_the_Moon" title="Origin of the Moon">
        giant impact hypothesis
       </a>
       , a widely accepted theory, states that the Moon was created as a result of a catastrophic impact between another planet and Earth, resulting in a re-accumulation of fragments at an initial distance of 3.8
       <var>
        R
       </var>
       <sub>
        ⊕
       </sub>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Impact_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Impact-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this theory, the initial impact is assumed to have occurred 4.5 billion years ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheiaPothesis_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TheiaPothesis-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_measurement">
        History of measurement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History of measurement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Until the late 1950s all measurements of lunar distance were based on optical angular measurements. The space age marked a turning point which greatly advanced the precision and accuracy of our knowledge of this value. During the 1950s and 1960s, experiments were conducted that utilized radar, lasers, spacecraft, and computer modeling.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DE_102_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DE_102-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This section is intended to illustrate some of the historically significant or otherwise interesting methods of determining the lunar distance, and is not intended to be an exhaustive or all-encompassing list.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Parallax">
        Parallax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Parallax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The oldest method of determining the lunar distance involves measuring the angle between the Moon and a chosen reference point from multiple locations, simultaneously. The synchronization can be coordinated by making measurements at a pre-determined time, or during an event which is observable to all parties. Before accurate mechanical chronometers, the synchronization event was typically a lunar eclipse, or the moment when the moon crossed the meridian (if the observers shared the same longitude). This measurement technique is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Parallax#Lunar_parallax" title="Parallax">
        lunar parallax
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For increased accuracy, certain systematic errors must be accounted for, such as adjusting the measured angle to account for refraction and distortion of light through the atmosphere.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_eclipse">
        Lunar eclipse
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lunar eclipse">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Early attempts to measure the distance to the Moon exploited observations of a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       combined with knowledge of Earth's radius and an understanding that the Sun is much further than the Moon. By observing the geometry of a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       , the lunar distance can be calculated using
       <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">
        trigonometry
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The earliest account of an attempt to measure the distance to the Moon using this technique was by the 4th-century-BC Greek astronomer and mathematician
       <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samos" title="Aristarchus of Samos">
        Aristarchus of Samos
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gutzwiller_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gutzwiller-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and later by
       <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">
        Hipparchus
       </a>
       , whose calculations produced a result of 59-67
       <var>
        R
       </var>
       <sub>
        ⊕
       </sub>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transit_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-transit-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This method later found its way into the work of
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Measuring_the_Universe_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Measuring_the_Universe-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who produced a result of ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        64
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         6
        </sub>
       </span>
       <var>
        R
       </var>
       <sub>
        ⊕
       </sub>
       at its farthest point.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helden_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Helden-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meridian_crossing">
        Meridian crossing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Meridian crossing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       An expedition by French astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Claude_de_la_Cherois_Crommelin" title="Andrew Claude de la Cherois Crommelin">
        A.C.D Crommelin
       </a>
       observed lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">
        transits
       </a>
       through the
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(astronomy)" title="Meridian (astronomy)">
        meridian
       </a>
       (the moment when the Moon
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_(astronomy)" title="Transit (astronomy)">
        crosses
       </a>
       an imaginary
       <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">
        great circle
       </a>
       that passes
       <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">
        directly overhead
       </a>
       and through the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_pole" title="Celestial pole">
        celestial poles
       </a>
       ) on the same night from two different locations. Careful measurements from 1905 through 1910 measured the angle of elevation at the moment when a specific lunar crater (
       <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6sting_(crater)" title="Mösting (crater)">
        Mösting A
       </a>
       ) crossed the meridian, from stations at
       <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">
        Greenwich
       </a>
       and at
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">
        Cape of Good Hope
       </a>
       , which share nearly the same longitude.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commelin_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Commelin-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A distance was calculated with an uncertainty of ± 30 km, and remained the definitive lunar distance value for the next half century.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Occultations">
        Occultations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Occultations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       By recording the instant when the Moon
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occults
       </a>
       a background star, (or similarly, measuring the angle between the moon and a background star at a predetermined moment) the lunar distance can be determined, as long as the measurements are taken from multiple locations of known separation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Astronomers
       <a href="/wiki/John_A._O%27Keefe" title="John A. O'Keefe">
        O'Keefe
       </a>
       and Anderson calculated the lunar distance by observing 4 occultations from 9 locations in 1952.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Keefe_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-O'Keefe-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They calculated a mean distance of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7008384407600000000♠
        </span>
        384
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         407
        </span>
        .6
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        4.7 km
       </span>
       , however the value was refined by in 1962 by
       <a href="/wiki/Irene_Fischer" title="Irene Fischer">
        Irene Fischer
       </a>
       , who incorporated updated
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        geodetic
       </a>
       data to produce a value of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7008384403700000000♠
        </span>
        384
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         403
        </span>
        .7
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        2 km
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fischer_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fischer-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radar">
        Radar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Radar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       An experiment was conducted in 1957 at the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory that used the echo from radar signals to determine the Earth-Moon distance. Radar pulses lasting
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6994200000000000000♠
        </span>
        2 μs
       </span>
       were broadcast from a
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001152400000000000♠
        </span>
        50 ft
       </span>
       diameter radio dish. After the radio waves echoed off the surface of the Moon, the return signal was detected and the delay time measured. From that measurement, the distance could be calculated. In practice, however, the
       <a href="/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio" title="Signal-to-noise ratio">
        signal-to-noise ratio
       </a>
       was so low that an accurate measurement could not be reliably produced.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radar1_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Radar1-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The experiment was repeated in 1958 at the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Radar_Establishment" title="Royal Radar Establishment">
        Royal Radar Establishment
       </a>
       , in England. Radar pulses lasting
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6994499999999999999♠
        </span>
        5 μs
       </span>
       were transmitted with a peak power of 2 megawatts, at a repetition rate of 260 pulses per second. After the radio waves echoed off the surface of the Moon, the return signal was detected and the delay time measured. Multiple signals were added together to obtain a reliable signal by superimposing oscilloscope traces onto photographic film. From the measurements, the distance was calculated with an uncertainty of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7003125000000000000♠
        </span>
        1.25 km
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radar2_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Radar2-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       These initial experiments were intended to be proof-of-concept experiments and only lasted one day. Follow-on experiments lasting one month produced a mean value of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7008384402000000000♠
        </span>
        384
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         402
        </span>
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        1.2 km
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radar3_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Radar3-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which was the most accurate measurement of the lunar distance at the time.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Laser_ranging">
        Laser ranging
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Laser ranging">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       An experiment which measured the round-trip
       <a href="/wiki/Time_of_flight" title="Time of flight">
        time of flight
       </a>
       of laser pulses reflected directly off the surface of the Moon was performed in 1962, by a team from
       <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">
        Massachusetts Institute of Technology
       </a>
       , and a Soviet team at the
       <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Crimean Astrophysical Observatory">
        Crimean Astrophysical Observatory
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lunar_Laser_Ranging_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lunar_Laser_Ranging-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the Apollo missions in 1969, astronauts placed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroreflectors" title="Retroreflectors">
        retroreflectors
       </a>
       on the surface of the Moon for the purpose of refining the accuracy of this technique. The measurements are ongoing and involve multiple laser facilities. The instantaneous accuracy of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Laser_Ranging_experiment" title="Lunar Laser Ranging experiment">
        Lunar Laser Ranging experiments
       </a>
       can exceed sub-millimeter resolution, and is the most reliable method of determining the lunar distance, to date.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Amateur_astronomers_and_citizen_scientists">
        Amateur astronomers and citizen scientists
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_distance_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Amateur astronomers and citizen scientists">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Due to the modern accessibility to accurate timing devices, high resolution digital cameras, GPS receivers, powerful computers and near instantaneous communication, it has become possible for amateur astronomers to make high accuracy measurements of the lunar distance.
      </p>
      <p>
       On May 23, 2007 digital photographs of the Moon during a near-occultation of
       <a href="/wiki/Regulus" title="Regulus">
        Regulus
       </a>
       were taken from two locations, in Greece and England. By measuring the parallax between the moon and the chosen background star, the lunar distance was calculated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Citizen1_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Citizen1-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A more ambitious project called the "Aristarchus Campaign" was conducted during the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       of 15 April 2014.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zuluaga_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Zuluaga-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During this event, participants were invited to record a series of 5 digital photographs from moonrise through
       <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">
        culmination
       </a>
       - the point of greatest altitude. The method took advantage of the fact that the Moon is actually closest to an observer when it is at its highest point in the sky, compared to when it is on the horizon. Although it appears that the Moon is biggest when it is near the horizon, the opposite is true. This phenomenon is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_illusion" title="Moon illusion">
        moon illusion
       </a>
       . The reason for the change in distance results from the fact that the distance from the center of the Moon to the center of Earth is nearly constant throughout the night, but an observer on the surface of Earth is actually 1 Earth radius from the center of Earth. This offset brings them closest to the Moon when it is overhead.
      </p>
      <p>
       Modern cameras have now reached the resolution level capable of capturing the Moon with a precision enough to perceive and more importantly to measure this tiny variation in apparent size. The results of this experiment were calculated as LD =
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001605100000000000♠
        </span>
        60.51
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;">
         <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">
          +3.91
          <br/>
          −4.19
         </span>
        </span>
       </span>
       <var>
        R
       </var>
       <sub>
        ⊕
       </sub>
       . The accepted value for that night was 60.61, which implied a
       <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
        7000300000000000000♠
       </span>
       3% accuracy. The benefit of this method is that the only measuring equipment needed is a modern digital camera (equipped with an accurate clock, and GPS receiver).
      </p>
      <p>
       Other experimental methods of measuring the lunar distance that can be performed by amateur astronomers involve:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Taking pictures of the Moon before it enters into the penumbra and after it is completely eclipsed.
       </li>
       <li>
        Measuring, as precise as possible, the time of the eclipse contacts.
       </li>
       <li>
        Taking good pictures of the partial eclipse when the shape and size of the Earth shadow is clearly visible.
       </li>
       <li>
        Taking a picture of the Moon including in the same field of view, Spica and Mars - from various locations.
       </li>
      </ul>
