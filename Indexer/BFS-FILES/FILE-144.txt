      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Allais effect
       </b>
       refers to the alleged anomalous behavior of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pendulums" title="Pendulums">
        pendulums
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravimeters" title="Gravimeters">
        gravimeters
       </a>
       , which is sometimes purportedly observed during a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       . The effect was first reported as an anomalous
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        precession
       </a>
       of the plane of oscillation of a
       <a href="/wiki/Foucault_pendulum" title="Foucault pendulum">
        Foucault pendulum
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_June_30,_1954" title="Solar eclipse of June 30, 1954">
        solar eclipse of June 30, 1954
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Allais" title="Maurice Allais">
        Maurice Allais
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        French
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">
        polymath
       </a>
       who went on to win the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bank_of_Sweden_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences_in_Memory_of_Alfred_Nobel" title="Bank of Sweden Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel">
        Nobel Prize in Economics
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21st_CST_2010_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-21st_CST_2010-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He reported another observation of the effect during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_October_2,_1959" title="Solar eclipse of October 2, 1959">
        solar eclipse of October 2, 1959
       </a>
       using the
       <a href="/wiki/Paraconical_pendulum" title="Paraconical pendulum">
        paraconical pendulum
       </a>
       he invented.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais_1959_A_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais_1959_A-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais_1959_B_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais_1959_B-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This study earned him the 1959 Galabert Prize of the
       <a href="/wiki/Association_A%C3%A9ronautique_et_Astronautique_de_France" title="Association Aéronautique et Astronautique de France">
        French Astronautical Society
       </a>
       and made him a laureate of the US
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Research_Foundation" title="Gravity Research Foundation">
        Gravity Research Foundation
       </a>
       for his 1959 memoir on gravity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais_1959_memoir_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais_1959_memoir-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_observations">
        Experimental observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allais_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Experimental observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Maurice Allais emphasized the "dynamic character" of the observed effects:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais_NASA_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais_NASA-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         The observed effects are only seen when the pendulum is moving. They are not connected with the intensity of weight (gravimetry), but with the
         <i>
          variation of weight
         </i>
         (or of inertia)
         <i>
          in the space swept by the pendulum
         </i>
         . Actually, while the movement of the plane of oscillation of the pendulum is
         <i>
          inexplicable
         </i>
         by the theory of gravitation, the deviations from the vertical are
         <i>
          explained perfectly
         </i>
         by that theory. The deviations from the vertical […] correspond to a
         <i>
          static
         </i>
         phenomenon, while my experiments correspond to a
         <i>
          dynamic
         </i>
         phenomenon.
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Besides Allais's own experiments, related research about a possible effect of the Moon's shielding, absorption or bending of the Sun's gravitational field have been conducted by various scientists. Some observations gave positive results and some failed to detect any noticeable effect.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Anomalous_observations">
        Anomalous observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allais_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Anomalous observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Romanian physicist Gheorghe Jeverdan
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       observed the Allais effect and the so-called
       <i>
        Jeverdan-Rusu-Antonescu effect
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Jeverdan effect
       </i>
       , i.e. the change of oscillation period of a pendulum during an eclipse, monitoring a Foucault pendulum during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_February_15,_1961" title="Solar eclipse of February 15, 1961">
        solar eclipse of February 15, 1961
       </a>
       . The authors made two hypotheses regarding their observation: during an eclipse, the Moon exerts a screening effect on the
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravitational attraction
       </a>
       of the Sun so that the attraction of the Earth is indirectly increased, a phenomenon that could also be studied with
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        tides
       </a>
       . If the hypothesis of the screening effect is wrong, another explanation could be that the variation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth" title="Gravity of Earth">
        gravity of Earth
       </a>
       might be considered as a result of the
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">
        diffraction
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">
        gravitational waves
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeverdan_1961_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jeverdan_1961-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeverdan_1981_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jeverdan_1981-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Saxl" title="Erwin Saxl">
        Erwin Saxl
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mildred_Allen" title="Mildred Allen">
        Mildred Allen
       </a>
       reported strong anomalous changes in the period of a
       <a href="/wiki/Torsion_spring#Torsion_balance" title="Torsion spring">
        torsion pendulum
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_March_7,_1970" title="Solar eclipse of March 7, 1970">
        solar eclipse of March 7, 1970
       </a>
       and concluded that "gravitational theory needs to be modified".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saxl-Allen_1971_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Saxl-Allen_1971-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dr Leonid Savrov of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sternberg_Astronomical_Institute" title="Sternberg Astronomical Institute">
        Sternberg Astronomical Institute
       </a>
       built a dedicated paraconical pendulum to test the Allais effect during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_July_11,_1991" title="Solar eclipse of July 11, 1991">
        solar eclipse of July 11, 1991
       </a>
       in Mexico and the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_November_3,_1994" title="Solar eclipse of November 3, 1994">
        eclipse of November 3, 1994
       </a>
       in Brazil. While he could not observe Allais's claim that there is a diurnal periodicity in the motion of a paraconical pendulum, he however wrote: "The most interesting result of the Mexico and Brazil experiments is the increase of rotational velocity of the pendulum oscillation plane in the direction of the Foucault effect during the eclipse. It seems that we have some kind of special effect."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savrov_1995_A_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Savrov_1995_A-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savrov_1995_B_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Savrov_1995_B-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savrov_2009_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Savrov_2009-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savrov_1997_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Savrov_1997-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Various other experiments using
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_clock" title="Atomic clock">
        atomic clocks
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gravimeter" title="Gravimeter">
        gravimeters
       </a>
       instead of pendulums also recorded significant anomalous gravitational effects which can neither be caused by a tidal effect or drift of the gravimeters, nor by high frequency noise which have special patterns. These experiments were set up by different teams during solar eclipses in
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_December_24,_1992" title="Solar eclipse of December 24, 1992">
        China, 1992
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_October_24,_1995" title="Solar eclipse of October 24, 1995">
        India, 1995
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_March_9,_1997" title="Solar eclipse of March 9, 1997">
        China, 1997
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yang_2000_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Yang_2000-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dutch physicist Chris Duif, who surveys the field of gravitational anomalies in general, concludes that the question remains open because Allais observations do not satisfy conventional explanations, and that such investigations should be pursued, in view of their relatively inexpensive nature and the enormous implications if genuine anomalies are actually confirmed, but the article was self-published and has not undergone any
       <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">
        peer review
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duif_2004_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Duif_2004-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Result confirming observation of the Allais and Jeverdan-Rusu-Antonescu effects during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse#Types" title="Solar eclipse">
        annular solar eclipse
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_September_22,_2006" title="Solar eclipse of September 22, 2006">
        September 22, 2006
       </a>
       has been presented the year after by a Romanian team, with a quantization of the behavior of the paraconical pendulum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Popescu_2007_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Popescu_2007-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_August_1,_2008" title="Solar eclipse of August 1, 2008">
        solar eclipse of August 1, 2008
       </a>
       , a Ukrainian team and two Romanian teams worked together hundreds of kilometers apart with different apparatuses: five independent miniature torsion balances for the Ukrainian team, two independent short ball-borne pendulums for a Romanian team and a long Foucault pendulum for the third team. All three teams detected unexplained and mutually correlated disturbances.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pugach-Olenici_2010_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pugach-Olenici_2010-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Same teams repeated a dual experiment during the annular
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_January_26,_2009" title="Solar eclipse of January 26, 2009">
        solar eclipse of January 26, 2009
       </a>
       , this time outside of the shadow zone, with the same significant correlation between the behavior of light torsion balances and a Foucault pendulum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pugach-Olenici_2012_A_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pugach-Olenici_2012_A-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They also registered similar anomalies using a Foucault pendulum and a very light torsion balance, both located underground in a disused salt mine with minimal interference, during the partial
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_June_1,_2011" title="Solar eclipse of June 1, 2011">
        solar eclipse of June 1, 2011
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Inconclusive_or_negative_experiments">
        Inconclusive or negative experiments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allais_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inconclusive or negative experiments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_B._Slichter" title="Louis B. Slichter">
        Louis B. Slichter
       </a>
       , using a gravimeter during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_February_15,_1961" title="Solar eclipse of February 15, 1961">
        solar eclipse of February 15, 1961
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">
        Florence
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       , failed to detect an associated gravitational signal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slichter_1961_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Slichter_1961-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_July_22,_1990" title="Solar eclipse of July 22, 1990">
        solar eclipse of July 22, 1990
       </a>
       , no anomalous period increase of a torsion pendulum during the eclipse have been detected independently by a team in
       <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">
        Finland
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuusela_1991_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kuusela_1991-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and another in
       <a href="/wiki/Belomorsk" title="Belomorsk">
        Belomorsk
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        USSR
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jun_1991_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jun_1991-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_August_11,_1999" title="Solar eclipse of August 11, 1999">
        solar eclipse of August 11, 1999
       </a>
       had been a good opportunity to solve a 45-year mystery, thanks to an international collaboration.
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">
        Marshall Space Flight Center
       </a>
       first inquired about experimental protocols to Maurice Allais,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais_NASA_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais_NASA-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in order to coordinate ahead of the event a worldwide effort to test the Allais effect, between observatories and universities over seven countries (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US" title="US">
        United States
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
        Austria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       and four sites in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">
        United Arab Emirates
       </a>
       ). The lead supervisor then stated:
       <i>
        "The initial interpretation of the record points to three possibilities: a systematic error, a local effect, or the unexplored. To eliminate the first two possibilities, we and several other observers will use different kinds of measuring instruments in a distributed global network of observing stations."
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1999_A_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA_1999_A-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1999_B_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA_1999_B-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, after the eclipse, Allais criticized the experiments in his final NASA report, writing the period of observation was
       <i>
        "much too short […] to detect anomalies properly"
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais_NASA_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais_NASA-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moreover, the lead supervisor left NASA shortly thereafter with the gathered data and the NASA study has never been published.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Further observations of the team lead by
       <a href="/wiki/Xin-She_Yang" title="Xin-She Yang">
        Xin-She Yang
       </a>
       appear to have yielded much weaker evidence of anomalies than their first 1997 study. The authors first posited a more conventional explanation based on temperature changes causing ground tilting, but they suggested that this explanation was unlikely.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yang_2002_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Yang_2002-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A possible but yet controversial explanation has been finally proposed by the same author and
       <a href="/wiki/Tom_Van_Flandern" title="Tom Van Flandern">
        Tom Van Flandern
       </a>
       , who conjecture the anomaly is due to the gravitational effect of an increased air density spot in the upper atmosphere created by cooling winds during the solar eclipse. They conclude there have been "no unambiguous detections [of an Allais effect] within the past 30 years when consciousness of the importance of [experimental] controls was more widespread." They point out that "the gravitation anomaly discussed here is about a factor of 100,000 too small to explain the Allais excess pendulum precession […] during eclipses" and from this conclude that the original Allais anomaly was merely due to poor controls.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Eight gravimeters and two pendulums were deployed across six monitoring sites in China for the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_July_22,_2009" title="Solar eclipse of July 22, 2009">
        solar eclipse of July 22, 2009
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewScientist_2009_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NewScientist_2009-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although one of the scientists involved described in an interview to have observed the Allais effect,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Atlantic_2009_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_Atlantic_2009-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       no result has been published in any
       <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">
        academic journal
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       An automated Foucault pendulum was used during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_July_11,_2010" title="Solar eclipse of July 11, 2010">
        solar eclipse of July 11, 2010
       </a>
       in Argentina, with no evidence of a precession change of the pendulum's oscillation plane (&lt; 0.3 degree per hour).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salva_2011_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Salva_2011-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aether_hypothesis">
        Aether hypothesis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allais_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aether hypothesis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">
        Luminiferous aether
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Aether_drag_hypothesis" title="Aether drag hypothesis">
        Aether drag hypothesis
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Maurice Allais states that the eclipse effect is related to a gravitational anomaly, that is inexplicable in the framework of the currently admitted
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        theory of gravitation
       </a>
       , without giving any explanation of his own.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais's_book_1997_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais's_book_1997-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Allais's explanation for another anomaly (the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">
        lunisolar
       </a>
       periodicity in variations of the
       <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">
        azimuth
       </a>
       of a pendulum) is that space evinces certain
       <a href="/wiki/Anisotropy" title="Anisotropy">
        anisotropic
       </a>
       characteristics, which he ascribes to motion through an
       <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">
        aether
       </a>
       which is partially entrained by planetary bodies.
      </p>
      <p>
       His hypothesis leads to a
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
        speed of light
       </a>
       dependent on the moving direction with respect to a terrestrial observer, since the Earth moves within the aether but the rotation of the Moon induces a "wind" of about 8 km/s. Thus Allais rejects
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Einstein
       </a>
       's interpretation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment" title="Michelson–Morley experiment">
        Michelson–Morley experiment
       </a>
       and the subsequent verification experiments of
       <a href="/wiki/Dayton_Miller" title="Dayton Miller">
        Dayton Miller
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Miller-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais_1998_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais_1998-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In particular, the Michelson–Morley experiment did not give a zero speed difference, but at most 8 km/s, without being able to detect any regularity. This difference was therefore interpreted as due to measurement uncertainties. Similarly, Miller's experiments corroborated these results over a long period of time, but Miller could not explain the source of the irregularities. At the time, temperature problems were invoked to explain the cause, as concluded by
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_S._Shankland" title="Robert S. Shankland">
        Robert S. Shankland
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shankland_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Shankland-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By re-analyzing the data from this experiment, Allais reported a
       <a href="/wiki/Periodic_function" title="Periodic function">
        periodicity
       </a>
       using
       <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_time" title="Sidereal time">
        sidereal time
       </a>
       rather than
       <a href="/wiki/Civil_time" title="Civil time">
        civil time
       </a>
       used by Miller (daytime sidereal variation of the speed of light over a period of 23 hours 56 minutes with an amplitude of about 8 km/s).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller_reanalyzed_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Miller_reanalyzed-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Applying
       <a href="/wiki/Titius%E2%80%93Bode_law" title="Titius–Bode law">
        Titius–Bode law
       </a>
       to the Earth-Moon system, which he generalizes to aether, Allais calculates a "wind" of 7.95 km/s, which is comparable to the values found by the experiments of Michelson and Miller. Hence Allais deduces that the aether turns with the stars as proposed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Aether_drag_hypothesis" title="Aether drag hypothesis">
        aether drag hypothesis
       </a>
       , and is not fixed as
       <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz" title="Hendrik Lorentz">
        Lorentz
       </a>
       thought when inventing his famous
       <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_transformation" title="Lorentz transformation">
        transformation
       </a>
       and his
       <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_ether_theory" title="Lorentz ether theory">
        ether theory
       </a>
       . But the majority of scientists of the end of the nineteenth century imagining that such an aether crossed the Earth through, so that the rotation of the Earth around the Sun would cause an important variation of 30 km/s. Consequently, since the third postulate on which is based
       <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
        special relativity
       </a>
       is the constancy of the speed of light in a vacuum, Allais considers it as unfounded. In order to measure a change in the speed of light, one would have to get back to the definition of the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_metre#Krypton_standard" title="History of the metre">
        1960 meter
       </a>
       , since the confidence in the
       <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">
        theory of relativity
       </a>
       nowadays is such that current
       <a href="/wiki/Metrology" title="Metrology">
        metrology
       </a>
       uses constancy of the speed of light as an
       <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">
        axiom
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Allais summarized his experimental work in English in his 1999 memoir on behalf of NASA.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais_NASA_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais_NASA-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He detailed his aether hypothesis in the books
       <i>
        L'Anisotropie de l'Espace
       </i>
       published in 1997,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais's_book_1997_31-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais's_book_1997-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        L'Effondrement de la Théorie de la Relativité
       </i>
       published in 2004.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allais's_book_2004_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allais's_book_2004-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A book on Allais's scientific legacy has been edited in English in 2011,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Book_2011_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Book_2011-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       yet his aether hypothesis has not gained significant traction amongst mainstream scientists. Nevertheless, after Allais's death in 2010, experiments on the Allais effect carry on.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deloly_2016_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Deloly_2016-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
