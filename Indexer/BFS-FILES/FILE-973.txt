   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Limb darkening
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:258px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2012_Transit_of_Venus_from_SF.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2577" data-file-width="2577" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/2012_Transit_of_Venus_from_SF.jpg/256px-2012_Transit_of_Venus_from_SF.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/2012_Transit_of_Venus_from_SF.jpg/384px-2012_Transit_of_Venus_from_SF.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/2012_Transit_of_Venus_from_SF.jpg/512px-2012_Transit_of_Venus_from_SF.jpg 2x" width="256"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2012_Transit_of_Venus_from_SF.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_filter" title="Solar filter">
          filtered
         </a>
         image of the Sun in visible light, showing the limb-darkening effect as a dimmer luminosity towards the edge or limb of the solar disk. The image was taken during the 2012
         <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">
          transit of Venus
         </a>
         (seen here as the dark spot at the upper right).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Limb darkening
       </b>
       is an optical effect seen in stars (including the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       ), where the center part of the disk appears brighter than the edge or
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/limb#Etymology_2" title="wikt:limb">
         limb
        </a>
       </i>
       of the image. Its understanding offered early solar astronomers an opportunity to construct models with such gradients. This encouraged the development of the theory of
       <a href="/wiki/Radiative_transfer" title="Radiative transfer">
        radiative transfer
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_theory">
        Basic theory
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Limb_darkening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic theory">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:258px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Limb_darkening_layers.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="642" data-file-width="607" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Limb_darkening_layers.svg/256px-Limb_darkening_layers.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Limb_darkening_layers.svg/384px-Limb_darkening_layers.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Limb_darkening_layers.svg/512px-Limb_darkening_layers.svg.png 2x" width="256"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Limb_darkening_layers.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         An idealized case of limb darkening. The outer boundary is the radius at which photons emitted from the star are no longer absorbed.
         <i>
          L
         </i>
         is a distance for which the optical depth is unity. High-temperature photons emitted at A will just barely escape from the star, as will the low-temperature photons emitted at B. Note that for a typical star, this drawing is not to scale. E.g., for the
         <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
          Sun
         </a>
         ,
         <i>
          L
         </i>
         would be only a few hundred km.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Crucial to understanding limb darkening is the idea of
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">
        optical depth
       </a>
       . A distance equal to one optical depth is the thickness of the absorbing gas from which a fraction of 1/
       <a href="/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant)" title="E (mathematical constant)">
        e
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">
        photons
       </a>
       can escape. This is what defines the visible edge of a star, since it is at a few optical depths that the star becomes opaque. The radiation reaching us is closely approximated by the sum of all the emission along the entire line of sight, up to that point where the optical depth is unity. In particular, if the intensity of radiation in the star varies linearly with optical depth, then the radiation reaching us will be of the intensity at an optical depth of unity. When we look near the edge of a star, we cannot "see" to the same depth as when we look at the center because the line of sight must travel at an oblique angle through the stellar gas when looking near the limb. In other words, the star radius at which we see the optical depth as being unity increases as we move our line of sight towards the limb.
      </p>
      <p>
       The second effect is the fact that the
       <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">
        effective temperature
       </a>
       of the stellar atmosphere is (usually) decreasing for an increasing distance from the center of the star. The radiation emitted from a gas is a strong function of temperature. For a
       <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">
        black body
       </a>
       , for example, the spectrally integrated intensity is proportional to the fourth power of the temperature (
       <a href="/wiki/Stefan%E2%80%93Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan–Boltzmann law">
        Stefan–Boltzmann law
       </a>
       ). Since when we look at a star, at first approximation, the radiation comes from the point at which the optical depth is unity, and that point is deeper in when looking at the center, the temperature will be higher, and the intensity will be greater, than when we look at the limb.
      </p>
      <p>
       In fact, the temperature in the atmosphere of a star does not always decrease with increasing height, and for certain
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
        spectral lines
       </a>
       , the optical depth is unity in a region of increasing temperature. In this case we see the phenomenon of "limb brightening"; for the Sun the existence of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_atmosphere" title="Solar atmosphere">
        temperature minimum
       </a>
       region means that limb brightening should start to dominate at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far-infrared" title="Far-infrared">
        far-infrared
       </a>
       or radio wavelengths. Outside the lower atmosphere, and well above the temperature-minimum region, we find the million-
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        kelvin
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">
        solar corona
       </a>
       . For most wavelengths this region is optically thin, i.e. has small
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">
        optical depth
       </a>
       , and must therefore be limb-brightened if spherically symmetric.
      </p>
      <p>
       Further complication comes from the existence of rough (three-dimensional) structure. The classical analysis of stellar limb darkening, as described below, assumes the existence of a smooth hydrostatic equilibrium, and at some level of precision this assumption must fail (most obviously in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">
        sunspots
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faculae" title="Faculae">
        faculae
       </a>
       , but generally everywhere). Instead, the boundary between the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">
        corona
       </a>
       consists of a very complicated
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_transition_region" title="Solar transition region">
        transition region
       </a>
       , best observed at ultraviolet wavelengths only detectable from
       <a href="/wiki/Interface_Region_Imaging_Spectrograph" title="Interface Region Imaging Spectrograph">
        space
       </a>
       .
      </p>
