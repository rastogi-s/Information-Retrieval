   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Coronal hole
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Coronal holes
       </b>
       are areas where the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       is colder, hence darker, and has lower-density
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       than average because there is lower energy and gas levels. Coronal holes are part of the Sun's corona and are constantly changing and reshaping because the corona is not uniform.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freedman,_Roger_A._2008_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freedman,_Roger_A._2008-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Sun contains
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_fields" title="Magnetic fields">
        magnetic fields
       </a>
       that arch away from areas in the corona that are very thin due to the lower levels of energy and gas,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freedman,_Roger_A._2008_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freedman,_Roger_A._2008-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which cause coronal holes to appear when they do not fall back. Thus, solar particles escape at a rate great enough to create a lower density and lower temperature in that area.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-r1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="First_discoveries">
        First discoveries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: First discoveries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the 1960s, the Sydney Chris Cross radio telescope took x-ray images, although it was unclear what they were. Some research also points out that coronal holes were first spotted in the early 1970s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-r1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r2_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-r2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These were found when
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-ray
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_telescope" title="X-ray telescope">
        telescopes
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">
        Skylab
       </a>
       mission were flown above the Earth's atmosphere to reveal the structure of the corona.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_cycle">
        Solar cycle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Solar cycle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Coronal hole size and population correspond with the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r2_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-r2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As the sun heads toward solar maximum the coronal holes move closer and closer to the sun's poles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r2_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-r2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During solar maximum, the number of coronal holes decreases until the magnetic fields on the sun reverse. Afterwards new coronal holes appear near the new poles. The coronal holes then increase in size and number, extending farther from the poles as the sun moves toward solar minimum again.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r3_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-r3-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are permanent coronal holes on the north and south poles of the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freedman,_Roger_A._2008_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freedman,_Roger_A._2008-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Coronal_holes_and_solar_wind">
        Coronal holes and solar wind
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coronal holes and solar wind">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Coronal holes generally discharge
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       at a speed about twice the average.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r2_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-r2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The escaping solar wind is known to travel along open magnetic field lines that pass through the coronal hole area.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r3_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-r3-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since coronal holes are regions in the sun’s corona that have much lower densities and temperatures than most of the corona, these regions are very thin. This thinness contributes to the solar wind since particles within the chromosphere can more easily break through.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_space_weather">
        Influence on space weather
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influence on space weather">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       During solar minima coronal holes are the primary sources of
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">
        space weather
       </a>
       disturbances. Typically,
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        geomagnetic
       </a>
       (and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_particle_event" title="Solar particle event">
        proton
       </a>
       ) storms originating from coronal holes have a gradual commencement (over hours) and are not as severe as storms caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       (CMEs), which usually have a sudden onset. Due to the fact coronal holes can last for several months, it is often possible to predict the recurrence of this type of disturbance significantly farther in advance than for CME related disturbances.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-r1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
