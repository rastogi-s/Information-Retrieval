      <p>
       <b>
        Lunar craters
       </b>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact craters
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . The Moon's surface has many craters, almost all of which were formed by impacts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pike1977_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pike1977-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_craters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The word
       <i>
        crater
       </i>
       was adopted by
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
        Galileo Galilei
       </a>
       from the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       word for vessel - (Κρατήρ a Greek vessel used to mix wine and water). Galileo built his first telescope in late 1609, and turned it to the Moon for the first time on November 30, 1609. He discovered that, contrary to general opinion at that time, the Moon was not a perfect sphere, but had both mountains and cup-like depressions, the latter of which he gave the name
       <i>
        craters
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion" title="Scientific opinion">
        Scientific opinion
       </a>
       as to the origin of craters swung back and forth over the ensuing centuries. The competing theories were (a) volcanic eruptions blasting holes in the Moon, (b) meteoric impact, (c) a theory known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Welteislehre" title="Welteislehre">
        Welteislehre
       </a>
       developed in Germany between the two
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wars" title="World Wars">
        World Wars
       </a>
       which suggested glacial action creating the craters.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Craters_20151103.jpg">
         <img alt="Lunar craters as captured through the backyard telescope of an amateur astronomer, partially illuminated by the sun on a waning crescent moon." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1190" data-file-width="1190" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Lunar_Craters_20151103.jpg/220px-Lunar_Craters_20151103.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Lunar_Craters_20151103.jpg/330px-Lunar_Craters_20151103.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Lunar_Craters_20151103.jpg/440px-Lunar_Craters_20151103.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Craters_20151103.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Lunar craters as captured through the backyard telescope of an amateur astronomer, partially illuminated by the sun on a waning crescent moon.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Evidence collected during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Project" title="Apollo Project">
        Apollo Project
       </a>
       and from unmanned spacecraft of the same period proved conclusively that meteoric impact, or impact by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroids" title="Asteroids">
        asteroids
       </a>
       for larger craters, was the origin of almost all lunar craters, and by implication, most craters on other bodies as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       The formation of new craters is studied in the lunar impact monitoring program at NASA.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The biggest recorded creation was caused by an impact recorded on March 17, 2013.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Visible to the naked eye, the impact is believed to be from an approximately 40 kg meteoroid striking the surface at a speed of 90,000 km/h.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_craters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because of the Moon's lack of water,
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plates" title="Tectonic plates">
        tectonic plates
       </a>
       , there is little erosion, and craters are found that exceed two billion years in age. The age of large craters is determined by the number of smaller craters contained within it, older craters generally accumulating more small, contained craters.
      </p>
      <p>
       The smallest craters found have been microscopic in size, found in rocks returned to Earth from the Moon. The largest crater called such is about 290 kilometres (181 mi) across in diameter, located near the lunar South Pole. However, it is believed that many of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       were formed by giant impacts, with the resulting depression filled by upwelling
       <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
        lava
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Craters typically will have some or all of the following features:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        a surrounding area with materials splashed out of the ground when the crater was formed; this is typically lighter in shade than older materials due to exposure to solar radiation for a lesser time
       </li>
       <li>
        raised rim, consisting of materials ejected but landing very close by
       </li>
       <li>
        crater wall, the downward-sloping portion of the crater
       </li>
       <li>
        crater floor, a more or less smooth, flat area, which as it ages accumulates small craters of its own
       </li>
       <li>
        central peak, found only in some craters with a diameter exceeding 26 kilometres (16 mi); this is generally a splash effect caused by the kinetic energy of the impacting object being turned to heat and melting some lunar material.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_crater_categorization">
        Lunar crater categorization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_craters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lunar crater categorization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1978,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chuck_Wood_(astronomer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chuck Wood (astronomer) (page does not exist)">
        Chuck Wood
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Leif_Erland_Andersson" title="Leif Erland Andersson">
        Leif Andersson
       </a>
       of the Lunar &amp; Planetary Lab devised a system of categorization of lunar impact craters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood_1978_fresh_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wood_1978_fresh-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They used a sampling of craters that were relatively unmodified by subsequent impacts, then grouped the results into five broad categories. These successfully accounted for about 99% of all lunar impact craters.
      </p>
      <p>
       The LPC Crater Types were as follows:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ALC
        </i>
        — small, cup-shaped craters with a diameter of about 10 km or less, and no central floor. The
        <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">
         archetype
        </a>
        for this category is
        <a href="/wiki/Albategnius_(crater)" title="Albategnius (crater)">
         Albategnius C
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         BIO
        </i>
        — similar to an ALC, but with small, flat floors. Typical diameter is about 15 km. The lunar crater archetype is
        <a href="/wiki/Biot_(crater)" title="Biot (crater)">
         Biot
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         SOS
        </i>
        — the interior floor is wide and flat, with no central peak. The inner walls are not
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/terrace" title="wiktionary:terrace">
         terraced
        </a>
        . The diameter is normally in the range of 15–25 km. The archetype is
        <a href="/wiki/Sosigenes_(crater)" title="Sosigenes (crater)">
         Sosigenes
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         TRI
        </i>
        — these complex craters are large enough so that their inner walls have slumped to the floor. They can range in size from 15–50 km in diameter. The archetype crater is
        <a href="/wiki/Triesnecker_(crater)" title="Triesnecker (crater)">
         Triesnecker
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         TYC
        </i>
        — these are larger than 50 km, with
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/terrace" title="wiktionary:terrace">
         terraced
        </a>
        inner walls and relatively flat floors. They frequently have large central peak formations.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tycho_(crater)" title="Tycho (crater)">
         Tycho
        </a>
        is the archetype for this class.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Beyond a couple of hundred kilometers diameter, the central peak of the TYC class disappear and they are classed as
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        basins
       </a>
       . Large craters, similar in size to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_maria" title="Lunar maria">
        maria
       </a>
       , but without (or with small amount of) dark
       <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
        lava
       </a>
       filling, are sometimes called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thalassoid" title="Thalassoid">
        thalassoids
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [A]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossary_2005_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-glossary_2005-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mosher_1970_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mosher_1970-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Beginning in 2009 Dr. Nadine Barlow of Northern Arizona University began to convert the Wood and Andersson lunar impact-crater database into digital format.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Dr. Barlow is also creating a new lunar impact crater database similar to Wood and Andersson's, except hers will include all impact craters greater than or equal to five kilometers in diameter and is based on
       <a href="/wiki/Clementine_(spacecraft)" title="Clementine (spacecraft)">
        Clementine (spacecraft)
       </a>
       images of the lunar surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        moon zoo
       </i>
       project within the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zooniverse_(citizen_science_project)" title="Zooniverse (citizen science project)">
        Zooniverse
       </a>
       program aimed to use
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizen_scientist" title="Citizen scientist">
        citizen scientists
       </a>
       to map the size and shape of as many craters as possible using data from the NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       . However, it has since been retired.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Names">
        Names
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_craters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Names">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Craters constitute 95% of all
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nomenclature" title="Planetary nomenclature">
        named
       </a>
       lunar features.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazetteer_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gazetteer-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Usually they are named after deceased scientists and other explorers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazetteer_categories_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gazetteer_categories-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This tradition comes from
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Riccioli" title="Giovanni Battista Riccioli">
        Giovanni Battista Riccioli
       </a>
       , who started it in 1651.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greeley_1990_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Greeley_1990-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since 1919, assignment of these names is regulated by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greeley_1990_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Greeley_1990-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Small craters of special interest (for example, visited by lunar missions) receive human first names (Robert, José, Louise etc.). One of the biggest lunar craters,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_(crater)" title="Apollo (crater)">
        Apollo
       </a>
       , is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo missions
       </a>
       . Many smaller craters inside and near it bear the names of deceased American astronauts, and many craters inside and near
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Moscoviense" title="Mare Moscoviense">
        Mare Moscoviense
       </a>
       bear the names of deceased Soviet cosmonauts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazetteer_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-gazetteer-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazetteer_categories_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-gazetteer_categories-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Besides this, in 1970 twelve craters were named after twelve living astronauts (6 Soviet and 6 American).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazetteer_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-gazetteer-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The majority of named lunar craters are
       <i>
        satellite craters
       </i>
       : their names consist of the name of a nearby named crater and a capital letter (for example,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/Copernicus_(lunar_crater)" title="Copernicus (lunar crater)">
         Copernicus
        </a>
        A
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        Copernicus B
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        Copernicus C
       </span>
       and so on).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazetteer_12-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-gazetteer-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Crater_chain" title="Crater chain">
        crater chains
       </a>
       are usually named after a nearby crater. Their Latin names contain the word
       <i>
        Catena
       </i>
       ("chain"). For example,
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catena_Davy" title="Catena Davy">
         Catena Davy
        </a>
       </i>
       is situated near the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Davy_(crater)" title="Davy (crater)">
        Davy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazetteer_12-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-gazetteer-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazetteer_terms_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gazetteer_terms-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

         <li id="cite_note-7">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-7">

           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           This term was coined by Soviet explorers of the Moon after beginning of exploration of
           <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
            lunar farside
           </a>
           . Later, in 1967, on XIII General Assembly of the
           <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
            International Astronomical Union
           </a>
           this word was proposed to be included into the list of generic terms of
           <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nomenclature" title="Planetary nomenclature">
            nomenclature
           </a>
           of lunar surface features, but this proposal was declined. So, this term remains only a characterization of the features, but not a part of their names.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proceedings_1967_6-0">
            <a href="#cite_note-Proceedings_1967-6">
