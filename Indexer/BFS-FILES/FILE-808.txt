      <p>
       A
       <b>
        week
       </b>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_unit" title="Time unit">
        time unit
       </a>
       equal to seven
       <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">
        days
       </a>
       . It is the standard time period used for cycles of rest days in most parts of the world, mostly alongside—although not strictly part of—the
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian calendar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The days of the week
       <a href="/wiki/Names_of_the_days_of_the_week" title="Names of the days of the week">
        were named
       </a>
       after the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_planets" title="Classical planets">
        classical planets
       </a>
       (derived from the astrological system of
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_hours" title="Planetary hours">
        planetary hours
       </a>
       ) in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_era" title="Roman era">
        Roman era
       </a>
       . In English, the names are
       <a href="/wiki/Monday" title="Monday">
        Monday
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tuesday" title="Tuesday">
        Tuesday
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Wednesday" title="Wednesday">
        Wednesday
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thursday" title="Thursday">
        Thursday
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Friday" title="Friday">
        Friday
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Saturday" title="Saturday">
        Saturday
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sunday" title="Sunday">
        Sunday
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_8601" title="ISO 8601">
        ISO 8601
       </a>
       includes the
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_week_date" title="ISO week date">
        ISO week date
       </a>
       system, a numbering system for weeks within a given year – each week begins on a Monday and is associated with the year that contains that week's Thursday (so that if a year starts in a long weekend Friday–Sunday, week number one of the year will start after that). ISO 8601 assigns numbers to the days of the week, running from 1 to 7 for Monday through to Sunday.
      </p>
      <p>
       The term "week" is sometimes expanded to refer to other time units comprising a few days, such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nundinal_cycle" title="Nundinal cycle">
        nundinal cycle
       </a>
       of the ancient Roman calendar or the "work week" or "school week" referring only to the days spent on those activities.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The English word
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/week" title="wikt:week">
         week
        </a>
       </i>
       comes from the
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       <i>
        wice
       </i>
       , ultimately from a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_Germanic" title="Common Germanic">
        Common Germanic
       </a>
       <i>
        *
       </i>
       <span lang="gem" xml:lang="gem">
        wikōn-
       </span>
       , from a root
       <i>
        *
       </i>
       <span lang="gem" xml:lang="gem">
        wik-
       </span>
       "turn, move, change". The Germanic word probably had a wider meaning prior to the adoption of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_calendar" title="Roman calendar">
        Roman calendar
       </a>
       , perhaps "succession series", as suggested by
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">
        Gothic
       </a>
       <i>
        wikō
       </i>
       translating
       <i>
        taxis
       </i>
       "order" in
       <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">
        Luke
       </a>
       1:8.
      </p>
      <p>
       The seven-day week is named in many languages by a word derived from "seven". The
       <a href="/wiki/Archaism" title="Archaism">
        archaism
       </a>
       <b>
        sennight
       </b>
       ("seven-night") preserves the old Germanic practice of reckoning time by nights, as in the more common
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Fortnight" title="Fortnight">
         fortnight
        </a>
       </i>
       ("fourteen-night").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldwidewords_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-worldwidewords-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <b>
        Hebdomad
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        hebdomadal week
       </b>
       both derive from the
       <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">
        Greek
       </a>
       <i>
        hebdomás
       </i>
       (
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%91%CE%B2%CE%B4%CE%BF%CE%BC%CE%AC%CF%82" title="wikt:ἑβδομάς">
         ἑβδομάς
        </a>
       </span>
       , "a seven"). The obsolete
       <b>
        septimane
       </b>
       is cognate with the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance
       </a>
       terms derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/septimana" title="wikt:septimana">
         septimana
        </a>
       </i>
       ("a seven").
      </p>
      <p>
       Slavic has a formation
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Slavic/t%D1%8Ad%D1%8Cn%D1%8C" title="wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Slavic/tъdьnь">
         *tъ(žь)dьnь
        </a>
       </i>
       (Serbo-Croatian тједан, Ukrainian тиждень, Czech
       <i>
        týden
       </i>
       , Polish
       <i>
        tydzień
       </i>
       ), from
       <i>
        *tъ
       </i>
       "this" +
       <i>
        *dьnь
       </i>
       "day", in some cases alongside
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Slavic/ned%C4%9Blja" title="wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Slavic/nedělja">
         nedělja
        </a>
       </i>
       (OCS нєдѣлꙗ), a loan-translation of Latin
       <i>
        feria
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        sedmitsa
       </i>
       (седмица), as ἑβδομάς derived from "seven". Chinese has
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%98%9F%E6%9C%9F" title="wikt:星期">
        星期
       </a>
       , as it were "
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%98%9F" title="wikt:星">
        planetary
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9C%9F" title="wikt:期">
        time unit
       </a>
       ".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_duration">
        Definition and duration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition and duration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A week is defined as an interval of exactly seven
       <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">
        days
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       so that technically, except at
       <a href="/wiki/Daylight_saving_time" title="Daylight saving time">
        daylight saving time
       </a>
       transitions or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leap_seconds" title="Leap seconds">
        leap seconds
       </a>
       ,
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        1 week = 7 days = 168 hours = 10,080 minutes = 604,800 seconds.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       With respect to the
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian calendar
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        1 Gregorian calendar year = 52 weeks + 1 day (2 days in a
        <a href="/wiki/Leap_year" title="Leap year">
         leap year
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        1 week = ​
        <span class="frac nowrap">
         <sup>
          1600
         </sup>
         ⁄
         <sub>
          6957
         </sub>
        </span>
        ≈ 22.9984% of an average Gregorian month
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In a
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian
       </a>
       mean year, there are 365.2425 days, and thus exactly ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        52
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         71
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         400
        </sub>
       </span>
       or 52.1775 weeks (unlike the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_year_(calendar)" title="Julian year (calendar)">
        Julian year
       </a>
       of 365.25 days or ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        52
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         5
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         28
        </sub>
       </span>
       ≈ 52.1786 weeks, which cannot be represented by a finite decimal expansion). There are exactly 20,871 weeks in 400 Gregorian years, so 8 March 1618 was a Thursday just like 8 March 2018.
      </p>
      <p>
       Relative to the path of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , a week is 23.659% of an average
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">
        lunation
       </a>
       , or 94.637% of an average quarter lunation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Historically, the system of
       <a href="/wiki/Dominical_letter" title="Dominical letter">
        Dominical letters
       </a>
       (letters A to G identifying the weekday of the first day of a given year) has been used to facilitate calculation of the day of week. The day of the week can be easily calculated given a date's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_day_number" title="Julian day number">
        Julian day number
       </a>
       (JD, i.e. the integer value at
       <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">
        noon
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">
        UT
       </a>
       ): Adding one to the
       <a href="/wiki/Remainder" title="Remainder">
        remainder
       </a>
       after dividing the Julian day number by seven (JD
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Modulo_operation" title="Modulo operation">
         modulo
        </a>
       </i>
       7 + 1) yields that date's
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_8601" title="ISO 8601">
        ISO 8601
       </a>
       day of the week (for example, the Julian day number of 8 March 2018 is 2458186. Calculating (2458186 mod 7 + 1) yields 4, corresponding to Thursday.).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Days_of_the_week">
        Days of the week
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Days of the week">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Names_of_the_days_of_the_week" title="Names of the days of the week">
        Names of the days of the week
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Weekday_heptagram.ant.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="400" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Weekday_heptagram.ant.png/220px-Weekday_heptagram.ant.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Weekday_heptagram.ant.png/330px-Weekday_heptagram.ant.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/Weekday_heptagram.ant.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Weekday_heptagram.ant.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Schematic comparison of the ordering of the classical planets (arranged in a circle) and the sequence of days in the week (forming a {7/3}
         <a href="/wiki/Heptagram" title="Heptagram">
          heptagram
         </a>
         within the circle).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The days of the week were originally named for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_planets" title="Classical planets">
        classical planets
       </a>
       . This naming system persisted alongside an "ecclesiastical" tradition of numbering the days, in
       <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_Latin" title="Ecclesiastical Latin">
        ecclesiastical Latin
       </a>
       beginning with
       <i>
        dominica
       </i>
       (the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Day_of_the_Lord" title="Day of the Lord">
        Day of the Lord
       </a>
       ) as the first day. The Greco-Roman gods associated with the classical planets were rendered in their
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpretatio_germanica" title="Interpretatio germanica">
         interpretatio germanica
        </a>
       </i>
       at some point during the late Roman Empire, yielding the Germanic tradition of names based on indigenous deities.
      </p>
      <p>
       The ordering of the weekday names is not the classical order of the planets (by distance in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_sphere" title="Planetary sphere">
        planetary spheres
       </a>
       model, or, equivalently, by their apparent speed of movement in the night sky). Instead, the
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_hours" title="Planetary hours">
        planetary hours
       </a>
       systems resulted in succeeding days being named for planets that are three places apart in their traditional listing. This characteristic was apparently discussed in
       <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">
        Plutarch
       </a>
       in a treatise written in c. AD 100, which is reported to have addressed the question of
       <i>
        Why are the days named after the planets reckoned in a different order from the actual order?
       </i>
       (the text of Plutarch's treatise has been lost).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       An ecclesiastical, non-astrological, system of numbering the days of the week was adopted in Late Antiquity. This model also seems to have influenced (presumably via
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Christianity" title="Gothic Christianity">
        Gothic
       </a>
       ) the designation of Wednesday as "mid-week" in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">
        Old High German
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        mittawehha
       </i>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Church_Slavonic" title="Old Church Slavonic">
        Old Church Slavonic
       </a>
       (срѣда). Old Church Slavonic may have also modeled the name of Monday, понєдѣльникъ, after the Latin
       <i>
        feria secunda
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The ecclesiastical system became prevalent in
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">
        Eastern Christianity
       </a>
       , but in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_Christianity" title="Latin Christianity">
        Latin West
       </a>
       it remains extant only in modern
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
        Icelandic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A continuous seven-day cycle that runs throughout history paying no attention whatsoever to the phases of the moon was probably first practiced in
       <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">
        Judaism
       </a>
       , dated to the 6th century BC at the latest.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zerubavel14_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Zerubavel14-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are several hypotheses concerning the origin of the
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">
        biblical
       </a>
       seven-day cycle.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Delitzsch" title="Friedrich Delitzsch">
        Friedrich Delitzsch
       </a>
       and others suggested that the seven-day week being approximately a quarter of a lunation is the implicit astronomical origin of the seven-day week
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LSC_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LSC-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , and indeed the
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_calendar" title="Babylonian calendar">
        Babylonian calendar
       </a>
       used intercalary days to synchronize the last week of a month with the new moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to this theory, the Jewish week was adopted from the Babylonians while removing the moon-dependency.
      </p>
      <p>
       However,
       <a href="/wiki/Niels-Erik_Andreasen" title="Niels-Erik Andreasen">
        Niels-Erik Andreasen
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_H._Tigay" title="Jeffrey H. Tigay">
        Jeffrey H. Tigay
       </a>
       and others claimed that the
       <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Sabbath" title="Biblical Sabbath">
        sabbath
       </a>
       is mentioned as a day of rest in some of the earliest layers of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentateuch" title="Pentateuch">
        Pentateuch
       </a>
       dated to the 9th century BC at the latest, centuries before
       <a href="/wiki/Judea" title="Judea">
        Judea
       </a>
       's Babylonian exile. They also find the resemblance between the biblical Sabbath and the Babylonian system weak. Therefore they suggested that the seven-day week may reflect an independent
       <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">
        Israelite
       </a>
       tradition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Tigay writes:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        It is clear that among neighboring nations that were in position to have an influence over Israel - and in fact which did influence it in various matters - there is no precise parallel to the Israelite Sabbatical week. This leads to the conclusion that the Sabbatical week, which is as unique to Israel as the Sabbath from which it flows, is an independent Israelite creation.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_14-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-:1-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The seven-day week seems to have been adopted, at different stages, by the
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">
        Persian Empire
       </a>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_astrology" title="Hellenistic astrology">
        Hellenistic astrology
       </a>
       , and (via
       <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">
        Greek transmission
       </a>
       ) in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gupta_India" title="Gupta India">
        Gupta India
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_China" title="Tang China">
        Tang China
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Babylonian system was received by the Greeks in the 4th century BC (notably via
       <a href="/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus" title="Eudoxus of Cnidus">
        Eudoxus of Cnidus
       </a>
       ). However the designation of the seven days of the week to the seven planets is an innovation introduced in the time of Augustus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:2-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The astrological concept of
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_hours" title="Planetary hours">
        planetary hours
       </a>
       is rather an original innovation of Hellenistic astrology, probably first conceived in the 2nd century BC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zerubavel14_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Zerubavel14-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The seven day week was widely known throughout the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       by the 1st century AD,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:2-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       along with references to the Jewish Sabbath by Roman scholars such as
       <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">
        Seneca
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">
        Ovid
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The seven day cycle ultimately replaced the older Roman system of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nundinal_cycle" title="Nundinal cycle">
        nundinal cycle
       </a>
       , probably during the 4th century.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Near_East">
        Ancient Near East
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ancient Near East">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The earliest evidence of an astrological significance of a seven-day period is connected to
       <a href="/wiki/Gudea" title="Gudea">
        Gudea
       </a>
       , priest-king of
       <a href="/wiki/Lagash" title="Lagash">
        Lagash
       </a>
       in Sumer during the
       <a href="/wiki/Gutian_dynasty_of_Sumer" title="Gutian dynasty of Sumer">
        Gutian dynasty
       </a>
       , who built a seven-room temple, which he dedicated with a seven-day festival. In the flood story of the Assyro-Babylonian epic of
       <a href="/wiki/Gilgamesh" title="Gilgamesh">
        Gilgamesh
       </a>
       the storm lasts for seven days, the dove is sent out after seven days, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">
        Noah
       </a>
       -like character of
       <a href="/wiki/Utnapishtim" title="Utnapishtim">
        Utnapishtim
       </a>
       leaves the ark seven days after it reaches firm ground.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It seems likely that the Hebrew seven-day week is based on the Babylonian tradition, although going through certain adaptations.
       <a href="/wiki/George_Aaron_Barton" title="George Aaron Barton">
        George Aaron Barton
       </a>
       speculated that the seven-day creation account of Genesis is connected to the Babylonian creation epic,
       <a href="/wiki/En%C3%BBma_Eli%C5%A1" title="Enûma Eliš">
        Enûma Eliš
       </a>
       , which is recorded on seven tablets.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Counting from the
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       , the Babylonians celebrated the 7th, 14th, 21st and 28th as "holy-days", also called "evil days" (meaning "unsuitable" for prohibited activities). On these days, officials were prohibited from various activities and common men were forbidden to "make a wish", and at least the 28th was known as a "rest-day".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ere_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ere-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On each of them, offerings were made to a different god and goddess.
      </p>
      <p>
       In a frequently-quoted suggestion going back to the early 20th century
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2015)">
           by whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the Hebrew
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sabbath_etymology" title="Sabbath etymology">
         Sabbath
        </a>
       </i>
       is compared to the Sumerian
       <i>
        sa-bat
       </i>
       "mid-rest", a term for the
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       . The Sumerian term has been reconstructed as rendered
       <i>
        Sapattu
        <sup>
         m
        </sup>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Sabattu
        <sup>
         m
        </sup>
       </i>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_language" title="Babylonian language">
        Babylonian
       </a>
       , possibly present in the lost fifth tablet of the
       <a href="/wiki/En%C3%BBma_Eli%C5%A1" title="Enûma Eliš">
        Enûma Eliš
       </a>
       , tentatively reconstructed
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2015)">
           according to whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       "[Sa]bbath shalt thou then encounter, mid[month]ly".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ere_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ere-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Achaemenid_period">
        Achaemenid period
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Achaemenid period">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrian_calendar" title="Zoroastrian calendar">
        Zoroastrian calendar
       </a>
       follows the Babylonian in relating the seventh and other days of the month to
       <a href="/wiki/Ahura_Mazda" title="Ahura Mazda">
        Ahura Mazda
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSZ_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TSZ-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The forerunner of all modern Zoroastrian calendars is the system used to reckon dates in the
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
        Persian Empire
       </a>
       , adopted from the Babylonian calendar by the 4th century BC.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Senn" title="Frank Senn">
        Frank C. Senn
       </a>
       in his book
       <i>
        Christian Liturgy: Catholic and Evangelical
       </i>
       points to data suggesting evidence of an early continuous use of a seven-day week; referring to the Jews during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_Captivity" title="Babylonian Captivity">
        Babylonian Captivity
       </a>
       in the 6th century BC,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       after the destruction of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_of_Solomon" title="Temple of Solomon">
        Temple of Solomon
       </a>
       . While the seven-day week in Judaism is tied to
       <a href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_narrative" title="Genesis creation narrative">
        Creation account
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">
        Book of Genesis
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">
        Hebrew Bible
       </a>
       (where
       <a href="/wiki/Elohim" title="Elohim">
        God
       </a>
       creates the heavens and the earth in six days and rests on the seventh;
       <a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Genesis+1:1–2:3&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">
        Genesis 1:1–2:3
       </a>
       , in the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">
        Book of Exodus
       </a>
       , the fourth of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ten_Commandments" title="Ten Commandments">
        Ten Commandments
       </a>
       is to rest on the seventh day,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Sabbath" title="Biblical Sabbath">
         Shabbat
        </a>
       </i>
       , which can be seen as implying a socially instituted seven-day week), it is not clear whether the Genesis narrative predates the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_Captivity" title="Babylonian Captivity">
        Babylonian Captivity
       </a>
       of the Jews in the 6th century BC. At least since the
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Temple_Judaism" title="Second Temple Judaism">
        Second Temple period
       </a>
       under Persian rule, Judaism relied on the seven-day cycle of recurring
       <a href="/wiki/Sabbath" title="Sabbath">
        Sabbaths
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Tablets
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       from the Achaemenid period indicate that the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">
        lunation
       </a>
       of 29 or 30 days basically contained three seven-day weeks, and a final week of eight or nine days inclusive, breaking the continuous seven-day cycle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ere_22-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ere-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Babylonians additionally celebrated the 19th as a special "evil day", the "day of anger", because it was roughly the 49th day of the (preceding) month, completing a "week of weeks", also with sacrifices and prohibitions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ere_22-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-ere-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Difficulties with
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Delitzsch" title="Friedrich Delitzsch">
        Friedrich Delitzsch
       </a>
       's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_theory" title="Origin theory">
        origin theory
       </a>
       connecting Hebrew
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">
         Shabbat
        </a>
       </i>
       with the Babylonian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_cycle" title="Lunar cycle">
        lunar cycle
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landau_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-landau-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       include reconciling the differences between an unbroken week and a lunar week, and explaining the absence of texts naming the lunar week as
       <i>
        Shabbat
       </i>
       in any language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orr_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-orr-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_and_Roman_era">
        Hellenistic and Roman era
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hellenistic and Roman era">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Nundinae" title="Nundinae">
        Nundinae
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_Judaism" title="Hellenistic Judaism">
        Jewish sources
       </a>
       by the time of the
       <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">
        Septuagint
       </a>
       , the term "Sabbath" (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       <i>
        Sabbaton
       </i>
       ) by synecdoche also came to refer to an entire seven-day week,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the interval between two weekly Sabbaths.
       <a href="/wiki/Parables_of_Jesus" title="Parables of Jesus">
        Jesus's parable
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Pharisee_and_the_Publican" title="Pharisee and the Publican">
        Pharisee and the Publican
       </a>
       (
       <a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=luke+18%3A12" rel="nofollow">
        Luke 18:12
       </a>
       ) describes the Pharisee as fasting "twice in the week" (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblical_Greek" title="Biblical Greek">
        Greek
       </a>
       δὶς τοῦ σαββάτου
       <i>
        dis tou sabbatou
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The ancient Romans traditionally used the eight-day
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nundinum" title="Nundinum">
        nundinum
       </a>
       but, after the
       <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">
        Julian calendar
       </a>
       had come into effect in 45 BC, the seven-day week came into increasing use. For a while, the week and the nundinal cycle coexisted, but by the time the week was officially adopted by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I_(emperor)" title="Constantine I (emperor)">
        Constantine
       </a>
       in AD 321, the nundinal cycle had fallen out of use. The association of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Days_of_the_week" title="Days of the week">
        days of the week
       </a>
       with the Sun, the Moon and the five planets visible to the naked eye dates to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_era" title="Roman era">
        Roman era
       </a>
       (2nd century).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The continuous seven-day cycle of the days of the week can be traced back to the reign of
       <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">
        Augustus
       </a>
       ; the first identifiable date cited complete with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Day_of_the_week" title="Day of the week">
        day of the week
       </a>
       is 6 February
       <a href="/wiki/AD_60" title="AD 60">
        AD 60
       </a>
       , identified as a "
       <a href="/wiki/Sunday" title="Sunday">
        Sunday
       </a>
       " (as
       <i>
        viii idus Februarius dies solis
       </i>
       "eighth day before the ides of February, day of the Sun") in a Pompeiian graffito. According to the currently-used Julian calendar, 6 February 60 was, however, a
       <a href="/wiki/Wednesday" title="Wednesday">
        Wednesday
       </a>
       . This is explained by the existence of two conventions of naming days of the weeks based on the
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_hours" title="Planetary hours">
        planetary hours
       </a>
       system: 6 February was a "Sunday" based on the sunset naming convention, and a "Wednesday" based on the sunrise naming convention.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adoption_in_Asia">
        Adoption in Asia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Adoption in Asia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="China">
        China
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: China">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The earliest known reference in Chinese writings to a seven-day week is attributed to Fan Ning, who lived in the late 4th century in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_Dynasty_(265%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin Dynasty (265–420)">
        Jin Dynasty
       </a>
       , while diffusions from the
       <a href="/wiki/Manichaeism" title="Manichaeism">
        Manichaeans
       </a>
       are documented with the writings of the Chinese Buddhist monk
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Ching_(monk)" title="I Ching (monk)">
        Yi Jing
       </a>
       and the Ceylonese or Central Asian Buddhist monk
       <a href="/wiki/Amoghavajra" title="Amoghavajra">
        Bu Kong
       </a>
       of the 7th century (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">
        Tang Dynasty
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Chinese variant of the planetary system was brought to Japan by the Japanese monk
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kobo_Daishi" title="Kobo Daishi">
        Kobo Daishi
       </a>
       (9th century). Surviving diaries of the Japanese statesman
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fujiwara_Michinaga" title="Fujiwara Michinaga">
        Fujiwara Michinaga
       </a>
       show the seven-day system in use in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heian_Period" title="Heian Period">
        Heian
       </a>
       Japan as early as 1007. In Japan, the seven-day system was kept in use for astrological purposes until its promotion to a full-fledged Western-style calendrical basis during the
       <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">
        Meiji
       </a>
       era.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="India">
        India
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: India">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The seven-day week was known in India by the 6th century, referenced in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pancasiddhantika#Pancha-Siddhantika" title="Pancasiddhantika">
        Pañcasiddhāntikā
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Shashi (2000) mentions the
       <a href="/wiki/Yuga_Purana" title="Yuga Purana">
        Garga Samhita
       </a>
       , which he places in the 1st century BC or AD, as a possible earlier reference to a seven-day week in India. He concludes "the above references furnish a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terminus_ad_quem" title="Terminus ad quem">
        terminus ad quem
       </a>
       (viz. 1st century) The
       <a href="/wiki/Terminus_post_quem" title="Terminus post quem">
        terminus a quo
       </a>
       cannot be stated with certainty".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_Europe">
        Christian Europe
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Christian Europe">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week" title="Holy Week">
        Holy Week
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Easter_Week" title="Easter Week">
        Easter Week
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The seven-day weekly cycle has remained unbroken in
       <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">
        Christendom
       </a>
       , and hence in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_history" title="Western history">
        Western history
       </a>
       , for almost two millennia, despite changes to the
       <a href="/wiki/Coptic_calendar" title="Coptic calendar">
        Coptic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">
        Julian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian
       </a>
       calendars, demonstrated by the date of
       <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">
        Easter
       </a>
       Sunday having been traced back through numerous
       <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">
        computistic tables
       </a>
       to an Ethiopic copy of an early Alexandrian table beginning with the Easter of AD 311.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A tradition of divinations arranged for the days of the week on which certain feast days occur develops in the Early Medieval period. There are many later variants of this, including the German
       <i>
        Bauern-Praktik
       </i>
       and the versions of
       <i>
        Erra Pater
       </i>
       published in 16th to 17th century England, mocked in
       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Butler_(poet)" title="Samuel Butler (poet)">
        Samuel Butler
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Hudibras" title="Hudibras">
         Hudibras
        </a>
       </i>
       . South and East Slavic versions are known as
       <i>
        koliadniki
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        koliada
       </i>
       , a loan of Latin
       <i>
        calendae
       </i>
       ), with Bulgarian copies dating from the 13th century, and Serbian versions from the 14th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan380_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ryan380-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Medieval Christian traditions associated with the lucky or unlucky nature of certain days of the week survived into the modern period. This concerns primarily
       <a href="/wiki/Friday" title="Friday">
        Friday
       </a>
       , associated with the
       <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">
        crucifixion of Jesus
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Sunday" title="Sunday">
        Sunday
       </a>
       , sometimes personified as
       <a href="/wiki/Anastasia_the_Patrician" title="Anastasia the Patrician">
        Saint Anastasia
       </a>
       , was itself an object of worship in Russia, a practice denounced in a sermon extant in copies going back to the 14th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan383_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ryan383-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Sunday" title="Sunday">
        Sunday
       </a>
       , in the ecclesiastical numbering system also counted as the
       <i>
        feria prima
       </i>
       or the first day of the week; yet, at the same time, figures as the "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_eighth_day_(Christian)" title="The eighth day (Christian)">
        eighth day
       </a>
       ", and has occasionally been so called in Christian liturgy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Justin_Martyr" title="Justin Martyr">
        Justin Martyr
       </a>
       wrote: "the first day after the Sabbath, remaining the first of all the days, is called, however, the eighth, according to the number of all the days of the cycle, and [yet] remains the first".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A period of eight days, starting and ending on a Sunday, is called an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octave_(liturgical)" title="Octave (liturgical)">
        octave
       </a>
       , particularly in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_liturgy" title="Roman Catholic liturgy">
        Roman Catholic liturgy
       </a>
       . In German, the phrase
       <i>
        in acht Tagen
       </i>
       (literally "in eight days") means one week from today.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Week_numbering">
        Week numbering
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Week numbering">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Leap_week_calendar" title="Leap week calendar">
        Leap week calendar
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Weeks in a Gregorian calendar year can be numbered for each year. This style of numbering is commonly used (for example, by schools and businesses) in some European and Asian countries, but rare elsewhere.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_8601" title="ISO 8601">
        ISO 8601
       </a>
       includes the
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_week_date" title="ISO week date">
        ISO week date
       </a>
       system, a numbering system for weeks – each week begins on a Monday and is associated with the year that contains that week's Thursday (so that if a year starts in a long weekend Friday–Sunday, week number one of the year will start after that). For example, week 1 of 2004 (2004W01) ran from Monday, 29 December 2003 to Sunday, 4 January 2004, because its Thursday was 1 January 2004, whereas week 1 of 2005 (2005W01) ran from Monday, 3 January 2005 to Sunday, 9 January 2005, because its Thursday was 6 January 2005 and so the first Thursday of 2005. The highest week number in a year is either 52 or 53 (it was 53 in the year 2004). Schematically, this ISO convention translates as follows:

      <h2>
       <span id=".22Weeks.22_in_other_calendars">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='"Weeks"_in_other_calendars'>
        "Weeks" in other calendars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title='Edit section: "Weeks" in other calendars'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term "week" is sometimes expanded to refer to other time units comprising a few days. Such "weeks" of between four and ten days have been used historically in various places.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Intervals longer than 10 days are not usually termed "weeks" as they are closer in length to the
       <a href="/wiki/Fortnight" title="Fortnight">
        fortnight
       </a>
       or the
       <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">
        month
       </a>
       than to the seven-day week.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-modern_calendars">
        Pre-modern calendars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Week&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pre-modern calendars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Calendars unrelated to the Chaldean, Hellenistic, Christian or Jewish traditions often have time cycles between the day and the month of varying lengths, sometimes also called "weeks".
      </p>
      <p>
       An
       <a href="/wiki/Eight-day_week" title="Eight-day week">
        eight-day week
       </a>
       was used in
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_calendar#Nundinal_cycle" title="Roman calendar">
        Ancient Rome
       </a>
       and possibly in the pre-Christian
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_calendar" title="Celtic calendar">
        Celtic calendar
       </a>
       . Traces of a
       <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Nine-day_week_(disambiguation)" title="Nine-day week (disambiguation)">
        nine-day week
       </a>
       are found in Baltic languages and in
       <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
        Welsh
       </a>
       . The ancient Chinese calendar had a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_ten-day_week" title="Chinese ten-day week">
        ten-day week
       </a>
       , as did the ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_calendar" title="Egyptian calendar">
        Egyptian calendar
       </a>
       (and, incidentally, the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar" title="French Republican Calendar">
        French Republican Calendar
       </a>
       , dividing its 30-day months into thirds).
      </p>
      <p>
       A six-day week is found in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akan_Calendar" title="Akan Calendar">
        Akan Calendar
       </a>
       . Several cultures used a five-day week, including the 10th century
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icelandic_calendar" title="Icelandic calendar">
        Icelandic calendar
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Javanese_calendar" title="Javanese calendar">
        Javanese calendar
       </a>
       , and the traditional cycle of market days in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_culture" title="Korean culture">
        Korea
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igbo_Culture#Calendar_(Iguafo_Igbo)" title="Igbo Culture">
        Igbo
       </a>
       have a "market week" of four days. Evidence of a "three-day week" has been derived from the names of the days of the week in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guipuscoan_Basque" title="Guipuscoan Basque">
        Guipuscoan Basque
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knorr_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Knorr-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Aztecs and Mayas used the
       <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_calendars" title="Mesoamerican calendars">
        Mesoamerican calendars
       </a>
       . The most important of these calendars divided a ritual cycle of 260 days (known as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tonalpohualli" title="Tonalpohualli">
         Tonalpohualli
        </a>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tzolk%27in" title="Tzolk'in">
         Tzolk'in
        </a>
       </i>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucatec_Maya" title="Yucatec Maya">
        Yucatec Maya
       </a>
       ) into 20 weeks of 13 days (known in Spanish as
       <a href="/wiki/Trecena" title="Trecena">
        trecenas
       </a>
       ). They also divided the solar year into 18 periods of 20 days and five nameless days, creating a 20-day month divided into four five-day weeks. The end of each five-day week was a market day.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zerubavel50_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Zerubavel50-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Balinese
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pawukon" title="Pawukon">
        Pawukon
       </a>
       is a 210-day calendar consisting of 10 different simultaneously running weeks of 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9 and 10 days, of which the weeks of 4, 8 and 9 days are interrupted to fit into the 210-day cycle.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_calendar_reforms">
        Modern calendar reforms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Decimal_calendar" title="Decimal calendar">
        Decimal calendar
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Calendar#Ten_days_of_the_week" title="French Revolutionary Calendar">
        French Revolutionary Calendar § Ten days of the week
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_calendar" title="Bahá'í calendar">
        Bahá'í calendar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/International_Fixed_Calendar" title="International Fixed Calendar">
        International Fixed Calendar
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_calendar" title="Soviet calendar">
        Soviet calendar
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A 10-day week, called
       <i>
        décade
       </i>
       , was used in France for nine and a half years from October 1793 to April 1802; furthermore, the
       <a href="/wiki/Paris_Commune" title="Paris Commune">
        Paris Commune
       </a>
       adopted the Revolutionary Calendar for 18 days in 1871.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Bahá'í calendar features a 19-day period which some classify as a month and others classify as a week.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Fixed_Calendar" title="International Fixed Calendar">
        International Fixed Calendar
       </a>
       (also known as the "Eastman plan") fixed every date always on the same weekday. This plan kept a 7-day week while defining a year of 13 months with 28 days each. It was the official calendar of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak_Company" title="Eastman Kodak Company">
        Eastman Kodak Company
       </a>
       for decades.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        </a>

      <p>
       Between 1929 and 1931, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">
        USSR
       </a>
       changed from the seven-day week to a five-day week. There were 72 weeks and an additional five national holidays inserted within three of them, totaling a year of 365 days.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In 1931, after its brief experiment with a five-day week, the Soviet Union changed to a six-day week. Every sixth day (6th, 12th, 18th, 24th and 30th) of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregorian_Calendar" title="Gregorian Calendar">
        Gregorian Calendar
       </a>
       was a state rest day. The five additional national holidays in the earlier five-day week remained and did not fall on the state rest day. But, as January, March, May, July, August, October and December have 31 days, the week after the state rest day of the 30th was seven days long (31st–7th). This extra day was a working day for most or an extra holiday for others. Also as
       <a href="/wiki/February" title="February">
        February
       </a>
       is only 28 or 29 days depending on whether it is a
       <a href="/wiki/Leap_year" title="Leap year">
        leap year
       </a>
       or not, the first of March was also made a state rest day, although not every enterprise conformed to this. To clarify, the week after the state rest day, 24/25 February to 1 March, was only five or six days long, depending on whether it was a leap year or not. The week after that, 2 to 6 March, was only five days long.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The calendar was abandoned 26 June 1940 and the seven-day week reintroduced the next day.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
