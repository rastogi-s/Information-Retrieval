    Lunar month

      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">
        lunar calendars
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        lunar month
       </b>
       is the time between two successive
       <a href="/wiki/Syzygy_(astronomy)" title="Syzygy (astronomy)">
        syzygies
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moons
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moons
       </a>
       ). The use of the lunar month varies by which culture has utilized the method, the main difference being when the "new" month begins.
      </p>
      <p>
       This article deals with the definitions of a 'month' that are mainly of significance in astronomy. For other definitions, including a description of a month in the calendars of different cultures around the world, see:
       <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">
        month
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">
        Variations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Shona_people" title="Shona people">
        Shona
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">
        Middle-Eastern
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        European
       </a>
       traditions, the month starts when the
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        young crescent moon
       </a>
       becomes first visible at evening after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_conjunction" title="Astronomical conjunction">
        conjunction
       </a>
       with the Sun one or two days before that evening (
       <i>
        e.g.
       </i>
       , in the
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
        Islamic calendar
       </a>
       ). In
       <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_calendar" title="Egyptian calendar">
        ancient Egypt
       </a>
       the lunar month began on the day when the waning moon could no longer be seen just before sunrise. Others use calculation, of varying degrees of sophistication,
       <i>
        e.g.
       </i>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">
        Hebrew calendar
       </a>
       or the
       <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">
        ecclesiastical lunar calendar
       </a>
       . Yet others run from
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       to full moon. Calendars count integer days, so months may be 29 or 30 days in length, in some regular or irregular sequence. In India the month from conjunction to conjunction is divided into thirty parts known as
       <i>
        tithis
       </i>
       . The date is named after the
       <i>
        tithi
       </i>
       ruling at sunrise. As the
       <i>
        tithi
       </i>
       is shorter than the day the date sometimes jumps.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">
        common law
       </a>
       , a "lunar month" traditionally meant exactly 28 days or four weeks, thus a contact for 12 months ran for exactly 48 weeks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the United Kingdom, the lunar month was formally replaced by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calendar_month" title="Calendar month">
        calendar month
       </a>
       for deeds and other written contracts by the
       <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Property_Act_1925" title="Law of Property Act 1925">
        Law of Property Act 1925
       </a>
       and for all other legal purposes by the
       <a href="/wiki/Interpretation_Act_1978" title="Interpretation Act 1978">
        Interpretation Act 1978
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
        Terminology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are several types of lunar month. The term
       <i>
        lunar month
       </i>
       usually refers to the
       <a href="#Synodic_month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       because it is the cycle of the visible
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        phases of the Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Most of the following types of lunar month, except the distinction between the sidereal and tropical months, were first recognized in
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_theory#Babylon" title="Lunar theory">
        Babylonian lunar astronomy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sidereal_month">
        Sidereal month
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sidereal month">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The period of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon%27s_orbit" title="Moon's orbit">
        Moon's orbit
       </a>
       as defined with respect to the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       of apparently
       <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">
        fixed stars
       </a>
       (nowadays the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Celestial_Reference_Frame" title="International Celestial Reference Frame">
        International Celestial Reference Frame (ICRF)
       </a>
       ) is known as a
       <b>
        sidereal month
       </b>
       because it is the time it takes the Moon to return to a similar position among the
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         sidera
        </span>
       </i>
       ):
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001273216610000000♠
        </span>
        27.321
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         661
        </span>
       </span>
       days (27 d 7 h 43 min 11.6 s).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This type of month has been observed among cultures in the Middle East, India, and China in the following way: they divided the sky into 27 or 28
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mansion" title="Lunar mansion">
        lunar mansions
       </a>
       , one for each day of the month, identified by the prominent star(s) in them.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Synodic_month">
        Synodic month
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synodic month">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        synodic month
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        συνοδικός
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        sunodikos
       </i>
       , meaning "pertaining to a synod, i.e., a meeting"; in this case, of the Sun and the Moon) is the average period of the Moon's orbit with respect to the line joining the Sun and Earth. This is the period of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        lunar phases
       </a>
       , because the Moon's appearance depends on the position of the Moon with respect to the Sun as seen from the Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       While the Moon is orbiting the Earth, the Earth is progressing in its orbit around the Sun. After completing a sidereal month, the Moon must move a little further to reach the new position having the same angular distance from the Sun, appearing to move with respect to the stars since the previous month. Therefore, the synodic month takes 2.2 days longer than the sidereal month. Thus, about 13.37 sidereal months, but about 12.37 synodic months, occur in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregorian_year" title="Gregorian year">
        Gregorian year
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Since
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">
        Earth's orbit
       </a>
       around the Sun is
       <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">
        elliptical
       </a>
       and not
       <a href="/wiki/Circular_orbit" title="Circular orbit">
        circular
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_speed" title="Orbital speed">
        speed
       </a>
       of Earth's progression around the Sun varies during the year. Thus, the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Angualr_velocity&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Angualr velocity (page does not exist)">
        angular rate
       </a>
       is faster nearer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">
        periapsis
       </a>
       and slower near
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apoapsis" title="Apoapsis">
        apoapsis
       </a>
       . The same is so for the Moon's orbit around the Earth. Because of these variations in angular rate, the actual time between
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">
        lunations
       </a>
       may vary from about 29.18 to about 29.93 days. The long-term average duration is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001295305879810000♠
        </span>
        29.530
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         587
        </span>
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         981
        </span>
       </span>
       days
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (29 d 12 h 44 min 2.8016 s). The synodic month is used to calculate
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_cycle" title="Eclipse cycle">
        eclipse cycles
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_month">
        Tropical month
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tropical month">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       It is customary to specify positions of celestial bodies with respect to the vernal
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinox
       </a>
       . Because of Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">
        precession of the equinoxes
       </a>
       , this point moves back slowly along the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       . Therefore, it takes the Moon less time to return to an
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">
        ecliptic longitude
       </a>
       of 0° than to the same point amid the
       <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">
        fixed stars
       </a>
       :
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001273215820000000♠
        </span>
        27.321
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         582
        </span>
       </span>
       days (27 d 7 h 43 min 4.7 s). This slightly shorter period is known as
       <b>
        tropical month
       </b>
       (cf. the analogous
       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">
        tropical year
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Anomalistic_month">
        Anomalistic month
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Anomalistic month">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_precession" title="Lunar precession">
        Lunar precession
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">
        Apsidal precession
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        Moon's orbit
       </a>
       approximates an ellipse rather than a circle. However, the orientation (as well as the shape) of this orbit is not fixed. In particular, the position of the extreme points (the line of the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        apsides
       </a>
       :
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
        perigee
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
        apogee
       </a>
       ), rotates once (
       <a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">
        apsidal precession
       </a>
       ) in about 3,233 days (8.85 years). It takes the Moon longer to return to the same apsis because it has moved ahead during one revolution. This longer period is called the
       <b>
        anomalistic month
       </b>
       and has an average length of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001275545510000000♠
        </span>
        27.554
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         551
        </span>
       </span>
       days (27 d 13 h 18 min 33.2 s). The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_diameter" title="Apparent diameter">
        apparent diameter
       </a>
       of the Moon varies with this period, so this type has some relevance for the prediction of
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipses
       </a>
       (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saros_cycle" title="Saros cycle">
        Saros
       </a>
       ), whose extent, duration, and appearance (whether total or annular) depend on the exact apparent diameter of the Moon. The apparent diameter of the
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       varies with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_moon_cycle" title="Full moon cycle">
        full moon cycle
       </a>
       , which is the beat period of the synodic and anomalistic month, as well as the period after which the apsides point to the Sun again.
      </p>
      <p>
       An anomalistic month is longer than a sidereal month because the perigee moves in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prograde_motion" title="Prograde motion">
        same direction
       </a>
       as the Moon is orbiting the Earth, one revolution in nine years. Therefore, the Moon takes a little longer to return to perigee than to return to the same star.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Draconic_month">
        Draconic month
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Draconic month">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_precession" title="Lunar precession">
        Lunar precession
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nodal_precession" title="Nodal precession">
        Nodal precession
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Nodal_period" title="Nodal period">
        Nodal period
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        draconic month
       </b>
       is sometimes also known as a
       <b>
        draconitic month
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linton_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-linton-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <b>
        nodical month
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lockyer1870_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lockyer1870-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The name
       <i>
        draconic
       </i>
       refers to a mythical
       <a href="/wiki/Dragon" title="Dragon">
        dragon
       </a>
       , said to live in the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">
        lunar nodes
       </a>
       and eat the Sun or Moon during an
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipse
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linton_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-linton-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A solar or lunar eclipse is possible only when the Moon is at or near either of the two points where its orbit crosses the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       plane; i.e., the satellite is at or near either of its
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_node" title="Orbital node">
        orbital nodes
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The orbit of the Moon lies in a plane that is
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclined
       </a>
       about 5.14° with respect to the ecliptic plane. The line of intersection of these planes passes through the two points at which the Moon's orbit crosses the ecliptic plane: the
       <i>
        ascending node
       </i>
       , where the Moon enters the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Celestial_Hemisphere" title="Northern Celestial Hemisphere">
        Northern Celestial Hemisphere
       </a>
       , and the
       <i>
        descending node
       </i>
       , where the Moon moves into the
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Celestial_Hemisphere" title="Southern Celestial Hemisphere">
        Southern
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The draconic or nodical month is the average interval between two successive transits of the Moon through the same
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">
        node
       </a>
       . Because of the
       <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">
        torque
       </a>
       exerted by the Sun's gravity on the
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       of the Earth–Moon system, the plane of the Moon's orbit
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        gradually rotates
       </a>
       westward, which means the nodes gradually rotate around Earth. As a result, the time it takes the Moon to return to the same node is shorter than a sidereal month. It lasts
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001272122200000000♠
        </span>
        27.212
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         220
        </span>
       </span>
       days (27 d 5 h 5 min 35.8 s). The nodes of the Moon's orbit
       <a href="/wiki/Nodal_precession" title="Nodal precession">
        precesses
       </a>
       360° in about 6,798 days (18.6 years).
      </p>
      <p>
       A draconic month is shorter than a sidereal month because the nodes precess in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        opposite direction
       </a>
       to that in which the Moon is orbiting Earth, one rotation every 18.6 years. Therefore, the Moon returns to the same node slightly earlier than it returns to the same star.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cycle_lengths">
        Cycle lengths
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_month&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cycle lengths">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Regardless of the culture, all lunar months approximate the mean length of the synodic month, the average period the Moon takes to cycle through
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        its phases
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new
       </a>
       , first quarter,
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full
       </a>
       , last quarter) and back again: 29–30
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">
        days
       </a>
       . The Moon completes one orbit around Earth every 27.3 days (a sidereal month), but due to
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">
        Earth's orbital
       </a>
       motion around the Sun, the Moon does not yet finish a synodic cycle until it has reached the point in
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        its orbit
       </a>
       where the Sun is in the same
       <a href="/wiki/Position_of_the_Sun" title="Position of the Sun">
        relative position
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
