      <p>
       <b>
        Lunar swirls
       </b>
       are enigmatic features found across the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       ’s surface, which are characterized by having a high
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       , appearing optically immature (i.e. having the optical characteristics of a relatively young
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       ), and (often) having a sinuous shape. Their curvilinear shape is often accentuated by low albedo regions that wind between the bright swirls. They appear to overlay the lunar surface, superposed on top of craters and ejecta deposits, but impart no observable topography. Swirls have been identified on the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Moon" title="Geology of the Moon">
        highlands
       </a>
       - they are not associated with a specific lithologic composition. Swirls on the maria are characterized by strong albedo contrasts and complex, sinuous morphology, whereas those on highland terrain appear less prominent and exhibit simpler shapes, such as single loops or diffuse bright spots.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Association_with_magnetic_anomalies">
        Association with magnetic anomalies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_swirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Association with magnetic anomalies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The lunar swirls are coincident with regions of relatively high magnetic field strength on a planetary body that does not, and may never have had, an active core dynamo with which to generate its own magnetic field. Every swirl has an associated magnetic anomaly, but not every magnetic anomaly has an identifiable swirl. Orbital magnetic field mapping by the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">
        16
       </a>
       sub-satellites,
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
        Kaguya
       </a>
       show regions with a local magnetic field. Because the Moon has no currently active global magnetic field, these regional anomalies are regions of remanent magnetism; their origin remains controversial.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_models">
        Formation models
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_swirls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation models">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are three leading models for swirl formation. There are two characteristics of lunar swirls that any model of their formation must take into account:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        the lunar swirls are optically immature, and
       </li>
       <li>
        the lunar swirls are associated with magnetic anomalies.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Models for creation of the magnetic anomalies relevant to swirl formation, although independent of the swirls' optical characteristics, point to the observation that several of the magnetic anomalies are antipodal to the younger, large impact basins.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoodETAL1979_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HoodETAL1979-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Magnetization of these antipodal regions could occur in the presence of an amplified magnetic field, such as would result from a plasma cloud generated by the basin forming impact interacting with a weak magnetic field present at the Moon at the time of basin formation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoodWilliams1989_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HoodWilliams1989-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An unusually thick and/or strongly magnetized deposit of basin ejecta has been invoked to explain swirls that are not antipodal to a basin, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Reiner_Gamma" title="Reiner Gamma">
        Reiner Gamma
       </a>
       Formation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoodETAL2001_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HoodETAL2001-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An alternative model to a plasma-induced magnetic anomaly, but which also draws upon the basin-antipode correlation is by impact-induced currents and seismic waves ringing the planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KletetschkaETAL2005_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-KletetschkaETAL2005-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Once the magnetic anomaly has been installed, the swirls are formed because they are protected from the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       . The
       <i>
        <b>
         solar wind shielding model
        </b>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoodSchubert1980_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HoodSchubert1980-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       proposes that the swirls represent exposed silicate materials whose albedos have been selectively preserved over time from the effects of space weathering via deflection of solar wind ion bombardment. According to this model, optical maturation of exposed silicate surfaces in the inner solar system is at least partly a function of the solar wind ion bombardment. This model suggests that swirl formation is a continuing process, which began after placement of the magnetic anomaly.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <b>
         cometary impact model
        </b>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchultzSrnka1980_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SchultzSrnka1980-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       argues that the high albedo of the swirls is the result of scouring of the top-most surface regolith by the coma’s turbulent flow of gas and dust, which exposed fresh material and redeposited the fine, scoured material in discrete deposits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PinetETAL2000_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PinetETAL2000-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to this model, the associated strong magnetic anomalies are the result of magnetization of near-surface materials heated above the Curie temperature through hyper-velocity gas collisions and micro-impacts as the coma impacts the surface. Proponents of the cometary impact model consider the occurrence of many swirls antipodal to relatively young, major basins to be coincidental or the result of incomplete mapping of swirl locations.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <b>
         dust transport model
        </b>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garrick-BethellETAL2011_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Garrick-BethellETAL2011-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       proposes that weak electric fields created by interaction between the crustal magnetic anomalies and the solar wind plasma could attract or repel electrically charged fine dust. High albedo, feldspathic material is the dominant component of the finest particles of lunar soil. Electrostatic movement of dust lofted above the surface during terminator crossings could cause this material to preferentially accumulate and form the bright, looping swirl patterns.
      </p>
