      <p>
       <b>
        Spectroscopy
       </b>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">
            ɛ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       is the study of the interaction between
       <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">
        matter
       </a>
       and electromagnetic
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-em-spec_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-em-spec-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Historically, spectroscopy originated through the study of
       <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">
        visible light
       </a>
       dispersed according to its
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelength
       </a>
       , by a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prism_(optics)" title="Prism (optics)">
        prism
       </a>
       . Later the concept was expanded greatly to include any interaction with radiative energy as a function of its wavelength or
       <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">
        frequency
       </a>
       . Spectroscopic data are often represented by an
       <a href="/wiki/Emission_spectrum" title="Emission spectrum">
        emission spectrum
       </a>
       , a plot of the response of interest as a function of wavelength or frequency.
      </p>

      <p>
       Spectroscopy and spectrography are terms used to refer to the measurement of radiation intensity as a function of wavelength and are often used to describe
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Experimental" title="Experimental">
        experimental
       </a>
       spectroscopic methods. Spectral measurement devices are referred to as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectrometers" title="Spectrometers">
        spectrometers
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectrophotometers" title="Spectrophotometers">
        spectrophotometers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrograph" title="Spectrograph">
        spectrographs
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_analyzer" title="Spectral analyzer">
        spectral analyzers
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Daily observations of
       <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">
        color
       </a>
       can be related to spectroscopy.
       <a href="/wiki/Neon_lighting" title="Neon lighting">
        Neon lighting
       </a>
       is a direct application of
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_spectroscopy" title="Atomic spectroscopy">
        atomic spectroscopy
       </a>
       . Neon and other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noble_gases" title="Noble gases">
        noble gases
       </a>
       have characteristic emission frequencies (colors). Neon lamps use collision of electrons with the gas to excite these emissions.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inks" title="Inks">
        Inks
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyes" title="Dyes">
        dyes
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paints" title="Paints">
        paints
       </a>
       include chemical compounds selected for their spectral characteristics in order to generate specific colors and hues. A commonly encountered
       <a href="/wiki/Emission_spectrum" title="Emission spectrum">
        molecular spectrum
       </a>
       is that of
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">
        nitrogen dioxide
       </a>
       . Gaseous nitrogen dioxide has a characteristic red absorption feature, and this gives air polluted with nitrogen dioxide a reddish-brown color.
       <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">
        Rayleigh scattering
       </a>
       is a spectroscopic scattering phenomenon that accounts for the color of the sky.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spectroscopic studies were central to the development of
       <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">
        quantum mechanics
       </a>
       and included
       <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck" title="Max Planck">
        Max Planck
       </a>
       's explanation of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody_radiation" title="Blackbody radiation">
        blackbody radiation
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Albert Einstein
       </a>
       's explanation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Photoelectric_effect" title="Photoelectric effect">
        photoelectric effect
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Niels_Bohr" title="Niels Bohr">
        Niels Bohr
       </a>
       's explanation of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_structure" title="Atomic structure">
        atomic structure
       </a>
       and spectra. Spectroscopy is used in
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_chemistry" title="Physical chemistry">
        physical
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Analytical_chemistry" title="Analytical chemistry">
        analytical chemistry
       </a>
       because
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">
        atoms
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">
        molecules
       </a>
       have unique spectra. As a result, these spectra can be used to detect, identify and quantify information about the atoms and molecules. Spectroscopy is also used in
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">
        remote sensing
       </a>
       on Earth. Most research
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telescopes" title="Telescopes">
        telescopes
       </a>
       have spectrographs. The measured spectra are used to determine the chemical composition and
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_property" title="Physical property">
        physical properties
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_objects" title="Astronomical objects">
        astronomical objects
       </a>
       (such as their
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">
        velocity
       </a>
       ).
      </p>

      <p>
       One of the central concepts in spectroscopy is a
       <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">
        resonance
       </a>
       and its corresponding resonant frequency. Resonances were first characterized in mechanical systems such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pendulums" title="Pendulums">
        pendulums
       </a>
       . Mechanical systems that vibrate or oscillate will experience large amplitude oscillations when they are driven at their resonant frequency. A plot of amplitude vs. excitation frequency will have a peak centered at the resonance frequency. This plot is one type of spectrum, with the peak often referred to as a
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
        spectral line
       </a>
       , and most spectral lines have a similar appearance.
      </p>
      <p>
       In quantum mechanical systems, the analogous resonance is a coupling of two quantum mechanical
       <a href="/wiki/Stationary_state" title="Stationary state">
        stationary states
       </a>
       of one system, such as an
       <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
        atom
       </a>
       , via an oscillatory source of energy such as a
       <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
        photon
       </a>
       . The coupling of the two states is strongest when the energy of the source matches the energy difference between the two states. The energy

       </span>
       of a photon is related to its frequency

      </p>
      <p>
       Spectra of atoms and molecules often consist of a series of spectral lines, each one representing a resonance between two different quantum states. The explanation of these series, and the spectral patterns associated with them, were one of the experimental enigmas that drove the development and acceptance of quantum mechanics. The
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_spectral_series" title="Hydrogen spectral series">
        hydrogen spectral series
       </a>
       in particular was first successfully explained by the
       <a href="/wiki/Bohr_model" title="Bohr model">
        Rutherford-Bohr quantum model
       </a>
       of the hydrogen atom. In some cases spectral lines are well separated and distinguishable, but spectral lines can also overlap and appear to be a single transition if the
       <a href="/wiki/Density_of_states" title="Density of states">
        density of energy states
       </a>
       is high enough. Named series of lines include the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principal_series" title="Principal series">
        principal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sharp_series" title="Sharp series">
        sharp
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_series" title="Diffuse series">
        diffuse
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_series" title="Fundamental series">
        fundamental series
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       Spectroscopy is a sufficiently broad field that many sub-disciplines exist, each with numerous implementations of specific spectroscopic techniques. The various implementations and techniques can be classified in several ways.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Type_of_radiative_energy">
        Type of radiative energy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Type of radiative energy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Types of spectroscopy are distinguished by the type of radiative energy involved in the interaction. In many applications, the spectrum is determined by measuring changes in the intensity or frequency of this energy. The types of radiative energy studied include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
         Electromagnetic radiation
        </a>
        was the first source of energy used for spectroscopic studies. Techniques that employ electromagnetic radiation are typically classified by the wavelength region of the spectrum and include
        <a href="/wiki/Microwave_spectroscopy" title="Microwave spectroscopy">
         microwave
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terahertz_spectroscopy" title="Terahertz spectroscopy">
         terahertz
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">
         infrared
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_infrared_spectroscopy" title="Near infrared spectroscopy">
         near infrared
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet%E2%80%93visible_spectroscopy" title="Ultraviolet–visible spectroscopy">
         visible and ultraviolet
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/X-ray_spectroscopy" title="X-ray spectroscopy">
         x-ray
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Gamma_spectroscopy" title="Gamma spectroscopy">
         gamma spectroscopy
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Particles, due to their
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Broglie_wavelength" title="De Broglie wavelength">
         de Broglie wavelength
        </a>
        , can also be a source of radiative energy and both
        <a href="/wiki/Electron_spectroscopy" title="Electron spectroscopy">
         electrons
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Neutron_spectroscopy" title="Neutron spectroscopy">
         neutrons
        </a>
        are commonly used. For a particle, its
        <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">
         kinetic energy
        </a>
        determines its wavelength.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Spectrogram" title="Spectrogram">
         Acoustic spectroscopy
        </a>
        involves radiated pressure waves.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamic_mechanical_spectroscopy" title="Dynamic mechanical spectroscopy">
         Mechanical
        </a>
        methods can be employed to impart radiating energy, similar to acoustic waves, to solid materials.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_of_the_interaction">
        Nature of the interaction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nature of the interaction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Types of spectroscopy can also be distinguished by the nature of the interaction between the energy and the material. These interactions include:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-em-spec_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-em-spec-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Absorption_spectroscopy" title="Absorption spectroscopy">
         Absorption
        </a>
        occurs when energy from the radiative source is absorbed by the material. Absorption is often determined by measuring the fraction of energy transmitted through the material; absorption will decrease the transmitted portion.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_spectroscopy" title="Emission spectroscopy">
         Emission
        </a>
        indicates that radiative energy is released by the material. A material's
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody_spectrum" title="Blackbody spectrum">
         blackbody spectrum
        </a>
        is a spontaneous emission spectrum determined by its temperature; this feature can be measured in the infrared by instruments such as the Atmospheric Emitted Radiance Interferometer (AERI).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Emission can also be induced by other sources of energy such as
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flame_spectroscopy" title="Flame spectroscopy">
         flames
        </a>
        or sparks or electromagnetic radiation in the case of
        <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence_spectroscopy" title="Fluorescence spectroscopy">
         fluorescence
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Elastic_scattering" title="Elastic scattering">
         Elastic scattering
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflectivity" title="Reflectivity">
         reflection
        </a>
        spectroscopy determine how incident radiation is reflected or scattered by a material.
        <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">
         Crystallography
        </a>
        employs the scattering of high energy radiation, such as
        <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">
         x-rays
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Electron_crystallography" title="Electron crystallography">
         electrons
        </a>
        , to examine the arrangement of atoms in proteins and solid crystals.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impedance_spectroscopy" title="Impedance spectroscopy">
         Impedance spectroscopy
        </a>
        studies the ability of a medium to impede or slow the transmittance of energy. For
        <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">
         optical applications
        </a>
        , this is characterized by the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Index_of_refraction" title="Index of refraction">
         index of refraction
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Inelastic_scattering" title="Inelastic scattering">
         Inelastic scattering
        </a>
        phenomena involve an exchange of energy between the radiation and the matter that shifts the wavelength of the scattered radiation. These include
        <a href="/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy" title="Raman spectroscopy">
         Raman
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Compton_scattering" title="Compton scattering">
         Compton scattering
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Coherent_spectroscopy" title="Coherent spectroscopy">
         Coherent
        </a>
        or resonance spectroscopy are techniques where the radiative energy couples two quantum states of the material in a
        <a href="/wiki/Coherence_(physics)" title="Coherence (physics)">
         coherent
        </a>
        interaction that is sustained by the radiating field. The coherence can be disrupted by other interactions, such as particle collisions and energy transfer, and so often require high intensity radiation to be sustained.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" title="NMR spectroscopy">
         Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy
        </a>
        is a widely used resonance method and
        <a href="/wiki/Ultrafast_laser_spectroscopy" title="Ultrafast laser spectroscopy">
         ultrafast laser
        </a>
        methods are also now possible in the infrared and visible spectral regions.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Type_of_material">
        Type of material
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Type of material">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spectroscopic studies are designed so that the radiant energy interacts with specific types of matter.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Atoms">
        Atoms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Atoms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_spectroscopy" title="Atomic spectroscopy">
        Atomic spectroscopy
       </a>
       was the first application of spectroscopy developed.
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_absorption_spectroscopy" title="Atomic absorption spectroscopy">
        Atomic absorption spectroscopy
       </a>
       (AAS) and
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_emission_spectroscopy" title="Atomic emission spectroscopy">
        atomic emission spectroscopy
       </a>
       (AES) involve visible and ultraviolet light. These absorptions and emissions, often referred to as atomic spectral lines, are due to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_structure#Energy_levels" title="Atomic structure">
        electronic transitions
       </a>
       of outer shell electrons as they rise and fall from one electron orbit to another. Atoms also have distinct x-ray spectra that are attributable to the excitation of inner shell electrons to excited states.
      </p>
      <p>
       Atoms of different elements have distinct spectra and therefore atomic spectroscopy allows for the identification and quantitation of a sample's elemental composition.
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bunsen" title="Robert Bunsen">
        Robert Bunsen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">
        Gustav Kirchhoff
       </a>
       discovered new elements by observing their emission spectra. Atomic absorption lines are observed in the solar spectrum and referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines" title="Fraunhofer lines">
        Fraunhofer lines
       </a>
       after their discoverer. A comprehensive explanation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_spectral_series" title="Hydrogen spectral series">
        hydrogen spectrum
       </a>
       was an early success of quantum mechanics and explained the
       <a href="/wiki/Lamb_shift" title="Lamb shift">
        Lamb shift
       </a>
       observed in the hydrogen spectrum, which further led to the development of
       <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">
        quantum electrodynamics
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Modern implementations of atomic spectroscopy for studying visible and ultraviolet transitions include
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flame_emission_spectroscopy" title="Flame emission spectroscopy">
        flame emission spectroscopy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Inductively_coupled_plasma_atomic_emission_spectroscopy" title="Inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy">
        inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Glow_discharge#Use_in_analytical_chemistry" title="Glow discharge">
        glow discharge spectroscopy
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_induced_plasma" title="Microwave induced plasma">
        microwave induced plasma
       </a>
       spectroscopy, and spark or arc emission spectroscopy. Techniques for studying x-ray spectra include
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_spectroscopy" title="X-ray spectroscopy">
        X-ray spectroscopy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_fluorescence" title="X-ray fluorescence">
        X-ray fluorescence
       </a>
       (XRF).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Molecules">
        Molecules
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Molecules">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The combination of atoms into molecules leads to the creation of unique types of energetic states and therefore unique spectra of the transitions between these states. Molecular spectra can be obtained due to electron spin states (
       <a href="/wiki/Electron_paramagnetic_resonance" title="Electron paramagnetic resonance">
        electron paramagnetic resonance
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy" title="Rotational spectroscopy">
        molecular rotations
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Molecular_vibration" title="Molecular vibration">
        molecular vibration
       </a>
       and electronic states. Rotations are collective motions of the atomic nuclei and typically lead to spectra in the microwave and millimeter-wave spectral regions; rotational spectroscopy and microwave spectroscopy are synonymous. Vibrations are relative motions of the atomic nuclei and are studied by both
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">
        infrared
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy" title="Raman spectroscopy">
        Raman spectroscopy
       </a>
       . Electronic excitations are studied using
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet-visible_spectroscopy" title="Ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy">
        visible and ultraviolet spectroscopy
       </a>
       as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence_spectroscopy" title="Fluorescence spectroscopy">
        fluorescence spectroscopy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Studies in molecular spectroscopy led to the development of the first
       <a href="/wiki/Maser" title="Maser">
        maser
       </a>
       and contributed to the subsequent development of the
       <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">
        laser
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Crystals_and_extended_materials">
        Crystals and extended materials
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Crystals and extended materials">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The combination of atoms or molecules into crystals or other extended forms leads to the creation of additional energetic states. These states are numerous and therefore have a high density of states. This high density often makes the spectra weaker and less distinct, i.e., broader. For instance, blackbody radiation is due to the thermal motions of atoms and molecules within a material. Acoustic and mechanical responses are due to collective motions as well. Pure crystals, though, can have distinct spectral transitions, and the crystal arrangement also has an effect on the observed molecular spectra. The regular
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lattice_structure" title="Lattice structure">
        lattice structure
       </a>
       of crystals also scatters x-rays, electrons or neutrons allowing for crystallographic studies.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclei">
        Nuclei
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nuclei">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Nuclei also have distinct energy states that are widely separated and lead to
       <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">
        gamma ray
       </a>
       spectra. Distinct nuclear spin states can have their energy separated by a magnetic field, and this allows for
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance_spectroscopy" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy">
        NMR spectroscopy
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       Other types of spectroscopy are distinguished by specific applications or implementations:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_resonance_spectroscopy" title="Acoustic resonance spectroscopy">
         Acoustic resonance spectroscopy
        </a>
        is based on sound waves primarily in the
        <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">
         audible
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">
         ultrasonic
        </a>
        regions
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Auger_electron_spectroscopy" title="Auger electron spectroscopy">
         Auger spectroscopy
        </a>
        is a method used to study surfaces of materials on a micro-scale. It is often used in connection with
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_microscopy" title="Electron microscopy">
         electron microscopy
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cavity_ring_down_spectroscopy" title="Cavity ring down spectroscopy">
         Cavity ring down spectroscopy
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circular_Dichroism" title="Circular Dichroism">
         Circular Dichroism
        </a>
        spectroscopy
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Coherent_anti-Stokes_Raman_spectroscopy" title="Coherent anti-Stokes Raman spectroscopy">
         Coherent anti-Stokes Raman spectroscopy
        </a>
        (CARS) is a recent technique that has high sensitivity and powerful applications for
        <i>
         in vivo
        </i>
        spectroscopy and imaging.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Cold_vapour_atomic_fluorescence_spectroscopy" title="Cold vapour atomic fluorescence spectroscopy">
         Cold vapour atomic fluorescence spectroscopy
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlation_spectroscopy" title="Correlation spectroscopy">
         Correlation spectroscopy
        </a>
        encompasses several types of two-dimensional NMR spectroscopy.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Deep-level_transient_spectroscopy" title="Deep-level transient spectroscopy">
         Deep-level transient spectroscopy
        </a>
        measures concentration and analyzes parameters of electrically active defects in
        <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">
         semiconducting materials
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_polarisation_interferometry" title="Dual polarisation interferometry">
         Dual polarisation interferometry
        </a>
        measures the real and imaginary components of the complex refractive index
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Electron_energy_loss_spectroscopy" title="Electron energy loss spectroscopy">
         Electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS)
        </a>
        in transmission electron microscopy
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Electron_phenomenological_spectroscopy" title="Electron phenomenological spectroscopy">
         Electron phenomenological spectroscopy
        </a>
        measures physicochemical properties and characteristics of electronic structure of multicomponent and complex molecular systems.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_spin_resonance" title="Electron spin resonance">
         EPR spectroscopy
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Force_spectroscopy" title="Force spectroscopy">
         Force spectroscopy
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_transform_spectroscopy" title="Fourier transform spectroscopy">
         Fourier transform spectroscopy
        </a>
        is an efficient method for processing spectra data obtained using interferometers.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_transform_infrared_spectroscopy" title="Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy">
         Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy
        </a>
        (FTIR) is a common implementation of infrared spectroscopy. NMR also employs
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_transforms" title="Fourier transforms">
         Fourier transforms
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Hadron_spectroscopy" title="Hadron spectroscopy">
         Hadron spectroscopy
        </a>
        studies the energy/mass spectrum of hadrons according to
        <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">
         spin
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)">
         parity
        </a>
        , and other particle properties.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baryon_spectroscopy" title="Baryon spectroscopy">
         Baryon spectroscopy
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meson_spectroscopy" title="Meson spectroscopy">
         meson spectroscopy
        </a>
        are both types of hadron spectroscopy.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Hyperspectral_imaging" title="Hyperspectral imaging">
         Hyperspectral imaging
        </a>
        is a method to create a complete picture of the environment or various objects, each pixel containing a full visible, VNIR, NIR, or infrared spectrum.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Inelastic_electron_tunneling_spectroscopy" title="Inelastic electron tunneling spectroscopy">
         Inelastic electron tunneling spectroscopy
        </a>
        (IETS) uses the changes in current due to inelastic electron-vibration interaction at specific energies that can also measure optically forbidden transitions.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Inelastic_neutron_scattering" title="Inelastic neutron scattering">
         Inelastic neutron scattering
        </a>
        is similar to Raman spectroscopy, but uses
        <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">
         neutrons
        </a>
        instead of
        <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
         photons
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Laser-induced_breakdown_spectroscopy" title="Laser-induced breakdown spectroscopy">
         Laser-Induced Breakdown Spectroscopy
        </a>
        (LIBS), also called Laser-induced plasma spectrometry (LIPS)
       </li>
       <li>
        Laser spectroscopy uses
        <a href="/wiki/Tunable_laser" title="Tunable laser">
         tunable lasers
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and other types of coherent emission sources, such as optical parametric oscillators,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duarte2_7-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Duarte2-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        for selective excitation of atomic or molecular species.
       </li>
       <li>
        Mass spectroscopy is a historical term used to refer to
        <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">
         mass spectrometry
        </a>
        . Current recommendations
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MurrayBoyd2013_8-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-MurrayBoyd2013-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        are to use the latter term. Use of the term mass spectroscopy originated in the use of
        <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">
         phosphor
        </a>
        screens to detect ions.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_spectroscopy" title="Mössbauer spectroscopy">
         Mössbauer spectroscopy
        </a>
        probes the properties of specific
        <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">
         isotopic
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">
         nuclei
        </a>
        in different atomic environments by analyzing the resonant absorption of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-rays" title="Gamma-rays">
         gamma-rays
        </a>
        . See also
        <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_effect" title="Mössbauer effect">
         Mössbauer effect
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Multivariate_optical_computing" title="Multivariate optical computing">
         Multivariate optical computing
        </a>
        is an all optical
        <a href="/wiki/Compressed_sensing" title="Compressed sensing">
         compressed sensing
        </a>
        technique, generally used in harsh environments, that directly calculates chemical information from a spectrum as analogue output.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Neutron_spin_echo" title="Neutron spin echo">
         Neutron spin echo
        </a>
        spectroscopy measures internal dynamics in proteins and other
        <a href="/wiki/Soft_matter" title="Soft matter">
         soft matter
        </a>
        systems
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Photoacoustic_spectroscopy" title="Photoacoustic spectroscopy">
         Photoacoustic spectroscopy
        </a>
        measures the sound waves produced upon the absorption of radiation.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Photoemission_spectroscopy" title="Photoemission spectroscopy">
         Photoemission spectroscopy
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Photothermal_spectroscopy" title="Photothermal spectroscopy">
         Photothermal spectroscopy
        </a>
        measures heat evolved upon absorption of radiation.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pump-probe_spectroscopy" title="Pump-probe spectroscopy">
         Pump-probe spectroscopy
        </a>
        can use ultrafast laser pulses to measure
        <a href="/wiki/Reaction_intermediate" title="Reaction intermediate">
         reaction intermediates
        </a>
        in the femtosecond timescale.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Raman_optical_activity" title="Raman optical activity">
         Raman optical activity
        </a>
        spectroscopy exploits Raman scattering and optical activity effects to reveal detailed information on chiral centers in molecules.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy" title="Raman spectroscopy">
         Raman spectroscopy
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Saturated_spectroscopy" title="Saturated spectroscopy">
         Saturated spectroscopy
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_spectroscopy" title="Scanning tunneling spectroscopy">
         Scanning tunneling spectroscopy
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Spectrophotometry" title="Spectrophotometry">
         Spectrophotometry
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spin_noise_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spin noise spectroscopy (page does not exist)">
         Spin noise spectroscopy
        </a>
        traces spontaneous fluctuations of electronic and nuclear spins.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Time-resolved_spectroscopy" title="Time-resolved spectroscopy">
         Time-resolved spectroscopy
        </a>
        measures the decay rate(s) of excited states using various spectroscopic methods.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Time_stretch_analog-to-digital_converter" title="Time stretch analog-to-digital converter">
         Time-Stretch
        </a>
        Spectroscopy
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]

        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Thermal_infrared_spectroscopy" title="Thermal infrared spectroscopy">
         Thermal infrared spectroscopy

        <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_spectroscopy" title="History of spectroscopy">
        History of spectroscopy
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The history of spectroscopy began with
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Isaac Newton
       </a>
       's optics experiments (1666–1672). Newton applied the word "spectrum" to describe the rainbow of colors that combine to form white light and that are revealed when the white light is passed through a prism. During the early 1800s,
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_von_Fraunhofer" title="Joseph von Fraunhofer">
        Joseph von Fraunhofer
       </a>
       made experimental advances with dispersive spectrometers that enabled spectroscopy to become a more precise and quantitative scientific technique. Since then, spectroscopy has played and continues to play a significant role in chemistry, physics and astronomy.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "In 1672, in the first paper that he submitted to the
        <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">
         Royal Society
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Isaac_Newton" title="Sir Isaac Newton">
         Sir Isaac Newton
        </a>
        described an experiment in which he permitted sunlight to pass through a small hole and then through a prism. Newton found that sunlight, which looks white to us, is actually made up of a mixture of all the colors of the rainbow"
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-open-astro_14-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-open-astro-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        "In 1802,
        <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">
         William Hyde Wollaston
        </a>
        built an improved
        <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
         spectrometer
        </a>
        that included a lens to focus the Sun’s spectrum on a screen. Upon use, Wollaston realized that the colors were not spread uniformly, but instead had missing patches of colors, which appeared as dark bands in the
        <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
         spectrum
        </a>
        . Later, in 1815, German physicist
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Fraunhofer" title="Joseph Fraunhofer">
         Joseph Fraunhofer
        </a>
        also examined the solar spectrum, and found about 600 such dark lines (missing colors), which are now known as
        <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines" title="Fraunhofer lines">
         Fraunhofer lines
        </a>
        , or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_lines" title="Absorption lines">
         Absorption lines
        </a>
        ."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-open-astro_14-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-open-astro-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
