      <p>
       An
       <b>
        occultation
       </b>
       is an event that occurs when one object is hidden by another object that passes between it and the observer. The term is often used in
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       , but can also refer to any situation in which an object in the foreground blocks from view (occults) an object in the background. In this general sense, occultation applies to the visual scene observed from low-flying aircraft (or
       <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">
        computer-generated imagery
       </a>
       ) when foreground objects obscure distant objects dynamically, as the scene changes over time.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Occultations_by_the_Moon">
        Occultations by the Moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Occultation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occultations by the Moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <div class="PopUpMediaTransform" id="mwe_player_0" style="width:220px;" videopayload='&lt;div class="mediaContainer" style="width:360px"&gt;&lt;video id="mwe_player_1" poster="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Occultation.ogv/360px--Occultation.ogv.jpg" controls="" preload="none" autoplay="" style="width:360px;height:288px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="10.08" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Occultation.ogv" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"&gt;&lt;source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a0/Occultation.ogv" type="video/ogg; codecs=&amp;quot;theora, vorbis&amp;quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file, 360 × 288 (764 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="360" data-height="288" data-bandwidth="763980" data-framerate="25"/&gt;&lt;source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/a/a0/Occultation.ogv/Occultation.ogv.160p.webm" type="video/webm; codecs=&amp;quot;vp8, vorbis&amp;quot;" data-title="Low bandwidth WebM (160P)" data-shorttitle="WebM 160P" data-transcodekey="160p.webm" data-width="200" data-height="160" data-bandwidth="218448" data-framerate="25"/&gt;&lt;source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/a/a0/Occultation.ogv/Occultation.ogv.240p.webm" type="video/webm; codecs=&amp;quot;vp8, vorbis&amp;quot;" data-title="Small WebM (240P)" data-shorttitle="WebM 240P" data-transcodekey="240p.webm" data-width="300" data-height="240" data-bandwidth="330360" data-framerate="25"/&gt;&lt;/video&gt;&lt;/div&gt;'>

         Occultation of a star by the Moon

      <p>
       The term occultation is most frequently used to describe those relatively frequent occasions when the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       passes in front of a
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       during the course of its orbital motion around the Earth. Since the Moon, with an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_speed" title="Angular speed">
        angular speed
       </a>
       with respect to the stars of 0.55
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc#Symbols,_abbreviations_and_subdivisions" title="Minute of arc">
        arcsec
       </a>
       /s or 2.7 µrad/s, has a very thin atmosphere and stars have an
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter#Use_in_astronomy" title="Angular diameter">
        angular diameter
       </a>
       of at most 0.057
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">
        arcseconds
       </a>
       or 0.28 µrad, a star that is occulted by the Moon will disappear or reappear in 0.1 seconds or less on the Moon's edge, or limb. Events that take place on the Moon's dark limb are of particular interest to observers, because the lack of glare allows these occultations to more easily be observed and timed.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon's orbit is inclined to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        orbit of the Moon
       </a>
       ), and any stars with an ecliptic latitude of less than about 6.5 degrees may be occulted by it. There are three first magnitude stars that are sufficiently close to the ecliptic that they may be occulted by the Moon and by planets –
       <a href="/wiki/Regulus" title="Regulus">
        Regulus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spica" title="Spica">
        Spica
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Antares" title="Antares">
        Antares
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planets_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-planets-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Occultations of
       <a href="/wiki/Aldebaran" title="Aldebaran">
        Aldebaran
       </a>
       are presently only possible by the Moon, because the planets pass Aldebaran to the north. Neither planetary nor lunar occultations of
       <a href="/wiki/Pollux_(star)" title="Pollux (star)">
        Pollux
       </a>
       are currently possible. However, in the far future, occultations of Pollux will be possible, as they were in the far past. Some
       <a href="/wiki/Deep-sky_object" title="Deep-sky object">
        deep-sky objects
       </a>
       , such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">
        Pleiades
       </a>
       , can also be occulted by the Moon.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OccultJupiter_6-7-2005_6-07PM_Nelson_NZ.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="285" data-file-width="259" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/OccultJupiter_6-7-2005_6-07PM_Nelson_NZ.jpg/200px-OccultJupiter_6-7-2005_6-07PM_Nelson_NZ.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/OccultJupiter_6-7-2005_6-07PM_Nelson_NZ.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OccultJupiter_6-7-2005_6-07PM_Nelson_NZ.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Jupiter (the bright object in the upper right) a few minutes before being occulted by the Moon on 16 June 2005
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Within a few kilometres of the edge of an occultation's predicted path, referred to as its northern or southern limit, an observer may see the star intermittently disappearing and reappearing as the irregular limb of the Moon moves past the star, creating what is known as a
       <a href="/wiki/Grazing_lunar_occultation" title="Grazing lunar occultation">
        grazing lunar occultation
       </a>
       . From an observational and scientific standpoint, these "grazes" are the most dynamic and interesting of lunar occultations.
      </p>
      <p>
       The accurate timing of lunar occultations is performed regularly by (primarily amateur) astronomers. Lunar occultations timed to an accuracy of a few tenths of a second have various scientific uses, particularly in refining our knowledge of lunar topography. Photoelectric analysis of lunar occultations have also discovered some stars to be very close visual or spectroscopic
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">
        binaries
       </a>
       . Some angular diameters of stars have been measured by timing of lunar occultations, which is useful for determining
       <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">
        effective temperatures
       </a>
       of those stars. Early radio astronomers found occultations of radio sources by the Moon valuable for determining their exact positions, because the long wavelength of radio waves limited the resolution available through direct observation. This was crucial for the unambiguous identification of the radio source
       <a href="/wiki/3C_273" title="3C 273">
        3C 273
       </a>
       with the optical quasar and its jet,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HazardMackeyShimmins63_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HazardMackeyShimmins63-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_astronomy" title="Fundamental astronomy">
        fundamental
       </a>
       prerequisite for
       <a href="/wiki/Maarten_Schmidt" title="Maarten Schmidt">
        Maarten Schmidt
       </a>
       's discovery of the cosmological nature of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quasars" title="Quasars">
        quasars
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Several times during the year, someone on Earth can usually observe the Moon occulting a planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since planets, unlike stars, have significant angular sizes, lunar occultations of planets will create a narrow zone on Earth from which a partial occultation of the planet will occur. An observer located within that narrow zone could observe the planet's disk partly blocked by the slowly moving moon. The same mechanic can be seen with the Sun, where observers on Earth will view it as a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       . Therefore, a total solar eclipse is effectively the same event as the Moon occulting the Sun.
      </p>

          </a>
         </div>
         A grazing occultation of
         <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(moon)" title="Rhea (moon)">
          Rhea
         </a>
         , a moon of
         <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
          Saturn
         </a>
         , by another moon,
         <a href="/wiki/Dione_(moon)" title="Dione (moon)">
          Dione
         </a>
         , as photographed from the
         <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
          Cassini–Huygens
         </a>
         unmanned spaceprobe.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Stars may also be occulted by planets. Occultations of bright stars are rare. In 1959,
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       occulted
       <a href="/wiki/Regulus" title="Regulus">
        Regulus
       </a>
       , and the next occultation of a bright star (also Regulus by Venus) will be in 2044.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planets_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-planets-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       's rings were first discovered when that planet occulted a star in 1977. On 3 July 1989,
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       passed in front of the 5th magnitude star
       <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_(constellation)" title="Sagittarius (constellation)">
        28 Sagittarii
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       occulted stars in 1988, 2002, and 2006, allowing its tenuous atmosphere to be studied via
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_limb_sounding" title="Atmospheric limb sounding">
        atmospheric limb sounding
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mutual_planetary_occultations">
        Mutual planetary occultations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Occultation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mutual planetary occultations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In rare cases, one planet can pass in front of another. If the nearer planet appears larger than the more distant one, the event is called a mutual planetary occultation.
      </p>

      <p>
       An asteroid occultation occurs when an
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       (or more generically:
       <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">
        minor planet
       </a>
       ) passes in front of a
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       (occults a star), temporarily blocking its light (as seen from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MIT_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MIT-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Several events occur nearly every day over the world. From any particular place such events occur almost every night, although most require a
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       to see. Professionals and amateurs around the globe are collaborating over the Internet to exchange their observation for a joint analysis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preston_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Preston-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Asteroid occultations are useful for measuring the size and position of asteroids much more precisely than can be done by any other means. A cross-sectional profile of the shape of an asteroid can even be determined if a number of observers at different, nearby, locations observe the occultation. For example, on 12 March 2009 there were 8 minor planet occulations, including
       <a href="/wiki/85_Io" title="85 Io">
        85 Io
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/247_Eukrate" title="247 Eukrate">
        247 Eukrate
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/1585_Union" title="1585 Union">
        1585 Union
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/201_Penelope" title="201 Penelope">
        201 Penelope
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/70_Panopaea" title="70 Panopaea">
        70 Panopaea
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/980_Anacostia" title="980 Anacostia">
        980 Anacostia
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2448_Sholokhov" title="2448 Sholokhov">
        2448 Sholokhov
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/1746_Brouwer" title="1746 Brouwer">
        1746 Brouwer
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/191_Kolga" title="191 Kolga">
        191 Kolga
       </a>
       . Any one of these would be expected to occult at a time and place on the globe, at a certain magnitude, and with a certain star.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preston_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Preston-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, according to the 1998 European Asteroidal Occultation Results from Euraster, 39 Laetitia was observed by over 38 observatories in one occultation on 3 March 1998, which resulted in many chords being determined.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-euraster_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-euraster-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Regulus" title="Regulus">
        Regulus
       </a>
       was occulted by the asteroid
       <a href="/wiki/163_Erigone" title="163 Erigone">
        163 Erigone
       </a>
       in the early morning of March 20, 2014
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IOTA2006_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IOTA2006-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as first predicted by A. Vitagliano in 2004.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solex_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Solex-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This was the brightest asteroid occultation ever predicted to occur over a populated area. As the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_belt_asteroid" title="Main belt asteroid">
        main belt asteroid
       </a>
       passed in front of the star its 67-mile-wide (100 km) shadow swept across
       <a href="/wiki/Nassau_County,_New_York" title="Nassau County, New York">
        Nassau
       </a>
       and Suffolk counties, all of New York City and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hudson_River_Valley" title="Hudson River Valley">
        Hudson River Valley
       </a>
       , with the center of the shadow path following a line roughly connecting New York City, White Plains, Newburgh, Oneonta, Rome and Pulaski before crossing into Canada near Belleville and
       <a href="/wiki/North_Bay,_Ontario" title="North Bay, Ontario">
        North Bay, Ontario
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IOTA2006_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-IOTA2006-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solex_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Solex-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Bad weather obscured the occultation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       ,
       <a href="/wiki/28978_Ixion" title="28978 Ixion">

       , and
       <a href="/wiki/20000_Varuna" title="20000 Varuna">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_occultations_by_Kuiper_Belt_Objects">
        Stellar occultations by Kuiper Belt Objects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Occultation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stellar occultations by Kuiper Belt Objects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The dwarf planet
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       was observed in a stellar transit on 21 January 2017, identifying a ring.
      </p>
      <p>
       On June 3, 2017, a star was occulted by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuiper_Belt_Object" title="Kuiper Belt Object">
        Kuiper Belt Object
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_MU69" title="2014 MU69">
        2014 MU69
       </a>
       , the first such occultation detected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_KBO_Occultation_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-First_KBO_Occultation-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The multi-faceted campaign involved cooperation from the Argentinian government (including local governments – a major highway was closed for two hours, and street lights were turned off, in order to preclude light pollution), three spacecraft, 24 portable ground-based telescopes, and NASA's SOFIA airborne observatory in "the most challenging stellar occultation in the history of astronomy," in an effort spanning six months.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA's_New_Horizons_Team_Strikes_Gold_in_Argentina_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA's_New_Horizons_Team_Strikes_Gold_in_Argentina-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Double_occultations">
        Double occultations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Occultation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Double occultations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Moon or another celestial body can occult multiple celestial bodies at the same time. Because of its relatively large
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        angular diameter
       </a>
       , the Moon at any given time occults an indeterminate number of stars. However, an event when the Moon occults two bright objects (e.g. two planets or a bright star and a planet) simultaneously is extremely rare and can be seen only from a small part of the world. The last event of such type was on 23 April 1998 when the Moon occulted Venus and Jupiter for observers on
       <a href="/wiki/Ascension_Island" title="Ascension Island">
        Ascension Island
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Occulting_satellites">
        Occulting satellites
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Occultation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occulting satellites">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Big Occulting Steerable Satellite (BOSS)
       </i>
       was a proposed
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       that would work in conjunction with a
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       to detect
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       around distant stars. The satellite consists of a large, very lightweight sheet, and a set of maneuvering thrusters and navigation systems. It would maneuver to a position along the line of sight between the telescope and a nearby star. The satellite would thereby block the radiation from the star, permitting the orbiting planets to be observed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The proposed satellite would have a dimension of 70 m × 70 m, a mass of about 600 kg, and maneuver by means of an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_drive" title="Ion drive">
        ion drive
       </a>
       engine in combination with using the sheet as a light sail. Positioned at a distance of 100,000 km from the telescope, it would block more than 99.998% of the starlight.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are two possible configurations of this satellite. The first would work with a
       <a href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">
        space telescope
       </a>
       , most likely positioned near the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrangian point
       </a>
       . The second would place the satellite in a highly elliptical orbit about the Earth, and work in conjunction with a ground telescope. At the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
        apogee
       </a>
       of the orbit, the satellite would remain relatively stationary with respect to the ground, allowing longer exposure times.
      </p>
      <p>
       An updated version of this design is called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starshade" title="Starshade">
        Starshade
       </a>
       , which uses a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunflower" title="Sunflower">
        sunflower
       </a>
       -shaped
       <a href="/wiki/Coronagraph" title="Coronagraph">
        coronagraph
       </a>
       disc. A comparable proposal was also made for a satellite to occult bright X-ray sources, called an
       <i>
        X-ray Occulting Steerable Satellite
       </i>
       or XOSS.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
