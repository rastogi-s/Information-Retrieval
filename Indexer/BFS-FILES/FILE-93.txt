      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_power">
        Rise to power
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Xerxes_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rise to power">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Xerxes was born to
       <a href="/wiki/Darius_I" title="Darius I">
        Darius I
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Atossa" title="Atossa">
        Atossa
       </a>
       (daughter of
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">
        Cyrus the Great
       </a>
       ). Darius and Atossa were both
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
        Achaemenids
       </a>
       as they were both descendants of
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenes" title="Achaemenes">
        Achaemenes
       </a>
       . While Darius was preparing for another war against Greece, a revolt spurred in Egypt in 486¬†BC due to heavy taxes and the deportation of craftsmen to build the royal palaces at Susa and Perseopolis. Under Persian law, the king was required to choose a successor before setting out on dangerous expeditions. When Darius decided to leave (487-486¬†BC), Darius prepared his tomb at
       <a href="/wiki/Naqsh-e_Rustam" title="Naqsh-e Rustam">
        Naqsh-e Rustam
       </a>
       (five kilometers from his royal palace at Perseopolis) and appointed Xerxes, his eldest son by
       <a href="/wiki/Atossa" title="Atossa">
        Atossa
       </a>
       , as his successor. However, Darius could not lead the campaign due to his failing health and died in October 486¬†BC at the age of 64.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDandamaev1989178-179_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDandamaev1989178-179-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artobazan" title="Artobazan">
        Artobazan
       </a>
       claimed the crown as the eldest of all the children; while Xerxes, on the other hand, urged that he was sprung from Atossa, the daughter of Cyrus, and that it was Cyrus who had won the Persians their freedom. Xerxes was also helped by a Spartan king in exile who was present in Persia at the time, Eurypontid king
       <a href="/wiki/Demaratus" title="Demaratus">
        Demaratus
       </a>
       , who argued that the eldest son does not universally mean they have claim to the crown, as Spartan law states that the first son born while the father is king is the heir to the kingship.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some modern scholars also view the unusual decision of Darius to give the throne to Xerxes to be a result of his consideration of the unique positions that Cyrus the Great and his daughter Atossa enjoyed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artobazan" title="Artobazan">
        Artobazan
       </a>
       was born to "Darius the subject", while Xerxes was the eldest son
       <a href="/wiki/Born_in_the_purple" title="Born in the purple">
        born in the purple
       </a>
       after Darius's rise to the throne, and Artobazan's mother was a commoner while Xerxes's mother was the daughter of the founder of the empire.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Xerxes was crowned and succeeded his father in October‚ÄìDecember 486¬†BC
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chi-v2_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-chi-v2-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       when he was about 36 years old.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDandamaev1989180_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDandamaev1989180-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The transition of power to Xerxes was smooth due again in part to the great authority of Atossa
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atossa-EI_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-atossa-EI-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and his accession of royal power was not challenged by any person at court or in the Achaemenian family, or any subject nation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Almost immediately, Xerxes crushed revolts in Egypt and
       <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">
        Babylon
       </a>
       that had broken out the year before, and appointed his brother
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenes_(satrap)" title="Achaemenes (satrap)">
        Achaemenes
       </a>
       as satrap over Egypt. In 484¬†BC, he outraged the Babylonians by violently confiscating and melting down
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the golden statue of Bel (
       <a href="/wiki/Marduk" title="Marduk">
        Marduk
       </a>
       , Merodach), the hands of which the rightful king of Babylon had to clasp each New Year's Day. This sacrilege led the Babylonians to rebel in 484¬†BC and 482¬†BC, so that in contemporary Babylonian documents, Xerxes refused his father's title of King of Babylon, being named rather as King of Persia and
       <a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">
        Media
       </a>
       , Great King, King of Kings (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shahanshah" title="Shahanshah">
        Shahanshah
       </a>
       ) and King of Nations (i.e., of the world). This comes from the Daiva Inscriptions of Xerxes, lines 6-13.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Xerxes_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Invasion of the Greek mainland">

      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Persian_invasion_of_Greece" title="Second Persian invasion of Greece">
        Second Persian invasion of Greece
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Xerxes_lash_sea.JPG">

        </a>

      <p>
       Darius died while in the process of preparing a second army to invade the Greek mainland, leaving to his son the task of punishing the
       <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">
        Athenians
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naxos_Island" title="Naxos Island">
        Naxians
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Eretria" title="Eretria">
        Eretrians
       </a>
       for their interference in the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Revolt" title="Ionian Revolt">
        Ionian Revolt
       </a>
       , the burning of
       <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">
        Sardis
       </a>
       , and their victory over the Persians at
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Marathon" title="Battle of Marathon">
        Marathon
       </a>
       . From 483¬†BC, Xerxes prepared his expedition: The
       <a href="/wiki/Xerxes_Canal" title="Xerxes Canal">
        Xerxes Canal
       </a>
       was dug through the
       <a href="/wiki/Isthmus" title="Isthmus">
        isthmus
       </a>
       of the peninsula of
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Athos" title="Mount Athos">
        Mount Athos
       </a>
       , provisions were stored in the stations on the road through
       <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">
        Thrace
       </a>
       , and two
       <a href="/wiki/Pontoon_bridge" title="Pontoon bridge">
        pontoon bridges
       </a>
       later known as
       <a href="/wiki/Xerxes%27_Pontoon_Bridges" title="Xerxes' Pontoon Bridges">
        Xerxes' Pontoon Bridges
       </a>
       were built across the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellespont" title="Hellespont">
        Hellespont
       </a>
       . Soldiers of many nationalities served in the armies of Xerxes from all over his multi-ethnic massive Eurasian-sized empire and beyond, including the
       <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">
        Assyrians
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">
        Phoenicians
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonians" title="Babylonians">
        Babylonians
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">
        Egyptians
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">
        Jews
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macedon" title="Macedon">
        Macedonians
       </a>
       , European
       <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">
        Thracians
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paionia" title="Paionia">
        Paeonians
       </a>
       , Achaean
       <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">
        Greeks
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ionia" title="Ionia">
        Ionians
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Islands" title="Aegean Islands">
        Aegean islanders
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Aeolis" title="Aeolis">
        Aeolians
       </a>
       , Greeks from
       <a href="/wiki/Pontus_(region)" title="Pontus (region)">
        Pontus
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colchians" title="Colchians">
        Colchians
       </a>
       , and many more.
      </p>
      <p>
       According to the Greek historian
       <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
        Herodotus
       </a>
       , Xerxes's first attempt to bridge the Hellespont ended in failure when a storm destroyed the
       <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">
        flax
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">
        papyrus
       </a>
       cables of the bridges. In retaliation, Xerxes ordered the Hellespont (the strait itself) whipped three hundred times, and had
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fetters" title="Fetters">
        fetters
       </a>
       thrown into the water. Xerxes's second attempt to bridge the Hellespont was successful.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">
        Carthaginian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_Wars#The_First_Sicilian_War_(480_BC)" title="Sicilian Wars">
        invasion of Sicily
       </a>
       deprived Greece of the support of the powerful monarchs of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Italy" title="Syracuse, Italy">
        Syracuse
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agrigentum" title="Agrigentum">
        Agrigentum
       </a>
       - ancient sources assume Xerxes was responsible, modern scholarship is skeptical.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many smaller Greek states, moreover, took the side of the Persians, especially
       <a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">
        Thessaly
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thebes,_Greece" title="Thebes, Greece">
        Thebes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Argos" title="Argos">
        Argos
       </a>
       . Xerxes was victorious during the initial battles.
      </p>
      <p>
       Xerxes set out in the spring of 480¬†BC from
       <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">
        Sardis
       </a>
       with a fleet and army which
       <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
        Herodotus
       </a>
       estimated was roughly one million strong along with 10,000 elite warriors named the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_Immortals" title="Persian Immortals">
        Persian Immortals
       </a>
       . More recent estimates place the Persian force at around 60,000 combatants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermopylae_and_Athens">
        Thermopylae and Athens
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Xerxes_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Thermopylae and Athens">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       At the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thermopylae" title="Battle of Thermopylae">
        Battle of Thermopylae
       </a>
       , a small force of Greek warriors led by King
       <a href="/wiki/Leonidas_I" title="Leonidas I">
        Leonidas
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">
        Sparta
       </a>
       resisted the much larger Persian forces, but were ultimately defeated. According to Herodotus, the Persians broke the Spartan
       <a href="/wiki/Phalanx" title="Phalanx">
        phalanx
       </a>
       after a Greek man called
       <a href="/wiki/Ephialtes_of_Trachis" title="Ephialtes of Trachis">
        Ephialtes
       </a>
       betrayed his country by telling the Persians of another pass around the mountains. At Artemisium, large storms had destroyed ships from the Greek side and so the battle stopped prematurely as the Greeks received news of the defeat at Thermopylae and retreated. After Thermopylae,
       <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">
        Athens
       </a>
       was captured. Most of the Athenians had abandoned the city and fled to the island of
       <a href="/wiki/Salamis_Island" title="Salamis Island">
        Salamis
       </a>
       before Xerxes arrived. A small group attempted to defend the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athenian_Acropolis" title="Athenian Acropolis">
        Athenian Acropolis
       </a>
       , but they were defeated. Xerxes burnt the city; leaving an archaeologically attested destruction layer, known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Perserschutt" title="Perserschutt">
        Perserschutt
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Persians thus gained control of all of mainland Greece to the north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Corinth" title="Isthmus of Corinth">
        Isthmus of Corinth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warfare_in_the_Ancient_World_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Warfare_in_the_Ancient_World-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Xerxes was induced by the message of
       <a href="/wiki/Themistocles" title="Themistocles">
        Themistocles
       </a>
       (against the advice of
       <a href="/wiki/Artemisia_I_of_Caria" title="Artemisia I of Caria">
        Artemisia
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Halicarnassus" title="Halicarnassus">
        Halicarnassus
       </a>
       ) to attack the Greek fleet under unfavourable conditions, rather than sending a part of his ships to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peloponnesus" title="Peloponnesus">
        Peloponnesus
       </a>
       and awaiting the dissolution of the Greek armies. The
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Salamis" title="Battle of Salamis">
        Battle of Salamis
       </a>
       (September, 480¬†BC) was won by the Greek fleet, after which Xerxes set up a winter camp in
       <a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">
        Thessaly
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-h327_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-h327-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to Herodotus, fearing that the Greeks might attack the bridges across the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellespont" title="Hellespont">
        Hellespont
       </a>
       and trap his army in Europe, Xerxes decided to retreat back to Asia, taking the greater part of the army with him.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VIII97_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-VIII97-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another cause of the retreat might have been continued unrest in
       <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">
        Babylon
       </a>
       , which, being a key province of the
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
        Achaemenid Empire
       </a>
       , required the king's own attention.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-livius_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-livius-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He left behind a contingent in Greece to finish the campaign under
       <a href="/wiki/Mardonius" title="Mardonius">
        Mardonius
       </a>
       , who according to Herodotus had suggested the retreat in the first place. This force was defeated the following year at
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Plataea" title="Battle of Plataea">
        Plataea
       </a>
       by the combined forces of the Greek city states, ending the Persian offensive on Greece for good.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_projects">
        Construction projects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

         The rock-cut tomb at
         <a href="/wiki/Naqsh-e_Rustam" title="Naqsh-e Rustam">
          Naqsh-e Rustam
         </a>
         north of
         <a href="/wiki/Persepolis" title="Persepolis">

         , copying that of

      </div>
      <p>
       After the military blunders in Greece, Xerxes returned to Persia and oversaw the completion of the many construction projects left unfinished by his father at
       <a href="/wiki/Susa" title="Susa">
        Susa
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Persepolis" title="Persepolis">
        Persepolis
       </a>
       . He oversaw the building of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gate_of_All_Nations" title="Gate of All Nations">
        Gate of All Nations
       </a>
       and the Hall of a Hundred Columns at Persepolis, which are the largest and most imposing structures of the palace. He oversaw the completion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Apadana" title="Apadana">
        Apadana
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palace_of_Darius" title="Palace of Darius">
        Palace of Darius
       </a>
       and the Treasury, all started by Darius, as well as having his own palace built which was twice the size of his father's. His taste in architecture was similar to that of Darius, though on an even more gigantic scale.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He had colorful enameled brick laid on the exterior face of the
       <a href="/wiki/Apadana" title="Apadana">
        Apadana
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also maintained the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Road" title="Royal Road">
        Royal Road
       </a>
       built by his father and completed the Susa Gate and built a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palace_of_Darius_at_Susa" title="Palace of Darius at Susa">
        palace at Susa
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Death">
        Death
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Xerxes_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In August 465¬†BC,
       <a href="/wiki/Artabanus_of_Persia" title="Artabanus of Persia">
        Artabanus
       </a>
       , the commander of the royal bodyguard and the most powerful official in the Persian court, assassinated Xerxes with the help of a
       <a href="/wiki/Eunuch" title="Eunuch">
        eunuch
       </a>
       , Aspamitres. Although Artabanus bore the same name as the famed uncle of Xerxes, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyrcanian" title="Hyrcanian">
        Hyrcanian
       </a>
       , his rise to prominence was due to his popularity in religious quarters of the court and harem intrigues. He put his seven sons in key positions and had a plan to dethrone the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Achaemenid_dynasty" title="Achaemenid dynasty">
        Achaemenids
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Greek historians give contradicting accounts of events. According to
       <a href="/wiki/Ctesias" title="Ctesias">
        Ctesias
       </a>
       (in Persica 20), Artabanus then accused the Crown Prince Darius, Xerxes's eldest son, of the murder and persuaded another of Xerxes's sons,
       <a href="/wiki/Artaxerxes_I_of_Persia" title="Artaxerxes I of Persia">
        Artaxerxes
       </a>
       , to avenge the patricide by killing Darius. But according to
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       (in Politics 5.1311b), Artabanus killed Darius first and then killed Xerxes. After Artaxerxes discovered the murder, he killed Artabanus and his sons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Participating in these intrigues was the general
       <a href="/wiki/Megabyzus" title="Megabyzus">
        Megabyzus
       </a>
       , whose decision to switch sides probably saved the Achaemenids from losing their control of the Persian throne.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">
        Religion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Xerxes_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Religion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <i>
        Histories
       </i>
       , Herodotus relates that at the Strymon river, the Magi sought good omens by sacrificing white horses. And after using these enchantments and many others besides the river, the armies of Xerxes crossed its bridges at the Nine Ways in Edonian country. When they learned that Nine Ways was the name of the place, they buried alive nine boys and nine girls who were children of the local people. Herodotus further relates that burying people alive is a Persian custom and he adds that Xerxes' wife Amestris buried, twice, seven sons of notable Persians as an offering on her own behalf to the fabled god beneath the earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although Herodotus' report in the
       <i>
        Histories
       </i>
       has created debate concerning Xerxes's religious beliefs, modern scholars consider him a
       <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">
        Zoroastrian
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_depictions">
