   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Doppler broadening
   </h1>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_physics" title="Atomic physics">
        atomic physics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        Doppler broadening
       </b>
       is the broadening of
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
        spectral lines
       </a>
       due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">
        Doppler effect
       </a>
       caused by a distribution of velocities of
       <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
        atoms
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
        molecules
       </a>
       . Different velocities of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_emission" title="Spontaneous emission">
        emitting
       </a>
       particles result in different Doppler shifts, the cumulative effect of which is the line broadening.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This resulting line profile is known as a
       <b>
        Doppler profile
       </b>
       . A particular case is the
       <b>
        thermal Doppler broadening
       </b>
       due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases" title="Kinetic theory of gases">
        thermal motion
       </a>
       of the particles. Then, the broadening depends only on the
       <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">
        frequency
       </a>
       of the spectral line, the
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        mass
       </a>
       of the emitting particles, and their
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       , and therefore can be used for inferring the temperature of an emitting body.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Saturated_absorption_spectroscopy" title="Saturated absorption spectroscopy">
        Saturated absorption spectroscopy
       </a>
       , also known as Doppler-free spectroscopy, can be used to find the true frequency of an atomic transition without cooling a sample down to temperatures at which the Doppler broadening is minimal.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_and_caveats">
        Applications and caveats
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Doppler_broadening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications and caveats">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_physics" title="Plasma physics">
        plasma physics
       </a>
       , the thermal Doppler broadening is one of the explanations for the broadening of spectral lines, and as such gives an indication for the temperature of observed material. Other causes of velocity distributions may exist, though, for example due to
       <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
        turbulent
       </a>
       motion. For a fully developed turbulence, the resulting line profile is generally very difficult to distinguish from the thermal one.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another cause could be a large range of
       <i>
        macroscopic
       </i>
       velocities resulting, e.g., from the receding and approaching portions of a rapidly spinning
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">
        accretion disk
       </a>
       . Finally, there are many other factors which can also broaden the lines. For example, a sufficiently high particle
       <a href="/wiki/Number_density" title="Number density">
        number density
       </a>
       may lead to significant
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stark_broadening" title="Stark broadening">
        Stark broadening
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Doppler broadening can also be used to determine the velocity distribution of a gas given its absorption spectrum. In particular, this has been used to determine the velocity distribution of interstellar gas clouds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Doppler broadening has also been used as a design consideration in high temperature
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactors" title="Nuclear reactors">
        nuclear reactors
       </a>
       . In principle, as the reactor fuel heats up, the neutron absorption spectrum will broaden due to the relative thermal motion of the fuel atoms with respect to the neutrons. Given the shape of the neutron absorption spectrum, this has the result of reducing
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron_cross_section" title="Neutron cross section">
        neutron absorption cross section
       </a>
       , reducing the likelihood of absorption and fission. The end result is that reactors designed to take advantage of doppler broadening will decrease their reactivity as temperature increases, creating a
       <a href="/wiki/Passive_nuclear_safety" title="Passive nuclear safety">
        passive safety measure
       </a>
       . This tends to be more relevant to
       <a href="/wiki/Gas-cooled_reactor" title="Gas-cooled reactor">
        gas cooled reactors
       </a>
       as other mechanisms are dominant in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_water_reactor" title="Light water reactor">
        water cooled reactors
       </a>
       .
      </p>
