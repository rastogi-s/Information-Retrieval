      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">
        photography
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        long-focus lens
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_lens" title="Camera lens">
        camera lens
       </a>
       which has a
       <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">
        focal length
       </a>
       that is longer than the diagonal measure of the film or sensor that receives its image.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is used to make distant objects appear
       <a href="/wiki/Magnification" title="Magnification">
        magnified
       </a>
       with magnification increasing as longer focal length lenses are used. A long-focus lens is one of three basic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photographic_lens" title="Photographic lens">
        photographic lens
       </a>
       types classified by relative focal length, the other two being a
       <a href="/wiki/Normal_lens" title="Normal lens">
        normal lens
       </a>
       and a
       <a href="/wiki/Wide-angle_lens" title="Wide-angle lens">
        wide-angle lens
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As with other types of camera lenses, the focal length is usually expressed in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millimeter" title="Millimeter">
        millimeter
       </a>
       value written on the lens, for example: a 500 mm lens. The most common type of long-focus lens is the
       <a href="/wiki/Telephoto_lens" title="Telephoto lens">
        telephoto lens
       </a>
       , which incorporate a special lens group known as a
       <i>
        telephoto group
       </i>
       to make the physical length of the lens shorter than the focal length.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_long-focus_lenses">
        Effects of long-focus lenses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-focus_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects of long-focus lenses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Formula_One_Photographers.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1743" data-file-width="3681" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Formula_One_Photographers.jpg/300px-Formula_One_Photographers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Formula_One_Photographers.jpg/450px-Formula_One_Photographers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Formula_One_Photographers.jpg/600px-Formula_One_Photographers.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Formula_One_Photographers.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Sports photographers using long-focus lenses
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Long-focus lenses are best known for making distant objects appear
       <a href="/wiki/Magnification" title="Magnification">
        magnified
       </a>
       . This effect is similar to moving closer to the object, but is not the same, since
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_(visual)" title="Perspective (visual)">
        perspective
       </a>
       is a function solely of viewing location. Two images taken from the same location, one with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wide_angle_lens" title="Wide angle lens">
        wide angle lens
       </a>
       and the other with a long-focus lens, will show identical perspective, in that near and far objects appear the same relative size to each other. Comparing magnification by using a long lens to magnification by moving closer, however, the long-focus-lens shot appears to compress the distance between objects due to the perspective from the more distant location. Long lenses thus give a photographer an alternative to the type of
       <a href="/wiki/Perspective_distortion_(photography)" title="Perspective distortion (photography)">
        perspective distortion
       </a>
       exhibited by shorter focal length lenses where (when the photographer stands closer to the given subject) different portions of a subject in a photograph can appear out of proportion to each other.
      </p>
      <p>
       Long lenses also make it easier to blur the background more, even when the
       <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">
        depth of field
       </a>
       is the same; photographers will sometimes use this effect to defocus the background in an image to "separate" it from the subject. This background blurring is often referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Bokeh" title="Bokeh">
        bokeh
       </a>
       by photographers. Long lenses are often used with a
       <a href="/wiki/Tripod" title="Tripod">
        tripod
       </a>
       , because of the increased weight and the fact that the effect of camera shake is magnified.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Constant_object_size">
        Constant object size
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-focus_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Constant object size">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The photographer often moves to keep the same image size on the film for a particular object. Observe in the comparison images below that although the foreground object remains the same size, the background changes size; thus, perspective is dependent on the distance between the photographer and the subject. The longer focus lenses compress the perception of depth, and the shorter focus exaggerate it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This effect is also used for
       <a href="/wiki/Dolly_zoom" title="Dolly zoom">
        dolly zooms
       </a>
       . The perspective of the so-called
       <i>
        normal
       </i>
       lens, 50 mm focal length for 35 mm film format, is conventionally regarded as a "correct" perspective, though a longer lens is usually preferred for a more pleasing perspective for portraits.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Telescopes_as_long-focus_lenses">
        Telescopes as long-focus lenses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-focus_lens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Telescopes as long-focus lenses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       From the invention of photography in the 19th century, images have been captured using standard
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">
        optical telescopes
       </a>
       including telescope
       <a href="/wiki/Objective_(optics)" title="Objective (optics)">
        objectives
       </a>
       adapted as early portrait lenses.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Besides being used in an astronomical role in
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophotography" title="Astrophotography">
        astrophotography
       </a>
       , telescopes are adapted as long-focus lenses in nature photography,
       <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">
        surveillance
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Machine_vision" title="Machine vision">
        machine vision
       </a>
       and long-focus microscopy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macrolensdb_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-macrolensdb-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       To use a telescope as a camera lens requires an adapter for the standard 1.25 inch tube
       <a href="/wiki/Eyepiece" title="Eyepiece">
        eyepiece
       </a>
       mount, usually a
       <a href="/wiki/T-mount" title="T-mount">
        T-mount
       </a>
       adapter, which in turn attaches to an adapter for the
       <a href="/wiki/System_camera" title="System camera">
        system camera
       </a>
       's particular
       <a href="/wiki/Lens_mount" title="Lens mount">
        lens mount
       </a>
       . Controlling exposure is done by
       <a href="/wiki/Shutter_speed" title="Shutter speed">
        exposure time
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Film_speed#Digital_camera_ISO_speed_and_exposure_index" title="Film speed">
        gain
       </a>
       , or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(optics)" title="Filter (optics)">
        filters
       </a>
       since telescopes almost always lack diaphragms for
       <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">
        aperture
       </a>
       adjustment. The 1.25 inch mount is smaller than many film and
       <a href="/wiki/Image_sensor_format" title="Image sensor format">
        sensor formats
       </a>
       so they tend to show
       <a href="/wiki/Vignetting" title="Vignetting">
        vignetting
       </a>
       around the field edges.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astropix_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-astropix-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Telescopes are normally intended for visual use, so they are not corrected to produce a large flat field like dedicated camera lenses and tend to show
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_aberration" title="Optical aberration">
        optical aberration
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the late 1990s compact digital cameras have been used in
       <a href="/wiki/Afocal_photography" title="Afocal photography">
        afocal photography
       </a>
       , a technique where the camera lens is left attached, taking a picture directly through the telescope's eyepiece lens itself, also referred to as "
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Digiscoping" title="Digiscoping">
         digiscoping
        </a>
       </i>
       ."
      </p>
