      <p>
       <b>
        arXiv
       </b>
       (pronounced "
       <a href="/wiki/Archive" title="Archive">
        archive
       </a>
       ")
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steele_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Steele-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is a repository of electronic
       <a href="/wiki/Preprint" title="Preprint">
        preprints
       </a>
       (known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-prints" title="E-prints">
        e-prints
       </a>
       ) approved for publication after moderation, that consists of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_paper" title="Scientific paper">
        scientific papers
       </a>
       in the fields of
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
        mathematics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
        physics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">
        computer science
       </a>
       , quantitative
       <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">
        biology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">
        statistics
       </a>
       , and quantitative finance, which can be accessed online. In many fields of mathematics and physics, almost all scientific papers are
       <a href="/wiki/Self-archiving" title="Self-archiving">
        self-archived
       </a>
       on the arXiv repository. Begun on August 14, 1991, arXiv.org passed the half-million article milestone on October 3, 2008,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and hit a million by the end of 2014.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dddmag2015_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dddmag2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By October 2016 the submission rate had grown to more than 10,000 per month.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dddmag2015_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-dddmag2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ArXiv&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ArXiv_1994.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="319" data-file-width="313" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e2/ArXiv_1994.png/220px-ArXiv_1994.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e2/ArXiv_1994.png 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ArXiv_1994.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A screenshot of the arXiv taken in 1994,
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
          <a href="#cite_note-8">
           [8]
          </a>
         </sup>
         using the browser
         <a href="/wiki/Mosaic_(web_browser)" title="Mosaic (web browser)">
          NCSA Mosaic
         </a>
         . At the time,
         <a href="/wiki/Form_(HTML)" title="Form (HTML)">
          HTML forms
         </a>
         were a new technology.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The arXiv was made possible by the low-bandwidth
       <a href="/wiki/TeX" title="TeX">
        TeX
       </a>
       file format, which allowed scientific papers to be easily transmitted over the
       <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
        Internet
       </a>
       and rendered
       <a href="/wiki/Client-side" title="Client-side">
        client-side
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Around 1990,
       <a href="/wiki/Joanne_Cohn" title="Joanne Cohn">
        Joanne Cohn
       </a>
       began emailing
       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
        physics
       </a>
       preprints to colleagues as TeX files, but the number of papers being sent soon filled mailboxes to capacity.
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ginsparg" title="Paul Ginsparg">
        Paul Ginsparg
       </a>
       recognized the need for central storage, and in August 1991 he created a central
       <a href="/wiki/Disciplinary_repository" title="Disciplinary repository">
        repository
       </a>
       mailbox stored at the
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">
        Los Alamos National Laboratory
       </a>
       which could be accessed from any computer. Additional modes of access were soon added:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FTP" title="FTP">
        FTP
       </a>
       in 1991,
       <a href="/wiki/Gopher_(protocol)" title="Gopher (protocol)">
        Gopher
       </a>
       in 1992, and the
       <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">
        World Wide Web
       </a>
       in 1993.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dddmag2015_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-dddmag2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-print" title="E-print">
        e-print
       </a>
       was quickly adopted to describe the articles.
      </p>
      <p>
       It began as a physics archive, called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LANL" title="LANL">
        LANL
       </a>
       preprint archive, but soon expanded to include astronomy, mathematics, computer science,
       <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_biology" title="Quantitative biology">
        quantitative biology
       </a>
       and, most recently, statistics. Its original domain name was
       <tt>
        xxx.lanl.gov
       </tt>
       . Due to LANL's lack of interest in the rapidly expanding technology, in 2001 Ginsparg changed institutions to
       <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">
        Cornell University
       </a>
       and changed the name of the repository to
       <tt>
        arXiv.org
       </tt>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is now hosted principally by Cornell, with eight
       <a href="/wiki/Mirror_website" title="Mirror website">
        mirrors
       </a>
       around the world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Its existence was one of the precipitating factors that led to the current movement in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_publishing" title="Scientific publishing">
        scientific publishing
       </a>
       known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_access_(publishing)" title="Open access (publishing)">
        open access
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">
        Mathematicians
       </a>
       and scientists regularly upload their papers to arXiv.org for worldwide access
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and sometimes for reviews before they are published in
       <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">
        peer-reviewed
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">
        journals
       </a>
       . Ginsparg was awarded a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MacArthur_Fellowship" title="MacArthur Fellowship">
        MacArthur Fellowship
       </a>
       in 2002 for his establishment of arXiv.
      </p>
      <p>
       The annual budget for arXiv is approximately $826,000 for 2013 to 2017, funded jointly by Cornell University Library, the Simons Foundation (in both gift and challenge grant forms) and annual fee income from member institutions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This model arose in 2010, when Cornell sought to broaden the financial funding of the project by asking institutions to make annual voluntary contributions based on the amount of download usage by each institution. Annual donations were envisaged to vary in size between $2,300 to $4,000, based on each institution’s usage. As of 14 January 2014
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ArXiv&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , 174 institutions have pledged support for the period 2013–2017 on this basis, with a projected revenue from this source of approximately $340,000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In September 2011, Cornell University Library took overall administrative and financial responsibility for arXiv's operation and development. Ginsparg was quoted in the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronicle_of_Higher_Education" title="Chronicle of Higher Education">
         Chronicle of Higher Education
        </a>
       </i>
       as saying it "was supposed to be a three-hour tour, not a life sentence".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHE_2011_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CHE_2011-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, Ginsparg remains on the
       <span class="plainlinks">
        <a class="external text" href="https://arxiv.org/help/scientific_ad_board" rel="nofollow">
         arXiv Scientific Advisory Board
        </a>
        and on the
        <a class="external text" href="https://arxiv.org/new/physics.html" rel="nofollow">
         arXiv Physics Advisory Committee
        </a>
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Peer_review">
        Peer review
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ArXiv&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Peer review">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although the arXiv is not
       <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">
        peer reviewed
       </a>
       , a collection of moderators for each area review the
       <a href="/wiki/Electronic_submission" title="Electronic submission">
        submissions
       </a>
       ; they may recategorize any that are deemed off-topic,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mckinney_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-mckinney-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or reject submissions that are not scientific papers. The lists of moderators for many sections of the arXiv are publicly available,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but moderators for most of the physics sections remain unlisted.
      </p>
      <p>
       Additionally, an "endorsement" system was introduced in 2004 as part of an effort to ensure content is relevant and of interest to current research in the specified disciplines.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Under the system, for categories that use it, an author must be endorsed by an established arXiv author before being allowed to submit papers to those categories. Endorsers are not asked to review the paper for errors, but to check whether the paper is appropriate for the intended subject area.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mckinney_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-mckinney-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       New authors from recognized academic institutions generally receive automatic endorsement, which in practice means that they do not need to deal with the endorsement system at all. However, the endorsement system has attracted criticism for allegedly restricting scientific inquiry.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A majority of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-print" title="E-print">
        e-prints
       </a>
       are also submitted to
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">
        journals
       </a>
       for publication, but some work, including some very influential papers, remain purely as e-prints and are never published in a peer-reviewed journal. A well-known example of the latter is an outline of a proof of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thurston%27s_geometrization_conjecture" title="Thurston's geometrization conjecture">
        Thurston's geometrization conjecture
       </a>
       , including the
       <a href="/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_conjecture" title="Poincaré conjecture">
        Poincaré conjecture
       </a>
       as a particular case, uploaded by
       <a href="/wiki/Grigori_Perelman" title="Grigori Perelman">
        Grigori Perelman
       </a>
       in November 2002.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Perelman appears content to forgo the traditional peer-reviewed journal process, stating: "If anybody is interested in my way of solving the problem, it's all there [on the arXiv] – let them go and read about it".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite this non-traditional method of publication, other mathematicians recognized this work by offering the
       <a href="/wiki/Fields_Medal" title="Fields Medal">
        Fields Medal
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Prize_Problems" title="Millennium Prize Problems">
        Clay Mathematics Millennium Prizes
       </a>
       to Perelman, both of which he refused.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       While the arXiv does contain some dubious e-prints, such as those claiming to refute famous theorems or proving famous conjectures such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermat%27s_last_theorem" title="Fermat's last theorem">
        Fermat's last theorem
       </a>
       using only high-school mathematics, they are "surprisingly rare".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS">
          <span title="this source is 14 years old, when arXiv was only 11 years old (February 2016)">
           better source needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The arXiv generally re-classifies these works, e.g. in "General mathematics", rather than deleting them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Submission_formats">
        Submission formats
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ArXiv&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Submission formats">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Papers can be submitted in any of several formats, including
       <a href="/wiki/LaTeX" title="LaTeX">
        LaTeX
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PDF" title="PDF">
        PDF
       </a>
       printed from a
       <a href="/wiki/Word_processor" title="Word processor">
        word processor
       </a>
       other than TeX or LaTeX. The
       <a href="/wiki/Electronic_submission" title="Electronic submission">
        submission
       </a>
       is rejected by the arXiv software if generating the final PDF file fails, if any image file is too large, or if the total size of the submission is too large. arXiv now allows one to store and modify an incomplete submission, and only finalize the submission when ready. The time stamp on the article is set when the submission is finalized.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Access">
        Access
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ArXiv&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Access">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The standard access route is through the arXiv.org website or one of several mirrors. Several other interfaces and access routes have also been created by other un-associated organisations. These include the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Davis" title="University of California, Davis">
        University of California, Davis
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        front
       </i>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Web_portal" title="Web portal">
        web portal
       </a>
       that offers additional search functions and a more self-explanatory interface for arXiv.org, and is referred to by some mathematicians as (the) Front.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A similar function used to be offered by eprintweb.org, launched in September 2006 by the
       <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Physics" title="Institute of Physics">
        Institute of Physics
       </a>
       , and was switched off on June 30, 2014. Carnegie Mellon provides Table
       <sup>
        arXiv
       </sup>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a search engine for tables extracted from arXiv publications.
       <a href="/wiki/Google_Scholar" title="Google Scholar">
        Google Scholar
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Live_Search_Academic" title="Live Search Academic">
        Live Search Academic
       </a>
       (now defunct) can also be used to search for items in arXiv.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A full text and author search engine for arXiv is provided by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Scientillion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Scientillion (page does not exist)">
        Scientillion
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Finally, researchers can select sub-fields and receive daily e-mailings or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RSS_feed" title="RSS feed">
        RSS feeds
       </a>
       of all submissions in them.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_status_of_files">
        Copyright status of files
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ArXiv&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Copyright status of files">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Files on arXiv can have a number of different copyright statuses:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Some are
        <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">
         public domain
        </a>
        , in which case they will have a statement saying so.
       </li>
       <li>
        Some are available under either the
        <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons" title="Creative Commons">
         Creative Commons
        </a>
        3.0
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_licenses" title="Creative Commons licenses">
         Attribution-ShareAlike license
        </a>
        or the Creative Commons 3.0
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_licenses" title="Creative Commons licenses">
         Attribution-Noncommercial-ShareAlike license
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Some are copyright to the publisher, but the author has the right to distribute them and has given arXiv a non-exclusive irrevocable license to distribute them.
       </li>
       <li>
        Most are copyright to the author, and arXiv has only a non-exclusive irrevocable license to distribute them.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">
        Controversy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ArXiv&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Controversy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some authors have voiced concern over the lack of transparency in the arXiv academic peer-review process.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Demetris Christopoulos from the National and Kapodistrian University of Athens likens arXiv to a non-declared Journal without a known editor in chief, without a specific written policy regarding submitted papers, and that applies hidden censorship to all papers that do not fall within established scientific dogma.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
