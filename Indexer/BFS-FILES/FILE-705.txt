   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Helioseismology
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Helioseismology
       </b>
       is the process of inferring the internal structure and kinematics of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       from the propagation of seismic waves, particularly
       <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">
        acoustic
       </a>
       waves (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P_wave" title="P wave">
        p waves
       </a>
       ) and surface
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_wave" title="Gravity wave">
        gravity waves
       </a>
       (f waves).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deubner1984_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Deubner1984-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was developed by analogy to geoseismology (originally called simply
       <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">
        seismology
       </a>
       ), and subsequently there emerged
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroseismology" title="Asteroseismology">
        asteroseismology
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gough1985_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gough1985-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in which seismic waves are analysed to constrain the internal structures of other stars. Because the Sun is fluid, to a first approximation it cannot support shear waves (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-waves" title="S-waves">
        s-waves
       </a>
       ), unlike the
       <a href="/wiki/Seismic_wave" title="Seismic wave">
        seismic waves on Earth
       </a>
       . An exception is the magneto-acoustic waves which appear to be important only in the atmosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts1989_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Roberts1989-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The helioseismic waves are generated by the turbulence in the
       <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">
        convection zone
       </a>
       immediately beneath the Sun's surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keeley1977_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Keeley1977-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Certain frequencies are amplified by constructive interference, leading to resonance. In other words, the turbulence "rings" the sun like a bell. The resonant waves are reflected near the photosphere, the visible surface of the sun, where they can be observed. The oscillations are detectable in almost any time series of solar images, but are best observed by measuring the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">
        Doppler shift
       </a>
       of atmospheric absorption lines. Details of the propagation of the seismic waves through the Sun, inferred from the resonant frequencies, reveal the Sun's inner structure, allowing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrophysicist" title="Astrophysicist">
        astrophysicists
       </a>
       to develop an extremely detailed representation of the hydrostatic stratification
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jcd1985_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jcd1985-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the internal angular velocity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duvall1984_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-duvall1984-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schou1998_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-schou1998-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That has permitted the evaluation of the quadrupole moment,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duvall1984_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-duvall1984-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
         <math alttext="{\displaystyle J_{2}=1.8\times 10^{-7}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

             </mo>

              </mn>

                <!-- − -->
               </mo>

        </span>
        <img alt="{\displaystyle J_{2}=1.8\times 10^{-7}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd50feb093df0ec966fd0071d5cb0d8974800bd2" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:15.913ex; height:3.009ex;"/>
       </span>
       , and higher-order moments
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-antia2008_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-antia2008-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of the Sun's external gravitational potential. It is a more accurate and more robust procedure than trying to infer it from the oblateness of the visible disc.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fivian2008_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-fivian2008-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kuhn2012_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kuhn2012-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Together with measurements of the orbits of Mercury and of spacecraft, the result is consistent with General Relativity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro1976_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-shapiro1976-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Helioseismology has been able to rule out the possibility that the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_neutrino_problem" title="Solar neutrino problem">
        solar neutrino problem
       </a>
       was a result of incorrect static models of the interior of the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gough1991_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gough1991-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bahcall1997_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bahcall1997-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gough1999_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gough1999-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Features revealed by helioseismology include that the outer convective zone and the inner radiative zone rotate differently, which is thought by some to generate the main magnetic field at least in the outer layers of the Sun by a
       <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">
        dynamo effect
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson2003_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Thompson2003-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Broadly speaking, the angular velocity of the convection zone decreases from equator to the poles, varying only weakly with depth; the radiative envelope beneath rotates almost uniformly. These two regions are separated by a shear layer called the
       <a href="/wiki/Tachocline" title="Tachocline">
        tachocline
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which is too thin to be resolved directly by seismological analysis alone. The convective zone has "
       <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">
        jet streams
       </a>
       " of plasma (called torsional oscillations) thousands of kilometers below the surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The jet streams form broad fronts at the equator, breaking into smaller cyclonic storms at high latitudes. Torsional oscillations are the time variation in solar
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_rotation" title="Differential rotation">
        differential rotation
       </a>
       . They are alternating bands of faster and slower rotation. So far there is no generally accepted theoretical explanation for them, even though a close relation to the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       is evident, as they have a period of eleven years, as was known since they were first observed in 1980.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Helioseismology can also be used to image the far side of the Sun from the Earth,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braun2001_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Braun2001-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       including
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspots
       </a>
       . In simple terms, sunspots both absorb and deflect helioseismic waves, causing a seismic deficit where next they encounter the photosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To facilitate spaceweather forecasting, seismic images of the central portion of the solar far side have been produced nearly continuously since late 2000 by analysing data from the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO spacecraft
       </a>
       , and since 2001 the entire far side has been imaged with these data.
      </p>

      </p>

         instrument between 19 February and 25 March 1996. The horizontal axis is frequency in millihertz, or thousandths of a hertz (mHz); the vertical axis is power density. The "5-minute oscillation" is the series of p-mode lines on the right between about 2 and 7 mHz.

      <p>
       Individual oscillations in the Sun are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damping" title="Damping">
        damped
       </a>
       ; in the absence of continual excitation they would die out in a few days. Resonating interference between propagating waves produces global
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standing_waves" title="Standing waves">
        standing waves
       </a>
       , known also as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_modes" title="Normal modes">
        normal modes
       </a>
       . Analysis of these modes constitutes the discipline of
       <i>
        global helioseismology
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Solar oscillation modes are divided up into three basic categories, according to their dominant restoring force: pressure dominates in p modes, and
       <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">
        buoyancy
       </a>
       in gravity modes, both internal (g modes) and surface (f modes):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         p-mode
        </b>
        dynamics is determined by the variation of the speed of sound inside the sun. Oscillations with amplitudes great enough to be detectable have frequencies between about 1 and 5 mHz, and are particularly strong in the 2-4 mHz range, where they are often referred to as "5-minute oscillations". (Note: 5 minutes per cycle is 1/300 cycles per second = 3.33 mHz.) At the solar surface, individual p modes have velocity amplitudes of order 10 cm/s, implying displacement amplitudes of a few meters and causing intensity fluctuations of a few parts per million, and are readily detectable with
        <a href="/wiki/Dopplergraph" title="Dopplergraph">
         Doppler imaging
        </a>
        or sensitive
        <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
         spectral line
        </a>
        intensity imaging. Thousands of p modes of high and intermediate degree l (see
        <a href="#Analysis_of_oscillation_data">
         below
        </a>
        for the wavenumber degree l) have been detected by both GONG and the Michelson Doppler Imager (MDI) instrument aboard the
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
         SOHO spacecraft
        </a>
        , with those of degree l below 200 clearly separated and higher degree modes ridged together.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
         <a href="#cite_note-23">
          [23]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Modes of very low degree have been observed most successfully in light integrated over the entire image of the Sun, by both ground-based networks of observatories such as BiSON
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
         <a href="#cite_note-24">
          [24]
         </a>
        </sup>
        distributed about the world to obtain continuous temporal coverage, and by the GOLF instrument aboard the
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
         SOHO spacecraft
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
         <a href="#cite_note-25">
          [25]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         g modes
        </b>
        are standing internal gravity waves whose principal restoring force is negative buoyancy of vertically displaced material, hence the name "g mode". They are of relatively low frequency (0-0.4 mHz). They are confined either to the interior of the sun below the convection zone (the inner 70 per cent by radius), or to the atmosphere. Because they cannot propagate through convectively unstable regions (in which the temperature gradient exceeds the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Adiabatic lapse rate">
         adiabatic lapse rate
        </a>
        , buoyancy is positive and the force on a displaced element of fluid is not restoring), the former are practically unobservable at the surface. The g modes are said to be
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evanescent_wave" title="Evanescent wave">
         evanescent
        </a>
        in the convection zone, and are thought to have residual velocity amplitudes of only millimeters per second at the photosphere, though perhaps they are more prominent as temperature perturbations.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
         <a href="#cite_note-26">
          [26]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Since the 1980s, there have been several claims of g-mode detection, including one claimed in 2007 using the GOLF data.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
         <a href="#cite_note-27">
          [27]
         </a>
        </sup>
        At the GONG2008 / SOHO XXI conference held in Boulder, the
        <a href="/wiki/Phoebus_group" title="Phoebus group">
         Phoebus group
        </a>
        reported that it could not confirm these findings, putting an upper limit of 3 mm/s on g-mode amplitudes, right at the detection limit of the GOLF instrument. The
        <a href="/wiki/Phoebus_group" title="Phoebus group">
         Phoebus group
        </a>
        has recently published a review of the current state of g-mode knowledge.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
         <a href="#cite_note-28">
          [28]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         f modes
        </b>
        are surface gravity waves, and, aside from the modes of lowest degree l, are confined to the near-surface layers of the Sun, penetrating to a depth of about R/l beneath the photosphere, where R is the radius of the Sun. The frequencies of high-degree f modes are determined essentially by just the surface gravity and the horizontal wavelength, and depend only very weakly on the structure of the Sun. Their terrestrial counterparts are
        <a href="/wiki/Stokes_wave" title="Stokes wave">
         deep water waves
        </a>
        , and in the limit of high degree l they share the same dispersion relation. Deviations from that limiting relation provide information principally about the density stratification of the surface layers of the Sun
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
         <a href="#cite_note-29">
          [29]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Doppler shifts brought about by advection have been measured by MDI (see
        <a href="#Analysis_of_oscillation_data">
         below
        </a>
        ), to set constraints on the horizontal subsurface flow
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corbard2002_30-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Corbard2002-30">
          [30]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Inversion">
        Inversion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Helioseismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inversion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:299px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tachocline.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="314" data-file-width="297" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/Tachocline.gif" width="297"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tachocline.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Internal rotation in the Sun, showing differential rotation in the outer convective region and almost uniform rotation in the central radiative region. The transition between these regions is called the tachocline.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Information about helioseismic waves (such as mode frequencies and frequency-splitting) collected by transforming the oscillation data can be used to infer numerical details of internal features of the Sun such as the internal sound speed and the internal
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_rotation" title="Differential rotation">
        differential rotation
       </a>
       . Equations and analytic relations such as integrals can be manipulated to relate the desired internal properties to the transformed data. The numerical methods used are adapted to the particular internal features examined so as to extract the maximum amount of information, with the least error, from the oscillations about the internal features. This process is termed
       <i>
        helioseismic inversion
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       As an example in slightly more detail, the oscillation frequency splitting can be related, via an integral, to the angular velocity within the sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalsgaard2003_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dalsgaard2003-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Helioseismic observations reveal the inner uniformly rotating zone and the
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_rotation" title="Differential rotation">
        differentially rotating
       </a>
       envelope of the Sun, roughly corresponding to the
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation_zone" title="Radiation zone">
        radiation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">
        convection
       </a>
       zones, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson2003_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Thompson2003-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       See the diagram on the right. The transition layer is called the
       <a href="/wiki/Tachocline" title="Tachocline">
        tachocline
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Helioseismic_dating">
        Helioseismic dating
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Helioseismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Helioseismic dating">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The age of the sun can be inferred with helioseismic studies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dziembowskietal1999_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dziembowskietal1999-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gough2001_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gough2001-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bonannoetal2002_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bonannoetal2002-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goughetal2011_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-goughetal2011-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is because the propagation of acoustic waves deep within the sun depends on the composition of the sun, in particular the relative abundance of helium and hydrogen in the core. Since the sun has been fusing hydrogen into helium throughout its lifetime, the present day abundance of helium in the core can be used to infer the age of the sun, using
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_structure" title="Stellar structure">
        numerical models of stellar evolution
       </a>
       applied to the Sun (
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_solar_model" title="Standard solar model">
        standard solar model
       </a>
       ). This method provides verification of the age of the solar system gathered from the radiometric dating of meteorites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guenther1995_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guenther1995-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Local_helioseismology">
        Local helioseismology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Helioseismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Local helioseismology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The goal of
       <i>
        local helioseismology
       </i>
       , a term first used in 1993,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is to interpret the full wave field observed at the surface, not just the mode (more precisely,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenmode" title="Eigenmode">
        eigenmode
       </a>
       ) frequencies. Another way to look at it, is that global helioseismology studies standing waves of the entire Sun and local helioseismology studies propagating waves in parts of the Sun. A variety of solar phenomena are being studied, including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">
        sunspots
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Plage_(astronomy)" title="Plage (astronomy)">
        plage
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Supergranulation" title="Supergranulation">
        supergranulation
       </a>
       , giant cell convection, magnetically active region evolution,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meridional" title="Meridional">
        meridional
       </a>
       circulation, and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_rotation" title="Solar rotation">
        solar rotation
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Local helioseismology provides a three-dimensional view of the solar interior, which is important to understand large-scale flows, magnetic structures, and their interactions in the solar interior.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are many techniques used in this new and expanding field, which include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Fourier–Hankel spectral method, first introduced by Braun and Duvall,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braun1987_40-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Braun1987-40">
          [40]
         </a>
        </sup>
        was originally used to search for wave absorption by sunspots.
       </li>
       <li>
        Ring-diagram analysis, first introduced by F. Hill,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
         <a href="#cite_note-41">
          [41]
         </a>
        </sup>
        is used to infer the speed and direction of horizontal flows below the solar surface by observing the Doppler shifts of ambient acoustic waves from power spectra of solar oscillations computed over patches of the solar surface (typically 15° × 15°). Thus ring analysis is a generalization of global helioseismology applied to local areas on the Sun (as opposed to half of the Sun). For example, sound speed and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_index" title="Adiabatic index">
         adiabatic index
        </a>
        can be compared within magnetically active and inactive (quiet Sun) regions.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
         <a href="#cite_note-42">
          [42]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Time-distance helioseismology, introduced by Duvall et al.,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
         <a href="#cite_note-43">
          [43]
         </a>
        </sup>
        aims to measure and interpret the travel times of solar waves between any two locations on the solar surface. A travel time anomaly contains the seismic signature of buried inhomogeneities within the proximity of the ray path that connects two surface locations. An inverse problem must then be solved to infer the local structure and dynamics of the solar interior.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jensen2003_44-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Jensen2003-44">
          [44]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Helioseismic holography, introduced in detail by Lindsey and Braun for the purpose of far-side (magnetic) imaging,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braun2001_21-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Braun2001-21">
          [21]
         </a>
        </sup>
        a special case of phase-sensitive holography. The idea is to use the wavefield on the visible disk to learn about active regions on the far side of the Sun. The basic idea in helioseismic holography is that the wavefield, e.g., the line-of-sight Doppler velocity observed at the solar surface, can be used to make an estimate of the wavefield at any location in the solar interior at any instant in time. In this sense, holography is much like
        <a href="/wiki/Seismic_migration" title="Seismic migration">
         seismic migration
        </a>
        , a technique in geophysics that has been in use since the 1940s. As another example, this technique has been used to give a seismic image of a solar flare.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
         <a href="#cite_note-45">
          [45]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Acoustic holography, applied to MDI data, is ideal for the detection of sources and sinks of acoustic waves on the Sun. Braun and Fan
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
         <a href="#cite_note-46">
          [46]
         </a>
        </sup>
        discovered a region of lower acoustic emission in the 3 – 4 mHz frequency band which extends far beyond the sunspots (the ‘acoustic moat’). Acoustic moats extend beyond magnetic regions into the quiet Sun. In addition, Braun and Lindsey
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
         <a href="#cite_note-47">
          [47]
         </a>
        </sup>
        discovered high-frequency emission (‘acoustic glories’) surrounding active regions.
       </li>
       <li>
        Direct modelling, after Woodard.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woodard2002_48-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Woodard2002-48">
          [48]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Here the idea is to estimate subsurface flows from direct inversion of the frequency-wavenumber correlations seen in the wavefield in the Fourier domain. Woodard
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woodard2002_48-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Woodard2002-48">
          [48]
         </a>
        </sup>
        gave a practical demonstration of the ability of the technique to recover near-surface flows from the f-mode part of the spectrum.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This section is adapted from Laurent Gizon and Aaron C. Birch, "Local Helioseismology", Living Rev. Solar Phys. 2, (2005), 6.
       <a class="external text" href="http://solarphysics.livingreviews.org/Articles/lrsp-2005-6/" rel="nofollow">
        online article
       </a>
       (cited on November 22, 2009).
      </p>
