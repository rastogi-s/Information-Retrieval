   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Giant-impact hypothesis
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

         Artist's depiction of a collision between two planetary bodies. Such an impact between Earth and a
         <a href="/wiki/Theia_(planet)" title="Theia (planet)">
          Mars-sized object
         </a>
         likely formed the Moon.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        giant-impact hypothesis
       </b>
       , sometimes called the
       <b>
        Big Splash
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        the Theia Impact
       </b>
       suggests that the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       formed out of the debris left over from a collision between Earth and an astronomical body the size of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       , approximately 4.5 billion years ago, in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">
        Hadean
       </a>
       eon; about 20 to 100 million years after the solar system coalesced.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140907_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140907-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The colliding body is sometimes called
       <a href="/wiki/Theia_(planet)" title="Theia (planet)">
        Theia
       </a>
       , from the name of
       <a href="/wiki/Theia" title="Theia">
        the mythical Greek Titan
       </a>
       who was the mother of
       <a href="/wiki/Selene" title="Selene">
        Selene
       </a>
       , the goddess of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eapsl176_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-eapsl176-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Analysis of lunar rocks, published in a 2016 report, suggests that the impact may have been a direct hit, causing a thorough mixing of both parent bodies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YoungEtAl_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-YoungEtAl-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The giant-impact hypothesis is currently the favoured
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_hypothesis" title="Scientific hypothesis">
        scientific hypothesis
       </a>
       for the formation of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature412_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nature412-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Supporting evidence includes:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Earth's spin and the Moon's orbit have similar orientations.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mackenzie03_5-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-mackenzie03-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Moon samples indicate that
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_magma_ocean" title="Lunar magma ocean">
         the Moon's surface was once molten
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The Moon has a relatively small iron
        <a href="/wiki/Planetary_core" title="Planetary core">
         core
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The Moon has a lower density than Earth.
       </li>
       <li>
        Evidence exists of similar collisions in other star systems (that result in
        <a href="/wiki/Debris_disk" title="Debris disk">
         debris disks
        </a>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Giant collisions are consistent with the leading theories of the
        <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">
         formation of the Solar System
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The stable-isotope ratios of lunar and terrestrial rock are identical, implying a common origin.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiechert_6-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-wiechert-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       There remain several questions concerning the best current models of the giant-impact hypothesis, however.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The energy of such a giant impact is predicted to have heated Earth to produce a global
       <a href="/wiki/Magma_ocean" title="Magma ocean">
        "ocean" of magma
       </a>
       , and evidence of the resultant
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_differentiation" title="Planetary differentiation">
        planetary differentiation
       </a>
       of the heavier material sinking into Earth's mantle has been documented.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, as of 2015
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       there is no self-consistent model that starts with the giant-impact event and follows the evolution of the debris into a single moon. Other remaining questions include when the Moon lost its share of
       <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">
        volatile elements
       </a>
       and why
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       —which experienced giant impacts during its formation—does not host a similar moon.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1898,
       <a href="/wiki/George_Darwin" title="George Darwin">
        George Darwin
       </a>
       made the suggestion that the Earth and Moon were once a single body. Darwin's hypothesis was that a molten Moon had been spun from the Earth because of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force_(rotating_reference_frame)" title="Centrifugal force (rotating reference frame)">
        centrifugal forces
       </a>
       , and this became the dominant academic explanation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Binder_1974_53–76_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Binder_1974_53–76-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Using Newtonian mechanics, he calculated that the Moon had orbited much more closely in the past and was drifting away from the Earth. This drifting was later confirmed by
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        American
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federal_Space_Agency" title="Russian Federal Space Agency">
        Soviet
       </a>
       experiments, using
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Laser_Ranging_experiment" title="Lunar Laser Ranging experiment">
        laser ranging targets
       </a>
       placed on the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Nonetheless, Darwin's calculations could not resolve the mechanics required to trace the Moon backward to the surface of the Earth. In 1946,
       <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Aldworth_Daly" title="Reginald Aldworth Daly">
        Reginald Aldworth Daly
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
        Harvard University
       </a>
       challenged Darwin's explanation, adjusting it to postulate that the creation of the Moon was caused by an impact rather than centrifugal forces.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dalypaps_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dalypaps-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Little attention was paid to Professor Daly's challenge until a conference on satellites in 1974, during which the idea was reintroduced and later published and discussed in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Icarus_(journal)" title="Icarus (journal)">
         Icarus
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1975 by Drs.
       <a href="/wiki/William_Kenneth_Hartmann" title="William Kenneth Hartmann">
        William K. Hartmann
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Donald_R._Davis" title="Donald R. Davis">
        Donald R. Davis
       </a>
       . Their models suggested that, at the end of the planet formation period, several satellite-sized bodies had formed that could collide with the planets or be captured. They proposed that one of these objects may have collided with the Earth, ejecting refractory, volatile-poor dust that could coalesce to form the Moon. This collision could potentially explain the unique geological and geochemical properties of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icarus24_504_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-icarus24_504-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar approach was taken by Canadian astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Alastair_G._W._Cameron" title="Alastair G. W. Cameron">
        Alastair G. W. Cameron
       </a>
       and American astronomer
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_R._Ward&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William R. Ward (page does not exist)">
        William R. Ward
       </a>
       , who suggested that the Moon was formed by the tangential impact upon Earth of a body the size of Mars. It is hypothesized that most of the outer silicates of the colliding body would be vaporized, whereas a metallic core would not. Hence, most of the collisional material sent into orbit would consist of silicates, leaving the coalescing Moon deficient in iron. The more volatile materials that were emitted during the collision probably would escape the Solar System, whereas silicates would tend to coalesce.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpc7_120_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-alpc7_120-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Theia">
        <span id="Theia">
        </span>
        Theia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Theia_(planet)" title="Theia (planet)">
        Theia (planet)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The name of the hypothesized
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanet" title="Protoplanet">
        protoplanet
       </a>
       is derived from the mythical
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">
        titan
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Theia" title="Theia">
        Theia
       </a>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/θ/: 'th' in 'thigh'">
            θ
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       , who gave birth to the Moon goddess
       <a href="/wiki/Selene" title="Selene">
        Selene
       </a>
       . This designation was proposed initially by the English geochemist
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Halliday" title="Alexander Halliday">
        Alex N. Halliday
       </a>
       in 2000 and has become accepted in the scientific community.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eapsl176_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-eapsl176-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jrasc97_6_299_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jrasc97_6_299-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to modern theories of planet formation, Theia was part of a population of Mars-sized bodies that existed in the Solar System 4.5 billion years ago. One of the attractive features of the giant-impact hypothesis is that the formation of the Moon and Earth align; during the course of its formation, the Earth is thought to have experienced dozens of collisions with planet-sized bodies. The Moon-forming collision would have been only one such "giant impact" but certainly the last significant impactor event. The
       <a href="/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment" title="Late Heavy Bombardment">
        Late Heavy Bombardment
       </a>
       occurred later - approximately 3.9 billion years ago.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_model">
        Basic model
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Basic model">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bigsplashsimple.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="2000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Bigsplashsimple.png/220px-Bigsplashsimple.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Bigsplashsimple.png/330px-Bigsplashsimple.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Bigsplashsimple.png/440px-Bigsplashsimple.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bigsplashsimple.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Simplistic representation of the giant-impact hypothesis.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Astronomers think the collision between Earth and Theia happened at about 4.4 to 4.45
       <a href="/wiki/Bya" title="Bya">
        bya
       </a>
       ; about 100 million years after the
       <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">
        Solar System began to form
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soderman_2016_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Soderman_2016-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In astronomical terms, the impact would have been of moderate velocity. Theia is thought to have struck the Earth at an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblique_angle" title="Oblique angle">
        oblique angle
       </a>
       when the Earth was nearly fully formed. Computer simulations of this "late-impact" scenario suggest an impact angle of about 45° and an initial impactor velocity below 4 km/s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icarus168_2_433_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-icarus168_2_433-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However,
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">
        isotope
       </a>
       abundance in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_rock" title="Lunar rock">
        lunar rock
       </a>
       suggests "vigorous mixing" of Theia and Earth, indicating a steep impact angle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YoungEtAl_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-YoungEtAl-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Theia's iron
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_(geology)" title="Core (geology)">
        core
       </a>
       would have sunk into the young Earth's core, and most of Theia's
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       accreted onto the Earth's mantle. However, a significant portion of the mantle material from both Theia and the Earth would have been
       <a href="/wiki/Ejecta" title="Ejecta">
        ejected
       </a>
       into orbit around the Earth (if ejected with velocities between
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_speed" title="Orbital speed">
        orbital velocity
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">
        escape velocity
       </a>
       ) or into individual orbits around the sun (if ejected at higher velocities). The material in orbits around the Earth quickly coalesced into the Moon (possibly within less than a month, but in no more than a century). The material in orbits around the sun stayed on its
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler_orbit" title="Kepler orbit">
        Kepler orbits
       </a>
       , which are stable in space, and was thus likely
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_heavy_bombardment" title="Late heavy bombardment">
        to hit the earth-moon system
       </a>
       sometime later (because the Earth-Moon system's Kepler orbit around the sun also remains stable). Estimates based on
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">
        computer simulations
       </a>
       of such an event suggest that some twenty percent of the original mass of Theia would have ended up as an orbiting ring of debris around the Earth, and about half of this matter coalesced into the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Earth would have gained significant amounts of
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        mass
       </a>
       from such a collision. Regardless of the speed and tilt of the Earth's rotation before the impact, it would have experienced a day some five hours long after the impact, and the Earth's equator and the Moon's orbit would have become
       <a href="/wiki/Coplanarity" title="Coplanarity">
        coplanar
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-areps15_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-areps15-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Not all of the ring material would necessarily have been swept up right away; the thickened crust of the Far Side suggests the possibility that a second moon about 1,000-km in diameter formed in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange_point" title="Lagrange point">
        Lagrange point
       </a>
       of the Moon. The secondary moon was smaller than the present day Moon and was left in orbit for tens of millions of years. As the two moons migrated outward from the Earth, solar tidal effects would have made the Lagrange orbit unstable, resulting in a slow-velocity collision that "pancaked" the smaller moon onto what is now the Far Side.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This would cover and add material to the crust of the far side. Because of the thick crust of the far side, lunar magma cannot pierce through the crust there, causing less lunar maria while the near side of the Moon has a thin crust so that lunar magma can pierce through the crust resulting in the large maria we observe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
        Composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 2001, a team at the
       <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Institution_for_Science" title="Carnegie Institution for Science">
        Carnegie Institution of Washington
       </a>
       reported the surprising finding that the rocks from the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       carried an isotopic signature that was identical with rocks from Earth, and were different from almost all other bodies in the Solar System.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiechert_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-wiechert-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2014, a team in Germany reported that the Apollo samples had a slightly different isotopic signature from Earth rocks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herwartz2014_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Herwartz2014-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The difference was slight, but statistically significant. One possible explanation is that Theia formed near the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herwartz2014_BBC_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Herwartz2014_BBC-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Energetic_aftermath_theory">
        Energetic aftermath theory
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Energetic aftermath theory">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2007, researchers from the California Institute of Technology showed that the likelihood of Theia having an identical isotopic signature as the Earth was very small (less than 1 percent).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps2007_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ps2007-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They proposed that in the aftermath of the giant impact, while the Earth and the proto-lunar disk were molten and vaporized, the two reservoirs were connected by a common silicate vapour atmosphere, and that the Earth–Moon system became homogenized by convective stirring while the system existed in the form of a continuous fluid. Such an "equilibration" between the post-impact Earth and the proto-lunar disk is the only proposed scenario that explains the isotopic similarities of the Apollo rocks with rocks from the Earth's interior. For this scenario to be viable, however, the proto-lunar disk would have to endure for about 100 years. Work is ongoing to determine whether or not this is possible.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence">
        Evidence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Evidence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Indirect evidence for the giant impact scenario comes from rocks collected during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Moon_landing" title="Apollo Moon landing">
        Apollo Moon landings
       </a>
       , which show
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">
        isotope
       </a>
       ratios nearly identical to those of Earth. The highly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anorthositic" title="Anorthositic">
        anorthositic
       </a>
       composition of the lunar crust, as well as the existence of
       <a href="/wiki/KREEP" title="KREEP">
        KREEP
       </a>
       -rich samples, suggest that a large portion of the Moon once was molten; and a giant impact scenario could easily have supplied the energy needed to form such a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_magma_ocean" title="Lunar magma ocean">
        magma ocean
       </a>
       . Several lines of evidence show that if the Moon has an
       <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
        iron
       </a>
       -rich core, it must be a small one. In particular, the mean density, moment of inertia, rotational signature, and magnetic induction response of the Moon all suggest that the radius of its core is less than about 25% the radius of the Moon, in contrast to about 50% for most of the other
       <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">
        terrestrial
       </a>
       bodies. Appropriate impact conditions satisfying the angular momentum constraints of the Earth–Moon system yield a Moon formed mostly from the mantles of the Earth and the impactor, while the core of the impactor accretes to the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature412_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nature412-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is noteworthy that the Earth has the highest density of all the planets in the Solar system ; the absorption of the core of the impactor body renders this observation explicable, given the proposed properties of the early Earth and Theia.
      </p>
      <p>
       Comparison of the
       <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">
        zinc
       </a>
       isotopic composition of Lunar samples with that of Earth and
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       rocks provides further evidence for the impact hypothesis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paniello2012_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Paniello2012-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">
        Zinc
       </a>
       is strongly
       <a href="/wiki/Isotope_fractionation" title="Isotope fractionation">
        fractionated
       </a>
       when
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatilization" title="Volatilization">
        volatilized
       </a>
       in planetary rocks,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moynier2006_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moynier2006-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moynier2009_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moynier2009-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but not during normal
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous" title="Igneous">
        igneous
       </a>
       processes,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Othman2006_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Othman2006-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       so zinc abundance and isotopic composition can distinguish the two geological processes. Moon rocks contain more heavy isotopes of zinc, and overall less zinc, than corresponding igneous Earth or Mars rocks, which is consistent with zinc being depleted from the Moon through evaporation, as expected for the giant impact origin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paniello2012_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Paniello2012-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Collisions between ejecta escaping Earth's gravity and asteroids would have left impact heating signatures in stony meteorites; analysis based on assuming the existence of this effect has been used to date the impact event to 4.47 billion years ago, in agreement with the date obtained by other means.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bottke-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Warm silica-rich dust and abundant SiO gas, products of high velocity (&gt; 10 km/s) impacts between rocky bodies, have been detected by the
       <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">
        Spitzer Space Telescope
       </a>
       around the nearby (29
       <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">
        pc
       </a>
       distant) young (~12 My old) star
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HD172555" title="HD172555">
        HD172555
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Beta_Pictoris_moving_group" title="Beta Pictoris moving group">
        Beta Pictoris moving group
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj701_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-apj701-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A belt of warm dust in a zone between 0.25AU and 2AU from the young star
       <a href="/wiki/HD_23514" title="HD 23514">
        HD 23514
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">
        Pleiades
       </a>
       cluster appears similar to the predicted results of Theia's collision with the embryonic Earth, and has been interpreted as the result of planet-sized objects colliding with each other.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj675_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-apj675-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A similar belt of warm dust was detected around the star
       <a href="/wiki/BD_%2B20%C2%B0307" title="BD +20°307">
        BD +20°307
       </a>
       (HIP 8920, SAO 75016).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature436_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nature436-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Difficulties">
        Difficulties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Difficulties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This lunar origin hypothesis has some difficulties that have yet to be resolved. For example, the giant-impact hypothesis implies that a surface magma ocean would have formed following the impact. Yet there is no evidence that the Earth ever had such a magma ocean and it is likely there exists material that has never been processed by a magma ocean.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4045.pdf_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-4045.pdf-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_2">
        Composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are a number of compositional inconsistencies that need to be addressed.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The ratios of the Moon's volatile elements are not explained by the giant-impact hypothesis. If the giant-impact hypothesis is correct, they must be due to some other cause.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4045.pdf_33-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-4045.pdf-33">
          [33]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The presence of volatiles such as water trapped in lunar
        <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
         basalts
        </a>
        is more difficult to explain if the Moon was caused by an impact that would entail a catastrophic heating event.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature454_34-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-nature454-34">
          [34]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The iron oxide (FeO) content (13%) of the Moon, which is intermediate between Mars (18%) and the terrestrial mantle (8%), rules out most of the source of the proto-lunar material from the Earth's mantle.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taylor07_35-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-taylor07-35">
          [35]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        If the bulk of the proto-lunar material had come from an impactor, the Moon should be enriched in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siderophile_element" title="Siderophile element">
         siderophilic
        </a>
        elements, when, in fact, it is deficient in those.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jess114_36-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-jess114-36">
          [36]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The Moon's oxygen isotopic ratios are essentially identical to those of Earth.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiechert_6-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-wiechert-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Oxygen isotopic ratios, which may be measured very precisely, yield a unique and distinct signature for each solar system body.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott010312_37-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-scott010312-37">
          [37]
         </a>
        </sup>
        If a separate proto-planet
        <a href="/wiki/Theia" title="Theia">
         Theia
        </a>
        had existed, it probably would have had a different oxygen isotopic signature than Earth, as would the ejected mixed material.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonwalk_38-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-moonwalk-38">
          [38]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The Moon's
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanium_isotope" title="Titanium isotope">
         titanium isotope
        </a>
        ratio (
        <sup>
         50
        </sup>
        Ti/
        <sup>
         47
        </sup>
        Ti) appears so close to the Earth's (within 4 ppm), that little if any of the colliding body's mass could likely have been part of the Moon.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
         <a href="#cite_note-39">
          [39]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
         <a href="#cite_note-40">
          [40]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_a_Venusian_moon">
        Lack of a Venusian moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Lack of a Venusian moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       If the Moon was formed by such an impact, it is possible that other inner planets also may have been subjected to comparable impacts. A moon that formed around Venus by this process would have been unlikely to escape. If such a moon-forming event had occurred there, a possible explanation of why the planet does not have such a moon might be that a second collision occurred that countered the
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       from the first impact.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baas38_491_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-baas38_491-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another possibility is that the strong tidal forces from the Sun would tend to destabilize the orbits of moons around close-in planets. For this reason, if Venus's slow rotation rate began early in its history, any satellites larger than a few kilometres in diameter would likely have spiralled inwards and collided with Venus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icarus202_1_12_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-icarus202_1_12-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Simulations of the chaotic period of terrestrial planet formation suggest that impacts like those hypothesized to have formed the Moon were common. For typical terrestrial planets with a mass of 0.5 to 1 Earth masses, such an impact typically results in a single moon containing 4% of the host planet's mass. The inclination of the resulting moon's orbit is random, but this tilt affects the subsequent dynamic evolution of the system. For example, some orbits may cause the moon to spiral back into the planet. Likewise, the proximity of the planet to the star will also affect the orbital evolution. The net effect is that it is more likely for impact-generated moons to survive when they orbit more distant terrestrial planets and are aligned with the planetary orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis2011_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lewis2011-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_origin_of_Theia">
        Possible origin of Theia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Possible origin of Theia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BigSplashEnglish.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1063" data-file-width="400" height="585" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/BigSplashEnglish.svg/220px-BigSplashEnglish.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/BigSplashEnglish.svg/330px-BigSplashEnglish.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/BigSplashEnglish.svg/440px-BigSplashEnglish.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BigSplashEnglish.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         One suggested pathway for the Big Splash as viewed from the direction of the south pole
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 2004,
       <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
        Princeton University
       </a>
       mathematician
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Belbruno" title="Edward Belbruno">
        Edward Belbruno
       </a>
       and astrophysicist
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._Richard_Gott_III" title="J. Richard Gott III">
        J. Richard Gott III
       </a>
       proposed that Theia coalesced at the
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         5
        </sub>
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrangian point
       </a>
       relative to Earth (in about the same orbit and about 60° ahead or behind),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-belbruno-2005_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-belbruno-2005-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mps40_1115_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-mps40_1115-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       similar to a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trojan_asteroid" title="Trojan asteroid">
        trojan asteroid
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mackenzie03_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-mackenzie03-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Two-dimensional computer models suggest that the stability of Theia's proposed
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_(astronomy)" title="Trojan (astronomy)">
        trojan orbit
       </a>
       would have been affected when its growing mass exceeded a threshold of approximately 10% of the Earth's mass (the mass of Mars).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-belbruno-2005_44-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-belbruno-2005-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this scenario, gravitational perturbations by
       <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">
        planetesimals
       </a>
       caused Theia to depart from its stable Lagrangian location, and subsequent interactions with proto-Earth led to a collision between the two bodies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-belbruno-2005_44-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-belbruno-2005-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2008, evidence was presented that suggests that the collision may have occurred later than the accepted value of 4.53
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gya" title="Gya">
        Gya
       </a>
       , at approximately 4.48 Gya.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halliday-2008_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-halliday-2008-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A 2014 comparison of computer simulations with elemental abundance measurements in the Earth's mantle indicated that the collision occurred approximately 95 My after the formation of the Solar System.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jacobson-2014_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jacobson-2014-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It has been suggested that other significant objects may have been created by the impact, which could have remained in orbit between the Earth and Moon, stuck in Lagrangian points. Such objects may have stayed within the Earth–Moon system for as long as 100 million years, until the gravitational tugs of other planets destabilized the system enough to free the objects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ns080506_48-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ns080506-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A study published in 2011 suggested that a subsequent collision between the Moon and one of these smaller bodies caused the notable differences in physical characteristics between the two hemispheres of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature476_69_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nature476_69-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This collision, simulations have supported, would have been at a low enough velocity so as not to form a crater; instead, the material from the smaller body would have spread out across the Moon (in what would become its
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side
       </a>
       ), adding a thick layer of highlands crust.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahoo20110803_50-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-yahoo20110803-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The resulting mass irregularities would subsequently produce a gravity gradient that resulted in
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal locking
       </a>
       of the Moon so that today, only the near side remains visible from Earth. However, mapping by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravity_Recovery_and_Interior_Laboratory" title="Gravity Recovery and Interior Laboratory">
        GRAIL
       </a>
       mission has apparently ruled out this scenario.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modified_hypothesis">
        Modified hypothesis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modified hypothesis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The giant-impact hypothesis fails to properly explain the similar composition of Earth and the Moon. Especially, the indistinguishable relation of oxygen isotopes cannot be explained by the classical form of this hypothesis. According to research on the subject that is based on new simulations at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bern" title="University of Bern">
        University of Bern
       </a>
       by physicist Andreas Reufer and his colleagues, Theia collided directly with Earth instead of barely swiping it. The collision speed may have been higher than originally assumed, and this higher velocity may have totally destroyed Theia. According to this modification, the composition of Theia is not so restricted, making a composition of up to 50% water ice possible.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_hypotheses">
        Alternative hypotheses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Giant-impact_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alternative hypotheses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_the_Moon" title="Origin of the Moon">
        Origin of the Moon
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Other mechanisms that have been suggested at various times for the Moon's origin are that the Moon was spun off from the Earth's molten surface by
       <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force" title="Centrifugal force">
        centrifugal force
       </a>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Binder_1974_53–76_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Binder_1974_53–76-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that it was formed elsewhere and was subsequently
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_capture" title="Lunar capture">
        captured
       </a>
       by the Earth's gravitational field;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icarus24_256_52-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-icarus24_256-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or that the Earth and the Moon formed at the same time and place from the same
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">
        accretion disk
       </a>
       . None of these hypotheses can account for the high
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       of the Earth–Moon system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-areps15_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-areps15-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another hypothesis attributes the formation of the Moon to the impact of a large asteroid with the Earth much later than previously thought, creating the satellite primarily from debris from Earth. In this hypothesis, the formation of the Moon occurs 60–140 million years after the formation of the Solar System. Previously, the age of the Moon had been thought to be 4.527 ± 0.010 billion years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taylor1998_53-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-taylor1998-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The impact in this scenario would have created a magma ocean on Earth and the proto-Moon with both bodies sharing a common plasma metal vapour atmosphere. The shared metal vapour bridge would have allowed material from the Earth and proto-Moon to exchange and equilibrate into a more common composition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature450_1206_54-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nature450_1206-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngn20071219_55-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ngn20071219-55">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Yet another hypothesis proposes that the Moon and the Earth have formed together instead of separately like the giant-impact hypothesis suggests. The new model, developed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robin_M._Canup" title="Robin M. Canup">
        Robin M. Canup
       </a>
       , suggests that the Moon and the Earth have formed as a part of a massive collision of two planetary bodies, each larger than Mars, which then re-collided to form what we now call Earth. After the recollision, Earth was surrounded by a disk of material, which accreted to form the Moon. This hypothesis could explain facts that others do not.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SimultaneousFormation_56-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SimultaneousFormation-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
