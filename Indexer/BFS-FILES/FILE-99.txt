      <p>
       <b>
        Shi Shen
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
        石申
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">
        Wade–Giles
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile" xml:lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">
         <b>
          Shih Shen
         </b>
        </span>
       </i>
       , fl. 4th century BC) was a
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_people" title="Chinese people">
        Chinese
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">
        astronomer
       </a>
       and contemporary of
       <a href="/wiki/Gan_De" title="Gan De">
        Gan De
       </a>
       born in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_Wei" title="State of Wei">
        State of Wei
       </a>
       , also known as the
       <b>
        Shi Shenfu
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">
        Observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shi_Shen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Shi is credited with positioning the 121 stars found in the preserved texts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Shen also made the earliest surviving deliberate sunspot observation,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       sometimes erroneously credited to Gan De. He assumed that these spots were
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipses
       </a>
       that began at the center of the sun and spread outward. Although he was wrong, he recognised the spots for what they were – solar phenomena.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       His works included the 8-volume
       <i>
        Astronomy
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the one-volume
       <i>
        Celestial Map
       </i>
       and the one-volume
       <i>
        Star Catalogue of Shi
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The latter two are now believed to be written by his school followers. Most of his works did not survive intact, but a few of his crucial writings were preserved in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Treatise_on_Astrology_of_the_Kaiyuan_Era" title="Treatise on Astrology of the Kaiyuan Era">
         Treatise on Astrology of the Kaiyuan Era
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Books">
        Books
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shi_Shen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Books">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Shi Shen wrote the
       <i>
        Shi Shen Astronomy
       </i>
       (石申天文, Shi Shen Tianwen).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">
        Influence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shi_Shen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Gan_De" title="Gan De">
        Gan De
       </a>
       and Shi Shen are widely quoted across a number of astronomical texts after their time, though they should not be confused with other books sharing similar names that were not written by them. One example is the
       <i>
        Star Manual of the Masters Gan and Shi
       </i>
       (甘石星經, Gan Shi Xingjing), which was actually compiled by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ma_Xian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ma Xian (page does not exist)">
        Ma Xian
       </a>
       (馬顯) circa 579 AD as an appendix to a calendar treatise.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The crater
       <a href="/wiki/Shi_Shen_(crater)" title="Shi Shen (crater)">
        Shi Shen
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       is named after him.
      </p>
