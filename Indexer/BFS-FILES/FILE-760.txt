      <p>
       A
       <b>
        caldera
       </b>
       is a large
       <a href="/wiki/Cauldron" title="Cauldron">
        cauldron
       </a>
       -like
       <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">
        depression
       </a>
       that forms following the evacuation of a
       <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">
        magma chamber
       </a>
       /reservoir. When large volumes of magma are erupted over a short time, structural support for the crust above the magma chamber is lost. The ground surface then collapses downward into the partially emptied magma chamber, leaving a massive depression at the surface (from one to dozens of kilometers in diameter). Although sometimes described as a
       <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_crater" title="Volcanic crater">
        crater
       </a>
       , the feature is actually a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">
        sinkhole
       </a>
       , as it is formed through subsidence and collapse rather than an explosion or impact. Only seven known caldera-forming collapses have occurred since the start of the 20th century, most recently at
       <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1r%C3%B0arbunga" title="Bárðarbunga">
        Bárðarbunga
       </a>
       volcano in Iceland.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The word comes from
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caldera#Spanish" title="wikt:caldera">
          caldera
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caldaria#Latin" title="wikt:caldaria">
          caldaria
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       , meaning "cooking pot". In some texts the English term
       <i>
        cauldron
       </i>
       is also used. The term
       <i>
        caldera
       </i>
       was introduced into the geological vocabulary by the German geologist
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopold_von_Buch" title="Leopold von Buch">
        Leopold von Buch
       </a>
       when he published his memoirs of his 1815 visit to the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-note_1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-note_1-2">
         [note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       where he first saw the Las Cañadas caldera on
       <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">
        Tenerife
       </a>
       , with Montaña
       <a href="/wiki/Teide" title="Teide">
        Teide
       </a>
       dominating the landscape, and then the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caldera_de_Taburiente" title="Caldera de Taburiente">
        Caldera de Taburiente
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/La_Palma" title="La Palma">
        La Palma
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Caldera_formation">
        Caldera formation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Caldera formation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Origin_of_volcanic_caldera_via_analogue_model.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="396" data-file-width="594" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Origin_of_volcanic_caldera_via_analogue_model.gif/220px-Origin_of_volcanic_caldera_via_analogue_model.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Origin_of_volcanic_caldera_via_analogue_model.gif/330px-Origin_of_volcanic_caldera_via_analogue_model.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Origin_of_volcanic_caldera_via_analogue_model.gif/440px-Origin_of_volcanic_caldera_via_analogue_model.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Origin_of_volcanic_caldera_via_analogue_model.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Animation of analogue experiment showing origin of volcanic caldera in box filled with flour.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Toba_zoom.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Toba_zoom.jpg/220px-Toba_zoom.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Toba_zoom.jpg/330px-Toba_zoom.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Toba_zoom.jpg/440px-Toba_zoom.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Toba_zoom.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landsat" title="Landsat">
          Landsat
         </a>
         image of
         <a href="/wiki/Lake_Toba" title="Lake Toba">
          Lake Toba
         </a>
         , on the island of
         <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">
          Sumatra
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
          Indonesia
         </a>
         (100 km/62 mi long and 30 km/19 mi wide, one of the world's largest calderas). A
         <a href="/wiki/Resurgent_dome" title="Resurgent dome">
          resurgent dome
         </a>
         formed the island of
         <a href="/wiki/Samosir" title="Samosir">
          Samosir
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A collapse is triggered by the emptying of the
       <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">
        magma chamber
       </a>
       beneath the volcano, sometimes as the result of a large explosive
       <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
        volcanic eruption
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/1815_eruption_of_Mount_Tambora" title="1815 eruption of Mount Tambora">
        Tambora
       </a>
       in 1815), but also during effusive eruptions on the flanks of a volcano (see
       <a href="/wiki/Piton_de_la_Fournaise" title="Piton de la Fournaise">
        Piton de la Fournaise
       </a>
       in 2007) or in a connected fissure system (see
       <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1r%C3%B0arbunga" title="Bárðarbunga">
        Bárðarbunga
       </a>
       in 2014–2015). If enough
       <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">
        magma
       </a>
       is ejected, the emptied chamber is unable to support the weight of the volcanic edifice above it. A roughly circular
       <a href="/wiki/Fracture_(geology)" title="Fracture (geology)">
        fracture
       </a>
       , the "ring fault", develops around the edge of the chamber. Ring fractures serve as feeders for fault
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrusion" title="Intrusion">
        intrusions
       </a>
       which are also known as
       <a href="/wiki/Ring_dike" title="Ring dike">
        ring dykes
       </a>
       . Secondary volcanic vents may form above the ring fracture. As the magma chamber empties, the center of the volcano within the ring fracture begins to collapse. The collapse may occur as the result of a single cataclysmic eruption, or it may occur in stages as the result of a series of eruptions. The total area that collapses may be hundreds or thousands of square kilometers.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mineralization">
        Mineralization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mineralization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some calderas are known to host rich
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ore_deposit" title="Ore deposit">
        ore deposits
       </a>
       . One of the world's best-preserved
       <a href="/wiki/Mineralization_(geology)" title="Mineralization (geology)">
        mineralized
       </a>
       calderas is the
       <a href="/wiki/Sturgeon_Lake_Caldera" title="Sturgeon Lake Caldera">
        Sturgeon Lake Caldera
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Ontario" title="Northwestern Ontario">
        northwestern Ontario
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       , which formed during the
       <a href="/wiki/Neoarchean" title="Neoarchean">
        Neoarchean
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Era_(geology)" title="Era (geology)">
        era
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       about 2,700 million years ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Explosive_caldera_eruptions">
        Explosive caldera eruptions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Explosive caldera eruptions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Explosive_eruption" title="Explosive eruption">
        Explosive eruption
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       If the
       <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">
        magma
       </a>
       is rich in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">
        silica
       </a>
       , the caldera is often filled in with
       <a href="/wiki/Ignimbrite" title="Ignimbrite">
        ignimbrite
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tuff" title="Tuff">
        tuff
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">
        rhyolite
       </a>
       , and other
       <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">
        igneous rocks
       </a>
       . Silica-rich magma has a high
       <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
        viscosity
       </a>
       , and therefore does not flow easily like
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       . As a result, gases tend to become trapped at high pressure within the magma. When the magma approaches the surface of the Earth, the rapid off-loading of overlying material causes the trapped gases to decompress rapidly, thus triggering explosive destruction of the magma and spreading
       <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">
        volcanic ash
       </a>
       over wide areas. Further
       <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
        lava
       </a>
       flows may be erupted.
      </p>
      <p>
       If volcanic activity continues, the center of the caldera may be uplifted in the form of a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Resurgent_dome" title="Resurgent dome">
         resurgent dome
        </a>
       </i>
       such as is seen at
       <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1n" title="Galán">
        Cerro Galán
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Toba" title="Lake Toba">
        Lake Toba
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_Caldera" title="Yellowstone Caldera">
        Yellowstone
       </a>
       , etc., by subsequent intrusion of magma. A
       <i>
        silicic
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">
         rhyolitic
        </a>
        caldera
       </i>
       may erupt hundreds or even thousands of cubic kilometers of material in a single event. Even small caldera-forming eruptions, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Krakatoa" title="Krakatoa">
        Krakatoa
       </a>
       in 1883 or
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">
        Mount Pinatubo
       </a>
       in 1991, may result in significant local destruction and a noticeable drop in temperature around the world. Large calderas may have even greater effects.
      </p>
      <p>
       When Yellowstone Caldera last erupted some 650,000 years ago, it released about 1,000 km
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       of material (as measured in dense rock equivalent (DRE)), covering a substantial part of
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       in up to two metres of debris. By comparison, when
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_St._Helens" title="Mount St. Helens">
        Mount St. Helens
       </a>
       erupted in 1980, it released ~1.2 km
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       (DRE) of ejecta. The ecological effects of the eruption of a large caldera can be seen in the record of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Toba" title="Lake Toba">
        Lake Toba
       </a>
       eruption in
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
        Indonesia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Toba">
        Toba
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Toba">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Toba" title="Lake Toba">
        Lake Toba
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       About 74,000 years ago, this Indonesian volcano released about 2,800 km
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Dense-rock_equivalent" title="Dense-rock equivalent">
        DRE
       </a>
       of ejecta, the largest known eruption within the
       <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">
        Quaternary
       </a>
       Period (last 1.8 million years) and the largest known explosive eruption within the last 25 million years. In the late 1990s,
       <a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">
        anthropologist
       </a>
       Stanley Ambrose
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       proposed that a
       <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_winter" title="Volcanic winter">
        volcanic winter
       </a>
       induced by this eruption reduced the
       <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">
        human
       </a>
       population to about 2,000–20,000 individuals, resulting in a
       <a href="/wiki/Population_bottleneck" title="Population bottleneck">
        population bottleneck
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        see
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Toba_catastrophe_theory" title="Toba catastrophe theory">
        Toba catastrophe theory
       </a>
       ). More recently several geneticists, including Lynn Jorde and
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Harpending" title="Henry Harpending">
        Henry Harpending
       </a>
       have proposed that the human species was reduced to approximately five to ten thousand people.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, there is no direct evidence that the theory is correct, and there is no evidence for any other animal decline or extinction, even in environmentally sensitive species.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is evidence that human habitation continued in
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       after the eruption.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The theory in its strongest form may be incorrect.
      </p>
      <p>
       Eruptions forming even larger calderas are known, especially
       <a href="/wiki/La_Garita_Caldera" title="La Garita Caldera">
        La Garita Caldera
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Mountains" title="San Juan Mountains">
        San Juan Mountains
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">
        Colorado
       </a>
       , where the 5,000-km
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Fish_Canyon_Tuff" title="Fish Canyon Tuff">
        Fish Canyon Tuff
       </a>
       was blasted out in a single major eruption about 27.8 million years ago.
      </p>
      <p>
       At some points in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_time" title="Geological time">
        geological time
       </a>
       , rhyolitic calderas have appeared in distinct clusters. The remnants of such clusters may be found in places such as the San Juan Mountains of Colorado (formed during the
       <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">
        Oligocene
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">
        Miocene
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">
        Pliocene
       </a>
       periods) or the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Francois_Mountain_Range" title="Saint Francois Mountain Range">
        Saint Francois Mountain Range
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">
        Missouri
       </a>
       (erupted during the
       <a href="/wiki/Proterozoic" title="Proterozoic">
        Proterozoic
       </a>
       ).
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-explosive_calderas">
        Non-explosive calderas
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Non-explosive calderas">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iss038e012569,_Caldera_Sollipulli.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Iss038e012569%2C_Caldera_Sollipulli.jpg/220px-Iss038e012569%2C_Caldera_Sollipulli.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Iss038e012569%2C_Caldera_Sollipulli.jpg/330px-Iss038e012569%2C_Caldera_Sollipulli.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Iss038e012569%2C_Caldera_Sollipulli.jpg/440px-Iss038e012569%2C_Caldera_Sollipulli.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iss038e012569,_Caldera_Sollipulli.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Sollipulli Caldera, located in central Chile near the border with Argentina, filled with ice. The volcano sits in the southern Andes Mountains within Chile's Parque Nacional Villarica.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
          <a href="#cite_note-9">
           [8]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Some volcanoes, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Shield_volcano" title="Shield volcano">
        shield volcanoes
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/K%C4%ABlauea" title="Kīlauea">
        Kīlauea
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa" title="Mauna Loa">
        Mauna Loa
       </a>
       (respectively the most active and second largest on Earth, are both on the island of
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">
        Hawaii
       </a>
       ), form calderas in a different fashion. The magma feeding these volcanoes is
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       which is silica poor. As a result, the magma is much less
       <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
        viscous
       </a>
       than the magma of a rhyolitic volcano, and the magma chamber is drained by large lava flows rather than by explosive events. The resulting calderas are also known as subsidence calderas, and can form more gradually than explosive calderas. For instance, the caldera atop
       <a href="/wiki/Fernandina_Island" title="Fernandina Island">
        Fernandina Island
       </a>
       underwent a collapse in 1968, when parts of the caldera floor dropped 350 meters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gvp_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gvp-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilauea" title="Kilauea">
        Kilauea
       </a>
       Caldera has an inner crater known as
       <a href="/wiki/Halemaumau_Crater" title="Halemaumau Crater">
        Halema‘uma‘u
       </a>
       , which has often been filled by a lava lake.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the April 2007 eruption of the
       <a href="/wiki/Piton_de_la_Fournaise" title="Piton de la Fournaise">
        Piton de la Fournaise
       </a>
       on the island of
       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">
        Réunion
       </a>
       , the floor of the main crater suddenly dropped about 300 m. This was attributed to the withdrawal of magma which was being erupted through a vent lower down on the southern flank of the volcano.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another process that may allow a caldera to form can occur if molten lava can escape through a breach on the caldera's rim.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_calderas">
        Extraterrestrial calderas
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial calderas">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Since the early 1960s, it has been known that volcanism has occurred on other planets and moons in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       . Through the use of manned and unmanned spacecraft, volcanism has been discovered on
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       , a satellite of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       . None of these worlds have
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        plate tectonics
       </a>
       , which contributes approximately 60% of the Earth's volcanic activity (the other 40% is attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">
        hotspot
       </a>
       volcanism).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wilson-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Caldera structure is similar on all of these planetary bodies, though the size varies considerably. The average caldera diameter on Venus is 68 km. The average caldera diameter on Io is close to 40 km, and the mode is 6 km;
       <a href="/wiki/Tvashtar_Paterae" title="Tvashtar Paterae">
        Tvashtar Paterae
       </a>
       is likely the largest caldera with a diameter of 290 km. The average caldera diameter on Mars is 48 km, smaller than Venus. Calderas on Earth are the smallest of all planetary bodies and vary from 1.6 to 80 km as a maximum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Moon">
        The Moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       has an outer shell of low-density crystalline rock that is a few hundred kilometers thick, which formed due to a rapid creation. The craters of the moon have been well preserved through time and were once thought to have been the result of extreme volcanic activity, but actually were formed by meteorites, nearly all of which took place in the first few hundred million years after the Moon formed. Around 500 million years afterward, the Moon's mantle was able to be extensively melted due to the decay of radioactive elements. Massive basaltic eruptions took place generally at the base of large impact craters. Also, eruptions may have taken place due to a magma reservoir at the base of the crust. This forms a dome, possibly the same morphology of a shield volcano where calderas universally are known to form.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wilson-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although caldera-like structures are rare on the Moon, they are not completely absent. The
       <a href="/wiki/Compton%E2%80%93Belkovich_Thorium_Anomaly" title="Compton–Belkovich Thorium Anomaly">
        Compton-Belkovich Volcanic Complex
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side of the Moon
       </a>
       is thought to be a caldera, possibly an
       <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_flow" title="Pyroclastic flow">
        ash-flow
       </a>
       caldera.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Compton-Belkovich_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Compton-Belkovich-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mars">
        Mars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Volcanology_of_Mars" title="Volcanology of Mars">
        Volcanology of Mars
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The volcanic activity of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       is concentrated in two major provinces:
       <a href="/wiki/Tharsis" title="Tharsis">
        Tharsis
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Elysium_(volcanic_province)" title="Elysium (volcanic province)">
        Elysium
       </a>
       . Each province contains a series of giant shield volcanoes that are similar to what we see on Earth and likely are the result of mantle
       <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">
        hot spots
       </a>
       . The surfaces are dominated by lava flows, and all have one or more collapse calderas.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wilson-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Mars has the largest volcano in the Solar System,
       <a href="/wiki/Olympus_Mons" title="Olympus Mons">
        Olympus Mons
       </a>
       , which is more than three times the height of Mount Everest, with a diameter of 520 km (323 miles). The summit of the mountain has six nested calderas.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Venus">
        Venus
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Venus">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Volcanology_of_Venus" title="Volcanology of Venus">
        Volcanology of Venus
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Because there is no
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        plate tectonics
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       , heat is mainly lost by conduction through the
       <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">
        lithosphere
       </a>
       . This causes enormous lava flows, accounting for 80% of Venus' surface area. Many of the mountains are large
       <a href="/wiki/Shield_volcano" title="Shield volcano">
        shield volcanoes
       </a>
       that range in size from 150–400 km in diameter and 2–4 km high. More than 80 of these large shield volcanoes have summit calderas averaging 60 km across.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_11-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wilson-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Io">
        Io
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caldera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Io">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Volcanology_of_Io" title="Volcanology of Io">
        Volcanology of Io
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Io, unusually, is heated by solid flexing due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal
       </a>
       influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       and Io's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       with neighboring large moons
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       , which keeps its orbit slightly
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentric
       </a>
       . Unlike any of the planets mentioned, Io is continuously volcanically active. For example, the NASA
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft detected nine erupting volcanoes while passing Io in 1979. Io has many calderas with diameters tens of kilometers across.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_11-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wilson-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
