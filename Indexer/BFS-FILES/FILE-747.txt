      <p>
       <b>
        Selenography
       </b>
       is the study of the surface and physical features of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . Historically, the principal concern of selenographists was the mapping and naming of the lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        maria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        craters
       </a>
       , mountain ranges, and other various features. This task was largely finished when high resolution images of the
       <a href="/wiki/Near_side_of_the_Moon" title="Near side of the Moon">
        near
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far
       </a>
       sides of the Moon were obtained by orbiting spacecraft during the early space era. Nevertheless, some regions of the Moon remain poorly imaged (especially near the poles) and the exact locations of many features (like
       <a href="/wiki/Crater_depth" title="Crater depth">
        crater depths
       </a>
       ) are uncertain by several kilometers. Today, selenography is considered to be a subdiscipline of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selenology" title="Selenology">
        selenology
       </a>
       , which itself is most often referred to as simply "lunar science." The word selenography is derived from the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       lunar deity Σελήνη
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Selene" title="Selene">
         Selene
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CF%81%CE%AC%CF%86%CF%89" title="wiktionary:γράφω">
        γράφω
       </a>
       graphō, "I write".
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_the_Moon" title="Exploration of the Moon">
        Exploration of the Moon
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The idea that the Moon was not perfectly smooth can be traced as far back as approximately 450 BC, when
       <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">
        Democritus
       </a>
       believed that there were "lofty mountains and hollow valleys" on the Moon. However, it was not until the end of the 15th century when serious study of selenography began. Around 1603,
       <a href="/wiki/William_Gilbert_(astronomer)" title="William Gilbert (astronomer)">
        William Gilbert
       </a>
       compiled the first lunar drawing based on naked-eye observations. Others soon followed, and when the
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       made its appearance, drawings were begun that at first were not very accurate, but soon became better as
       <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">
        optics
       </a>
       improved. In the early 18th century, the
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        librations
       </a>
       of the Moon were measured, showing that more than 50 percent of the lunar surface was visible to observers. In 1750,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Meyer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Johann Meyer (page does not exist)">
        Johann Meyer
       </a>
       produced the first reliable set of lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">
        coordinates
       </a>
       that would enable astronomers to locate features on the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon mapping became systematic in 1779 when
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Hieronymus_Schr%C3%B6ter" title="Johann Hieronymus Schröter">
        Johann Schröter
       </a>
       started making meticulous observations and measurements of the lunar features. The first published large map of the Moon, four sheets in size, was published in 1834 by
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Heinrich_von_M%C3%A4dler" title="Johann Heinrich von Mädler">
        Johann Heinrich von Mädler
       </a>
       , who followed this up by publishing a book entitled, "The Universal Selenography".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       All measurements were done by direct observation until March 1840, when
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J.W._Draper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J.W. Draper (page does not exist)">
        J.W. Draper
       </a>
       , using a five-inch reflector, produced a
       <a href="/wiki/Daguerreotype" title="Daguerreotype">
        daguerreotype
       </a>
       of the Moon, thus introducing photography to the astronomical world. At first, the images were of very poor quality, but like with the telescope two hundred years earlier, they very quickly became better. By 1890 lunar photography had become a recognized branch of astronomical research.
      </p>
      <p>
       The 20th century brought more advances to study of the Moon. In 1959, Russia's
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_3" title="Luna 3">
        Luna 3
       </a>
       sent back the first photographs of the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side of the Moon
       </a>
       , giving the world the first glimpse of the until-then unseen side of our
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       . The United States launched the
       <a href="/wiki/Ranger_program" title="Ranger program">
        Ranger
       </a>
       spacecraft between 1961 and 1965 to take photographs right down to the instant they impacted the surface, the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_program" title="Lunar Orbiter program">
        Lunar Orbiters
       </a>
       between 1966 and 1967 to photograph the Moon from orbit, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_program" title="Surveyor program">
        Surveyors
       </a>
       between 1966 and 1968 to take photos and soft land on the lunar surface. The Russian
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_programme" title="Lunokhod programme">
        Lunokhods
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_1" title="Lunokhod 1">
        1
       </a>
       (1970) and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_2" title="Lunokhod 2">
        2
       </a>
       (1973) traversed almost 50 km of the lunar surface, obtaining detailed images of the lunar surface. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_mission" title="Clementine mission">
        Clementine
       </a>
       spacecraft obtained the first near global map of the Moon's topography, as well as multispectral images. All of these missions sent back photographs that were of increasingly better resolution.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mapping_and_naming_the_Moon">
        Mapping and naming the Moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mapping and naming the Moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hevelius_Map_of_the_Moon_1647.jpg">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

         </div>
         Map of the Moon by

        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The oldest known illustration of the Moon was found in a
       <a href="/wiki/Passage_grave" title="Passage grave">
        passage grave
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Knowth" title="Knowth">
        Knowth
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/County_Meath" title="County Meath">
        County Meath
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">
        Ireland
       </a>
       . The tomb was
       <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">
        carbon dated
       </a>
       to 3330–2790 B.C.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">
        Leonardo da Vinci
       </a>
       made and annotated a small number of sketches of the Moon around 1500.
       <a href="/wiki/William_Gilbert_(astronomer)" title="William Gilbert (astronomer)">
        William Gilbert
       </a>
       made a drawing in the late 16th century, in which he gave names to a dozen surface features, published posthumously in a volume called
       <i>
        De Mondo Nostro Sublunari Philosophia Nova
       </i>
       . Following the invention of the telescope, drawings of the Moon were made by
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Harriot" title="Thomas Harriot">
        Thomas Harriot
       </a>
       (1609),
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
        Galileo Galilei
       </a>
       (1609) and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Scheiner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charles Scheiner (page does not exist)">
        Charles Scheiner
       </a>
       (1614).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first serious attempts at naming the features of the Earth's Moon as seen through a telescope were made by
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_van_Langren" title="Michael van Langren">
        Michel Florent van Langren
       </a>
       in 1645. His work is considered the first true map of the Moon, as it portrayed the various
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        craters
       </a>
       , and mountain peaks and ranges. Many of the features were given names that had a distinctly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">
        Catholic
       </a>
       religious character, using the period names of Catholic royalty for craters and the names of Catholic saints for the capes and promontories. The
       <i>
        maria
       </i>
       were given
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       names of seas and oceans. Minor craters were given the names of astronomers, mathematicians, and other notable scholars of the past and present periods.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Micrographia_Schem_38.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1683" data-file-width="1187" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Micrographia_Schem_38.jpg/220px-Micrographia_Schem_38.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Micrographia_Schem_38.jpg/330px-Micrographia_Schem_38.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Micrographia_Schem_38.jpg/440px-Micrographia_Schem_38.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Micrographia_Schem_38.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A study of the moon from
         <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hooke" title="Robert Hooke">
          Robert Hooke's
         </a>
         <a href="/wiki/Micrographia" title="Micrographia">
          Micrographia
         </a>
         , 1665
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1647,
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Hevelius" title="Johannes Hevelius">
        Johannes Hevelius
       </a>
       produced the rival work titled
       <i>
        Selenographia
       </i>
       , which formed the first lunar atlas. Hevelius ignored the nomenclature of Van Langren, and instead adopted the names of terrestrial features. These were mapped in a manner that corresponded to their place names on the Earth, particularly with respect to the ancient world as known to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Roman
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        Greek
       </a>
       civilizations. This work of Hevelius proved influential among European astronomers of the period, and the
       <i>
        Selenographia
       </i>
       served as the standard reference work for over a century.
      </p>
      <p>
       The modern scheme of lunar nomenclature was devised by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giambattista_Riccioli" title="Giambattista Riccioli">
        Giambattista Riccioli
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">
        Jesuit
       </a>
       priest and scholar who lived in northern
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       . His
       <i>
        Almagestum Novum
       </i>
       was published in 1651 as a defense of the Catholic views during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_Reformation" title="Counter Reformation">
        Counter Reformation
       </a>
       . In particular he argued against the views espoused by
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
        Galileo
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copernicus" title="Copernicus">
        Copernicus
       </a>
       that favored a heliocentric model with perfectly circular orbits. The work contained scientific reference material based on knowledge of the period and was widely used by Jesuit teachers of the time. However the only significant aspect of the work to survive to the present period is Riccioli's system of lunar nomenclature.
      </p>
      <p>
       The lunar illustrations in the
       <i>
        Almagestum Novum
       </i>
       were drawn by a fellow Jesuit teacher by the name of
       <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Maria_Grimaldi" title="Francesco Maria Grimaldi">
        Francesco Grimaldi
       </a>
       . The nomenclature was devised based on a subdivision of the visible lunar surface into octants, numbered in the Roman style from I through VIII. Octant I formed the northwest section, and subsequent octants proceeded in a clockwise direction aligned with the compass directions. Thus the octant VI lay to the south and would include
       <a href="/wiki/Clavius_(crater)" title="Clavius (crater)">
        Clavius
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tycho_(crater)" title="Tycho (crater)">
        Tycho
       </a>
       craters.
      </p>
      <p>
       The naming scheme had two components, the first used for the broad features of land and seas and the second for the craters. Riccioli used the names of various historical effects and weather conditions attributed to the Moon throughout history. Thus there were the seas of crises (Mare Crisium), serenity (Mare Serenitatis), and fertility (Mare Fecunditatis). There were also the seas of rain (Mare Imbrium), clouds (Mare Nubium), and cold (Mare Frigoris). These were given their names in the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       form.
      </p>
      <p>
       The continental areas between the seas were given comparable names, but were opposite the names used for the seas. Thus there were the lands of sterility (Terra Sterilitatis), heat (Terra Caloris), and liveliness (Terra Vitae). However these names for the highland regions are no longer used on recent maps. See
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_features_on_the_Moon#Terra" title="List of features on the Moon">
        List of features on the Moon#Terra
       </a>
       for a full list.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Schr%C3%B6ter_selenotopographische_fragmente_beispiel_karten.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1015" data-file-width="800" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Schr%C3%B6ter_selenotopographische_fragmente_beispiel_karten.png/170px-Schr%C3%B6ter_selenotopographische_fragmente_beispiel_karten.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Schr%C3%B6ter_selenotopographische_fragmente_beispiel_karten.png/255px-Schr%C3%B6ter_selenotopographische_fragmente_beispiel_karten.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Schr%C3%B6ter_selenotopographische_fragmente_beispiel_karten.png/340px-Schr%C3%B6ter_selenotopographische_fragmente_beispiel_karten.png 2x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Schr%C3%B6ter_selenotopographische_fragmente_beispiel_karten.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Samples of lunar maps in the
         <i>
          Selenetopographische Fragmente
         </i>
         by
         <a href="/wiki/Johann_Hieronymus_Schr%C3%B6ter" title="Johann Hieronymus Schröter">
          Johann Hieronymus Schröter
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Many of the craters were named based on the octant in which they were found. Octants I, II, and III were use primarily for names from ancient Greece, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
        Plato
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(mythology)" title="Atlas (mythology)">
        Atlas
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">
        Archimedes
       </a>
       . Toward the middle in octants IV, V, and VI were names from the period of the ancient
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_empire" title="Roman empire">
        Roman empire
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
        Julius Caesar
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cornelius_Tacitus" title="Cornelius Tacitus">
        Tacitus
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucius_Taruntius_Firmanus" title="Lucius Taruntius Firmanus">
        Taruntius
       </a>
       . Toward the bottom half of the map were placed scholars, writers, and philosophers from medieval Europe and Arabic regions.
      </p>
      <p>
       The outer extremes of octants V, VI, VII, as well as all of octant VIII were devoted to contemporaries of Riccioli. The last also contained features named for Copernicus, Kepler, and Galileo. These were "banished" here far from the "ancients", as a political gesture to the Catholic Church.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       A number of craters around the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Nectaris" title="Mare Nectaris">
        Mare Nectaris
       </a>
       were given the names of saints of the Catholic Church, following the tradition of Van Langren. These, however, were all connected in some aspect with astronomy. Later maps dropped the "St." from the names of these craters.
      </p>
      <p>
       Riccioli's system of nomenclature was widely adopted after the publication of his
       <i>
        Almagestum novum
       </i>
       , and many of the names remain in common use today. The system was scientifically inclusive and was considered elegant and poetic in style, so it appealed widely to the thinkers of the period. It was also readily extensible with new names following the same scheme. Thus it came to replace the nomenclature of Van Langren and Hevelius.
      </p>
      <p>
       Later astronomers and lunar mappers augmented the nomenclature with additional names of features. The most notable among these contributors was
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Hieronymus_Schr%C3%B6ter" title="Johann Hieronymus Schröter">
        Johann Schröter
       </a>
       who published a highly detailed map of the Moon in 1791: the
       <i>
        Selenotopografisches Fragmenten
       </i>
       . Schröter's adoption of Riccioli's often-used naming scheme effectively made it the standard system of lunar nomenclature. Riccioli's naming scheme was formally established as the doctrinal lunar nomenclature by a vote of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       in 1935, which gave standard names to 600 lunar features.
      </p>
      <p>
       The system was later expanded and updated by the IAU during the 1960s, but new designations were limited to the names of deceased scientists. After
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet
       </a>
       spacecraft photographed the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far_side_(Moon)" title="Far side (Moon)">
        far side
       </a>
       of the Moon, many of the newly discovered features were named after Soviet scientists and engineers. All subsequent names have been assigned by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       . Some craters were
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_lunar_craters_named_for_space_explorers" title="List of lunar craters named for space explorers">
        named for space explorers
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_craters">
        Satellite craters
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Satellite craters">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The nomenclature system for identifying satellite craters was originally devised by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Heinrich_M%C3%A4dler" title="Johann Heinrich Mädler">
        Johann Mädler
       </a>
       . The craters that surrounded a major crater were identified by means of a letter. These subsidiary craters were usually smaller than the crater with which they were associated, but there are some exceptions. The craters could be assigned letters A through Z, with I omitted. (As the large majority of crater names were masculine, the main craters were termed "
       <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">
        patronymic
       </a>
       " craters.)
      </p>
      <p>
       The assignment of the letters to satellite craters was originally a somewhat haphazard process. Letters were typically assigned to craters in order of significance rather than by their location. Precedence depended on the angle of illumination from the Sun at the time of the telescope observation, which could change over the course of the lunar day. In many cases the assignments would seemingly be made at random.
      </p>
      <p>
       In a number of cases the satellite crater would lie closer to a main crater with which it was not associated. To identify the patronymic crater, Mädler placed the identifying letter to the side of the feature midpoint closest to the associated main crater. This also had the advantage of allowing the names of the main craters to be omitted from the maps when identifying the subsidiary features.
      </p>
      <p>
       Over the course of time, many of the satellite craters were assigned an
       <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">
        eponym
       </a>
       by lunar observers. The process of naming lunar features was assumed by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       (IAU) in 1919. The commission for naming these features formally adopted the convention of using capital Roman letters to identify craters and valleys.
      </p>
      <p>
       When suitable maps of the far side of the moon became available by 1966, Ewen A. Whitaker assigned the names to satellite features based on the angle of the location relative to the main crater. A satellite crater located due north of the main crater was given the identifier 'Z'. The full 360° circle about the crater was then subdivided evenly into 24 parts, like a 24-hour clock. Each "hour" angle running clockwise was assigned a letter, beginning with 'A' at 1 o'clock. (The letters I and O were omitted, resulting in only 24 letters.) Thus a crater due south of the main crater was given the letter 'M'.
      </p>
