   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Eclipse season
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       An
       <b>
        eclipse season
       </b>
       is one of only two periods during a year when
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipses
       </a>
       can occur, due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        orbital inclination
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . Each season lasts for approximately 34 days and repeats just short of six months, thus two full eclipse seasons always occur each year. Either two or three eclipses happen each eclipse season. During the eclipse season, the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon#Inclination" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        inclination
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        Moon's orbit
       </a>
       is low, hence the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , Moon, and
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       become aligned straight enough (in
       <a href="/wiki/Syzygy_(astronomy)" title="Syzygy (astronomy)">
        syzygy
       </a>
       ) for an eclipse to occur.
      </p>
      <p>
       During each season, the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse#Types" title="Solar eclipse">
        type of solar eclipse
       </a>
       (whether
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Magnitude_(eclipse)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Magnitude (eclipse) (page does not exist)">
        total or annular
       </a>
       , as seen from the sublunar point) depends on the
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        apparent sizes
       </a>
       of the Sun and Moon, which is a function of the
       <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">
        elliptical distance
       </a>
       of Earth from the Sun and of the Moon from Earth, respectively, as seen from Earth's surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       If the Earth had a perfectly
       <a href="/wiki/Circular_orbit" title="Circular orbit">
        circular orbit
       </a>
       centered around the Sun, if the Moon's orbit was also perfectly circular and centered around the Earth, and if both orbits were
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coplanar" title="Coplanar">
        coplanar
       </a>
       (on the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        same plane
       </a>
       ) with each other, then two eclipses would happen every
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">
        lunar month
       </a>
       (29.53 days). A
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       would occur at every
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       every
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       , and all solar eclipses would be the same type.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Details">
        Details
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eclipse_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Details">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       An eclipse season is the only time during which the Sun (from the perspective of the Earth) is close enough to one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">
        Moon's nodes
       </a>
       to allow for an
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipse
       </a>
       to occur. During the season, whenever there is a full moon a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       will occur and whenever there is a new moon a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       will occur. If the Sun is close enough to a node, then a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_eclipse" title="Total eclipse">
        total eclipse
       </a>
       will occur. Each season lasts from 31 to 37 days, recurring about every 6 months. At least two (one solar and one lunar, in any order), and at most three eclipses (solar, lunar, then solar again, or vice versa), will occur during every eclipse season. This is because it is about 15 days (a
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_cycle#Eclipse_cycles" title="Eclipse cycle">
        fortnight
       </a>
       ) between full moon and new moon and vice versa. If there is an eclipse at the very beginning of the season, then there is enough time (30 days) for two more eclipses.
      </p>
      <p>
       In other words, because the eclipse season (34 days long on average) is longer than the
       <a href="/wiki/Month#Synodic_month" title="Month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       (one lunation, or the time for the Moon to return to a particular phase and about 29.5 days), the Moon will be new or full at least two, and up to three, times during the season. Eclipse seasons occur slightly shy of six months apart (successively occurring every 173.31 days - half of an
       <a href="/wiki/Year#Draconic_year" title="Year">
        eclipse year
       </a>
       ), the time it takes the Sun to travel from one node to the next along the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       . If the last eclipse of an eclipse season occurs at the very beginning of a calendar year, it is possible for a total of seven eclipses to occur since there is still time before the end of the calendar year for two full eclipse seasons, each having up to three eclipses.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-totality_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-totality-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">

        </a>
       </sup>

      </p>
