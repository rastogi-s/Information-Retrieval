      <p>
       <b>
        Duncan I. Steel
       </b>
       <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Astronomical Society">
        FRAS
       </a>
       (born 1955), is a British scientist born in
       <a href="/wiki/Midsomer_Norton" title="Midsomer Norton">
        Midsomer Norton
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somerset,_England" title="Somerset, England">
        Somerset
       </a>
       . Currently he lives in
       <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">
        Wellington
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       , but holds visiting positions as a Professor of
       <a href="/wiki/Astrobiology" title="Astrobiology">
        Astrobiology
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Buckingham" title="University of Buckingham">
        University of Buckingham
       </a>
       in England; as a Space Scientist at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA-Ames_Research_Center" title="NASA-Ames Research Center">
        NASA-Ames Research Center
       </a>
       in California; and as an Astronomer at
       <a href="/wiki/Armagh_Observatory" title="Armagh Observatory">
        Armagh Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">
        Northern Ireland
       </a>
       . Duncan is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_science" title="Space science">
        space science
       </a>
       authority who has worked with
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       to assess the threat of
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comet
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       collisions and investigate technologies to avert such impacts. He is also the author of four popular-level science books on space, and regularly writes for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">
         The Guardian
        </a>
       </i>
       and various other newspapers and magazines. He is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discoverer_of_minor_planets" title="Discoverer of minor planets">
        discoverer of minor planets
       </a>
       including the main-belt asteroid
       <a href="/wiki/9767_Midsomer_Norton" title="9767 Midsomer Norton">
        9767 Midsomer Norton
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-Discoverers_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPC-Discoverers-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Duncan attended the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">
        University of London
       </a>
       , studying as an undergraduate at
       <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_College" title="Queen Elizabeth College">
        Queen Elizabeth College
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">
        BSc
       </a>
       in Physics and Astrophysics, 1977) and also
       <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">
        University College
       </a>
       , and as a graduate student in
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
        astrophysics
       </a>
       at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Mary_College" title="Queen Mary College">
        Queen Mary College
       </a>
       (1977–78) and then at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_College_of_Science_and_Technology" title="Imperial College of Science and Technology">
        Imperial College of Science and Technology
       </a>
       , where he took an
       <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">
        MSc
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Diploma_of_Imperial_College" title="Diploma of Imperial College">
        DIC
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Applied_Optics" title="Applied Optics">
        Applied Optics
       </a>
       (1978–79). From September 1979 to January 1982 he worked at the
       <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Atmospheric_and_Space_Physics" title="Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics">
        Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_at_Boulder" title="University of Colorado at Boulder">
        University of Colorado at Boulder
       </a>
       on NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Venus_Orbiter" title="Pioneer Venus Orbiter">
        Pioneer Venus Orbiter
       </a>
       spacecraft. For the following three years he was at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Canterbury" title="University of Canterbury">
        University of Canterbury
       </a>
       in New Zealand, building a radar for meteor studies, being awarded a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">
        PhD
       </a>
       degree in 1985. Between 1985 and 1996 he was associated with the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Adelaide" title="University of Adelaide">
        University of Adelaide
       </a>
       , South Australia, undertaking research in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_meteorology" title="Radar meteorology">
        radar meteors
       </a>
       , and asteroid and comet dynamics. In 1987–88 he was a
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        European Space Agency
       </a>
       Research Fellow at
       <a href="/wiki/Lund_Observatory" title="Lund Observatory">
        Lunds Observatoriet
       </a>
       , Sweden. From 1990–95 he also worked at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Australian_Observatory" title="Anglo-Australian Observatory">
        Anglo-Australian Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Coonabarabran" title="Coonabarabran">
        Coonabarabran
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">
        New South Wales
       </a>
       , where he established and directed the first southern hemisphere program for the discovery and tracking of near-Earth asteroids. From 1988–99 he additionally ran his own companies, amongst them Spaceguard Australia Pty Ltd. He was Associate Professor of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Technology" title="Space Technology">
        Space Technology
       </a>
       at the Joule Physics Laboratory,
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Salford" title="University of Salford">
        University of Salford
       </a>
       in 1999–2003. From 2004–2012 he worked in
       <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">
        Canberra
       </a>
       for Ball Aerospace Australia and QinetiQ Pty Ltd, providing expert advice on technical matters to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Department_of_Defence" title="Australian Department of Defence">
        Australian Department of Defence
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       He has been involved in investigations on small bodies in the solar system using optical telescopes, meteor radar systems, and theoretical techniques to investigate their dynamical evolution. Amongst his scientific achievements have been the identification of the first asteroid spinning so fast that it must be a
       <a href="/wiki/Monolith" title="Monolith">
        monolith
       </a>
       , the first identification of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_dust" title="Interstellar dust">
        interstellar dust
       </a>
       entering the atmosphere, and an influx of comet-derived meteoroids ablating high in the atmosphere that appear to be made of tarry organics. He is also interested in the astronomy and
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_calendars" title="History of calendars">
        history of calendars
       </a>
       , and the life of
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Babbage" title="Charles Babbage">
        Charles Babbage
       </a>
       among many other things.
      </p>
      <p>
       He named the asteroids
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5263_Arrius" title="5263 Arrius">
        5263 Arrius
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/6828_Elbsteel" title="6828 Elbsteel">
        6828 Elbsteel
       </a>
       after his sons Harrison Callum Bertram Steel (b. 1992) and Elliot Lewis Barnaby Steel (b. 1995). Harry's asteroid couldn't be called Harrison because there was already one of those (George Harrison), and 'Arrius' was the title of a poem by
       <a href="/wiki/Catullus" title="Catullus">
        Catullus
       </a>
       that Duncan had to translate in Latin class at MNGS (about a Roman who dropped his aitches); whilst Elliot's asteroid couldn't be called that because there was already one named Eliot (for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T.S._Eliot" title="T.S. Eliot">
        T.S. Eliot
       </a>
       ).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_achievements">
        Other achievements
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Duncan_Steel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other achievements">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Duncan is a Fellow of the
        <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">
         Royal Astronomical Society
        </a>
        and a member of the
        <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
         International Astronomical Union
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        He has discovered a dozen numbered minor planets including the
        <a href="/wiki/9767_Midsomer_Norton" title="9767 Midsomer Norton">
         9767 Midsomer Norton
        </a>
        , in 1992, which he named for his home town.
       </li>
       <li>
        The Hungaria asteroid
        <a href="/wiki/4713_Steel" title="4713 Steel">
         4713 Steel
        </a>
        , discovered by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_McNaught" title="Robert McNaught">
         Robert McNaught
        </a>
        in 1989, is named after him.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-springer_4-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-springer-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        He also discovered the asteroids
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5263_Arrius" title="5263 Arrius">
         5263 Arrius
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/6828_Elbsteel" title="6828 Elbsteel">
         6828 Elbsteel
        </a>
        which were named for his sons by the International Astronomical Union. Amongst his other discoveries are: 10107 Kenny, named for his father; 16578 Essjayess, named for his mother (her initials are SJS); 24734 Kareness, named for his elder sister; 55815 Melindakim, named for his middle sister; 58196 Ashleyess, named for his youngest sister; and 69311 Russ, named for his brother. However, his favourite is
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7345_Happer" title="7345 Happer">
         7345 Happer
        </a>
        (discovered by
        <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._McNaught" title="Robert H. McNaught">
         Robert H. McNaught
        </a>
        ), which is named for the character Felix Happer in the movie
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Local_Hero" title="Local Hero">
          Local Hero
         </a>
        </i>
        , who really wanted a comet to be called after him.
       </li>
       <li>
        There is a robot named after Duncan (viz. Robot Steel) in
        <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
         Arthur C. Clarke
        </a>
        's novel
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Hammer_of_God_(Clarke_novel)" title="The Hammer of God (Clarke novel)">
          The Hammer of God
         </a>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        He has worked for both
        <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
         NASA
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
         European Space Agency
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Duncan is author of over 140 refereed scientific research papers and a thousand newspaper and magazine articles.
       </li>
       <li>
        He has appeared in many TV and radio programmes, including BBC TV's
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Sky_at_Night" title="The Sky at Night">
          The Sky at Night
         </a>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Duncan was Science Adviser for the two-hour documentary
        <i>
         Three Minutes to Impact
        </i>
        , which was awarded an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emmy" title="Emmy">
         Emmy
        </a>
        for Best Script in 1998.
       </li>
       <li>
        He was one of six foreign members of NASA's
        <a href="/wiki/Spaceguard" title="Spaceguard">
         Spaceguard
        </a>
        committee in 1991–92 and was the only non-US member of the Near-Earth Object Interception and Deflection committee.
       </li>
       <li>
        Duncan twice served as Vice-President of
        <a href="/wiki/The_Spaceguard_Foundation" title="The Spaceguard Foundation">
         The Spaceguard Foundation
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        He was a member of the British Delegation to the OECD Global Science Forum conference on Near-Earth Objects, Frascati, Italy, 2003.
       </li>
      </ul>
