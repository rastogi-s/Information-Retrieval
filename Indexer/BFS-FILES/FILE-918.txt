      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Vice
        </b>
       </i>
       is a print magazine and website focused on arts, culture, and news topics. Founded in 1994 in
       <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">
        Montreal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
        Quebec
       </a>
       , Canada, the magazine later expanded into
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_Media,_Inc." title="Vice Media, Inc.">
        Vice Media
       </a>
       , which consists of divisions including the magazine and website, a film production company, a record label, and a publishing
       <a href="/wiki/Imprint_(trade_name)" title="Imprint (trade name)">
        imprint
       </a>
       . As of February 2015, the magazine's Chief Creative Officer was Eddy Moretti, Andrew Creighton is President, the editor-in-chief is Ellis Jones
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Alex Miller was the global head of content.
      </p>
      <p>
       The monthly publication is frequently focused on a single theme.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vice_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Founded by
       <a href="/wiki/Suroosh_Alvi" title="Suroosh Alvi">
        Suroosh Alvi
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gavin_McInnes" title="Gavin McInnes">
        Gavin McInnes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Shane_Smith_(journalist)" title="Shane Smith (journalist)">
        Shane Smith
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rob_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rob-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the magazine was launched in 1994 as the
       <i>
        Voice of Montreal
       </i>
       with government funding, and the intention of the founders was to provide work and a community service.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gua_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gua-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When the editors later sought to dissolve their commitments with the original publisher Alix Laurent, they bought him out and changed the name to
       <i>
        Vice
       </i>
       in 1996.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-smith-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Richard Szalwinski, a Canadian software millionaire, acquired the magazine and relocated the operation to New York City, U.S. in the late 1990s. Following the relocation, the magazine quickly developed a reputation for provocative and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Politically_incorrect" title="Politically incorrect">
        politically incorrect
       </a>
       content. Under Szalwinski's ownership, a few retail stores were opened in New York City and customers could purchase fashion items that were advertised in the magazine. However, due to the end of the
       <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">
        dot-com bubble
       </a>
       , the three founders eventually regained ownership of the Vice brand, followed by the closure of the stores.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rob_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rob-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The British edition of
       <i>
        Vice
       </i>
       was launched in 2002 and
       <a href="/wiki/Andy_Capper" title="Andy Capper">
        Andy Capper
       </a>
       was its first editor. Capper explained in an interview shortly after the UK debut that the publication's remit was to cover "the things we're meant to be ashamed of", and articles were published on topics such as
       <a href="/wiki/Bukkake" title="Bukkake">
        bukkake
       </a>
       and bodily functions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pug_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pug-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By the end of 2007, 13 foreign editions of
       <i>
        Vice
       </i>
       magazine were published, the Vice independent record label was functional, and the online video channel VBS.com had 184,000 unique viewers from the U.S. during the month of August. The media company was still based in New York City, but the magazine began featuring articles on topics that were considered more serious, such as armed conflict in Iraq, than previous content. Alvi explained to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       in November 2007: "The world is much bigger than the Lower East Side and the East Village."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rob_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rob-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       McInnes left the publication in 2008, citing "creative differences" as the primary issue. In an email communication dated 23 January, McInnes explained: "I no longer have anything to do with Vice or VBS or DOs &amp; DON'Ts or any of that. It's a long story but we've all agreed to leave it at 'creative differences,' so please don't ask me about it."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At the commencement of 2012, an article in
       <i>
        Forbes
       </i>
       magazine referred to the Vice company as "Vice Media", but the precise time when this title development occurred is not public knowledge.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Vice acquired the fashion magazine
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/I-D" title="I-D">
         i-D
        </a>
       </i>
       in December 2012 and, by February 2013, Vice produced 24 global editions of the magazine, with a global circulation of 1,147,000 (100,000 in the UK). By this stage, Alex Miller had replaced Capper as the editor-in-chief of the UK edition. Furthermore, Vice consisted of 800 worldwide employees, including 100 in London, and around 3,500 freelancers also produced content for the company.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pug_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pug-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_harassment_at_Vice">
        Sexual harassment at Vice
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vice_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sexual harassment at Vice">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In December 2017,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       published an article on
       <a href="/wiki/Sexual_harassment" title="Sexual harassment">
        sexual harassment
       </a>
       at Vice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Four settlements involving allegations of sexual harassment or defamation against Vice employees were mentioned.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A settlement in 2016 involving
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Creighton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andrew Creighton (page does not exist)">
        Andrew Creighton
       </a>
       , president of the company, paid $135,000 to a former employee after she claimed she was fired after rejecting an intimate relationship with Creighton according to people briefed on the matter and documents seen by the NYT.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In early 2017 Vice reached a settlement with Martina Veltroni, a former employee who alleged that her supervisor, Jason Mojica, retaliated against her after they had a sexual relationship along with other allegations according to people briefed on the matter and documents seen by the NYT.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Mojica, former head of Vice News, was fired late November 2017.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In January 2017 Vice reached a $24,000 settement with Joanna Fuertes-Knight, a former journalist in the London office, who had been the victim of sexual harassment, racial and gender discrimination and bullying, according to documents viewed by the NYT.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A 2003 settlement involved claims that Vice had defamed a woman writer by publishing that she had agreed to have sex with a rapper she had interviewed when she had not.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sandra Miller said "There is a toxic environment where men can say the most disgusting things, joke about sex openly, and overall a toxic environment where women are treated far inferior than men."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       She hadn't faced harassment herself, but had witnessed "the complicity of accepting that behavior, covering up for it, and having even the most progressive people look the other way."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       She had worked as head of branded production from 2014 to 2016.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Shane Smith and Suroosh Alvi issued a statement admitting that a "boys club" atmosphere at Vice had "fostered inappropriate behavior that had permeated throughout the company."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Vice claimed that it had updated its harassment policies, clarified reporting procedures and created an employee hotline.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The company has also said that it has committed to reaching gender pay parity by the end of 2018.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In November 2017 top managers held a "state of the union" session with employees that was criticised for not mentioning sexual harassment, a topic which was roiling workplaces at the time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-at-vice_9-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-at-vice-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In January 2018 it was announced that president Andrew Creighton and chief digital officer Mike Germano were suspended as the company investigated charges against them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">
        Scope
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vice_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scope">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Vice
       </i>
       magazine includes the work of journalists, columnists, fiction writers, graphic artists and cartoonists, and photographers. Both
       <i>
        Vice
       </i>
       <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">
        '
       </span>
       s online and magazine content has shifted from dealing mostly with independent arts and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_culture" title="Pop culture">
        pop cultural
       </a>
       matters to covering more serious news topics. Due to the large array of contributors and the fact that often writers will only submit a small number of articles with the publication,
       <i>
        Vice
       </i>
       <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">
        '
       </span>
       s content varies dramatically and its political and cultural stance is often unclear or contradictory. Articles on the site feature a range of subjects, often things not covered as by mainstream media. The magazine's editors have championed the
       <a href="/wiki/Immersion_journalism" title="Immersion journalism">
        immersionist school of journalism
       </a>
       , which has been passed to other properties of
       <a href="/wiki/Vice_Media" title="Vice Media">
        Vice Media
       </a>
       such as the documentary television show
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balls_Deep_(TV_Series)" title="Balls Deep (TV Series)">
        <i>
         Balls Deep
        </i>
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Viceland" title="Viceland">
        Viceland
       </a>
       Channel. This style of journalism is regarded as something of a DIY antithesis to the methods practiced by mainstream news outlets, and has published an entire issue of articles written in accordance with this ethos. Entire issues of the magazine have also been dedicated to the concerns of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraqi_people" title="Iraqi people">
        Iraqi people
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iraqissue_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-iraqissue-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
        Native Americans
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nativeissue_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nativeissue-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_people" title="Russian people">
        Russian people
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ru_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ru-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       people with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_disorders" title="Mental disorders">
        mental disorders
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ill_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ill-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and people with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_disabilities" title="Mental disabilities">
        mental disabilities
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-special_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-special-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Vice
       </i>
       also publishes an annual guide for students in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-student_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-student-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2007, a
       <i>
        Vice
       </i>
       announcement was published on the Internet:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        After umpteen years of putting out what amounted to a reference book every month, we started to get bored with it. Besides, too many other magazines have ripped it and started doing their own lame take on themes. So we're going to do some issues, starting now, that have whatever we feel like putting in them.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dearreaders_18-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-dearreaders-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">
        Politics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vice_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Politics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In a March 2008 interview with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">
         The Guardian
        </a>
       </i>
       , Smith was asked about the magazine's political allegiances and he stated, "We're not trying to say anything politically in a paradigmatic left/right way ... We don't do that because we don't believe in either side. Are my politics
       <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">
        Democrat
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">
        Republican
       </a>
       ? I think both are horrific. And it doesn't matter anyway. Money runs America; money runs everywhere."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gua_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-gua-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He has also stated:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        I grew up being a socialist and I have problems with it because I grew up in Canada [and] I've spent a lot of time in
        <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">
         Scandinavia
        </a>
        , where I believe countries legislate out creativity. They cut off the tall trees. Everyone's a C-minus. I came to America from Canada because Canada is stultifyingly boring and incredibly hypocritical. Thanks, Canada.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_5-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-smith-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>

       </i>

       <a href="/wiki/VBS.tv" title="VBS.tv">
        VBS.tv
       </a>

        </a>

      <p>
       <i>
        Vice News
       </i>
       is Vice Media's current affairs brand. Launched in December 2013, its presence consists of a YouTube channel and the websites
       <i>
        Vice News
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Motherboard
       </i>
       .
       <i>
        Vice New
       </i>
       s content primarily consists of documentaries and video news digests, which range from prison systems, such as Guantanamo Bay (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gitmo" title="Gitmo">
        Gitmo
       </a>
       ), to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_market/political_corruption" title="American market/political corruption">
        American market/political corruption
       </a>
       , to international drug addiction and far beyond.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vice_books">
        Vice books

      </h2>
      <p>
       The magazine has published the collections
       <i>
        The DOs and DON'Ts Book
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        The Vice Guide to Sex and Drugs and Rock and Roll
       </i>
       . In 2008, the photograph compilation
       <i>
        The Vice Photo Book
       </i>
       was released and featured published works from previous editions of the magazine.
