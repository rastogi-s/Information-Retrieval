      <p>
       A
       <b>
        solar telescope
       </b>
       is a special purpose
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       used to observe the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       . Solar telescopes usually detect light with wavelengths in, or not far outside, the
       <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">
        visible spectrum
       </a>
       . Obsolete names for sun telescopes include
       <b>
        heliograph
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        photoheliograph
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_solar_telescopes">
        Professional solar telescopes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Professional solar telescopes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Solar telescopes need optics large enough to achieve the best possible
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction#Diffraction_limit_of_telescopes" title="Diffraction">
        diffraction limit
       </a>
       but less so for the associated light-collecting power of other astronomical telescopes. However, recently newer narrower
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_filter" title="Optical filter">
        filters
       </a>
       and higher framerates have also driven solar telescopes towards photon-starved operations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Both the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Solar_Telescope" title="European Solar Telescope">
        European Solar Telescope (EST)
       </a>
       as well as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Technology_Solar_Telescope" title="Advanced Technology Solar Telescope">
        Advanced Technology Solar Telescope (ATST)
       </a>
       have larger apertures not only to increase the resolution, but also to increase the light-collecting power.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because solar telescopes operate during the day, seeing is generally worse than for night-time telescopes, because the ground around the telescope is heated which causes
       <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
        turbulence
       </a>
       and degrades the resolution. To alleviate this, solar telescopes are usually built on towers and the structures are painted white. The
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Open_Telescope" title="Dutch Open Telescope">
        Dutch Open Telescope
       </a>
       is built on an open framework to allow the wind to pass through the complete structure and provide cooling around the telescope's main mirror.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another solar telescope-specific problem is the heat generated by the tightly-focused sunlight. For this reason, a
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_stop&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Heat stop (page does not exist)">
        heat stop
       </a>
       is an integral part of the design of solar telescopes. For the upcoming
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Technology_Solar_Telescope" title="Advanced Technology Solar Telescope">
        ATST
       </a>
       , the heat load is 2.5 MW/m
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       , with peak powers of 11.4 kW.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The goal of such a heat stop is not only to survive this heat load, but also to remain cool enough not to induce any additional turbulence inside the telescope's dome.
      </p>
      <p>
       Professional solar observatories may have main optical elements with very long
       <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">
        focal lengths
       </a>
       (although not always,
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Open_Telescope" title="Dutch Open Telescope">
        Dutch Open Telescope
       </a>
       ) and light paths operating in a
       <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">
        vacuum
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       to eliminate air motion due to
       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
        convection
       </a>
       inside the telescope. However, this is not possible for apertures over 1 meter, at which the pressure difference at the entrance window of the vacuum tube becomes too large. Therefore, the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Solar_Telescope" title="European Solar Telescope">
        EST
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Technology_Solar_Telescope" title="Advanced Technology Solar Telescope">
        ATST
       </a>
       have active cooling of the dome to minimize the temperature difference between the air inside and outside the telescope.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because the Sun travels on a narrow fixed path across the sky, some solar telescopes are fixed in position (and are sometimes buried underground), with the only moving part being a
       <a href="/wiki/Heliostat" title="Heliostat">
        heliostat
       </a>
       to track the Sun. One example of this is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McMath-Pierce_Solar_Telescope" title="McMath-Pierce Solar Telescope">
        McMath-Pierce Solar Telescope
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_solar_telescopes">
        Selected solar telescopes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Selected solar telescopes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_solar_telescopes" title="List of solar telescopes">
        List of solar telescopes
       </a>
      </div>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Tower" title="Einstein Tower">
         Einstein Tower
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         Einsteinturm
        </i>
        ) became operational in 1924
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McMath-Pierce_Solar_Telescope" title="McMath-Pierce Solar Telescope">
         McMath-Pierce Solar Telescope
        </a>
        (1.6 m diameter, 1961–)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McMath-Hulbert_Observatory" title="McMath-Hulbert Observatory">
         McMath-Hulbert Observatory
        </a>
        (24"/61 cm diameter, 1941–1979)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Vacuum_Solar_Telescope" title="Swedish Vacuum Solar Telescope">
         Swedish Vacuum Solar Telescope
        </a>
        (47.5 cm diameter, 1985–2000)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Solar_Telescope" title="Swedish Solar Telescope">
         Swedish 1-m Solar Telescope
        </a>
        (1 m diameter, 2002–)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Richard_B._Dunn_Solar_Telescope" title="Richard B. Dunn Solar Telescope">
         Richard B. Dunn Solar Telescope
        </a>
        (1.63 m diameter, 1969–)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory" title="Mount Wilson Observatory">
         Mount Wilson Observatory
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Open_Telescope" title="Dutch Open Telescope">
         Dutch Open Telescope
        </a>
        (45 cm diameter, 1997–)
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Teide_Observatory" title="Teide Observatory">
         Teide Observatory
        </a>
        hosts multiple solar telescopes, including
        <ul>
         <li>
          the 70 cm
          <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_Tower_Telescope" title="Vacuum Tower Telescope">
           Vacuum Tower Telescope
          </a>
          (1989–) and
         </li>
         <li>
          the 1.5 m
          <a href="/wiki/GREGOR_Solar_Telescope" title="GREGOR Solar Telescope">
           GREGOR Solar Telescope
          </a>
          (2012–,
          <a class="external text" href="http://www.kis.uni-freiburg.de/index.php?id=223&amp;L=1" rel="nofollow">
           webcam
          </a>
          ).
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Technology_Solar_Telescope" title="Advanced Technology Solar Telescope">
         Advanced Technology Solar Telescope
        </a>
        , a planned telescope with 4m aperture.
        <a class="external autonumber" href="http://atst.nso.edu/" rel="nofollow">
         [1]
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/European_Solar_Telescope" title="European Solar Telescope">
         European Solar Telescope
        </a>
        , a future 4-meter class aperture telescope.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/National_Large_Solar_Telescope" title="National Large Solar Telescope">
         National Large Solar Telescope
        </a>
        (NLST), is a Gregorian multi-purpose open telescope proposed to be built and Installed in India and aims to study the sun's microscopic structure.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types_of_observation">
        Other types of observation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other types of observation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Most solar observatories observe optically at visible, UV, and near infrared wavelengths, but other solar phenomena can be observed — albeit not from the Earth's surface due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#Absorption" title="Atmosphere of Earth">
        absorption
       </a>
       of the atmosphere:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Solar X-ray astronomy, observations of the Sun in x-rays
       </li>
       <li>
        Multi-spectral solar telescope array (
        <a href="/wiki/MSSTA" title="MSSTA">
         MSSTA
        </a>
        ), a rocket launched payload of UV telescopes in the 1990s
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Leoncito_Astronomical_Complex" title="Leoncito Astronomical Complex">
         Leoncito Astronomical Complex
        </a>
        operated a submillimeter wavelength solar telescope.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Radio_Solar_Telescope_Network" title="Radio Solar Telescope Network">
         Radio Solar Telescope Network
        </a>
        (RSTN) is a network of solar observatories maintained and operated by the U.S.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Weather_Agency" title="Air Force Weather Agency">
         Air Force Weather Agency
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/CERN_Axion_Solar_Telescope" title="CERN Axion Solar Telescope">
         CERN Axion Solar Telescope
        </a>
        (CAST), looks for solar
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axions" title="Axions">
         axions
        </a>
        in the early 2000s
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Amateur_solar_telescopes">
        Amateur solar telescopes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Amateur solar telescopes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solarborg.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="390" data-file-width="512" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Solarborg.jpg/250px-Solarborg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Solarborg.jpg/375px-Solarborg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Solarborg.jpg/500px-Solarborg.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solarborg.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Example of amateur solar telescope equipped with a
         <a href="/wiki/H-alpha#Filter" title="H-alpha">
          hydrogen-alpha filter
         </a>
         system.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the field of
       <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">
        amateur astronomy
       </a>
       there are many methods used to observe the sun. Amateurs use everything from simple systems to project the sun on a piece of white paper, light blocking
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_filter" title="Astronomical filter">
        filters
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Herschel_wedge" title="Herschel wedge">
        Herschel wedges
       </a>
       which redirect 95% of the light away from the eyepiece,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       up to
       <a href="/wiki/H-alpha#Filter" title="H-alpha">
        hydrogen-alpha filter
       </a>
       systems and even home built
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrohelioscope" title="Spectrohelioscope">
        spectrohelioscopes
       </a>
       . In contrast to professional telescopes, amateur solar telescopes are usually much smaller.
      </p>
