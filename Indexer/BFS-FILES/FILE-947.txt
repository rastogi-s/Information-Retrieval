      <p>
       The
       <b>
        ångström
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="/ŋ/: 'ng' in 'sing'">
            ŋ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
          </span>
          , -
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">
            ʌ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpd_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lpd-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          ANG
         </span>
         -strəm
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          ANG
         </span>
         -strum
        </i>
       </a>
       <small>
        Swedish:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Swedish" title="Help:IPA/Swedish">
         [²ɔŋstrœm]
        </a>
       </span>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpd_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-lpd-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <b>
        angstrom
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_length" title="Unit of length">
        unit of length
       </a>
       equal to
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6990100000000000000♠
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −10
        </sup>
        m
       </span>
       (one ten-billionth of a
       <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">
        metre
       </a>
       ) or 0.1
       <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">
        nanometre
       </a>
       . Its symbol is
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%85" title="Å">
        Å
       </a>
       , a letter in the
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_alphabet" title="Swedish alphabet">
        Swedish alphabet
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">
        natural sciences
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">
        technology
       </a>
       often use ångström to express sizes of
       <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
        atoms
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
        molecules
       </a>
       , microscopic biological structures, and lengths of
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">
        chemical bonds
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">
        arrangement of atoms in crystals
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelengths
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
        electromagnetic radiation
       </a>
       , and dimensions of
       <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">
        integrated circuit
       </a>
       parts. Atoms of phosphorus, sulfur, and chlorine are about an ångström in covalent radius, while a hydrogen atom is about half an ångström; see
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_radius" title="Atomic radius">
        atomic radius
       </a>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">
        Visible light
       </a>
       has wavelengths in the range of 4000–7000 Å.
      </p>
      <p>
       The unit is named after the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_people" title="Swedish people">
        Swedish
       </a>
       physicist
       <a href="/wiki/Anders_Jonas_%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Anders Jonas Ångström">
        Anders Jonas Ångström
       </a>
       (1814–1874). The symbol is always written with a
       <a href="/wiki/Ring_(diacritic)" title="Ring (diacritic)">
        ring diacritic
       </a>
       , as the letter in the Swedish alphabet. The unit's name is often written in English without the diacritics,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webster_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-webster-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but the official definitions do contain them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is not a part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">
        SI
       </a>
       system of units.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Use">
        Use
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The ångström is used extensively in
       <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">
        crystallography
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_physics" title="Solid-state physics">
        solid-state physics
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">
        chemistry
       </a>
       as a unit for d-spacings (the distance between atomic planes in a
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">
        crystal
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), cell parameters, inter-atomic distances and
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        x-ray
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelengths
       </a>
       , as these values are often in the 1–10 Å range. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_Crystal_Structure_Database" title="Inorganic Crystal Structure Database">
        Inorganic Crystal Structure Database
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       presents all these values using the ångström.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Anders Jonas Ångström was a pioneer in the field of
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectroscopy
       </a>
       , and he is also well known for his studies of
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
        astrophysics
       </a>
       , heat transfer,
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">
        terrestrial magnetism
       </a>
       , and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">
        aurora borealis
       </a>
       . In 1852, Ångström formulated in
       <i>
        Optiska undersökningar
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in English translation
       <i>
        Optical Researches
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a law of absorption, later modified somewhat and known as
       <a href="/wiki/Kirchhoff%27s_law_of_thermal_radiation" title="Kirchhoff's law of thermal radiation">
        Kirchhoff's law of thermal radiation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1868, Ångström created a chart of the spectrum of
       <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
        sunlight
       </a>
       , in which he expressed the wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation in the
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        electromagnetic spectrum
       </a>
       in multiples of one ten-millionth of a millimetre (or
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6990100000000000000♠
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −7
        </sup>
        mm
       </span>
       .)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because the human eye is sensitive to wavelengths from about
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7003400000000000000♠
        </span>
        4000
       </span>
       to
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7003700000000000000♠
        </span>
        7000
       </span>
       Å (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">
        visible light
       </a>
       ), that choice of unit supported sufficiently accurate measurements of visible wavelengths without resorting to fractional numbers.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Ångström's chart and table of wavelengths in the solar spectrum became widely used in
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_physics" title="Solar physics">
        solar physics
       </a>
       , which adopted the unit and named it after him. It subsequently spread to the rest of
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">
        astronomical spectroscopy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_spectroscopy" title="Atomic spectroscopy">
        atomic spectroscopy
       </a>
       , and subsequently to other sciences that deal with atomic-scale structures.
      </p>
      <p>
       Though intended to correspond to
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6990100000000000000♠
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −10
        </sup>
       </span>
       metres, for precise spectral analysis, the ångström had to be defined more accurately than the metre, which until 1960 was still defined based on the length of a bar of metal held in Paris. The use of metal bars had been involved in an early error in the value of the ångström of about one part in 6000. Ångström took the precaution of having the standard bar he used checked against a standard in Paris, but the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metrologist" title="Metrologist">
        metrologist
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Henri_Tresca" title="Henri Tresca">
        Henri Tresca
       </a>
       reported it to be so much shorter than it really was that Ångström's corrected results were more in error than the uncorrected ones.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1907, the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       defined the international ångström by declaring the wavelength of the red line of
       <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">
        cadmium
       </a>
       in air equal to 6438.46963 international ångströms, and this definition was endorsed by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">
        International Bureau of Weights and Measures
       </a>
       in 1927.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From 1927 to 1960, the ångström remained a secondary unit of length for use in spectroscopy, defined separately from the metre. In 1960, the metre itself was redefined in spectroscopic terms, which allowed the ångström to be redefined as being exactly 0.1 nanometres.
      </p>
      <p>
       The ångström is internationally recognized, but is not a formal part of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">
        International System of Units
       </a>
       (SI). The closest SI unit is the
       <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">
        nanometre
       </a>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6991100000000000000♠
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −9
        </sup>
        m
       </span>
       ). The International Committee for Weights and Measures officially discourages its use, and it is not included in the
       <a href="/wiki/European_units_of_measurement_directives" title="European units of measurement directives">
        European Union's catalogue of units of measure
       </a>
       that may be used within its internal market.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Symbol">
        Symbol
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Symbol">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Angstrom_unicode_sample.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="138" data-file-width="431" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Angstrom_unicode_sample.svg/220px-Angstrom_unicode_sample.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Angstrom_unicode_sample.svg/330px-Angstrom_unicode_sample.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Angstrom_unicode_sample.svg/440px-Angstrom_unicode_sample.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Angstrom_unicode_sample.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Example of the
         <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">
          Unicode
         </a>
         codification
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       For compatibility reasons,
       <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">
        Unicode
       </a>
       includes the formal symbol at
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="monospaced" style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">
         U+212B
        </span>
       </span>
       <span style="font-size:125%">
        Å
       </span>
       <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">
        ANGSTROM SIGN
       </span>
       (HTML
       <code>
        &amp;#8491;
       </code>
       ). However, the ångström sign is also
       <a href="/wiki/Unicode_equivalence" title="Unicode equivalence">
        normalized
       </a>
       into
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="monospaced" style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">
         U+00C5
        </span>
       </span>
       <span style="font-size:125%">
        Å
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%85" title="Å">
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">
         LATIN CAPITAL LETTER A WITH RING ABOVE
        </span>
       </a>
       (HTML
       <code>
        &amp;#197;
       </code>
       <b>
        ·
       </b>
       <code>
        &amp;Aring;
       </code>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Unicode consortium recommends to use the regular letter (00C5).
      </p>
      <p>
       Before digital typesetting, the ångström (or ångström unit) was sometimes written as "A.U." (also an abbreviation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical unit
       </a>
       ). This use is evident in Bragg's paper on the structure of ice,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which gives the c- and a-axis lattice constants as 4.52 A.U. and 7.34 A.U., respectively.
      </p>
