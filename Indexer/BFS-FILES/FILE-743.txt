   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Tidal locking
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="565" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Tidal_locking_of_the_Moon_with_the_Earth.gif/300px-Tidal_locking_of_the_Moon_with_the_Earth.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Tidal_locking_of_the_Moon_with_the_Earth.gif/450px-Tidal_locking_of_the_Moon_with_the_Earth.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/56/Tidal_locking_of_the_Moon_with_the_Earth.gif 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tidal_locking_of_the_Moon_with_the_Earth.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Tidal locking results in the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         rotating about its axis in about the same time it takes to orbit
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         . Except for
         <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
          libration
         </a>
         effects, this results in the Moon keeping the same face turned toward Earth, as seen in the left figure. (The Moon is shown in polar view, and is not drawn to scale.) If the Moon were not rotating at all, it would alternately show its near and far sides to Earth, while moving around Earth in orbit, as shown in the right figure.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Tidal locking
       </b>
       (also called
       <b>
        gravitational locking
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        captured rotation
       </b>
       ) occurs when the long-term interaction between a pair of co-orbiting
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_body" title="Astronomical body">
        astronomical bodies
       </a>
       drives the rotation rates into a harmonic ratio with the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       . This effect arises from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_gradient" title="Gravitational gradient">
        gravitational gradient
       </a>
       (tidal force) between the co-orbiting bodies, acting over a sufficiently long period of time. Once tidal locking is achieved for one of the bodies, there is no more net transfer of
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       between the two objects, although there can be some back and forth transfer over the course of an orbit. In the special case where the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        orbital eccentricity
       </a>
       is nearly zero, tidal locking results in one hemisphere of the revolving object constantly facing its partner, an effect known as
       <b>
        synchronous rotation
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, the same side of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       always faces the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , although there is some
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       because the Moon's orbit is not perfectly circular. A tidally locked body in synchronous rotation takes just as long to rotate around its own axis as it does to revolve around its partner.
      </p>
      <p>
       Usually, only the
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       is tidally locked to the larger body.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, if both the mass difference between the two bodies and the distance between them are relatively small, each may be tidally locked to the other; this is the case for
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       This effect is employed to
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity-gradient_stabilization" title="Gravity-gradient stabilization">
        stabilize
       </a>
       some artificial satellites.
      </p>
      <p>
       One form of hypothetical tidal locked planets are
       <a href="/wiki/Eyeball_planet" title="Eyeball planet">
        eyeball planets
       </a>
       , that in turn are divided into "hot" and "cold" eyeball planets.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">
        Mechanism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_locking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mechanism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:181px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%C3%81rap%C3%A1ly_forgat%C3%B3nyomat%C3%A9k.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="179" height="375" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0c/%C3%81rap%C3%A1ly_forgat%C3%B3nyomat%C3%A9k.png" width="179"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%C3%81rap%C3%A1ly_forgat%C3%B3nyomat%C3%A9k.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         If the tidal bulges on a body (green) are misaligned with the major axis (red), the tidal forces (blue) exert a net torque on that body that twists the body toward the direction of realignment
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Consider a pair of co-orbiting objects, A and B. The change in
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation_period" title="Rotation period">
        rotation rate
       </a>
       necessary to tidally lock body B to the larger body A is caused by the
       <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">
        torque
       </a>
       applied by A's
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       on bulges it has induced on B by
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The gravitational force from object A upon B will vary with distance, being greatest at the nearest surface to A and least at the most distant. This creates a gravitational
       <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">
        gradient
       </a>
       across object B that will distort its
       <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">
        equilibrium
       </a>
       shape slightly. The body of object B will become elongated along the axis oriented toward A, and conversely, slightly reduced in dimension in directions
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal" title="Orthogonal">
        orthogonal
       </a>
       to this axis. The elongated distortions are known as tidal bulges. (For the solid Earth, these bulges can reach displacements of up to around 0.4 metres (1.3 ft).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) When B is not yet tidally locked, the bulges travel over its surface due to orbital motions, with one of the two "high" tidal bulges traveling close to the point where body A is overhead. For large astronomical bodies that are nearly
       <a href="/wiki/Sphericity" title="Sphericity">
        spherical
       </a>
       due to self-gravitation, the tidal distortion produces a slightly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prolate_spheroid" title="Prolate spheroid">
        prolate spheroid
       </a>
       , i.e. an axially symmetric
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">
        ellipsoid
       </a>
       that is elongated along its major axis. Smaller bodies also experience distortion, but this distortion is less regular.
      </p>
      <p>
       The material of B exerts resistance to this periodic reshaping caused by the tidal force. In effect, some time is required to reshape B to the gravitational equilibrium shape, by which time the forming bulges have already been carried some distance away from the A–B axis by B's rotation. Seen from a vantage point in space, the points of maximum bulge extension are displaced from the axis oriented toward A. If B's rotation period is shorter than its orbital period, the bulges are carried forward of the axis oriented toward A in the direction of rotation, whereas if B's rotation period is longer, the bulges instead lag behind.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because the bulges are now displaced from the A–B axis, A's gravitational pull on the mass in them exerts a torque on B. The torque on the A-facing bulge acts to bring B's rotation in line with its orbital period, whereas the "back" bulge, which faces away from A, acts in the opposite sense. However, the bulge on the A-facing side is closer to A than the back bulge by a distance of approximately B's diameter, and so experiences a slightly stronger gravitational force and torque. The net resulting torque from both bulges, then, is always in the direction that acts to synchronize B's rotation with its orbital period, leading eventually to tidal locking.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_changes">
        Orbital changes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_locking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbital changes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MoonTorque.jpg">
         <img alt="Tidal Locking" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="640" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/MoonTorque.jpg/220px-MoonTorque.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/MoonTorque.jpg/330px-MoonTorque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/MoonTorque.jpg/440px-MoonTorque.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MoonTorque.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         If rotational frequency is larger than orbital frequency, a small torque counteracting the rotation arises, eventually locking the frequencies (situation depicted in green)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       of the whole A–B system is conserved in this process, so that when B slows down and loses rotational angular momentum, its
       <i>
        orbital
       </i>
       angular momentum is boosted by a similar amount (there are also some smaller effects on A's rotation). This results in a raising of B's orbit about A in tandem with its rotational slowdown. For the other case where B starts off rotating too slowly, tidal locking both speeds up its rotation, and
       <i>
        lowers
       </i>
       its orbit.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Locking_of_the_larger_body">
        Locking of the larger body
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_locking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Locking of the larger body">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        Synchronous orbit
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The tidal locking effect is also experienced by the larger body A, but at a slower rate because B's gravitational effect is weaker due to B's smaller mass. For example, Earth's rotation is gradually being slowed by the Moon, by an amount that becomes noticeable over geological time as revealed in the fossil record.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Current estimations are that this (together with the tidal influence of the Sun) has helped lengthen the Earth day from about 6 hours to the current 24 hours. Currently,
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_clock" title="Atomic clock">
        atomic clocks
       </a>
       show that Earth's day lengthens by about 15 microseconds every year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Given enough time, this would create a mutual tidal locking between Earth and the Moon, where the length of a
       <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">
        day
       </a>
       has increased and the length of a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">
        lunar month
       </a>
       has shortened until the two are the same. However, Earth is not expected to become tidally locked to the Moon before the Sun becomes a
       <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">
        red giant
       </a>
       and engulfs Earth and the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For bodies of similar size the effect may be of comparable size for both, and both may become tidally locked to each other on a much shorter timescale. An example is the
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and its satellite
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       . They have already reached a state where Charon is only visible from one hemisphere of Pluto and vice versa.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michaely2017_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Michaely2017-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Rotation.E2.80.93orbit_resonance">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation–orbit_resonance">
        Rotation–orbit resonance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_locking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rotation–orbit resonance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Finally, in some cases where the orbit is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentric
       </a>
       and the tidal effect is relatively weak, the smaller body may end up in a so-called
       <i>
        spin-orbit resonance
       </i>
       , rather than being tidally locked. Here, the ratio of the rotation period of a body to its own orbital period is some simple fraction different from 1:1. A well known case is the rotation of
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       , which is locked to its own orbit around the Sun in a 3:2 resonance.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many exoplanets (especially the close-in ones) are expected to be in spin–orbit resonances higher than 1:1. A Mercury-like terrestrial planet can, for example, become captured in a 3:2, 2:1, or 5:2 spin-orbit resonance, with the probability of each being dependent on the orbital eccentricity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Makarov2012_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Makarov2012-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">
        Occurrence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_locking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Occurrence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Moons">
        Moons
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_locking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Moons">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Synchronous_rotation.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="814" data-file-width="829" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Synchronous_rotation.svg/220px-Synchronous_rotation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Synchronous_rotation.svg/330px-Synchronous_rotation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Synchronous_rotation.svg/440px-Synchronous_rotation.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Synchronous_rotation.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Due to tidal locking, the inhabitants of the central body will never be able to see the satellite's green area.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Most major moons in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       − the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_gravitationally_rounded_objects_of_the_Solar_System" title="List of gravitationally rounded objects of the Solar System">
        gravitationally rounded satellites
       </a>
       − are tidally locked with their primaries, because they orbit very closely and tidal force increases rapidly (as a
       <a href="/wiki/Cubic_function" title="Cubic function">
        cubic function
       </a>
       ) with decreasing distance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Notable exceptions are the irregular outer satellites of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">
        gas giants
       </a>
       , which orbit much farther away than the large well-known moons.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       are an extreme example of a tidal lock. Charon is a relatively large moon in comparison to its primary and also has a very close
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       . This results in Pluto and Charon being mutually tidally locked. Pluto's other moons are not tidally locked;
       <a href="/wiki/Styx_(moon)" title="Styx (moon)">
        Styx
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nix_(moon)" title="Nix (moon)">
        Nix
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kerberos_(moon)" title="Kerberos (moon)">
        Kerberos
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(moon)" title="Hydra (moon)">
        Hydra
       </a>
       all rotate chaotically due to the influence of Charon.
      </p>
      <p>
       The tidal locking situation for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_moon" title="Asteroid moon">
        asteroid moons
       </a>
       is largely unknown, but closely orbiting binaries are expected to be tidally locked, as well as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contact_binary_(asteroid)" title="Contact binary (asteroid)">
        contact binaries
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Moon">
        The Moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_locking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FullMoon2010.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2452" data-file-width="2580" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/FullMoon2010.jpg/220px-FullMoon2010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/FullMoon2010.jpg/330px-FullMoon2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/FullMoon2010.jpg/440px-FullMoon2010.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FullMoon2010.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Because the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         is 1:1 tidally locked, only
         <a href="/wiki/Near_side_of_the_Moon" title="Near side of the Moon">
          one side
         </a>
         is visible from
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Moon's rotation and orbital periods are tidally locked with each other, so no matter when the Moon is observed from Earth the same hemisphere of the Moon is always seen. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far_side_(Moon)" title="Far side (Moon)">
        far side of the Moon
       </a>
       was not seen until 1959, when photographs of most of the far side were transmitted from the
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet
       </a>
       spacecraft
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_3" title="Luna 3">
        Luna 3
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When the Earth is observed from the moon, the Earth does not appear to translate across the sky but appears to remain in the same place, rotating on its own axis.
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite the Moon's rotational and orbital periods being exactly locked, about 59% of the Moon's total surface may be seen with repeated observations from Earth due to the phenomena of
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">
        parallax
       </a>
       . Librations are primarily caused by the Moon's varying orbital speed due to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of its orbit: this allows up to about 6° more along its perimeter to be seen from Earth. Parallax is a geometric effect: at the surface of Earth we are offset from the line through the centers of Earth and Moon, and because of this we can observe a bit (about 1°) more around the side of the Moon when it is on our local horizon.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Planets">
        Planets
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_locking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Planets">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       It was thought for some time that
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       was in synchronous rotation with the Sun. This was because whenever Mercury was best placed for observation, the same side faced inward. Radar observations in 1965 demonstrated instead that Mercury has a 3:2 spin–orbit resonance, rotating three times for every two revolutions around the Sun, which results in the same positioning at those observation points. Modeling has demonstrated that Mercury was captured into the 3:2 spin–orbit state very early in its history, within 20 (and more likely even 10) million years after its formation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noyelles2012_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Noyelles2012-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       's 583.92-day interval between successive close approaches to Earth is equal to 5.001444 Venusian solar days, making approximately the same face visible from Earth at each close approach. Whether this relationship arose by chance or is the result of some kind of tidal locking with Earth is unknown.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Proxima_Centauri_b" title="Proxima Centauri b">
        Proxima Centauri b
       </a>
       , the "Earth-like planet" discovered in 2016 that orbits around the star
       <a href="/wiki/Proxima_Centauri" title="Proxima Centauri">
        Proxima Centauri
       </a>
       is tidally locked, either in synchronized rotation,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or otherwise expresses a 3:2 spin-orbit resonance like that of Mercury.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stars">
        Stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_locking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Close
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">
        binary stars
       </a>
       throughout the universe are expected to be tidally locked with each other, and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">
        extrasolar planets
       </a>
       that have been found to orbit their primaries extremely closely are also thought to be tidally locked to them. An unusual example, confirmed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microvariability_and_Oscillations_of_STars_telescope" title="Microvariability and Oscillations of STars telescope">
        MOST
       </a>
       , may be
       <a href="/wiki/Tau_Bo%C3%B6tis" title="Tau Boötis">
        Tau Boötis
       </a>
       , a star that is probably tidally locked by a planet. If so, the tidal locking is almost certainly mutual. However, since stars are gaseous bodies that can rotate with a different rate at different latitudes, the tidal lock is with τ Boötis'
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
