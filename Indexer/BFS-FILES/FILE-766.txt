      <p>
       The
       <b>
        South Pole–Aitken basin
       </b>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side of the Moon
       </a>
       . At roughly 2,500 km (1,600 mi) in diameter and 13 km (8.1 mi) deep, it is one of the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_craters_in_the_Solar_System" title="List of largest craters in the Solar System">
        largest known impact craters
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       . It is the largest, oldest, and deepest
       <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">
        basin
       </a>
       recognized on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petro_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Petro-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was named for two features on opposing sides: the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Aitken_(crater)" title="Aitken (crater)">
        Aitken
       </a>
       on the northern end and the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_south_pole" title="Lunar south pole">
        lunar south pole
       </a>
       at the other end. The outer rim of this basin can be seen from Earth as a huge chain of
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">
        mountains
       </a>
       located on the Moon's southern limb, sometimes called "Leibnitz mountains", although this name has not been considered official by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pole%E2%80%93Aitken_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:South_Pole-Aitken_basin_rim_As08-13-2319hr.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2992" data-file-width="2992" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/South_Pole-Aitken_basin_rim_As08-13-2319hr.jpg/220px-South_Pole-Aitken_basin_rim_As08-13-2319hr.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/South_Pole-Aitken_basin_rim_As08-13-2319hr.jpg/330px-South_Pole-Aitken_basin_rim_As08-13-2319hr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/South_Pole-Aitken_basin_rim_As08-13-2319hr.jpg/440px-South_Pole-Aitken_basin_rim_As08-13-2319hr.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:South_Pole-Aitken_basin_rim_As08-13-2319hr.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">
          Apollo 8
         </a>
         photograph showing the mountains along the northern rim of the basin
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The existence of a giant far side basin was suspected as early as 1962 based on early probe images (namely
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_3" title="Luna 3">
        Luna 3
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Zond_3" title="Zond 3">
        Zond 3
       </a>
       ), but it was not until the acquisition of global photography by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_program" title="Lunar Orbiter program">
        Lunar Orbiter program
       </a>
       in the mid-1960s that geologists recognized its true size. Laser altimeter data obtained during the Apollo 15 and 16 missions showed that the northern portion of this basin was very deep,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but since these data were only available along the near-equatorial
       <a href="/wiki/Ground_track" title="Ground track">
        ground tracks
       </a>
       of the orbiting
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Command_and_Service_Module" title="Command and Service Module">
        Command and Service Modules
       </a>
       , the topography of the rest of the basin remained unknown. The geologic map showing the northern half of this basin and with its edge depicted was published in 1978 by the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">
        United States Geological Survey
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Little was known about the basin until the 1990s, when the spacecraft
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">
        Galileo
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Clementine_(spacecraft)" title="Clementine (spacecraft)">
        Clementine
       </a>
       visited the Moon. Multispectral images obtained from these missions showed that this basin contains more
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FeO" title="FeO">
        FeO
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">
        TiO
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </a>
       than typical lunar highlands, and hence has a darker appearance. The topography of the basin was mapped in its entirety for the first time using altimeter data and the analysis of stereo image pairs taken during the Clementine mission. Most recently, the composition of this basin has been further constrained by the analysis of data obtained from a gamma-ray spectrometer that was on board the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       mission.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pole%E2%80%93Aitken_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_back-view_(Clementine,_cropped).png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="2000" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Moon_back-view_%28Clementine%2C_cropped%29.png/200px-Moon_back-view_%28Clementine%2C_cropped%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Moon_back-view_%28Clementine%2C_cropped%29.png/300px-Moon_back-view_%28Clementine%2C_cropped%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Moon_back-view_%28Clementine%2C_cropped%29.png/400px-Moon_back-view_%28Clementine%2C_cropped%29.png 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_back-view_(Clementine,_cropped).png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
          Far side of the Moon
         </a>
         . The South Pole–Aitken basin is the darker area at the bottom of this image.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The South Pole–Aitken basin is the largest, deepest and oldest basin recognized on the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petro_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Petro-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The lowest elevations of the Moon (about −6000 m) are located within the South Pole–Aitken basin, and the highest elevations (about +8000 m) are found on this basin's north-eastern rim. Because of this basin's great size, the crust at this locale is expected to be thinner than typical as a result of the large amount of material that was excavated due to an impact. Crustal thickness maps constructed using the Moon's topography and gravity field imply a thickness of about 30 km beneath the floor of this basin, in comparison to 60–80 km around it and the global average of about 50 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Potter_2012_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Potter_2012-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The composition of this basin, as estimated from the
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">
        Galileo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Clementine_(spacecraft)" title="Clementine (spacecraft)">
        Clementine
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       missions, show that it is different from typical highland regions. Most importantly, none of the samples obtained from the American
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       and Russian
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">
        Luna
       </a>
       missions, nor the handful of identified
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_meteorites" title="Lunar meteorites">
        lunar meteorites
       </a>
       , have a composition that is comparable. The orbital data indicate that the floor of this basin has slightly elevated abundances of iron, titanium, and thorium. In terms of mineralogy, the basin floor is much richer in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clinopyroxene" title="Clinopyroxene">
        clinopyroxene
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthopyroxene" title="Orthopyroxene">
        orthopyroxene
       </a>
       than the surrounding highlands that are largely
       <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">
        anorthositic
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L06_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-L06-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Several possibilities exist for this distinctive chemical signature. One is that this composition might simply represent lower crustal materials that are somewhat more rich in iron, titanium and thorium than the upper crust. Another possibility is that this composition reflects the widespread distribution of ponds of iron-rich
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalts
       </a>
       , similar to those that make up the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       . Alternatively, the rocks here could contain a component from the lunar mantle if the basin excavated all the way through the crust. The origin of the anomalous composition of this basin is not known with certainty at this time, however, and a sample return mission will most likely be required to settle this debate. Complicating matters is the fact that all three of the above hypotheses could contribute to the anomalous geochemical signature of this giant crater. Furthermore, it is possible that a large portion of the lunar surface in the vicinity of this basin was melted during the impact event, and differentiation of this impact melt sheet could have given rise to additional geochemical anomalies.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pole%E2%80%93Aitken_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Sample.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="684" data-file-width="840" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Lunar_Sample.jpg/220px-Lunar_Sample.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Lunar_Sample.jpg/330px-Lunar_Sample.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Lunar_Sample.jpg/440px-Lunar_Sample.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Sample.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A possible configuration of South Pole–Aitken basin sample return spacecraft
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Simulations of near vertical impacts show that this basin should have dug up vast amounts of mantle materials from depths as great as 200 km below the surface. However, observations thus far do not favor a mantle composition for this basin, and crustal thickness maps seem to indicate the presence of about 10 kilometers of crustal materials beneath this basin's floor. This has suggested to some that the basin was not formed by a typical high-velocity impact, but may instead have been formed by a low-velocity projectile around 200 km in diameter that hit at a low angle (about 30 degrees or less), and hence did not dig very deeply into the Moon. Putative evidence for this comes from the high elevations north-east of the rim of the South Pole–Aitken basin that might represent ejecta from such an oblique impact. The impact theory would also account for magnetic anomalies on the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
