    National Grid (Great Britain)
   </h1>

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        National Grid
       </b>
       is the high-voltage
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">
        electric power transmission
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grid_(electricity)" title="Grid (electricity)">
        network
       </a>
       in Great Britain, connecting
       <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">
        power stations
       </a>
       and major
       <a href="/wiki/Electrical_substation" title="Electrical substation">
        substations
       </a>
       and ensuring that electricity generated anywhere in England, Scotland and Wales can be used to satisfy demand elsewhere.
      </p>
      <p>
       The UK grid is connected as a
       <a href="/wiki/Wide_area_synchronous_grid" title="Wide area synchronous grid">
        wide area synchronous grid
       </a>
       nominally running at 50 hertz. There are also undersea interconnections to northern France (
       <a href="/wiki/HVDC_Cross-Channel" title="HVDC Cross-Channel">
        HVDC Cross-Channel
       </a>
       ), Northern Ireland (
       <a href="/wiki/HVDC_Moyle" title="HVDC Moyle">
        HVDC Moyle
       </a>
       ), the Isle of Man (
       <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man_to_England_Interconnector" title="Isle of Man to England Interconnector">
        Isle of Man to England Interconnector
       </a>
       ), the Netherlands (
       <a href="/wiki/BritNed" title="BritNed">
        BritNed
       </a>
       ) and the Republic of Ireland (
       <a href="/wiki/EirGrid" title="EirGrid">
        EirGrid
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       On the breakup of the
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Electricity_Generating_Board" title="Central Electricity Generating Board">
        Central Electricity Generating Board
       </a>
       in 1990, the ownership and operation of the National Grid in
       <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">
        England and Wales
       </a>
       passed to National Grid Company plc, later to become National Grid Transco, and now
       <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_plc" title="National Grid plc">
        National Grid plc
       </a>
       . In Scotland the grid split into two separate entities, one for southern and central Scotland and the other for northern Scotland, connected by interconnectors to each other. The first is owned and maintained by SP Energy Networks, a subsidiary of
       <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Power" title="Scottish Power">
        Scottish Power
       </a>
       , and the other by
       <a href="/wiki/SSE_plc" title="SSE plc">
        SSE
       </a>
       . However,
       <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_plc" title="National Grid plc">
        National Grid plc
       </a>
       remains the System Operator
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2017)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       for the whole UK Grid.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Electricity_Pylon_-_Chatsworth_Crescent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_488767.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="480" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Electricity_Pylon_-_Chatsworth_Crescent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_488767.jpg/220px-Electricity_Pylon_-_Chatsworth_Crescent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_488767.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Electricity_Pylon_-_Chatsworth_Crescent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_488767.jpg/330px-Electricity_Pylon_-_Chatsworth_Crescent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_488767.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Electricity_Pylon_-_Chatsworth_Crescent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_488767.jpg/440px-Electricity_Pylon_-_Chatsworth_Crescent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_488767.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Electricity_Pylon_-_Chatsworth_Crescent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_488767.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Electricity pylons in an urban area in
         <a href="/wiki/Pudsey" title="Pudsey">
          Pudsey
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">
          West Yorkshire
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       At the end of the 19th century,
       <a href="/wiki/Nikola_Tesla" title="Nikola Tesla">
        Nikola Tesla
       </a>
       established the principles of
       <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_electric_power" title="Three-phase electric power">
        three-phase
       </a>
       high-voltage
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_distribution" title="Electric power distribution">
        electric power distribution
       </a>
       while he was working for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_(1886)" title="Westinghouse Electric (1886)">
        Westinghouse
       </a>
       in the United States. The first to use this system in the United Kingdom was
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Merz" title="Charles Merz">
        Charles Merz
       </a>
       , of the
       <a href="/wiki/Merz_%26_McLellan" title="Merz &amp; McLellan">
        Merz &amp; McLellan
       </a>
       consulting partnership, at his
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune_Bank_Power_Station" title="Neptune Bank Power Station">
        Neptune Bank Power Station
       </a>
       near
       <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">
        Newcastle upon Tyne
       </a>
       . This opened in 1901,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and by 1912 had developed into the largest integrated power system in Europe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The rest of the country, however, continued to use a patchwork of small supply networks.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1925, the British government asked
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Douglas_Weir" title="William Douglas Weir">
        Lord Weir
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">
        Glaswegian
       </a>
       industrialist, to solve the problem of Britain's inefficient and fragmented electricity supply industry. Weir consulted Merz, and the result was the Electricity (Supply) Act 1926, which recommended that a "national
       <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">
        gridiron
       </a>
       " supply system be created.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 1926 Act created the
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Electricity_Board" title="Central Electricity Board">
        Central Electricity Board
       </a>
       , which set up the UK's first synchronised, nationwide AC grid, running at 132 kV, 50 Hz.
      </p>
      <p>
       The grid was created with 4,000 miles of cables- mostly
       <a href="/wiki/Overhead_cable" title="Overhead cable">
        overhead cables
       </a>
       , linking the 122 most efficient power stations. The first "grid tower" was erected near Edinburgh on 14 July 1928
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , and work was completed in September 1933, ahead of schedule and on budget.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It began operating in 1933 as a series of regional grids with auxiliary interconnections for emergency use. Following the unauthorised but successful short term parallelling of all regional grids by the night-time engineers on 29 October 1937,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cochrane_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cochrane-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       by 1938 the grid was operating as a national system. The growth by then in the number of electricity users was the fastest in the world, rising from three quarters of a million in 1920 to nine million in 1938.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indigilb_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-indigilb-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It proved its worth during
       <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">
        the Blitz
       </a>
       when South Wales provided power to replace lost output from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battersea_power_station" title="Battersea power station">
        Battersea
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Fulham_Power_Station" title="Fulham Power Station">
        Fulham
       </a>
       power stations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indigilb_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-indigilb-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The grid was nationalised by the
       <a href="/wiki/Electricity_Act_1947" title="Electricity Act 1947">
        Electricity Act 1947
       </a>
       , which also created the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Electricity_Authority" title="British Electricity Authority">
        British Electricity Authority
       </a>
       . In 1949, the British Electricity Authority decided to upgrade the grid by adding 275 kV links.
      </p>
      <p>
       The 275 kV Transmission System at the time of its inception in 1950, was designed to form part of a national supply system with an anticipated total demand of 30,000 MW by 1970. The predicted demand was already exceeded by 1960. The rapid load growth led the
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Electricity_Generating_Board" title="Central Electricity Generating Board">
        Central Electricity Generating Board
       </a>
       to carry out a study in 1960 of future transmission needs. The report was completed in September 1960, and its study is described in a paper presented to the
       <a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Electrical_Engineers" title="Institution of Electrical Engineers">
        Institution of Electrical Engineers
       </a>
       by Messrs E.S. Booth, D. Clark, J.L. Egginton and J.S. Forrest in 1962.
      </p>
      <p>
       Considered in the study, together with the increased demand, was the effect on the transmission system of the rapid advances in generator design resulting in projected power stations of 2,000–3,000 MW's installed capacity. These new stations were mostly to be sited where advantage could be taken of a surplus of cheap low-grade fuel and adequate supplies of cooling water, but these situations did not coincide with the load centres.
       <a href="/wiki/West_Burton_power_stations" title="West Burton power stations">
        West Burton
       </a>
       with 4 x 500 MW machines, sited at the
       <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">
        Nottinghamshire
       </a>
       coalfield near the
       <a href="/wiki/River_Trent" title="River Trent">
        River Trent
       </a>
       , is a typical example. These developments shifted the emphasis on the transmission system, from interconnection to the primary function of bulk power transfers from the generation areas to the load centres, such as the anticipated transfer in 1970 of some 6,000 MW from
       <a href="/wiki/The_Midlands" title="The Midlands">
        The Midlands
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Home_counties" title="Home counties">
        Home counties
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Continued reinforcement and extension of the existing 275 kV systems was examined as a possible solution. However, in addition to the technical problem of very high fault levels many more lines would have been required to obtain the estimated transfers at 275 kV. As this was not consistent with the
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Electricity_Generating_Board" title="Central Electricity Generating Board">
        Central Electricity Generating Board
       </a>
       's policy of preservation of amenities a further solution was sought. Consideration was given to both a 400 kV and 500 kV scheme as the alternatives, either of which gave a sufficient margin for future expansion. The decision in favour of a 400 kV system was made for two main reasons. Firstly the majority of the 275 kV lines could be uprated to 400 kV, and secondly it was envisaged that the operation at 400 kV could commence in 1965 compared with 1968 for a 500 kV scheme. Design work was started and in order to meet the programme for 1965 it was necessary for the contract engineering for the first projects to run concurrently with the design. One of these projects was the
       <a href="/wiki/West_Burton_power_stations" title="West Burton power stations">
        West Burton
       </a>
       400 kV Indoor Substation, the first section of which was commissioned in June 1965. From 1965, the grid was partly upgraded to 400 kV, beginning with a 150-mile (241 km) line from
       <a href="/wiki/Sundon" title="Sundon">
        Sundon
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/West_Burton_power_stations" title="West Burton power stations">
        West Burton
       </a>
       , to become the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergrid" title="Supergrid">
         Supergrid
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the most recent issue of the code that governs the British Grid, the
       <i>
        Grid Code
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the Supergrid is defined as referring to those parts of the British electricity transmission system that are connected at voltages in excess of 200 kV. British power system planners and operational staff therefore invariably speak of the Supergrid in this context although in practice the definition used captures all of the infrastructure owned by the National Grid company in England and Wales, and (in England and Wales) no other equipment.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2013 the construction of the 2.2 GW undersea
       <a href="/wiki/Western_HVDC_Link" title="Western HVDC Link">
        Western HVDC Link
       </a>
       from Scotland to North Wales started, with power expected to flow in 2017.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-westernhvdclink_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-westernhvdclink-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is the first major non-
       <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">
        alternating current
       </a>
       grid link within the UK, though interconnects to foreign grids already use
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HVDC" title="HVDC">
        HVDC
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Network_size">
        Network size
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Network size">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The following figures are taken from the 2005 Seven Year Statement (SYS)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Maximum demand (2005/6): 63
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigawatt" title="Gigawatt">
         GW
        </a>
        (approx.) (81.39% of capacity)
       </li>
       <li>
        Annual electrical energy used in the UK is around 360 TWh (1.3 EJ)
       </li>
       <li>
        Capacity (2005/6): 79.9 GW (or 80 GW per the 2008 Seven Year Statement)
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Number of large
        <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">
         power stations
        </a>
        connected to it: 181
       </li>
       <li>
        Length of 400 kV grid: 11,500 km (circuit)
       </li>
       <li>
        Length of 275 kV grid: 9,800 km (circuit)
       </li>
       <li>
        Length of 132 kV (or lower) grid; 5,250 km (circuit)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Total generating capacity is supplied roughly equally by
       <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Renewable energy in the United Kingdom">
        renewable
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">
        nuclear
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">
        coal
       </a>
       fired and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">
        gas
       </a>
       fired
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_stations" title="Power stations">
        power stations
       </a>
       . Annual energy used in the UK is around 360 TWh (1.3 EJ), with an average load factor of 72% (i.e. 3.6×10
       <sup>
        11
       </sup>
       /(8,760 × 57×10
       <sup>
        6
       </sup>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Losses">
        Losses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Losses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Figures are again from the 2005 SYS.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Joule_heating" title="Joule heating">
         Joule heating
        </a>
        in cables: 857.8 MW
       </li>
       <li>
        Fixed losses: 266 MW (consists of
        <a href="/wiki/Corona_discharge" title="Corona discharge">
         corona
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_loss" title="Core loss">
         iron loss
        </a>
        ; can be 100 MW higher in adverse weather)
       </li>
       <li>
        Substation transformer heating losses: 142.4 MW
       </li>
       <li>
        Generator transformer heating losses: 157.3 MW
       </li>
       <li>
        Total losses: 1,423.5 MW (2.29% of peak demand)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Although overall losses in the national grid are low, there are significant further losses in onward
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electricity_distribution" title="Electricity distribution">
        electricity distribution
       </a>
       to the consumer, causing a total distribution loss of about 7.7%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However losses differ significantly for customers connected at different voltages; connected at high voltage the total losses are about 2.6%, at medium voltage 6.4% and at low voltage 12.2%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Generated power entering the grid is metered at the high-voltage side of the generator transformer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Any power losses in the generator transformer are therefore accounted to the generating company, not to the grid system. The power loss in the generator transformer does not contribute to the grid losses.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Power_flow">
        Power flow
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Power flow">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There is an average power flow of about 11 GW from the north of the UK, particularly from Scotland and northern England, to the south of the UK across the grid. This flow was anticipated to grow to about 12 GW by 2014.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because of the power loss associated with this north to south flow, the effectiveness and efficiency of new generation capacity is significantly affected by its location. For example, new generating capacity on the south coast has about 12% greater effectiveness due to reduced transmission system power losses compared to new generating capacity in north England, and about 20% greater effectiveness than northern Scotland.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Transmission_losses_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Transmission_losses-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interconnectors">
        Interconnectors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interconnectors">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The UK grid is connected to adjacent European and Irish electrical grids by
       <a href="/wiki/Submarine_power_cable" title="Submarine power cable">
        submarine power cables
       </a>
       at an
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_grid_of_Continental_Europe#List_of_electricity_interconnection_level" title="Synchronous grid of Continental Europe">
        electricity interconnection level
       </a>
       (transmission capacity relative to production capacity) of 6%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eil_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-eil-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The connections include direct-current cables to northern France (2 GW
       <a href="/wiki/HVDC_Cross-Channel" title="HVDC Cross-Channel">
        HVDC Cross-Channel
       </a>
       ), the Netherlands (1 GW
       <a href="/wiki/BritNed" title="BritNed">
        BritNed
       </a>
       ), Northern Ireland (250 MW
       <a href="/wiki/HVDC_Moyle" title="HVDC Moyle">
        HVDC Moyle
       </a>
       ), and Republic of Ireland (500 MW
       <a href="/wiki/East%E2%80%93West_Interconnector" title="East–West Interconnector">
        East–West Interconnector
       </a>
       ). There is also the 40 MW AC cable to the Isle of Man (
       <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man_to_England_Interconnector" title="Isle of Man to England Interconnector">
        Isle of Man to England Interconnector
       </a>
       ). There are plans to lay cables to link the UK with Belgium (
       <a href="/wiki/Nemo_link" title="Nemo link">
        Nemo link
       </a>
       ), Norway (1.4 GW
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NSN_Link" title="NSN Link">
        NSN Link
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
        Denmark
       </a>
       via the 1.4 GW
       <a href="/wiki/Viking_Link" title="Viking Link">
        Viking Link
       </a>
       , a second link with France,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Iceland in the future.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The internal
       <a href="/wiki/Western_HVDC_Link" title="Western HVDC Link">
        Western HVDC Link
       </a>
       is being built to connect Scotland and Wales at 2.2 GW.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reserve_services_and_frequency_response">
        Reserve services and frequency response
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reserve services and frequency response">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_Reserve_Service" title="National Grid Reserve Service">
        National Grid Reserve Service
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       National Grid is responsible for contracting short term generating provision to cover demand prediction errors and sudden failures at power stations. This covers a few hours of operation giving time for market contracts to be established to cover longer term balancing.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency-response_reserve" title="Frequency-response reserve">
        Frequency-response reserves
       </a>
       act to keep the system's
       <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">
        AC
       </a>
       frequency within ±1% of 50
       <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">
        Hz
       </a>
       , except in exceptional circumstances. These are used on a second by second basis to either lower the demand or to provide extra generation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ng-consultation-june2009_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ng-consultation-june2009-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Reserve services are a group of services each acting within different response times:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ng-consultation-june2009_26-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ng-consultation-june2009-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Fast Reserve: rapid delivery (within two minutes) of increased generation or reduced demand, sustainable for a minimum of 15 minutes.
       </li>
       <li>
        Fast Start: generation units that start from a standstill and deliver power within five minutes automatically, or within seven minutes of a manual instruction, with generation maintained for a minimum of four hours.
       </li>
       <li>
        Demand Management: reduction in demand of at least 25MW from large power users, for at least an hour.
       </li>
       <li>
        Short Term Operating Reserve (STOR): generation of at least 3MW, from a single or aggregation of sites, within four hours of instruction and maintained for at least two hours.
       </li>
       <li>
        BM Start-Up: mainstream major generation units maintained in either an
        <i>
         energy readiness
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         hot standby
        </i>
        state.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These reserves are sized according to three factors:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UKERC-march2006_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UKERC-march2006-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The largest credible single generation failure event, which is currently either
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sizewell_B_nuclear_power_station" title="Sizewell B nuclear power station">
         Sizewell B nuclear power station
        </a>
        (1,260 MW) or one cable of the
        <a href="/wiki/HVDC_Cross-Channel" title="HVDC Cross-Channel">
         HVDC Cross-Channel
        </a>
        interconnector (1,000 MW)
       </li>
       <li>
        The general anticipated availability of all generation plants
       </li>
       <li>
        Anticipated demand prediction errors
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Control_of_the_grid">
        Control of the grid
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Control of the grid">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>

      <p>
       The costs of operating the National Grid System are recouped by National Grid Electricity Transmission plc (NGET) through levying of Transmission Network Use of System (TNUoS) charges on the users of the system. The costs are split between the generators and the users of electricity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-claverton-energy1_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-claverton-energy1-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Tariffs are set annually by NGET, and are zonal in nature—that is, the country is divided up into different zones, each with a different tariff for generation and consumption. In general, tariffs are higher for generators in the north and consumers in the south. This is representative of the fact that there is currently a north-south flow of electricity, and the additional stresses on the system increasing demand in areas of currently high demand causes.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Triad_demand">
        Triad demand
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Triad demand">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Triad demand is measured as the average demand on the system over three half-hours between November and February (inclusive) in a financial year. These three half-hours comprise the half-hour of system demand peak and the two other half-hours of highest system demand which are separated from system demand peak and each other by at least ten days.
      </p>
      <p>
       These half-hours of peak demand are usually referred to as Triads.
      </p>
      <p>
       In April of each year, each licensed electricity supplier (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Centrica" title="Centrica">
        Centrica
       </a>
       , BGB, etc.) is charged a yearly fee for the load it imposed on the grid during those three half-hours of the previous winter. Exact charges vary depending on the distance from the centre of the network, but in the South West it is £21,000/MW.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The average for the whole country is about £15,000/MW. This is a means for National Grid to recover its charges, and to impose an incentive on users to minimise consumption at peak, thereby easing the need for investment in the system. It is estimated that these charges reduced peak load by about 1 GW out of say 57 GW.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This is the main source of income which National Grid uses to cover its costs and these charges are commonly also known as Transmission Network Use of System charges (TNUoS). (This is for high voltage long distance transmission: lower voltage distribution is charged separately.) The grid also charges an annual fee to cover the cost of generators, distribution networks and large industrial users connecting.
      </p>
      <p>
       These Triad charges encourage users to cut load at peak periods; this is often done using diesel generators. Such generators are also routinely used by National Grid.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Estimating_costs_per_kWh_of_transmission">
        Estimating costs per kWh of transmission
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </span>

      <p>
       If the total TNUoS or Triad receipts (say £15,000/MW·year × 50,000 MW = £750 million/year) is divided by the total number of units delivered by the UK generating system in a year (the total number of units sold – say 360 terawatt-hours (1.3 EJ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-claverton-energy1_34-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-claverton-energy1-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), then a crude estimate can be made of transmission costs, and one gets the figure of around 0.2p/kWh. Other estimates also give a figure of 0.2p/kWh.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-claverton-energy1_34-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-claverton-energy1-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, Bernard Quigg notes: "According to the 06/07 annual accounts for NGC UK transmission, NGC carried 350TWh for an income of £2012m in 2007, i. e. NGC receives 0.66p per kW hour. With two years inflation to 2008/9, say 0.71p per kWh.",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but this also includes generators' connection fees.
      </p>

      <p>
       In order to be allowed to supply electricity to the transmission system, generators must be licensed (by BEIS) and enter into a connection agreement with NGET which also grants Transmission Entry Capacity (TEC). Generators contribute to the costs of running the system by paying for TEC, at the generation TNUoS tariffs set by NGET. This is charged on a maximum-capacity basis. In other words, a generator with 100 MW of TEC who only generated at a maximum rate of 75 MW during the year would still be charged for the full 100 MW of TEC.
      </p>
      <p>
       In some cases, there are negative TNUoS tariffs. These generators are paid a sum based on their peak net supply over three
       <i>
        proving runs
       </i>
       over the course of the year. This represents the reduction in costs caused by having a generator so close to the centre of demand of the country.
      </p>

      <p>
       Consumers of electricity are split into two categories: half-hourly metered (HH) and non-half-hourly metered (NHH). Customers whose peak demand is sufficiently high are obliged to have a HH meter, which, in effect, takes a meter reading every 30 minutes. The rates at which charges are levied on these customers' electricity suppliers therefore varies 17,520 times a (non-leap) year.
      </p>
      <p>
       The TNUoS charges for a HH metered customer are based on their demand during three half-hour periods of greatest demand between November and February, known as the Triad. Due to the nature of electricity demand in the UK, the three Triad periods always fall in the early evening, and must be separated by at least ten clear working days. The TNUoS charges for a HH customer are simply their average demand during the triad periods multiplied by the tariff for their zone. Therefore, (as of 2007
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) a customer in London with a 1 MW average demand during the three triad periods would pay £19,430 in TNUoS charges.
      </p>
      <p>
       TNUoS charges levied on NHH metered customers are much simpler. A supplier is charged for the sum of their total consumption between 16:00 and 19:00 every day over a year, multiplied by the relevant tariff.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Major_incidents">
        Major incidents
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Major incidents">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Power cuts due to either problems on the actual infrastructure of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergrid" title="Supergrid">
        supergrid
       </a>
       (defined in the Grid Code, as the transmission system operated by National Grid, which in England and Wales, comprises lines energized at 275,000 volts and 400,000 volts), or due to lack of generation to supply it with sufficient energy at each point in time, are exceedingly rare. The nominal standard of security of supply is for power cuts due to lack of generation to occur in nine winters in a hundred.
      </p>
      <p>
       The overall performance measure for electricity transmission is published on NGET's website:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       – this site includes a simple high level figure on transmission availability and reliability of supply – for 2008-9 this was 99.99979%. It is issues affecting the low voltage distribution systems, for which National Grid is not responsible, that cause almost all the 60 minutes or so per year, on average, of actual domestic power cuts. Most of these low voltage distribution interruptions are in turn, the fault of third parties such as workmen drilling through the street mains (or subterranean higher voltage) cables; this does not happen to major transmission lines, which are for the most part overhead on pylons. For comparison with supergrid availability, Ofgem, the electricity regulator, has published figures on the performance of 14 electricity distributors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Since 1990, there have only been two power cuts of high national prominence, that were linked to National Grid (although one was actually due to generation issues).
      </p>

      <p>
       The first case was in 2003, and related to the condition of National Grid's assets. National Grid was implicated in a power cut affecting 10 per cent of London in August – see
       <a href="/wiki/2003_London_blackout" title="2003 London blackout">
        2003 London blackout
       </a>
       . In essence, some news stories accused Grid of under-investment in new assets at the time; this was not necessarily true, but it did transpire that a transformer oil leak had been left untreated, except for top-ups, for many months, pending a proper fix. It also transpired that there was a significant error in a protection relay setting which became evident, resulting in a power cut, only when the first fault, the oil leak, had a real effect. National Grid took some time to admit to these aspects of the incident. It is arguable either way whether, with more money spent on system planning and maintenance, the human error in the relay setting could have been prevented.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="May_2008_incident">
        May 2008 incident
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Grid_(Great_Britain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: May 2008 incident">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The second case was in May 2008, and related to some generation issues for which National Grid was not responsible. A power cut took place in which a protective shutdown of parts of the network was undertaken by the distribution network operators, under pre-arranged rules, due to a sudden loss of generating capacity causing a severe drop in system frequency. What happened first, was that two of Britain's largest power stations,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longannet" title="Longannet">
        Longannet
       </a>
       in Fife and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sizewell_B" title="Sizewell B">
        Sizewell B
       </a>
       in Suffolk, shut down unexpectedly ('tripped') within five minutes of one another. There was definitely no relationship between the two trips – the first did not cause the second. Such a loss is most unusual; Grid currently secures only against the loss of 1320 MW (the "infrequent infeed loss limit", due to rise to 1800 MW from 2014). There was, as a result, a sudden 1,510 megawatt adverse change in the balance of generation and demand on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergrid" title="Supergrid">
        supergrid
       </a>
       . Because National Grid only secures against the near-instantaneous loss of 1320 MW, the frequency dropped to 49.2 Hz. Whilst the frequency was dropping to 49.2 Hz, or just after it reached that point, 40 MW of wind farms and more than 92 MW of other embedded (meaning, connected to the distribution system, rather than directly connected to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergrid" title="Supergrid">
        supergrid
       </a>
       ) generation, such as landfill plant, tripped on the basis of the rate of change of frequency ('ROCOF') being high, just as it is supposed to do under the G 59/2 connection rules.
      </p>
      <p>
       The frequency stabilised at 49.2 Hz for a short while. This would have been an acceptable frequency excursion, even though it was below the usual lower limit of 49.5 Hz, and recovery would not have been problematic. In fact, the fact that frequency stabilised at this level in spite of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BDBE" title="BDBE">
        beyond-design-basis event
       </a>
       , could be viewed as reassuring. Ireland, which being a smaller system has a 'friskier' grid, sees about 10 frequency excursions below 49.5 Hz per year – her target frequency being 50 Hz, just as in Britain. Consumers would not have noticed the small drop in system frequency; other aspects of their supply such as voltage, remained perfect. There would, therefore, have been no consumer detriment; all would have been well at this point, had nothing further untoward occurred.
      </p>
      <p>
       Further issues, however, affecting smaller generators, arose. The problem was because the frequency had remained below 49.5 Hz for more than a few seconds, and because some generators' control settings were wrong. The current connection standard G 59/2 for embedded generation states that they must not trip (cease generating) as a result of sustained low frequency, until frequency has fallen below 47 Hz. However, a number of embedded generators are still using out of date control software that is not compliant with G59/2, as it erroneously trips them (as per the previous standard, G/59, in force when they were designed and specified) if frequency falls below 49.5 Hz for a few seconds. Because of the out of date software, another 279 MW of embedded generation tripped as a result of the low frequency whilst it was at 49.2 Hz. This was a problem as the Grid had no remaining available fast-acting generation, or demand-response, reserve margins. The frequency fell as a result to 48.792 Hz. The grid rules state that as frequency falls below 48.8 Hz, distribution network operators must enact compulsory demand control. This should start, if time permits, with voltage reduction, rapidly followed by the compulsory disconnection of, in stages, up to a final total of 60 per cent of all distribution-connected customers (a very small number of very large customers are connected directly to the supergrid; for them, other measures apply). There was no time to use voltage reduction (which keeps customers on supply, but subtly reduces their demand through reducing the voltage slightly); as a result, 546 MW of demand was automatically disconnected by distribution network operators. None of the directly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergrid" title="Supergrid">
        supergrid
       </a>
       -connected customers were cut off. National Grid had by now taken other measures to increase output at other generation sites (and demand had been reduced at those customer sites where the customer has volunteered for this to happen, in return for reimbursement, under demand-side response contracts with National Grid, or with their supplier). National Grid was then able to restore system frequency. The average duration of loss of supply to the 546 MW of mostly low-voltage-connected (e.g. domestic) demand affected was 20 minutes. As to communications during the incident, National Grid had time to issue a warning to all users of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergrid" title="Supergrid">
        supergrid
       </a>
       , (
       <i>
        demand control imminent
       </i>
       ), which is just one step away from its most serious warning "demand disconnection warning". During these incidents, the System was at risk to further generation loss which could have resulted in parts of the network being automatically disconnected by the operation of low frequency protection to ensure frequency is maintained within mandatory limits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">

        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
