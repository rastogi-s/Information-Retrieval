   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    X-ray telescope
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       An
       <b>
        X-ray telescope
       </b>
       (XRT) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       that is designed to observe remote objects in the
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-ray
       </a>
       spectrum. In order to get above the Earth's atmosphere, which is opaque to X-rays, X-ray telescopes must be mounted on high altitude rockets,
       <a href="/wiki/High-altitude_balloon" title="High-altitude balloon">
        balloons
       </a>
       or artificial satellites.
      </p>
      <p>
       The basic elements of the telescope are the optics (focusing or collimating), that collects the radiation entering the telescope, and the detector, on which the radiation is collected and measured. A variety of different designs and technologies have been used for these elements.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many of the existing telescopes on satellites are compounded of multiple copies or variations of a detector-telescope system, whose capabilities add or complement each other and additional fixed or removable elements
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (filters, spectrometers) that add functionalities to the instrument.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Optics">
        Optics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Optics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_optics" title="X-ray optics">
        X-ray optics
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The most common methods used in X-ray optics are
       <a href="/wiki/Wolter_telescope" title="Wolter telescope">
        grazing incidence mirrors
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Collimator" title="Collimator">
        collimated apertures
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Focusing_mirrors">
        Focusing mirrors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Focusing mirrors">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pointing_X-ray_Eyes_at_our_Resident_Supermassive_Black_Hole.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Pointing_X-ray_Eyes_at_our_Resident_Supermassive_Black_Hole.jpg/220px-Pointing_X-ray_Eyes_at_our_Resident_Supermassive_Black_Hole.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Pointing_X-ray_Eyes_at_our_Resident_Supermassive_Black_Hole.jpg/330px-Pointing_X-ray_Eyes_at_our_Resident_Supermassive_Black_Hole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Pointing_X-ray_Eyes_at_our_Resident_Supermassive_Black_Hole.jpg/440px-Pointing_X-ray_Eyes_at_our_Resident_Supermassive_Black_Hole.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pointing_X-ray_Eyes_at_our_Resident_Supermassive_Black_Hole.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         NuSTAR, has captured these first, focused views of the supermassive black hole at the heart of our galaxy in high-energy X-ray light.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The utilization of X-ray mirrors allows to focus the incident radiation on the detector plane.
      </p>
      <p>
       Different geometries (e.g. Kirkpartick-Baez or Lobster-eye) have been suggested or employed, but almost the totality of existing telescopes employs some variation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Wolter_telescope" title="Wolter telescope">
        Wolter I design
       </a>
       . The limitations of this type of
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_optics" title="X-ray optics">
        X-ray optics
       </a>
       result in much narrower fields of view (typically &lt;1 degree) than visible or UV telescopes.
      </p>
      <p>
       With respect to collimated optics, focusing optics allow:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        a high resolution imaging
       </li>
       <li>
        a high telescope sensitivity: since radiation is focused on a small area,
        <a href="/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio" title="Signal-to-noise ratio">
         Signal-to-noise ratio
        </a>
        is much higher for this kind of instruments.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The mirrors can be made of
       <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">
        ceramic
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_foil" title="Metal foil">
        metal foil
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xraysMirror_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-xraysMirror-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       coated with a thin layer of a reflective material (typically
       <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">
        gold
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">
        iridium
       </a>
       ). Mirrors based on this construction work on the basis of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_reflection" title="Total reflection">
        total reflection
       </a>
       of light at grazing incidence.
      </p>
      <p>
       This technology is limited in energy range by the inverse relation between critical angle for total reflection and radiation energy. The limit in the early 2000s with
       <a href="/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-ray Observatory">
        Chandra
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/XMM-Newton" title="XMM-Newton">
        XMM-Newton
       </a>
       X-ray
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_observatory" title="Space observatory">
        observatories
       </a>
       was about 15 kilo-
       <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">
        electronvolt
       </a>
       (keV) light.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nustar1_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nustar1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Using new multi-layered coated mirrors, the X-ray mirror for the
       <a href="/wiki/NuSTAR" title="NuSTAR">
        NuSTAR
       </a>
       telescope pushed this up to 79 keV light.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nustar1_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nustar1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To reflect at this level, glass layers were multi-coated with
       <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">
        tungsten
       </a>
       (W)/
       <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">
        silicon
       </a>
       (Si) or
       <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">
        platinum
       </a>
       (Pt)/
       <a href="/wiki/Silicon_carbide" title="Silicon carbide">
        silicon carbide
       </a>
       (SiC).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nustar1_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-nustar1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Collimating_Optics">
        Collimating Optics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Collimating Optics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Coded_aperture" title="Coded aperture">
        coded aperture
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       While earlier X-ray telescopes were using simple collimating techniques (e.g. rotating collimators, wire collimators),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the technology most currently used on present days employs coded aperture masks. This technique uses a flat aperture patterned grille in front of the detector. This design results less sensitive than focusing optics and imaging quality and identification of source position is much poorer, however it offers a larger
       <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">
        field of view
       </a>
       and can be employed at higher energies, where grazing incidence optics become ineffective. Also the imaging is not direct, but the image is rather reconstructed by post-processing of the signal.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Detectors">
        Detectors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Detectors">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_astronomy_detector" title="X-ray astronomy detector">
        X-ray astronomy detector
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Several technologies have been employed on detectors for X-ray telescopes, ranging from counters like Ionization chambers,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geiger_counters" title="Geiger counters">
        geiger counters
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">
        scintillators
       </a>
       to imaging detectors like
       <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
        CCDs
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CMOS_sensor" title="CMOS sensor">
        CMOS
       </a>
       sensors. The use of micro-calorimeters, that offer the added capability of measuring with great accuracy the energy of the radiation, is planned for future missions.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exosat">
        Exosat
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exosat">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The two low-energy imaging telescopes onboard
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exosat" title="Exosat">
        Exosat
       </a>
       used
       <a href="/wiki/Wolter_telescope" title="Wolter telescope">
        Wolter
       </a>
       I
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_optics" title="X-ray optics">
        X-ray optics
       </a>
       and were equipped with two focal plane detectors:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        a position-sensitive
        <a href="/wiki/Proportional_counter" title="Proportional counter">
         proportional counter
        </a>
        (PSD) and
       </li>
       <li>
        a channel multiplier array (CMA).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoff_6-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Hoff-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hard_X-ray_telescope">
        Hard X-ray telescope
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hard X-ray telescope">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On board
       <a href="/wiki/OSO_7" title="OSO 7">
        OSO 7
       </a>
       was a hard X-ray telescope. Its effective energy range: 7–550 keV, field of view (FOV) 6.5°, effective area ~64 cm
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Filin_telescope">
        Filin telescope
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Filin telescope">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Filin telescope carried aboard
       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_4" title="Salyut 4">
        Salyut 4
       </a>
       , consisted of four gas flow proportional counters, three of which had a total detection surface of 450 cm
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       in the energy range 2–10 keV, and one of which has an effective surface of 37 cm
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       for the range 0.2–2 keV. The FOV was limited by a slit collimator to 3° × 10° FWHM. The instrumentation included optical sensors mounted on the outside of the station together with the X-ray detectors. The power supply and measurement units were inside the station. Ground based calibration of the detectors occurred along with in-flight operation in three modes: inertial orientation, orbital orientation, and survey. Data were collected in 4 energy channels: 2–3.1 keV, 3.1–5.9 keV, 5.9–9.6 keV, and 2–9.6 keV in the larger detectors. The smaller detector had discriminator levels set at 0.2 keV, 0.55 keV, and 0.95 keV.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SIGMA_telescope">
        SIGMA telescope
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: SIGMA telescope">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The hard X-ray and low-energy gamma-ray
       <a href="/wiki/Granat#SIGMA" title="Granat">
        SIGMA
       </a>
       telescope covered the energy range 35–1300 keV,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mandrou_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mandrou-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with an effective area of 800 cm
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       and a maximum sensitivity field of view of ~5° × 5°. The maximum
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_resolution" title="Angular resolution">
        angular resolution
       </a>
       was 15  arcmin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Revnivtsev_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Revnivtsev-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The energy resolution was 8% at 511  keV.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IKI_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IKI-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its imaging capabilities were derived from the association of a coded mask and a position sensitive detector based on the Anger camera principle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA2007_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA2007-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="ART-P_X-ray_telescope">
        ART-P X-ray telescope
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: ART-P X-ray telescope">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Granat#ART-P" title="Granat">
        ART-P X-ray telescope
       </a>
       covered the energy range 4 to 60 keV for imaging and 4 to 100 keV for spectroscopy and timing. There were four identical modules of the ART-P telescope, each consisting of a position sensitive
       <a href="/wiki/Wire_chamber" title="Wire chamber">
        multi-wire proportional counter (MWPC)
       </a>
       together with a URA coded mask. Each module had an effective area of approximately 600 cm
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       , producing a
       <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">
        FOV
       </a>
       of 1.8° × 1.8°. The
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_resolution" title="Angular resolution">
        angular resolution
       </a>
       was 5
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">
        arcmin
       </a>
       ; temporal and energy resolutions were 3.9
       <a href="/wiki/Millisecond" title="Millisecond">
        ms
       </a>
       and 22% at 6 keV, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ART-P_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ART-P-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The instrument achieved a sensitivity of 0.001 of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crab_nebula" title="Crab nebula">
        Crab nebula
       </a>
       source (= 1 "mCrab") in an eight-hour exposure. The maximum time resolution was 4 ms.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IKI_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-IKI-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA2007_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA2007-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Focusing_X-ray_telescope">
        Focusing X-ray telescope
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Focusing X-ray telescope">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Broad_Band_X-ray_Telescope" title="Broad Band X-ray Telescope">
        Broad Band X-ray Telescope
       </a>
       (BBXRT) was flown on the
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia" title="Space Shuttle Columbia">
        Space Shuttle Columbia
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/STS-35" title="STS-35">
        STS-35
       </a>
       ) as part of the ASTRO-1 payload. BBXRT was the first focusing X-ray telescope operating over a broad energy range 0.3–12 keV with a moderate energy resolution (90 eV at 1 keV and 150 eV at 6 keV). The two Co-Aligned Telescopes with a segmented Si(Li) solid state spectrometer each (detector A and B) composite of five pixels. Total FOV 17.4´ diameter, Central pixel FOV 4´ diameter. Total area 765 cm
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       at 1.5 keV, and 300 cm
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       at 7 keV.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="XRT_on_the_Swift_MIDEX_mission">
        XRT on the Swift MIDEX mission
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: XRT on the Swift MIDEX mission">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The XRT on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swift_Gamma-Ray_Burst_Mission" title="Swift Gamma-Ray Burst Mission">
        <i>
         Swift
        </i>
       </a>
       MIDEX mission (0.2–10 keV energy range) uses a Wolter I telescope to focus X-rays onto a thermoelectrically cooled CCD.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrows_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Burrows-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was designed to measure the fluxes, spectra, and lightcurves of
       <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">
        Gamma-ray bursts
       </a>
       (GRBs) and afterglows over a wide dynamic range covering more than 7 orders of magnitude in flux. The XRT can pinpoint GRBs to 5-arcsec accuracy within 10 seconds of target acquisition for a typical GRB and can study the X-ray counterparts of GRBs beginning 20–70 seconds from burst discovery and continuing for days to weeks.
      </p>
      <p>
       The overall telescope length is 4.67 m with a focal length of 3.500 mm and a diameter of 0.51 m.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrows_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Burrows-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The primary structural element is an aluminum optical bench interface flange at the front of the telescope that supports the forward and aft telescope tubes, the mirror module, the electron deflector, and the internal alignment monitor optics and camera, plus mounting points to the
       <i>
        Swift
       </i>
       observatory.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrows_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Burrows-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 508 mm diameter telescope tube is made of graphite fiber/cyanate ester in two sections. The outer graphite fiber layup is designed to minimize the longitudinal coefficient of thermal expansion, whereas the inner composite tube is lined internally with an aluminum foil vapor barrier to guard against outgassing of water vapor or epoxy contaminants into the telescope interior.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrows_12-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Burrows-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The telescope has a forward tube which encloses the mirrors and supports the door assembly and star trackers, and an aft tube which supports the focal plane camera and internal optical baffles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrows_12-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Burrows-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The mirror module consists of 12 nested Wolter I grazing incidence mirrors held in place by front and rear spiders. The passively heated mirrors are gold-coated, electroformed nickel shells 600 mm long with diameters ranging from 191 to 300 mm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrows_12-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Burrows-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The X-ray imager has an effective area of &gt;120 cm
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       at 1.15 keV, a field of view of 23.6 × 23.6 arcmin, and
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_resolution" title="Angular resolution">
        angular resolution
       </a>
       (θ) of 18 arcsec at half-power diameter (HPD). The detection sensitivity is 2 × 10
       <sup>
        −14
       </sup>
       erg cm
       <sup>
        −2
       </sup>
       s
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       in 10
       <sup>
        4
       </sup>
       s. The mirror point spread function (PSF) has a 15 arcsec HPD at the best on-axis focus (at 1.5 keV). The mirror is slightly defocused in the XRT to provide a more uniform PSF for the entire field of view hence the instrument PSF θ = 18 arcsec.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrows_12-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Burrows-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_X-ray_telescopes">
        History of X-ray telescopes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=X-ray_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: History of X-ray telescopes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_X-ray_astronomy" title="History of X-ray astronomy">
        History of X-ray astronomy
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The first X-ray telescope employing Wolter Type I grazing-incidence optics was employed in a rocket-borne experiment in 1965 to obtain X-ray images of the Sun (R. Giacconi et al., ApJ
       <b>
        142
       </b>
       , 1274 (1965)).
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Observatory" title="Einstein Observatory">
        Einstein Observatory
       </a>
       (1978–1981), also known as HEAO-2, was the first orbiting X-ray observatory with a Wolter Type I telescope (R. Giacconi et al., ApJ
       <b>
        230
       </b>
       ,540 (1979)). It obtained high-resolution X-ray images in the energy range from 0.1 to 4 keV of stars of all types, supernova remnants, galaxies, and clusters of galaxies.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HEAO-1" title="HEAO-1">
        HEAO-1
       </a>
       (1977-1979) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HEAO-3" title="HEAO-3">
        HEAO-3
       </a>
       (1979-1981) were others in that series. Another large project was
       <a href="/wiki/ROSAT" title="ROSAT">
        ROSAT
       </a>
       (active from 1990-1999), which was a heavy X-ray space observatory with focusing X-ray optics.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chandra_X-Ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-Ray Observatory">
        Chandra X-Ray Observatory
       </a>
       is among the recent satellite observatories launched by NASA, and by the Space Agencies of Europe, Japan, and Russia. Chandra has operated for more than 10 years in a high elliptical orbit, returning thousands 0.5 arc-second images and high-resolution spectra of all kinds of astronomical objects in the energy range from 0.5 to 8.0 keV. Many of the spectacular images from Chandra can be seen on the NASA/Goddard website.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NuStar" title="NuStar">
        NuStar
       </a>
       is one of the latest X-ray space telescopes, launched in June 2012. The telescope observes radiation in a high-energy range (3 - 79 keV), and with high resolution. NuStar is sensitive to the 68 and 78 keV signals from decay of
       <sup>
        44
       </sup>
       Ti in supernovae.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_and_Extreme_Magnetism" title="Gravity and Extreme Magnetism">
        Gravity and Extreme Magnetism
       </a>
       (GEMS) would have measured X-ray polarization but was canceled in 2012.
      </p>
