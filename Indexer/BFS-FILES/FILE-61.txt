   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Subsolar point
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="1000" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Subsolar_point.png/400px-Subsolar_point.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Subsolar_point.png/600px-Subsolar_point.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Subsolar_point.png/800px-Subsolar_point.png 2x" width="400"/>

         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Subsolar_point.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A basic diagram showing the location of the subsolar point on the Earth's surface. The angle between the sun and the local ground level is exactly 90° at the subsolar point.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        subsolar point
       </b>
       on a
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       is where its sun is perceived to be directly overhead (in
       <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">
        zenith
       </a>
       );
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that is where the sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_(optics)" title="Ray (optics)">
        rays
       </a>
       are hitting the planet exactly
       <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">
        perpendicular
       </a>
       to its surface. It can also mean the point closest to the sun on an object in space, even though the sun might not be visible.
      </p>
      <p>
       For planets with an orientation and rotation similar to the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's, the subsolar point will move westward, circling the
       <a href="/wiki/Globe" title="Globe">
        globe
       </a>
       once a day, approximately moving along a
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">
        circle of latitude
       </a>
       . However, it will also move north and south between the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">
        tropics
       </a>
       over the course of a year, so it is spiraling, like a
       <a href="/wiki/Helix" title="Helix">
        helix
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/December_solstice" title="December solstice">
        December solstice
       </a>
       occurs when the subsolar point is on the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">
        Tropic of Capricorn
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/June_solstice" title="June solstice">
        June solstice
       </a>
       is at the instant when the subsolar point is on the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">
        Tropic of Cancer
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/March_equinox" title="March equinox">
        March
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/September_equinox" title="September equinox">
        September equinoxes
       </a>
       occur when the subsolar point crosses the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       When the point passes through
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">
        Hawaii
       </a>
       , the only
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        U.S. state
       </a>
       in which this happens, it is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Lahaina_Noon" title="Lahaina Noon">
        Lahaina Noon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
