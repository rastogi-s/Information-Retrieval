      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
        Early life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edmond_Halley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Halley was born in
       <a href="/wiki/Haggerston" title="Haggerston">
        Haggerston
       </a>
       , in east
       <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
        London
       </a>
       . His father, Edmond Halley Sr., came from a
       <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">
        Derbyshire
       </a>
       family and was a wealthy soap-maker in London. As a child, Halley was very interested in mathematics. He studied at
       <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_School,_London" title="St Paul's School, London">
        St Paul's School
       </a>
       , and from 1673 at
       <a href="/wiki/The_Queen%27s_College,_Oxford" title="The Queen's College, Oxford">
        The Queen's College, Oxford
       </a>
       . While still an undergraduate, Halley published papers on the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">
        sunspots
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WA-OD_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-WA-OD-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
        Career
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edmond_Halley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Publications_and_inventions">
        Publications and inventions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edmond_Halley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Publications and inventions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       While at Oxford University, Halley was introduced to
       <a href="/wiki/John_Flamsteed" title="John Flamsteed">
        John Flamsteed
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">
        Astronomer Royal
       </a>
       . Influenced by Flamsteed's project to compile a catalog of northern stars, Halley proposed to do the same for the Southern Hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1676, Halley visited the south Atlantic island of
       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena" title="Saint Helena">
        Saint Helena
       </a>
       and set up an observatory with a large sextant with telescopic sights to catalogue the stars of the southern hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While there he observed a
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Mercury" title="Transit of Mercury">
        transit of Mercury
       </a>
       , and realised that a similar
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">
        transit of Venus
       </a>
       could be used to determine the absolute size of the Solar System.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He returned to England in May 1678. In the following year he went to Danzig (
       <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk" title="Gdańsk">
        Gdańsk
       </a>
       ) on behalf of the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">
        Royal Society
       </a>
       to help resolve a dispute. Because astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Hevelius" title="Johannes Hevelius">
        Johannes Hevelius
       </a>
       did not use a
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       , his observations had been questioned by
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hooke" title="Robert Hooke">
        Robert Hooke
       </a>
       . Halley stayed with Hevelius and he observed and verified the quality of Hevelius' observations. In 1679, Halley published the results from his observations on St. Helena as
       <i>
        Catalogus Stellarum Australium
       </i>
       which included details of 341 southern stars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carter_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carter-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kanas_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kanas-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These additions to contemporary
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_map" title="Star map">
        star maps
       </a>
       earned him comparison with
       <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">
        Tycho Brahe
       </a>
       : e.g. "the southern Tycho" as described by Flamsteed. Halley was awarded his M.A. degree at Oxford and elected as a Fellow of the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">
        Royal Society
       </a>
       at the age of 22. In September 1682 he carried out a series of observations of what became known as Halley's Comet, though his name became associated with it because of his work on its orbit and predicting its return in 1758 (which he did not live to see)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Edmond_Halley_Royal_Greenwich_Observatory_Museum.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="639" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Edmond_Halley_Royal_Greenwich_Observatory_Museum.jpg/170px-Edmond_Halley_Royal_Greenwich_Observatory_Museum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Edmond_Halley_Royal_Greenwich_Observatory_Museum.jpg/255px-Edmond_Halley_Royal_Greenwich_Observatory_Museum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Edmond_Halley_Royal_Greenwich_Observatory_Museum.jpg/340px-Edmond_Halley_Royal_Greenwich_Observatory_Museum.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Edmond_Halley_Royal_Greenwich_Observatory_Museum.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Bust of Halley (
         <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich" title="Royal Observatory, Greenwich">
          Royal Observatory, Greenwich
         </a>
         )
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1686, Halley published the second part of the results from his
       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena" title="Saint Helena">
        Helenian
       </a>
       expedition, being a paper and chart on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">
        trade winds
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">
        monsoons
       </a>
       . The symbols he used to represent trailing winds still exist in most modern day weather chart representations. In this article he identified solar heating as the cause of
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">
        atmospheric
       </a>
       motions. He also established the relationship between
       <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">
        barometric pressure
       </a>
       and height above sea level. His charts were an important contribution to the emerging field of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_visualisation" title="Information visualisation">
        information visualisation
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Halley spent most of his time on lunar observations, but was also interested in the problems of
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       . One problem that attracted his attention was the proof of
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">
        Kepler's laws of planetary motion
       </a>
       . In August 1684, he went to
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">
        Cambridge
       </a>
       to discuss this with
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Isaac Newton
       </a>
       , much as
       <a href="/wiki/John_Flamsteed" title="John Flamsteed">
        John Flamsteed
       </a>
       had done four years earlier, only to find that Newton had solved the problem, at the instigation of Flamsteed with regard to the orbit of comet
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kirch%27s_Comet" title="Kirch's Comet">
        Kirch
       </a>
       , without publishing the solution. Halley asked to see the calculations and was told by Newton that he could not find them, but promised to redo them and send them on later, which he eventually did, in a short treatise entitled,
       <i>
        On the motion of bodies in an orbit.
       </i>
       Halley recognised the importance of the work and returned to Cambridge to arrange its publication with Newton, who instead went on to expand it into his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica">
         Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica
        </a>
       </i>
       published at Halley's expense in 1687.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Halley's first calculations with comets were thereby for the orbit of comet Kirch, based on Flamsteed's observations in 1680-1. Although he was to accurately calculate the orbit of the comet of 1682, he was inaccurate in his calculations of the orbit of comet Kirch. They indicated a periodicity of 575 years, thus appearing in the years 531 and 1106, and presumably heralding the death of
       <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
        Julius Caesar
       </a>
       in a like fashion in −44 (45 BCE). It is now known to have an orbital period of circa 10,000 years.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1691, Halley built a
       <a href="/wiki/Diving_bell" title="Diving bell">
        diving bell
       </a>
       , a device in which the atmosphere was replenished by way of weighted barrels of air sent down from the surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spums_hx_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-spums_hx-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In a demonstration, Halley and five companions dived to 60 feet (18 m) in the
       <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">
        River Thames
       </a>
       , and remained there for over an hour and a half. Halley's bell was of little use for practical salvage work, as it was very heavy, but he made improvements to it over time, later extending his underwater exposure time to over 4 hours.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Halley suffered one of the earliest recorded cases of middle ear
       <a href="/wiki/Barotrauma" title="Barotrauma">
        barotrauma
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spums_hx_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-spums_hx-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That same year, at a meeting of the Royal Society, Halley introduced a rudimentary working model of a magnetic
       <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">
        compass
       </a>
       using a liquid-filled housing to damp the swing and wobble of the magnetised needle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1691, Halley sought the post of
       <a href="/wiki/Savilian_Professor_of_Astronomy" title="Savilian Professor of Astronomy">
        Savilian Professor of Astronomy
       </a>
       at Oxford. While a candidate for the position, Halley faced the animosity of the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">
        Astronomer Royal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/John_Flamsteed" title="John Flamsteed">
        John Flamsteed
       </a>
       , and his religious views were questioned.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hughes-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His candidacy was opposed by both the
       <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">
        Archbishop of Canterbury
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/John_Tillotson" title="John Tillotson">
        John Tillotson
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Stillingfleet" title="Edward Stillingfleet">
        Bishop Stillingfleet
       </a>
       , and the post went instead to
       <a href="/wiki/David_Gregory_(mathematician)" title="David Gregory (mathematician)">
        David Gregory
       </a>
       , who had the support of Isaac Newton.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1692, Halley put forth the idea of a
       <a href="/wiki/Hollow_Earth" title="Hollow Earth">
        hollow Earth
       </a>
       consisting of a shell about 500 miles (800 km) thick, two inner concentric shells and an innermost core.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He suggested that atmospheres separated these shells, and that each shell had its own magnetic poles, with each sphere rotating at a different speed. Halley proposed this scheme to explain anomalous compass readings. He envisaged each inner region as having an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" title="Celestial body atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       and being
       <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">
        luminous
       </a>
       (and possibly inhabited), and speculated that escaping gas caused the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_Borealis" title="Aurora Borealis">
        Aurora Borealis
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1693 Halley published an article on
       <a href="/wiki/Life_annuity" title="Life annuity">
        life annuities
       </a>
       , which featured an analysis of age-at-death on the basis of the
       <a href="/wiki/Wroc%C5%82aw" title="Wrocław">
        Breslau
       </a>
       statistics
       <a href="/wiki/Caspar_Neumann" title="Caspar Neumann">
        Caspar Neumann
       </a>
       had been able to provide. This article allowed the British government to sell life annuities at an appropriate price based on the age of the purchaser. Halley's work strongly influenced the development of
       <a href="/wiki/Actuarial_science" title="Actuarial science">
        actuarial science
       </a>
       . The construction of the life-table for Breslau, which followed more primitive work by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Graunt" title="John Graunt">
        John Graunt
       </a>
       , is now seen as a major event in the history of
       <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">
        demography
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">
        Royal Society
       </a>
       censured Halley for suggesting in 1694 that the story of Noah's flood might be an account of a cometary impact.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, he was to be vindicated less than three centuries later after the publication of "Und die Sintflut gab es doch" by professors Alexander &amp; Edith Tollmann of the University of Vienna. Their book presents evidence from the
       <a href="/wiki/Geologic_record" title="Geologic record">
        geologic record
       </a>
       that a comet had split up and then struck the earth in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoemaker-Levy" title="Shoemaker-Levy">
        Shoemaker-Levy
       </a>
       fashion about 10 millennia ago.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration_years">
        Exploration years
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edmond_Halley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exploration years">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1698, Halley was given command of the
       <a href="/wiki/HMS_Paramour_(1694)" title="HMS Paramour (1694)">
        <i>
         Paramour
        </i>
       </a>
       , a 52 feet (16 m)
       <a href="/wiki/Pink_(ship)" title="Pink (ship)">
        pink
       </a>
       , so that he could carry out investigations in the South Atlantic into the laws governing the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">
        variation of the compass
       </a>
       . On 19 August 1698, he took command of the ship and, in November 1698, sailed on what was the first purely scientific voyage by an English naval vessel. Unfortunately problems of
       <a href="/wiki/Insubordination" title="Insubordination">
        insubordination
       </a>
       arose over questions of Halley's competence to command a vessel. Halley returned the ship to England to proceed against officers in July 1699. The result was a mild rebuke for his men, and dissatisfaction for Halley, who felt the court had been too lenient.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Halley thereafter received a temporary commission as a Captain in the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">
        Royal Navy
       </a>
       , recommissioned the
       <i>
        Paramour
       </i>
       on 24 August 1699 and sailed again in September 1699 to make extensive observations on the conditions of terrestrial
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">
        magnetism
       </a>
       . This task he accomplished in a second Atlantic voyage which lasted until 6 September 1700, and extended from 52 degrees north to 52 degrees south. The results were published in
       <i>
        General Chart of the Variation of the Compass
       </i>
       (1701). This was the first such chart to be published and the first on which
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isogon_(geomagnetism)" title="Isogon (geomagnetism)">
        isogonic
       </a>
       , or Halleyan, lines appeared.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tombstone_of_Edmond_Halley.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3888" data-file-width="2215" height="386" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Tombstone_of_Edmond_Halley.jpg/220px-Tombstone_of_Edmond_Halley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Tombstone_of_Edmond_Halley.jpg/330px-Tombstone_of_Edmond_Halley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Tombstone_of_Edmond_Halley.jpg/440px-Tombstone_of_Edmond_Halley.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tombstone_of_Edmond_Halley.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Edmond Halley's tombstone, re-positioned at the
         <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich" title="Royal Observatory, Greenwich">
          Royal Observatory, Greenwich
         </a>
         ; he is not buried there, but at
         <a href="/wiki/St_Margaret%27s,_Lee" title="St Margaret's, Lee">
          St Margaret's, Lee
         </a>
         , some 30 minutes' walk away
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In November 1703, Halley was appointed
       <a href="/wiki/Savilian_Professor_of_Geometry" title="Savilian Professor of Geometry">
        Savilian Professor of Geometry
       </a>
       at the University of Oxford, his theological enemies,
       <a href="/wiki/John_Tillotson" title="John Tillotson">
        John Tillotson
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Stillingfleet" title="Edward Stillingfleet">
        Bishop Stillingfleet
       </a>
       having died, and received an honorary degree of doctor of laws in 1710. In 1705, applying
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_astronomy" title="Historical astronomy">
        historical astronomy
       </a>
       methods, he published
       <i>
        Synopsis Astronomia Cometicae
       </i>
       , which stated his belief that the comet sightings of 1456, 1531, 1607, and 1682 were of the same comet, which he predicted would return in 1758. Halley did not live to witness the comet's return, but when it did, the comet became generally known as
       <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley's Comet">
        Halley's Comet
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       By 1706 Halley had learned Arabic and completed the translation started by
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Bernard" title="Edward Bernard">
        Edward Bernard
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of Books V-VII of
       <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga" title="Apollonius of Perga">
        Apollonius
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Conics
       </i>
       from copies found at
       <a href="/wiki/Leiden_University_Library" title="Leiden University Library">
        Leiden
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Bodleian_Library" title="Bodleian Library">
        Bodleian Library
       </a>
       at Oxford. He also completed a new translation of the first four books from the original Greek that had been started by the late
       <a href="/wiki/David_Gregory_(mathematician)" title="David Gregory (mathematician)">
        David Gregory
       </a>
       . He published these along with his own reconstruction of Book VIII
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in the first complete Latin edition in 1710.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1716, Halley suggested a high-precision measurement of the distance between the Earth and the Sun by timing the
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">
        transit of Venus
       </a>
       . In doing so, he was following the method described by
       <a href="/wiki/James_Gregory_(mathematician)" title="James Gregory (mathematician)">
        James Gregory
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        Optica Promota
       </i>
       (in which the design of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_telescope" title="Gregorian telescope">
        Gregorian telescope
       </a>
       is also described). It is reasonable to assume Halley possessed and had read this book given that the Gregorian design was the principal telescope design used in astronomy in Halley's day.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is not to Halley's credit that he failed to acknowledge Gregory's priority in this matter. In 1718 he discovered the
       <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">
        proper motion
       </a>
       of the "fixed" stars by comparing his
       <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">
        astrometric
       </a>
       measurements with those given in Ptolemy's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">
         Almagest
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Arcturus" title="Arcturus">
        Arcturus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sirius" title="Sirius">
        Sirius
       </a>
       were two noted to have moved significantly, the latter having progressed 30 arc minutes (about the diameter of the moon) southwards in 1800 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1720, together with his friend the
       <a href="/wiki/Antiquarian" title="Antiquarian">
        antiquarian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/William_Stukeley" title="William Stukeley">
        William Stukeley
       </a>
       , Halley participated in the first attempt to scientifically date
       <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">
        Stonehenge
       </a>
       . Assuming that the monument had been laid out using a magnetic compass, Stukeley and Halley attempted to calculate the perceived deviation introducing corrections from existing magnetic records, and suggested three dates (460 BC, 220 AD and 920 AD), the earliest being the one accepted. These dates were wrong by thousands of years, but the idea that scientific methods could be used to date ancient monuments was revolutionary in its day.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Halley succeeded
       <a href="/wiki/John_Flamsteed" title="John Flamsteed">
        John Flamsteed
       </a>
       in 1720 as
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">
        Astronomer Royal
       </a>
       , a position Halley held until his death.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Halley_Edmund_grave.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="576" data-file-width="842" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Halley_Edmund_grave.jpg/220px-Halley_Edmund_grave.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Halley_Edmund_grave.jpg/330px-Halley_Edmund_grave.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Halley_Edmund_grave.jpg/440px-Halley_Edmund_grave.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Halley_Edmund_grave.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Halley's grave
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Halley died in 1742 at the age of 85. He was buried in the graveyard of the old church of
       <a href="/wiki/St_Margaret%27s,_Lee" title="St Margaret's, Lee">
        St Margaret's, Lee
       </a>
       (since rebuilt), at
       <a href="/wiki/Lee,_London" title="Lee, London">
        Lee
       </a>
       Terrace,
       <a href="/wiki/Blackheath,_London" title="Blackheath, London">
        Blackheath
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was interred in the same vault as the Astronomer Royal
       <a href="/wiki/John_Pond" title="John Pond">
        John Pond
       </a>
       ; the unmarked grave of the Astronomer Royal
       <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Bliss" title="Nathaniel Bliss">
        Nathaniel Bliss
       </a>
       is nearby.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       His original
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tombstone" title="Tombstone">
        tombstone
       </a>
       was transferred by the
       <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">
        Admiralty
       </a>
       when the original Lee church was demolished and rebuilt – it can be seen today on the southern wall of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camera_Obscura" title="Camera Obscura">
        Camera Obscura
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich" title="Royal Observatory, Greenwich">
        Royal Observatory, Greenwich
       </a>
       . His marked grave can be seen at St Margaret's Church, Lee Terrace.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation_and_spelling">
        Pronunciation and spelling
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edmond_Halley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pronunciation and spelling">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are three pronunciations of the surname
       <i>
        Halley
       </i>
       . The most common, both in Great Britain
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-jones-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and in the United States,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenyon_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-kenyon-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'h' in 'hi'">
            h
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       . This is the personal pronunciation used by most Halleys living in London today.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The alternative
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'h' in 'hi'">
            h
           </span>
           <span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">
            eɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       is often preferred for the man and the comet by those who grew up with
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">
        rock and roll
       </a>
       singer
       <a href="/wiki/Bill_Haley" title="Bill Haley">
        Bill Haley
       </a>
       , who called his
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backing_band" title="Backing band">
        backing band
       </a>
       his "Comets" after the common pronunciation of Halley's Comet in the United States at the time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Colin_Ronan" title="Colin Ronan">
        Colin Ronan
       </a>
       , one of Halley's biographers, preferred
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'h' in 'hi'">
            h
           </span>
           <span title="/ɔː/: 'au' in 'fraud'">
            ɔː
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       . Contemporary accounts spell his name
       <i>
        Hailey, Hayley, Haley, Haly, Halley, Hawley
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Hawly
       </i>
       , and presumably pronunciations varied similarly.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As for his given name, although the spelling "Edmund" is quite common, "Edmond" is what Halley himself used, according to a 1902 article,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edmond_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-edmond-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       though a 2007
       <i>
        International Comet Quarterly
       </i>
       article disputes this, commenting that in his published works, he used "Edmund" 22 times and "Edmond" only 3 times,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with several other variations used as well, such as the Latinised "Edmundus". Much of the debate stems from the fact that, in Halley's own time, English spelling conventions were not yet standardised, and so he himself used multiple spellings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICQ_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ICQ-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
