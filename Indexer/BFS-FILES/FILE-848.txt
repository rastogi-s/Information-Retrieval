      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         The Sunday Times
        </b>
       </i>
       is the largest-selling British national newspaper in the "
       <a href="/wiki/Quality_press" title="Quality press">
        quality press
       </a>
       " market category. It is published by Times Newspapers Ltd, a subsidiary of
       <a href="/wiki/News_UK" title="News UK">
        News UK
       </a>
       , which is in turn owned by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/News_Corp_(company)" title="News Corp (company)">
        News Corp
       </a>
       . Times Newspapers also publishes
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       . The two papers were founded independently and have been under
       <a href="/wiki/Common_ownership" title="Common ownership">
        common ownership
       </a>
       only since 1966. They were bought by News International in 1981.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       occupies a dominant position in the quality Sunday market; its circulation of just under one million equals that of its main rivals,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Telegraph" title="The Sunday Telegraph">
         The Sunday Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">
         The Observer
        </a>
       </i>
       , combined.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While some other national newspapers moved to a tabloid format in the early 2000s,
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       has retained the larger
       <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet" title="Broadsheet">
        broadsheet
       </a>
       format and has said that it will continue to do so. It sells more than twice as many copies as its sister paper,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       , which is published Monday to Saturday.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       has acquired a reputation for the strength of its investigative reporting – much of it by its award-winning
       <a href="/wiki/Insight_(Sunday_Times)" title="Insight (Sunday Times)">
        Insight
       </a>
       team – and also for its wide-ranging foreign coverage. It has a number of popular writers, columnists and commentators including
       <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Clarkson" title="Jeremy Clarkson">
        Jeremy Clarkson
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Appleyard" title="Bryan Appleyard">
        Bryan Appleyard
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/A._A._Gill" title="A. A. Gill">
        A. A. Gill
       </a>
       was a prominent columnist for many years. It was Britain's first multi-section newspaper and remains substantially larger than its rivals. A typical edition contains the equivalent of 450 to 500 tabloid pages. Besides the main news section, it has standalone News Review, Business, Sport, Money and Appointments sections – all broadsheet. There are three magazines (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times_Magazine" title="The Sunday Times Magazine">
         The Sunday Times Magazine
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Culture
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        Style
       </i>
       ) and two tabloid supplements (Travel and Home). It has a website and separate digital editions configured for both the
       <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">
        iOS
       </a>
       operating system for the
       <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">
        Apple
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">
        iPad
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">
        Android
       </a>
       operating system for such devices as the
       <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">
        Google
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Google_Nexus" title="Google Nexus">
        Nexus
       </a>
       , all of which offer video clips, extra features and multimedia and other material not found in the printed version of the newspaper.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The paper publishes
       <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Times_Rich_List" title="Sunday Times Rich List">
        <i>
         The Sunday Times
        </i>
        Rich List
       </a>
       , an annual survey of the wealthiest people in Britain and Ireland, equivalent to the
       <a href="/wiki/Forbes_400" title="Forbes 400">
        Forbes 400
       </a>
       list in the United States, and a series of league tables with reviews of private British companies, in particular
       <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Times_Fast_Track_100" title="Sunday Times Fast Track 100">
        <i>
         The Sunday Times
        </i>
        Fast Track 100
       </a>
       . The paper also produces an annual league table of the best-performing state and independent schools at both junior and senior level across the United Kingdom, entitled
       <i>
        Parent Power
       </i>
       (with additional information available online), and an annual league table of
       <a href="/wiki/Universities_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Universities in the United Kingdom">
        British universities
       </a>
       and a similar one for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_higher_education_institutions_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="List of higher education institutions in the Republic of Ireland">
        Irish universities
       </a>
       . It publishes
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       Bestseller List of books in Britain, and a list of the "100 Best Companies to Work For", focusing on UK companies. It also organises
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       Oxford Literary Festival, held annually, and
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       Festival of Education, which takes place every year at
       <a href="/wiki/Wellington_College,_Berkshire" title="Wellington College, Berkshire">
        Wellington College
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sunday_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunday_Times_plaque_London_1822.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="994" data-file-width="1109" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Sunday_Times_plaque_London_1822.jpg/220px-Sunday_Times_plaque_London_1822.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Sunday_Times_plaque_London_1822.jpg/330px-Sunday_Times_plaque_London_1822.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Sunday_Times_plaque_London_1822.jpg/440px-Sunday_Times_plaque_London_1822.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sunday_Times_plaque_London_1822.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Plaque to the first edition of
         <i>
          The Sunday Times
         </i>
         at No. 4 Salisbury Court,
         <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
          London
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span id="Founding_and_early_history_.281821.E2.80.931915.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_and_early_history_(1821–1915)">
        Founding and early history (1821–1915)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sunday_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding and early history (1821–1915)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The paper began publication on 18 February 1821 as
       <i>
        The New Observer
       </i>
       , but from 21 April its title was changed to the
       <i>
        Independent Observer
       </i>
       . Its founder, Henry White, chose the name in an apparent attempt to take advantage of the success of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">
         The Observer
        </a>
       </i>
       , which had been founded in 1791, although there was no connection between the two papers. On 20 October 1822 it was reborn as
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       , although it had no relationship with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In January 1823, White sold the paper to
       <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Whittle_Harvey" title="Daniel Whittle Harvey">
        Daniel Whittle Harvey
       </a>
       , a radical politician.
      </p>
      <p>
       Under its new owner,
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       notched up several firsts: a wood engraving it published of the coronation of
       <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">
        Queen Victoria
       </a>
       in 1838 was the largest illustration to have appeared in a British newspaper;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in 1841, it became one of the first papers to serialise a novel:
       <a href="/wiki/William_Harrison_Ainsworth" title="William Harrison Ainsworth">
        William Harrison Ainsworth
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Old St Paul's
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The paper was bought in 1887 by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alice_Anne_Cornwell" title="Alice Anne Cornwell">
        Alice Anne Cornwell
       </a>
       who had made a fortune in mining in Australia and floating the Midas Mine Company of the London Stock Exchange. She bought the paper to promote her new company, The British and Australasian Mining Investment Company, and as a gift to her lover Frederick Stannard (‘Phil’) Robinson. Robinson was installed as editor and she married him in 1894.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       She then sold it in 1893 to Frederick Beer, who already owned
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">
         Observer
        </a>
       </i>
       . Beer appointed his wife,
       <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Beer" title="Rachel Beer">
        Rachel Sassoon Beer
       </a>
       , as editor. She was already editor of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">
         Observer
        </a>
       </i>
       – the first woman to run a national newspaper – and continued to edit both titles until 1901.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="The_Kemsley_years_.281915.E2.80.9359.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Kemsley_years_(1915–59)">
        The Kemsley years (1915–59)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sunday_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Kemsley years (1915–59)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There was a further change of ownership in 1903, and then in 1915 the paper was bought by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Berry,_1st_Viscount_Camrose" title="William Berry, 1st Viscount Camrose">
        William Berry
       </a>
       and his brother, Gomer Berry, later ennobled as Lord Camrose and
       <a href="/wiki/Viscount_Kemsley" title="Viscount Kemsley">
        Viscount Kemsley
       </a>
       respectively. Under their ownership,
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       continued its reputation for innovation: on 23 November 1930, it became the first Sunday newspaper to publish a 40-page issue and on 21 January 1940, news replaced advertising on the front page.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1943, the Kemsley Newspapers Group was established, with
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       becoming its flagship paper. At this time, Kemsley was the largest newspaper group in Britain.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 12 November 1945,
       <a href="/wiki/Ian_Fleming" title="Ian Fleming">
        Ian Fleming
       </a>
       , who later created
       <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">
        James Bond
       </a>
       , joined the paper as foreign manager (foreign editor) and special writer. The following month, circulation reached 500,000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 28 September 1958 the paper launched a separate Review section, becoming the first newspaper to publish two sections regularly.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="The_Thomson_years_.281959.E2.80.9381.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Thomson_years_(1959–81)">
        The Thomson years (1959–81)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sunday_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Thomson years (1959–81)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1959 the Kemsley group was bought by
       <a href="/wiki/Roy_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Thomson_of_Fleet" title="Roy Thomson, 1st Baron Thomson of Fleet">
        Lord Thomson
       </a>
       , and in October 1960 circulation reached one million for the first time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In another first, on 4 February 1962 the editor, Denis Hamilton, launched
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times_Magazine" title="The Sunday Times Magazine">
         The Sunday Times Magazine
        </a>
       </i>
       . (At the insistence of newsagents, worried at the impact on sales of standalone magazines, it was initially called the "colour section" and did not take the name
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times_Magazine" title="The Sunday Times Magazine">
         The Sunday Times Magazine
        </a>
       </i>
       until 9 August 1964.) The cover picture of the first issue was of
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Shrimpton" title="Jean Shrimpton">
        Jean Shrimpton
       </a>
       wearing a
       <a href="/wiki/Mary_Quant" title="Mary Quant">
        Mary Quant
       </a>
       outfit and was taken by
       <a href="/wiki/David_Bailey" title="David Bailey">
        David Bailey
       </a>
       . The magazine got off to a slow start, but the advertising soon began to pick up, and, over time, other newspapers launched magazines of their own.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1963, the
       <a href="/wiki/Insight_(Sunday_Times)" title="Insight (Sunday Times)">
        Insight
       </a>
       investigative team was established under Clive Irving. On 27 September 1964, the
       <i>
        Business
       </i>
       section was launched, making
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       Britain's first regular three-section newspaper. In September 1966, Thomson bought
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       , to form
       <a href="/wiki/News_UK" title="News UK">
        Times Newspapers Ltd
       </a>
       (TNL). It was the first time both
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       had been brought under the same ownership.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Evans" title="Harold Evans">
        Harold Evans
       </a>
       , editor from 1967 until 1981, established
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       as a leading campaigning and investigative newspaper. On 19 May 1968, the paper published its first major campaigning report on the drug
       <a href="/wiki/Thalidomide" title="Thalidomide">
        Thalidomide
       </a>
       , which had been reported by the Australian doctor
       <a href="/wiki/William_McBride_(doctor)" title="William McBride (doctor)">
        William McBride
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/The_Lancet" title="The Lancet">
        The Lancet
       </a>
       in 1961 as associated with birth defects, and quickly withdrawn. The newspaper published a four-page
       <a href="/wiki/Insight" title="Insight">
        Insight
       </a>
       investigation, entitled
       <i>
        The Thalidomide File
       </i>
       , in the
       <i>
        Weekly Review
       </i>
       section. A compensation settlement for the UK victims was eventually reached with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distillers_Company" title="Distillers Company">
        Distillers Company
       </a>
       (now part of
       <a href="/wiki/Diageo" title="Diageo">
        Diageo
       </a>
       ), which had distributed the drug in the UK.
      </p>
      <p>
       TNL was plagued by a series of industrial disputes at its plant at Gray's Inn Road in London, with the print unions resisting attempts to replace the old-fashioned hot-metal and labour-intensive
       <a href="/wiki/Linotype_machine" title="Linotype machine">
        Linotype
       </a>
       method with technology that would allow the papers to be composed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronically" title="Electronically">
        electronically
       </a>
       . Thomson offered to invest millions of pounds to buy out obstructive practices and overmanning, but the unions rejected every proposal. As a result, publication of
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       and other titles in the group was suspended in November 1978. It did not resume until November 1979.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although journalists at
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       had been on full pay during the suspension, they went on strike demanding more money after production was resumed.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenneth_Thomson" title="Kenneth Thomson">
        Kenneth Thomson
       </a>
       , the head of the company, felt betrayed and decided to sell. Evans tried to organise a management buyout of
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       , but Thomson decided instead to sell to
       <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Murdoch" title="Rupert Murdoch">
        Rupert Murdoch
       </a>
       , who he thought had a better chance of dealing with the trade unions.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="The_Murdoch_years_.281981.E2.80.93present.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Murdoch_years_(1981–present)">
        The Murdoch years (1981–present)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sunday_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Murdoch years (1981–present)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Murdoch" title="Rupert Murdoch">
        Murdoch
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/News_UK" title="News UK">
        News International
       </a>
       acquired the group in February 1981. Murdoch, an Australian who in 1985 became a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalised" title="Naturalised">
        naturalised
       </a>
       American citizen, already owned
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(United_Kingdom)" title="The Sun (United Kingdom)">
         The Sun
        </a>
       </i>
       and the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/News_of_the_World" title="News of the World">
         News of the World
        </a>
       </i>
       , but the Conservative government decided not to refer the deal to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monopolies_and_Mergers_Commission" title="Monopolies and Mergers Commission">
        Monopolies and Mergers Commission
       </a>
       , citing a clause in the
       <a href="/wiki/Fair_Trading_Act_1973" title="Fair Trading Act 1973">
        Fair Trading Act
       </a>
       that exempted uneconomic businesses from referral.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Thomson_Corporation" title="The Thomson Corporation">
        The Thomson Corporation
       </a>
       had threatened to close the papers down if they were not taken over by someone else within an allotted time, and it was feared that any legal delay to Murdoch's takeover might lead to the two titles' demise. In return, Murdoch provided legally binding guarantees to preserve the titles' editorial independence.
      </p>
      <p>
       Evans was appointed editor of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       in February 1981 and was replaced at
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Giles" title="Frank Giles">
        Frank Giles
       </a>
       . In 1983, the newspaper bought the serialisation rights to publish the faked
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Hitler_Diaries" title="Hitler Diaries">
         Hitler Diaries
        </a>
       </i>
       , thinking them to be genuine after they were authenticated by the own newspaper's own independent director,
       <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Trevor-Roper" title="Hugh Trevor-Roper">
        Hugh Trevor-Roper
       </a>
       , the historian and author of
       <i>
        The Last Days of Hitler
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Under
       <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Neil" title="Andrew Neil">
        Andrew Neil
       </a>
       , editor from 1983 until 1994,
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       took a strongly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thatcherite" title="Thatcherite">
        Thatcherite
       </a>
       slant that contrasted with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional" title="Traditional">
        traditional
       </a>
       paternalistic conservatism expounded by
       <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Worsthorne" title="Peregrine Worsthorne">
        Peregrine Worsthorne
       </a>
       at the rival
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunday_Telegraph" title="Sunday Telegraph">
         Sunday Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       . It also built on its reputation for investigations. Its scoops included the revelation in 1986 that
       <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
        Israel
       </a>
       had manufactured more than 100
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">
        nuclear
       </a>
       warheads
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the publication in 1992 of extracts from
       <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Morton_(writer)" title="Andrew Morton (writer)">
        Andrew Morton
       </a>
       's book,
       <i>
        Diana: Her True Story in Her Own Words
       </i>
       . In the early 1990s, the paper courted controversy with a series of articles in which it rejected the role of HIV in causing AIDS.
      </p>
      <p>
       In January 1986, after the announcement of a strike by print workers, production of
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       , along with other newspapers in the group, was shifted to a new plant in Wapping, and the strikers were dismissed. The plant, which allowed journalists to input copy directly, was activated with the help of the
       <a href="/wiki/Electrical,_Electronic,_Telecommunications_and_Plumbing_Union" title="Electrical, Electronic, Telecommunications and Plumbing Union">
        Electrical, Electronic, Telecommunications and Plumbing Union
       </a>
       (EETPU). The print unions posted pickets and organised demonstrations outside the new plant to try to dissuade journalists and others from working there, in what became known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Wapping_dispute" title="Wapping dispute">
        Wapping Dispute
       </a>
       . The demonstrations sometimes turned violent. The protest ended in failure in February 1987.
      </p>
      <p>
       During Neil's editorship, a number of new sections were added: the annual
       <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Times_Rich_List" title="Sunday Times Rich List">
        <i>
         The Sunday Times
        </i>
        Rich List
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Funday_Times" title="Funday Times">
        Funday Times
       </a>
       , in 1989, (the latter stopped appearing in print and was relaunched as a standalone website in March 2006 but was later closed); Style &amp; Travel, News Review and Arts in 1990, and Culture in 1992. In September 1994, Style and Travel became two separate sections.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/John_Witherow" title="John Witherow">
        John Witherow
       </a>
       , who became editor at the end of 1994 (after several months as acting editor), continued the newspaper's expansion. A website was launched in 1996 and new print sections added: Home in 2001, and Driving in 2002, which in 2006 was renamed InGear. (It reverted to the name Driving from 7 October 2012, to coincide with the launch of a new standalone website, Sunday Times Driving.) Technology coverage was expanded in 2000 with the weekly colour magazine Doors, and in 2003 The Month, an editorial section presented as an interactive CD-Rom. Magazine partworks were regular additions, among them 1000 Makers of Music, published over six weeks in 1997.
      </p>
      <p>
       John Witherow oversaw a rise in circulation to 1.3 million
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and reconfirmed
       <i>
        The Sunday Times's
       </i>
       reputation for publishing hard-hitting news stories – such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cash_for_Questions" title="Cash for Questions">
        Cash for Questions
       </a>
       in 1994 and
       <a href="/wiki/Cash_for_Honours" title="Cash for Honours">
        Cash for Honours
       </a>
       in 2006 and revelations of corruption at Fifa in 2010.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Sunday_Times_Insight_team_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_Sunday_Times_Insight_team-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The newspaper's foreign coverage has been especially strong, and its reporters,
       <a href="/wiki/Marie_Colvin" title="Marie Colvin">
        Marie Colvin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jon_Swain" title="Jon Swain">
        Jon Swain
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hala_Jaber" title="Hala Jaber">
        Hala Jaber
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_Franchetti" title="Mark Franchetti">
        Mark Franchetti
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Christina_Lamb" title="Christina Lamb">
        Christina Lamb
       </a>
       have dominated the Foreign Reporter of the Year category at the British Press Awards since 2000.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Marie_Colvin" title="Marie Colvin">
        Marie Colvin
       </a>
       , who worked for the paper from 1985, was killed in February 2012 by Syrian forces while covering the
       <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Homs" title="Siege of Homs">
        siege of Homs
       </a>
       during that country's civil war.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In common with other newspapers,
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       has been hit by a fall in circulation, which has declined from a peak of 1.3 million to just over 780,000. It has a number of digital-only subscribers, which numbered 59,000 by March 2014.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       During January 2013,
       <a href="/wiki/Martin_Ivens" title="Martin Ivens">
        Martin Ivens
       </a>
       became acting editor of
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       in succession to John Witherow, who became the 'acting' editor of
       <i>
        The Times
       </i>
       at the same time. The independent directors rejected a permanent position for Ivens as editors to avoid any possible merger of the Sunday Times and daily Times titles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rushton_1-1">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Online_presence">
        Online presence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sunday_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Online presence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       has its own website. It previously shared an online presence with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       , but in May 2010 they both launched their own sites to reflect their distinct brand identities. Since July 2010, the sites have charged for access.
      </p>
      <p>
       An iPad edition was launched in December 2010, and an Android version in August 2011. Since July 2012, the digital version of the paper has been available on Apple's Newsstand platform, allowing automated downloading of the news section. With over 500MB of content every week, it is the biggest newspaper app in the world.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="Is there evidence to support this claim? (March 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       iPad app was named newspaper app of the year at the 2011 Newspaper Awards and has twice been ranked best newspaper or magazine app in the world by iMonitor. Various subscription packages exist, giving access to both the print and digital versions of the paper.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 2 October 2012,
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       launched Sunday Times Driving, a separate classified advertising site for premium vehicles that also includes editorial content from the newspaper as well as specially commissioned articles. It can be accessed without cost.
      </p>

         <a href="#cite_note-38">
          [38]
         </a>
        </sup>
        After Rupert Murdoch tweeted that he considered it a "grotesque, offensive cartoon" and that Scarfe had "never reflected the opinions of
        <i>
         The Sunday Times
        </i>
        "
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
         <a href="#cite_note-39">
          [39]
         </a>
        </sup>
        the newspaper issued an apology.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
         <a href="#cite_note-40">
          [40]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Journalist Ian Burrell, writing in
        <i>
         The Independent
        </i>
        , described the apology as an "indication of the power of the Israel lobby in challenging critical media coverage of its politicians" and one that questions Rupert Murdoch's assertion that he does not "interfere in the editorial content of his papers".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
         <a href="#cite_note-41">
          [41]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In June 2015,
        <i>
         The Sunday Times
        </i>
        ran a lead front article titled "British spies betrayed to Russians and Chinese". The article was controversial because it contained numerous unlikely and unsubstantiated claims. Shortly after publication parts of the online version of the article were changed quietly by the newspaper. The article appeared to be an attempt to smear the American Whistleblower
        <a href="/wiki/Edward_Snowden" title="Edward Snowden">
         Edward Snowden
        </a>
        , thus fuelling further doubt as to its independent editorship.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
         <a href="#cite_note-42">
          [42]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
         <a href="#cite_note-43">
          [43]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
         <a href="#cite_note-44">
          [44]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phone_hacking_scandal">
        Phone hacking scandal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sunday_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Phone hacking scandal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In July 2011
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       was implicated in the wider
       <a href="/wiki/News_International_phone_hacking_scandal" title="News International phone hacking scandal">
        News International phone hacking scandal
       </a>
       which primarily involved the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/News_of_the_World" title="News of the World">
         News of the World
        </a>
       </i>
       , a Murdoch tabloid newspaper published in the UK from 1843 to 2011. Former British prime minister
       <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">
        Gordon Brown
       </a>
       accused
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       of employing "known criminals" to impersonate him and obtain his private financial records.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-brown_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-brown-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc-brown_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bbc-brown-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Brown's bank reported that an investigator employed by
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       repeatedly impersonated Brown to gain access to his bank account records.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-brown_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-guardian-brown-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       vigorously denied these accusations and said that the story was in the public interest and that it had followed the
       <a href="/wiki/Press_Complaints_Commission" title="Press Complaints Commission">
        Press Complaints Commission
       </a>
       code on using subterfuge.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_edition">
        Irish edition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sunday_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Irish edition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Irish edition of
       <i>
        The Sunday Times
       </i>
       was launched on a small scale on 1993 with just two staff, Alan Ruddock and John Burns (who is at present associate editor). It used the slogan "The English just don't get it".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is now the third biggest-selling newspaper in
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">
        Ireland
       </a>
       measured in terms of full-price cover sales (Source: ABC Jan–June 2012). Circulation has grown steadily over the past two decades and stands at 127,336 on the island of Ireland (106,113 of which in the Republic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Circulation, in the Republic of Ireland, future declined to average weekly sales of 101,851, according to the ABC, in November 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The paper is heavily editionalised, with extensive Irish coverage of politics, general news, business, personal finance, sport, culture and lifestyle. The office employs 25 people. The paper also has a number of well-known freelance columnists including
       <a href="/wiki/Brenda_Power" title="Brenda Power">
        Brenda Power
       </a>
       , Liam Fay,
       <a href="/wiki/Matt_Cooper_(Irish_journalist)" title="Matt Cooper (Irish journalist)">
        Matt Cooper
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Damien_Kiberd" title="Damien Kiberd">
        Damien Kiberd
       </a>
       , Jill Kerby and Stephen Price. The paper ended collaboration with
       <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Myers" title="Kevin Myers">
        Kevin Myers
       </a>
       after it published a controversial column. The Irish edition has had four editors since it was set up: Alan Ruddock, Rory Godson,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Fiona McHugh
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and, since 2005, Frank Fitzgibbon.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_edition">
        Scottish edition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sunday_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Scottish edition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       For more than 20 years the paper has published a separate Scottish edition, which has been edited since January 2012 by Jason Allardyce. While most of the articles that run in the English edition appear in the Scottish edition, its staff also produces about a dozen Scottish news stories, including a front-page article, most weeks. The edition also contains a weekly "Scottish Focus" feature and Scottish commentary, and covers Scottish sport in addition to providing Scottish television schedules. The Scottish issue is the biggest-selling quality newspaper in the market, outselling both
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Scotland_on_Sunday" title="Scotland on Sunday">
         Scotland on Sunday
        </a>
       </i>
       and the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Herald" title="Sunday Herald">
         Sunday Herald
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>

        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
