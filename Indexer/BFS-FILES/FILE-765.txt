      <p>
       <b>
        Shackleton
       </b>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       that lies at the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_south_pole" title="Lunar south pole">
        south pole
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . The peaks along the crater's rim are exposed to almost continual sunlight, while the interior is perpetually in shadow (a
       <a href="/wiki/Crater_of_eternal_darkness" title="Crater of eternal darkness">
        Crater of eternal darkness
       </a>
       ). The low-temperature interior of this crater functions as a cold trap that may capture and freeze
       <a href="/wiki/Volatiles" title="Volatiles">
        volatiles
       </a>
       shed during
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comet
       </a>
       impacts on the Moon. Measurements by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       spacecraft showed higher than normal amounts of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       within the crater, which may indicate the presence of water ice. The crater is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       explorer
       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Shackleton" title="Ernest Shackleton">
        Ernest Shackleton
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shackleton_(crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The rotational axis of the Moon lies within Shackleton, only a few kilometers from its center. The crater is 21 km in diameter and 4.2 km deep.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science322_5903_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-science322_5903-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From the Earth, it is viewed edge-on in a region of rough, cratered terrain. It is located within the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pole-Aitken_basin" title="South Pole-Aitken basin">
        South Pole-Aitken basin
       </a>
       on a
       <a href="/wiki/Massif" title="Massif">
        massif
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grl35_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-grl35-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The rim is slightly raised about the surrounding surface and it has an outer rampart that has been only lightly impacted. No significant craters intersect the rim, and it is sloped about 1.5° toward the direction 50–90° from the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science322_5903_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-science322_5903-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa060113_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-esa060113-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The age of the crater is about 3.6 billion years and it has been in the proximity of the south lunar pole for at least the last two billion years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grl35_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-grl35-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because the orbit of the Moon is tilted only 5° from the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       , the interior of this crater lies in perpetual darkness. Estimates of the area in permanent shadow were obtained from Earth-based radar studies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Margot1999_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Margot1999-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Peaks along the rim of the crater are almost continually illuminated by sunlight, spending about 80–90% of each lunar orbit exposed to the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spudis_etal_95_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-spudis_etal_95-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Continuously illuminated mountains have been termed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peak_of_Eternal_Light" title="Peak of Eternal Light">
        peaks of eternal light
       </a>
       and have been predicted to exist since the 1900s.
      </p>
      <p>
       The shadowed portion of the crater was imaged with the Terrain Camera of the
       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
        Japanese
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
        SELENE
       </a>
       spacecraft using the illumination of sunlight reflected off the rim. The interior of the crater consists of a symmetrical 30° slope that leads down to a 6.6 km diameter floor. The handful of craters along the interior span no more than a few hundred meters. The bottom is covered by an uneven mound-like feature that is 300 to 400 m thick. The central peak is about 200 m in height.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science322_5903_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-science322_5903-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haruyama07_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-haruyama07-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The continuous shadows in the south polar craters cause the floors of these formations to maintain a temperature that never exceeds about 100 K. For Shackleton, the average temperature was determined to be about 90 K, reaching 88 K at the crater floor. Under these conditions, the estimated rate of loss from any ice in the interior would be 10
       <sup>
        −26
       </sup>
       to 10
       <sup>
        −27
       </sup>
       m/s. Any water vapor that arrives here following a
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        cometary
       </a>
       impact on the Moon would lie permanently frozen on or below the surface. However, the surface
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       of the crater floor matches the lunar far-side, suggesting that there is no exposed surface ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science322_5903_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-science322_5903-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icarus100_1_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-icarus100_1-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This crater was named after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernest_Henry_Shackleton" title="Ernest Henry Shackleton">
        Ernest Henry Shackleton
       </a>
       , an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish" title="Anglo-Irish">
        Anglo-Irish
       </a>
       explorer of
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       from 1901 until his death in 1922. The name was officially adopted by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       in 1994.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gpr_usgs_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gpr_usgs-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nearby craters of note include
       <a href="/wiki/Shoemaker_(lunar_crater)" title="Shoemaker (lunar crater)">
        Shoemaker
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Haworth_(crater)" title="Haworth (crater)">
        Haworth
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/De_Gerlache_(crater)" title="De Gerlache (crater)">
        de Gerlache
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup_(crater)" title="Sverdrup (crater)">
        Sverdrup
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slater_(crater)" title="Slater (crater)">
        Slater
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Faustini_(crater)" title="Faustini (crater)">
        Faustini
       </a>
       . Somewhat farther away, on the eastern hemisphere of the lunar near side, are the larger craters
       <a href="/wiki/Amundsen_(crater)" title="Amundsen (crater)">
        Amundsen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Scott_(crater)" title="Scott (crater)">
        Scott
       </a>
       , named after two other early explorers of the
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       continent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bussey_spudis04_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bussey_spudis04-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shackleton_(crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:De_gerlache_shackleton_sverdrup_crater.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="720" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/De_gerlache_shackleton_sverdrup_crater.jpg/220px-De_gerlache_shackleton_sverdrup_crater.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/De_gerlache_shackleton_sverdrup_crater.jpg/330px-De_gerlache_shackleton_sverdrup_crater.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/De_gerlache_shackleton_sverdrup_crater.jpg/440px-De_gerlache_shackleton_sverdrup_crater.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:De_gerlache_shackleton_sverdrup_crater.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Shackleton as imaged by
         <a href="/wiki/Clementine_(spacecraft)" title="Clementine (spacecraft)">
          Clementine
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       From the perspective of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , this crater lies along the southern
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Limb" title="wiktionary:Limb">
        limb
       </a>
       of the Moon, making observation difficult. Detailed mapping of the polar regions and farside of the Moon did not occur until the advent of orbiting spacecraft. Shackleton lies entirely within the rim of the immense
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pole-Aitken_basin" title="South Pole-Aitken basin">
        South Pole-Aitken basin
       </a>
       , which is one of the largest known impact formations in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       . This basin is over 12 kilometers deep, and an exploration of its properties could provide useful information about the lunar interior.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pieters_etal03_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pieters_etal03-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">
        neutron
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
        spectrometer
       </a>
       on board the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       spacecraft detected enhanced concentrations of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       close to the northern and southern lunar poles, including the crater Shackleton.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feldman98_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-feldman98-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the end of this mission in July 1999, the spacecraft was crashed into the nearby crater
       <a href="/wiki/Shoemaker_(lunar_crater)" title="Shoemaker (lunar crater)">
        Shoemaker
       </a>
       in the hope of detecting from Earth-based telescopes an impact-generated plume containing water vapor. The impact event did not produce any detectable water vapor, and this may be an indication that the hydrogen is not in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">
        hydrated
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">
        minerals
       </a>
       , or that the impact site did not contain any ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science_nasa991013_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-science_nasa991013-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Alternatively, it is possible that the crash did not excavate deeply enough into the
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       to liberate significant quantities of water vapor.
      </p>
      <p>
       From Earth-based radar and spacecraft images of the crater edge, Shackleton appears to be relatively intact; much like a young crater that has not been significantly eroded from subsequent impacts. This may mean that the inner sides are relatively steep, which may make traversing the sides relatively difficult for a robotic vehicle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zakrajsek05_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-zakrajsek05-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition, it is possible that the interior floor might not have collected a significant quantity of
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/volatile" title="wiktionary:volatile">
        volatiles
       </a>
       since its formation. However other craters in the vicinity are considerably older, and may contain significant deposits of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       , possibly in the form of water ice. (See
       <a href="/wiki/Shoemaker_(lunar_crater)" title="Shoemaker (lunar crater)">
        Shoemaker (lunar crater)
       </a>
       , for example.)
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
        Radar
       </a>
       studies preceding and following the Lunar Prospector mission demonstrate that the inner walls of Shackleton are similar in
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        reflective characteristics
       </a>
       to those of some sunlit craters. In particular, the surroundings appear to contain a significant number of blocks in its ejecta blanket, suggesting that its radar properties are a result of surface roughness, and not ice deposits, as was previously suggested from a radar experiment involving the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_mission" title="Clementine mission">
        Clementine mission
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icarus180_1_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-icarus180_1-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This interpretation, however, is not universally agreed upon within the scientific community.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_review06_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-space_review06-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Radar images of the crater at a wavelength of 13 cm show no evidence for water ice deposits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature443_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nature443-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Optical imaging inside the crater was done for the first time by the Japanese lunar orbiter spacecraft
       <a href="/wiki/SELENE" title="SELENE">
        Kaguya
       </a>
       in 2007. It did not have any evidence of significant amount of water ice, down to the image resolution of 10 m per pixel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science322_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-science322-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaxa081024_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jaxa081024-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On November 15, 2008, a 34-kg probe made a hard landing near the crater.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jsr603_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jsr603-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The moon impact probe (MIP) was launched from the Indian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-I" title="Chandrayaan-I">
        Chandrayaan-I
       </a>
       spacecraft and reached the surface 25 minutes later. The probe carried a radar altimeter, video imaging system, and a mass spectrometer, which will be used to search for water.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chitramala081115_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-chitramala081115-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_uses">
        Potential uses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shackleton_(crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Potential uses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Degerlache_crater.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1125" data-file-width="1044" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Degerlache_crater.jpg/220px-Degerlache_crater.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Degerlache_crater.jpg/330px-Degerlache_crater.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Degerlache_crater.jpg/440px-Degerlache_crater.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Degerlache_crater.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Shackleton as imaged by
         <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
          LRO
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radar_Image_of_the_Lunar_South_Pole_from_Jean-Luc_Margot%27s_PhD_thesis.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Radar_Image_of_the_Lunar_South_Pole_from_Jean-Luc_Margot%27s_PhD_thesis.jpg/220px-Radar_Image_of_the_Lunar_South_Pole_from_Jean-Luc_Margot%27s_PhD_thesis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Radar_Image_of_the_Lunar_South_Pole_from_Jean-Luc_Margot%27s_PhD_thesis.jpg/330px-Radar_Image_of_the_Lunar_South_Pole_from_Jean-Luc_Margot%27s_PhD_thesis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Radar_Image_of_the_Lunar_South_Pole_from_Jean-Luc_Margot%27s_PhD_thesis.jpg/440px-Radar_Image_of_the_Lunar_South_Pole_from_Jean-Luc_Margot%27s_PhD_thesis.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radar_Image_of_the_Lunar_South_Pole_from_Jean-Luc_Margot%27s_PhD_thesis.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Shackleton Crater as imaged by Earth-based
         <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
          radar
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Some sites along Shackleton's rim receive almost constant illumination. At these locales sunlight is almost always available for conversion into
       <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
        electricity
       </a>
       using
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">
        solar panels
       </a>
       , potentially making them good locations for future
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">
        Moon landings
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpsc35_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lpsc35-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The temperature at this site is also more favorable than at more equatorial latitudes as it does not experience the daily temperature extremes of 100 °C when the Sun is overhead, to as low as −150 °C during the lunar night.
      </p>
      <p>
       While scientific experiments performed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_mission" title="Clementine mission">
        Clementine
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       could indicate the presence of water in the polar craters, the current evidence is far from definitive. There are doubts among scientists as to whether or not the hydrogen is in the form of ice, as well as to the concentration of this "ore" with depth below the surface. Resolution of this issue will require future missions to the Moon. The presence of water suggests that the crater floor could potentially be "mined" for deposits of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       in water form, a commodity that is expensive to deliver directly from the Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       This crater has also been proposed as a future site for a large
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cospar34_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cospar34-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The low temperature of the crater floor makes it ideal for infrared observations, and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">
        solar cells
       </a>
       placed along the rim could provide near-continuous power to the observatory. About 120 kilometers from the crater lies the 5-km tall
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malapert_Mountain" title="Malapert Mountain">
        Malapert Mountain
       </a>
       , a peak that is perpetually visible from the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , and which could serve as a
       <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
        radio
       </a>
       relay station when suitably equipped.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space02_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-space02-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2006,
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       named the rim of Shackleton as a potential candidate for its
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_outpost_(NASA)" title="Lunar outpost (NASA)">
        lunar outpost
       </a>
       , slated to be up and running by 2020 and continuously staffed by a crew by 2024. The location would promote self-sustainability for lunar residents, as perpetual sunlight on the south pole would provide energy for solar panels. Furthermore, the shadowed polar regions are believed to contain the frozen water necessary for human consumption and could also be harvested for fuel manufacture.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kluger06_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kluger06-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
