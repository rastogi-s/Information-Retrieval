   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Magnetic field of the Moon
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">

    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_ER_magnetic_field.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="578" data-file-width="800" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Moon_ER_magnetic_field.jpg/300px-Moon_ER_magnetic_field.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Moon_ER_magnetic_field.jpg/450px-Moon_ER_magnetic_field.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Moon_ER_magnetic_field.jpg/600px-Moon_ER_magnetic_field.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_ER_magnetic_field.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Total magnetic field strength at the surface of the Moon as derived from the Lunar Prospector electron reflectometer experiment.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
         magnetic field
        </a>
        of the
        <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
         Moon
        </a>
       </b>
       is very weak in comparison to that of the Earth. Other major differences are that the Moon does not currently have a
       <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">
        dipolar
       </a>
       magnetic field (as would be generated by a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodynamo" title="Geodynamo">
        geodynamo
       </a>
       in its core) and the varying magnetization that is present is almost entirely
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crustal
       </a>
       in origin. One hypothesis holds that the crustal magnetizations were acquired early in lunar history when a geodynamo was still operating. An analysis of magnetized moon rocks brought to Earth by Apollo astronauts showed that the moon must have had a strong magnetic field at least 4.25 billion years ago, which then slowly diminished up until 3.1 billion years ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The small size of the
       <a href="/wiki/Internal_structure_of_the_Moon" title="Internal structure of the Moon">
        lunar core
       </a>
       , however, is a potential obstacle to promoting that hypothesis to the status of
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_theory" title="Scientific theory">
        theory
       </a>
       . Alternatively, it is possible that on an airless body such as the Moon, transient magnetic fields could be generated during large
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">
        impact events
       </a>
       . In support of this, it has been noted that the largest crustal magnetizations appear to be located near the
       <a href="/wiki/Antipodes" title="Antipodes">
        antipodes
       </a>
       of the giant
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impact_basin" title="Impact basin">
        impact basins
       </a>
       . It has been proposed that such a phenomenon could result from the free expansion of an impact-generated
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma cloud
       </a>
       around the Moon in the presence of an ambient magnetic field.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-1" title="Chandrayaan-1">
        Chandrayaan-1
       </a>
       spacecraft mapped a "mini-magnetosphere" at the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Crisium" title="Mare Crisium">
        Crisium
       </a>
       antipode on the moon's far side, using its Sub-keV Atom Reflecting Analyzer (SARA) instrument. The mini-magnetosphere is 360 km across at the surface and is surrounded by a 300-km-thick region of enhanced plasma flux that results from the solar wind flowing around the mini-magnetosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There's growing evidence that fine particles of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moondust" title="Moondust">
        moondust
       </a>
       might actually float, ejected from the lunar surface by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic_repulsion" title="Electrostatic repulsion">
        electrostatic repulsion
       </a>
       . This could create a temporary nighttime "atmosphere" of dust. The moondust atmosphere might also gather itself into a sort of diaphanous wind. Drawn by differences in global charge accumulation, floating dust would naturally fly from the strongly negative nightside to the weakly negative dayside. This "dust storm" effect would be strongest at the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">
        terminator
       </a>
       . Much of these details are still speculative, but the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       spacecraft detected changes in the lunar nightside voltage during magnetotail crossings, jumping from -200 V to -1000 V. Further characterization was done by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Atmosphere_and_Dust_Environment_Explorer" title="Lunar Atmosphere and Dust Environment Explorer">
        Lunar Atmosphere and Dust Environment Explorer
       </a>
       orbiter in late 2013.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsf20130906_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nsf20130906-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FAQ-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_sheet" title="Plasma sheet">
        plasma sheet
       </a>
       is a very dynamic structure, in a constant state of motion, so as the Moon orbits through the magnetotail the plasma sheet can sweep across it many times with encounters lasting anywhere from minutes to hours or even days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">
        In fiction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_field_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In fiction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Space_Odyssey" title="Space Odyssey">
         Space Odyssey
        </a>
       </i>
       series by
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
        Arthur C. Clarke
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Monolith_(Space_Odyssey)" title="Monolith (Space Odyssey)">
        monolith
       </a>
       is found on the Moon near the crater
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tycho_(crater)" title="Tycho (crater)">
        Tycho
       </a>
       by its unnaturally powerful magnetic field and named
       <i>
        Tycho
        <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_anomaly" title="Magnetic anomaly">
         Magnetic Anomaly
        </a>
        1
       </i>
       (TMA-1).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
