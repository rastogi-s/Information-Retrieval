      <p>
       The
       <b>
        heliosphere
       </b>
       is the bubble-like
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space#Interplanetary_space" title="Outer space">
        region of space
       </a>
       dominated by the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , which extends far beyond the orbit of
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        Plasma
       </a>
       "blown" out from the Sun, known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       , creates and maintains this bubble against the outside
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
        pressure
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">
        interstellar medium
       </a>
       , the hydrogen and helium gas that permeates the
       <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">
        Milky Way Galaxy
       </a>
       . The solar wind flows outward from the Sun until encountering the
       <b>
        termination shock
       </b>
       , where motion slows abruptly. The
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">
        Voyager spacecraft
       </a>
       have explored the outer reaches of the heliosphere, passing through the shock and entering the
       <b>
        heliosheath
       </b>
       , a transitional region which is in turn bounded by the outermost edge of the heliosphere, called the
       <b>
        heliopause
       </b>
       . The shape of the heliosphere is controlled by the interstellar medium through which it is traveling, as well as the Sun and is not perfectly spherical.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa-voyager-proves_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa-voyager-proves-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The limited data available and unexplored nature of these structures have resulted in many theories.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scientificamerican-voyager_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican-voyager-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The word "heliosphere" is said to have been coined by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_J._Dessler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alexander J. Dessler (page does not exist)">
        Alexander J. Dessler
       </a>
       , who is credited with first use of the word in the scientific literature.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dessler-revgeophys_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dessler-revgeophys-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On September 12, 2013,
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       announced that
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
        Voyager 1
       </a>
       left the heliosphere on August 25, 2012, when it measured a sudden increase in plasma density of about forty times.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voyager-historic_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-voyager-historic-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because the heliopause marks
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System#Boundaries" title="Solar System">
        one boundary
       </a>
       between the Sun's solar wind and the rest of the galaxy, a spacecraft such as Voyager 1 which has departed the heliosphere, can be said to have reached
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space#Interstellar_space" title="Outer space">
        interstellar space
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       Except for
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        regions
       </a>
       near obstacles such as
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Comet#Coma_and_tail" title="Comet">
        comets
       </a>
       , the heliosphere is dominated by material emanating from the Sun, although
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">
        cosmic rays
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energetic_neutral_atoms" title="Energetic neutral atoms">
        fast-moving neutral atoms
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">
        cosmic dust
       </a>
       can penetrate the heliosphere from the outside. Originating at the extremely hot surface of the
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       , solar wind particles reach
       <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">
        escape velocity
       </a>
       , streaming outwards at 300 to 800 km/s (671 thousand to 1.79 million mph or 1 to 2.9 million km/h).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As it begins to interact with the
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">
        interstellar medium
       </a>
       , its velocity slows to a stop. The point where the solar wind becomes slower than the speed of sound is called the
       <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere#Termination_shock" title="Heliosphere">
        termination shock
       </a>
       ; the solar wind continues to slow as it passes through the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliosheath" title="Heliosheath">
        heliosheath
       </a>
       leading to a boundary called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)#Heliopause" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">
        heliopause
       </a>
       , where the interstellar medium and solar wind pressures balance. The termination shock was traversed by
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
        Voyager 1
       </a>
       in 2004,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voyager_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-voyager-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
        Voyager 2
       </a>
       in 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa-voyager-proves_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa-voyager-proves-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was thought that beyond the heliopause there was a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_shock" title="Bow shock">
        bow shock
       </a>
       , but data from
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer" title="Interstellar Boundary Explorer">
        Interstellar Boundary Explorer
       </a>
       suggested the velocity of the Sun through the interstellar medium is too low for it to form.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No-Bow-Shock_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-No-Bow-Shock-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zank_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-zank-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stag_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-stag-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 'stagnation region' within the heliosheath, starting around 113
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        au
       </a>

       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">

       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stag_9-1">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stag_9-2">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Heliospheric_current_sheet">
        Heliospheric current sheet
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heliospheric current sheet">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Heliospheric_current_sheet" title="Heliospheric current sheet">
        Heliospheric current sheet
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Heliospheric_current_sheet" title="Heliospheric current sheet">
        heliospheric current sheet
       </a>
       is a ripple in the heliosphere created by the rotating magnetic field of the Sun. Extending throughout the heliosphere, it is considered the largest structure in the Solar System and is said to resemble a "ballerina's skirt".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Outer_structure">
        Outer structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Outer structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The outer structure of the heliosphere is determined by the interactions between the solar wind and the winds of interstellar space. The solar wind streams away from the Sun in all directions at speeds of several hundred km/s in the Earth's vicinity. At some distance from the Sun, well beyond the orbit of
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       , this supersonic wind must slow down to meet the gases in the
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">
        interstellar medium
       </a>
       . This takes place in several stages:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The solar wind is traveling at
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">
         supersonic
        </a>
        speeds within the Solar System. At the termination shock, a standing
        <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">
         shock wave
        </a>
        , the solar wind falls below the speed of sound and becomes
        <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">
         subsonic
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        It was previously thought that, once subsonic, the solar wind would be shaped by the ambient flow of the interstellar medium, forming blunt nose on one side and comet-like heliotail behind, a region called the heliosheath. However, observations in 2009 showed that this model is incorrect.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassini-shape-solar-system_13-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Cassini-shape-solar-system-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEX-2009_14-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-IBEX-2009-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        As of 2011, it is thought to be filled with a magnetic bubble "foam".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surprise_17-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-surprise-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The outer surface of the heliosheath, where the heliosphere meets the interstellar medium, is called the
        <b>
         heliopause
        </b>
        . This is the edge of the entire heliosphere. Observations in 2009 led to changes to this model.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassini-shape-solar-system_13-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Cassini-shape-solar-system-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEX-2009_14-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-IBEX-2009-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In theory, the heliopause causes turbulence in the interstellar medium as the sun orbits the
        <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">
         Galactic Center
        </a>
        . Outside the heliopause, would be a turbulent region caused by the pressure of the advancing heliopause against the
        <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">
         interstellar medium
        </a>
        . However, the velocity of Solar wind relative to the interstellar medium is probably too low for a bow shock.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No-Bow-Shock_7-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-No-Bow-Shock-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Termination_shock">
        Termination shock
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Termination shock">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heliosphere-washbasin.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="790" data-file-width="1052" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Heliosphere-washbasin.svg/220px-Heliosphere-washbasin.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Heliosphere-washbasin.svg/330px-Heliosphere-washbasin.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Heliosphere-washbasin.svg/440px-Heliosphere-washbasin.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heliosphere-washbasin.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A termination shock in a sink basin
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The termination shock is the point in the heliosphere where the solar wind slows down to
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound#Speed_in_plasma" title="Speed of sound">
        subsonic
       </a>
       speed (relative to the Sun) because of interactions with the local
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">
        interstellar medium
       </a>
       . This causes
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_(physical)" title="Compression (physical)">
        compression
       </a>
       , heating, and a change in the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       . In the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       the termination shock is believed to be 75 to 90
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical units
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apod-heliosphere_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-apod-heliosphere-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       from the Sun. In 2004,
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
        Voyager 1
       </a>
       crossed the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's termination shock followed by
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
        Voyager 2
       </a>
       in 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voy1-term2004_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Voy1-term2004-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voy1-term2005_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Voy1-term2005-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voyager-historic_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-voyager-historic-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voy2-term2007_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Voy2-term2007-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa-voyager-proves_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa-voyager-proves-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The shock arises because solar wind particles are emitted from the Sun at about 400 km/s, while the speed of sound (in the interstellar medium) is about 100 km/s. (The exact speed depends on the density, which fluctuates considerably.) The interstellar medium, although very low in density, nonetheless has a constant pressure associated with it; the pressure from the solar wind decreases with the square of the distance from the Sun. As one moves far enough away from the Sun, the pressure of the solar wind drops to where it can no longer maintain supersonic flow against the pressure of the interstellar medium, at which point the solar wind slows to below its speed of sound, causing a
       <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">
        shock wave
       </a>
       . Further from the Sun, the termination shock is followed by the heliopause, where the two pressures become equal and solar wind particles are stopped by the interstellar medium.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other termination shocks can be seen in terrestrial systems; perhaps the easiest may be seen by simply running a water
       <a href="/wiki/Tap_(valve)" title="Tap (valve)">
        tap
       </a>
       into a
       <a href="/wiki/Sink" title="Sink">
        sink
       </a>
       creating a
       <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_jump" title="Hydraulic jump">
        hydraulic jump
       </a>
       . Upon hitting the floor of the sink, the flowing water spreads out at a speed that is higher than the local
       <a href="/wiki/Phase_velocity" title="Phase velocity">
        wave speed
       </a>
       , forming a disk of shallow, rapidly diverging flow (analogous to the tenuous, supersonic solar wind). Around the periphery of the disk, a shock front or wall of water forms; outside the shock front, the water moves slower than the local wave speed (analogous to the subsonic interstellar medium).
      </p>
      <p>
       Evidence presented at a meeting of the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">
        American Geophysical Union
       </a>
       in May 2005 by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ed_Stone" title="Ed Stone">
        Ed Stone
       </a>
       suggests that the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft passed the termination shock in December 2004, when it was about 94 AU from the Sun, by virtue of the change in magnetic readings taken from the craft. In contrast,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       began detecting returning particles when it was only 76 AU from the Sun, in May 2006. This implies that the heliosphere may be irregularly shaped, bulging outwards in the Sun's northern hemisphere and pushed inward in the south.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voyager_II_cnn_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-voyager_II_cnn-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Heliosheath">
        Heliosheath
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heliosheath">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Transitional_regions.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="730" data-file-width="973" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Transitional_regions.jpg/220px-Transitional_regions.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Transitional_regions.jpg/330px-Transitional_regions.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Transitional_regions.jpg/440px-Transitional_regions.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Transitional_regions.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         This picture about the heliosphere was released on June 28, 2013 and incorporates results from the Voyager spacecraft.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
          <a href="#cite_note-26">
           [26]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The heliosheath is the region of the heliosphere beyond the termination shock. Here the wind is slowed, compressed and made turbulent by its interaction with the interstellar medium. Its distance from the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       is approximately 80 to 100
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical units
       </a>
       (AU) at its closest point.
      </p>
      <p>
       A proposed model hypothesizes that the heliosheath is shaped like the
       <a href="/wiki/Coma_(cometary)" title="Coma (cometary)">
        coma
       </a>
       of a comet, and trails several times that distance in the direction opposite to the Sun's path through space. At its windward side, its thickness is estimated to be between 10 and 100 AU.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandt-2007_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brandt-2007-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, observations in 2009 showed that model may be incorrect.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassini-shape-solar-system_13-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cassini-shape-solar-system-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEX-2009_14-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-IBEX-2009-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft have studied the heliosheath. In late 2010,
       <i>
        Voyager 1
       </i>
       reached a region of the heliosheath where the solar wind's velocity had dropped to zero.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amos_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Amos-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.Com_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Space.Com-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brumfiel_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brumfiel-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krimigis_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Krimigis-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2011, astronomers announced that the
       <i>
        Voyagers
       </i>
       had determined that the heliosheath is not smooth, but is filled with 100 million-mile-wide bubbles created by the impact of the solar wind and the interstellar medium.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cook-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rayl_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rayl-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Voyager 1
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        2
       </i>
       began detecting evidence for the bubbles in 2007 and 2008, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rayl_33-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rayl-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The probably sausage-shaped bubbles are formed by
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       between oppositely oriented sectors of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_magnetic_field" title="Solar magnetic field">
        solar magnetic field
       </a>
       as the solar wind slows down.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rayl_33-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rayl-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They probably represent self-contained structures that have detached from the
       <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">
        interplanetary magnetic field
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cook-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rayl_33-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rayl-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Heliopause">
        Heliopause
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Heliopause">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The heliopause is the theoretical boundary where the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       is stopped by the
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">
        interstellar medium
       </a>
       ; where the solar wind's strength is no longer great enough to push back the
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">
        stellar winds
       </a>
       of the surrounding stars. This is the boundary where the interstellar medium and solar wind pressures balance. The crossing of the heliopause should be signaled by a sharp drop in the temperature of charged particles,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.Com_29-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Space.Com-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a change in the direction of the magnetic field, and an increase in the number of galactic cosmic rays.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rays_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-rays-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In May 2012,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       detected a rapid increase in such cosmic rays (a 9% increase in a month, following a more gradual increase of 25% from Jan. 2009 to Jan. 2012), suggesting it was approaching the heliopause.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rays_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-rays-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the fall of 2013, NASA announced that Voyager 1 had crossed the heliopause as of August 25, 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:2-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This was at a distance of 121 AU (18 billion km) from the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cowen2013_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cowen2013-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Contrary to predictions, data from Voyager 1 indicates the magnetic field of the galaxy is aligned with the solar magnetic field.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb noresize tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; overflow:hidden; width:auto; max-width:783px;">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="">
        <div class="overflowbugx" style="overflow:auto;">

         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg" title="File:Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg">
          </a>
         </div>
         "..solar wind and the interstellar medium interact to create a region known as the inner heliosheath, bounded on the inside by the termination shock, and on the outside by the heliopause."-NASA
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
          <a href="#cite_note-36">
           [36]

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Heliotail">
        Heliotail
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Heliotail">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The heliotail is the tail of the heliosphere, and thus the solar system's tail. It can be compared to the tail of a comet (however, a comet's tail does not stretch behind it as it moves; it is always pointing away from the Sun). The tail is a region where the Sun's solar wind slows down and ultimately escapes the heliosphere, slowly evaporating because of charge exchange.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The shape of the heliotail (newly found by NASA's Interstellar Boundary Explorer - IBEX), is that of a four-leaf clover.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The particles in the tail do not shine, therefore it cannot be seen with conventional optical instruments. IBEX made the first observations of the heliotail by measuring the energy of "energetic neutral atoms", neutral particles created by collisions in the solar system's boundary zone.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_38-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The tail has been shown to contain fast and slow particles; the slow particles are on the side and the fast particles are encompassed in the center. The shape of the tail can be linked to the sun sending out fast solar winds near its poles and slow solar wind near its equator more recently. The clover-shaped tail moves further away from the sun, which makes the charged particles begin to morph into a new orientation.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Space_beyond_the_heliosphere">
        Space beyond the heliosphere
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Space beyond the heliosphere">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA12375.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2031" data-file-width="3019" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/PIA12375.jpg/220px-PIA12375.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/PIA12375.jpg/330px-PIA12375.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/PIA12375.jpg/440px-PIA12375.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA12375.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The bubble-like heliosphere moving through the interstellar medium
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solarmap.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="605" data-file-width="1000" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Solarmap.png/220px-Solarmap.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Solarmap.png/330px-Solarmap.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Solarmap.png/440px-Solarmap.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solarmap.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Solar map with the location of the hypothetical hydrogen wall and bow shock
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The heliopause is the final known boundary between the heliosphere and the interstellar space that is filled with material, especially plasma, not from our own star, the Sun, but from other stars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Even so, just outside the heliosphere (i.e. the "solar bubble") there is a transitional region, as detected by Voyager 1.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interstellar_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-interstellar-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Just as some interstellar pressure was detected as early as 2004, some of the Sun's material seeps into the interstellar medium.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interstellar_40-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-interstellar-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The heliosphere is thought to reside in the
       <a href="/wiki/Local_Interstellar_Cloud" title="Local Interstellar Cloud">
        Local Interstellar Cloud
       </a>
       inside the
       <a href="/wiki/Local_Bubble" title="Local Bubble">
        Local Bubble
       </a>
       , which is a region in the
       <a href="/wiki/Orion_Arm" title="Orion Arm">
        Orion Arm
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_Galaxy" title="Milky Way Galaxy">
        Milky Way Galaxy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Outside the heliosphere there is a forty-fold increase in plasma density.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interstellar_40-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-interstellar-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is also a radical reduction in the detection of certain types of particles from the Sun, and a large increase in Galactic cosmic rays.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The flow of the
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">
        interstellar medium
       </a>
       (ISM) into the heliosphere has been measured by at least 11 different spacecraft as of 2013.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flow_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-flow-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By 2013, it was suspected that the direction of the flow had changed over time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flow_42-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-flow-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The flow, coming from Earth's perspective from the constellation Scorpius, has probably changed direction by several degrees since the 1970s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flow_42-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-flow-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen_wall">
        Hydrogen wall
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hydrogen wall">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       "Hydrogen wall" redirects here. For other topics, see
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_(disambiguation)" title="Hydrogen (disambiguation)">
        Hydrogen (disambiguation)
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       According to one hypothesis,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       there exists a region of hot hydrogen known as the
       <b>
        hydrogen wall
       </b>
       between the bow shock and the heliopause. The wall is composed of interstellar material interacting with the edge of the heliosphere. One paper released in 2013 studied the concept of a bow wave and hydrogen wall.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zank_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-zank-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another hypothesis suggests that the heliopause could be smaller on the side of the Solar System facing the Sun's orbital motion through the galaxy. It may also vary depending on the current velocity of the solar wind and the local density of the interstellar medium. It is known to lie far outside the orbit of
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       . The current mission of the
       <i>
        Voyager 1
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         2
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft is to find and study the termination shock, heliosheath, and heliopause. Meanwhile, the
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer" title="Interstellar Boundary Explorer">
        Interstellar Boundary Explorer
       </a>
       (IBEX) mission is attempting to image the heliopause from Earth orbit within two years of its 2008 launch. Initial results (October 2009) from IBEX suggest that previous assumptions are insufficiently cognisant of the true complexities of the heliopause.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When particles emitted by the sun bump into the interstellar ones, they slow down while releasing energy. Many particles accumulate in and around the heliopause, highly energised by their negative acceleration, creating a shock wave.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       An alternative definition is that the heliopause is the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetopause" title="Magnetopause">
        magnetopause
       </a>
       between the Solar System's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       and the galaxy's plasma currents.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Bow_shock">
        Bow shock
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bow shock">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics" title="Bow shocks in astrophysics">
        Bow shocks in astrophysics
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       It was long hypothesized that the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       produces a "shock wave" in its travels within the interstellar medium. It would occur if the interstellar medium is moving supersonically "toward" the Sun, since its solar wind moves "away" from the Sun supersonically. When the interstellar wind hits the heliosphere it slows and creates a region of turbulence. A bow shock was thought to possibly occur at about 230 AU,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apod-heliosphere_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-apod-heliosphere-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but in 2012 it was determined it probably does not exist.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No-Bow-Shock_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-No-Bow-Shock-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This conclusion resulted from new measurements: The velocity of the LISM (Local Interstellar Medium) relative to the Sun's was previously measured to be 26.3 km/s by
       <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(spacecraft)" title="Ulysses (spacecraft)">
        Ulysses
       </a>
       , whereas IBEX measured it at 23.2 km/s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This phenomenon has been observed outside the Solar System, around stars other than the Sun, by NASA's now retired orbital
       <a href="/wiki/GALEX" title="GALEX">
        GALEX
       </a>
       telescope. The red giant star
       <a href="/wiki/Mira" title="Mira">
        Mira
       </a>
       in the constellation
       <a href="/wiki/Cetus" title="Cetus">
        Cetus
       </a>
       has been shown to have both a debris tail of ejecta from the star and a distinct shock in the direction of its movement through space (at over 130 kilometers per second).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observational_methods">
        Observational methods
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Observational methods">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PioneerH2011.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/PioneerH2011.jpg/220px-PioneerH2011.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/PioneerH2011.jpg/330px-PioneerH2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/PioneerH2011.jpg/440px-PioneerH2011.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PioneerH2011.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Pioneer H, on display at the National Air and Space Museum, was a canceled probe to study the Sun.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pioneer-Swingby_46-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-Pioneer-Swingby-46">
           [46]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_by_spacecraft">
        Detection by spacecraft
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Detection by spacecraft">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The precise distance to, and shape of the heliopause is still uncertain. Interplanetary/interstellar
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_10" title="Pioneer 10">
         Pioneer 10
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_11" title="Pioneer 11">
         Pioneer 11
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
        New Horizons
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       are traveling outward through the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       and will eventually pass through the heliopause. Contact to Pioneer 10 and 11 has been lost.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cassini_results">
        Cassini results
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cassini results">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Rather than a comet-like shape, the heliosphere appears to be bubble-shaped according to data from
       <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
        Cassini's
       </a>
       Ion and Neutral Camera (MIMI / INCA). Rather than being dominated by the collisions between the solar wind and the interstellar medium, the INCA (
       <a href="/wiki/Energetic_neutral_atom" title="Energetic neutral atom">
        ENA
       </a>
       ) maps suggest that the interaction is controlled more by particle pressure and magnetic field energy density.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassini-shape-solar-system_13-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cassini-shape-solar-system-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-photojournal_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-photojournal-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="IBEX_results">
        IBEX results
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: IBEX results">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IBEXmagneticfieldinfluence.jpg">
         <img alt="IBEXmagneticfieldinfluence.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="264" data-file-width="398" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/IBEXmagneticfieldinfluence.jpg/220px-IBEXmagneticfieldinfluence.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/IBEXmagneticfieldinfluence.jpg/330px-IBEXmagneticfieldinfluence.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/da/IBEXmagneticfieldinfluence.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IBEXmagneticfieldinfluence.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Initial data from
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer" title="Interstellar Boundary Explorer">
        Interstellar Boundary Explorer
       </a>
       (IBEX), launched in October 2008, revealed a previously unpredicted "very narrow ribbon that is two to three times brighter than anything else in the sky."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEX-2009_14-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-IBEX-2009-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Initial interpretations suggest that "the interstellar environment has far more influence on structuring the heliosphere than anyone previously believed"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "No one knows what is creating the ENA (energetic neutral atoms) ribbon, ..."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerr-2009_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kerr-2009-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       "The IBEX results are truly remarkable! What we are seeing in these maps does not match with any of the previous theoretical models of this region. It will be exciting for scientists to review these (
       <a href="/wiki/Energetic_neutral_atom" title="Energetic neutral atom">
        ENA
       </a>
       ) maps and revise the way we understand our heliosphere and how it interacts with the galaxy."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In October 2010, significant changes were detected in the ribbon after 6 months, based on the second set of IBEX observations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-change_51-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-change-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       IBEX data did not support the existence of a bow shock,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No-Bow-Shock_7-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-No-Bow-Shock-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but there might be a 'bow wave' according to one study.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zank_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-zank-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Locally">
        Locally
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Locally">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evolving_Heliophysics_System_Observatory.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="749" data-file-width="686" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Evolving_Heliophysics_System_Observatory.jpg/220px-Evolving_Heliophysics_System_Observatory.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Evolving_Heliophysics_System_Observatory.jpg/330px-Evolving_Heliophysics_System_Observatory.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Evolving_Heliophysics_System_Observatory.jpg/440px-Evolving_Heliophysics_System_Observatory.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evolving_Heliophysics_System_Observatory.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Overview of
         <a href="/wiki/Heliophysics" title="Heliophysics">
          Heliophysics
         </a>
         spacecraft circa 2011
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Of particular interest is the Earth's interaction with the heliosphere, but its extent and interaction with other bodies in the solar system have also been studied. Some examples of missions that have or continue to collect data related to the heliosphere include
       <i>
        (see also
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_heliophysics_missions" title="List of heliophysics missions">
         List of heliophysics missions
        </a>
        )
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_Anomalous_and_Magnetospheric_Particle_Explorer" title="Solar Anomalous and Magnetospheric Particle Explorer">
         Solar Anomalous and Magnetospheric Particle Explorer
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
         Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
         Solar Dynamics Observatory
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">
         STEREO
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(spacecraft)" title="Ulysses (spacecraft)">
         Ulysses (spacecraft)
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       During a total eclipse the high-temperature corona can be more readily observed from Earth solar observatories. During the Apollo program the Solar wind was measured on the Moon via the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Wind_Composition_Experiment" title="Solar Wind Composition Experiment">
        Solar Wind Composition Experiment
       </a>
       . Some examples of Earth surface based Solar observatories include the
       <a href="/wiki/McMath%E2%80%93Pierce_solar_telescope" title="McMath–Pierce solar telescope">
        McMath–Pierce solar telescope
       </a>
       or the newer
       <a href="/wiki/GREGOR_Solar_Telescope" title="GREGOR Solar Telescope">
        GREGOR Solar Telescope
       </a>
       , and the refurbished
       <a href="/wiki/Big_Bear_Solar_Observatory" title="Big Bear Solar Observatory">
        Big Bear Solar Observatory
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline">
        Timeline
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Timeline">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        As of March 2005, it was reported that measurements by the Solar Wind Anisotropies (SWAN) instrument on board the
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
         Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
        </a>
        (SOHO) have shown that the heliosphere, the solar wind-filled volume which prevents the Solar System from becoming embedded in the local (ambient) interstellar medium, is not
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axisymmetrical" title="Axisymmetrical">
         axisymmetrical
        </a>
        , but is distorted, very likely under the effect of the local galactic magnetic field.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lallement-2005_52-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lallement-2005-52">
          [52]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        As of 2008, there is a previously unpredicted narrow ribbon of ENAs.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEX-2009_14-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-IBEX-2009-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        As of October 2009, the heliosphere may be bubble, not comet shaped.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassini-shape-solar-system_13-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-Cassini-shape-solar-system-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        As of October 2010, significant changes were detected in the ribbon after 6 months, based on the second set of IBEX observations.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-change_51-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-change-51">
          [51]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        As of June 2011, the heliosheath area is thought to be filled with magnetic bubbles (each about 1
        <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
         AU
        </a>
        wide), creating a "foamy zone".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surprise_17-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-surprise-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The theory helps explain
        <a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">
         in situ
        </a>
        heliosphere measurements by the two Voyager probes.
       </li>
       <li>
        As of May 2012, IBEX data implies there is probably not a bow "shock".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No-Bow-Shock_7-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-No-Bow-Shock-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        As of June 2012 at 119 AU Voyager 1 detected an increase in cosmic rays.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rays_11-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-rays-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Between late August and early September 2012, Voyager 1 witnessed a sharp drop in protons from the sun, from 25 particles per sec in late August, to about 2 particles per second by early October.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbc-voyager_53-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-nbc-voyager-53">
          [53]
         </a>
        </sup>
        It was later determined that it entered interstellar space on August 25, 2012.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voyager-historic_4-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-voyager-historic-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
