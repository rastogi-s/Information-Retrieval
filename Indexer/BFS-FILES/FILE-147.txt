   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Gravitational shielding
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">

     </a>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The term
       <b>
        gravitational shielding
       </b>
       refers to a
       <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">
        hypothetical
       </a>
       process of shielding an object from the influence of a
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_field" title="Gravitational field">
        gravitational field
       </a>
       . Such processes, if they existed, would have the effect of reducing the
       <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">
        weight
       </a>
       of an object. The shape of the shielded region would be similar to a shadow from the gravitational shield. For example, the shape of the shielded region above a disk would be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cone_(geometry)" title="Cone (geometry)">
        conical
       </a>
       . The height of the cone's apex above the disk would vary directly with the height of the shielding disk above the earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Experimental evidence to date indicates that no such effect exists. Gravitational shielding is considered to be a violation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_principle" title="Equivalence principle">
        equivalence principle
       </a>
       and therefore inconsistent with both Newtonian theory and
       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
        general relativity
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bert_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bert-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The concept of gravity shielding is a common concept in
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       literature, especially for
       <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">
        space travel
       </a>
       . One of the first and best known examples is the fictional gravity shielding substance "Cavorite" that appears in
       <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">
        H. G. Wells
       </a>
       ' classic 1901 novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_First_Men_in_the_Moon" title="The First Men in the Moon">
         The First Men in the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       . Wells was promptly criticized for using it by
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
        Jules Verne
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tests_of_the_equivalence_principle">
        Tests of the equivalence principle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gravitational_shielding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tests of the equivalence principle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As of 2008
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gravitational_shielding&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , no experiment was successful in detecting positive shielding results. To quantify the amount of shielding, at the beginning of 20th century
       <a href="/wiki/Quirino_Majorana" title="Quirino Majorana">
        Quirino Majorana
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majorana_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-majorana-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       suggested an extinction coefficient h that modifies Newton’s gravitational force law as follows:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

      <p>
       The best laboratory measurements have established an upper bound limit for shielding of 4.3×10
       <sup>
        −15
       </sup>
       m²/kg.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another recent analysis suggested a lower bound of 0.6×10
       <sup>
        −15
       </sup>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The best estimate based on the most accurate gravity anomaly data during the 1997 solar eclipse has provided a new constraint on the shielding parameter 6×10
       <sup>
        −19
       </sup>
       m²/kg.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, astronomical observations impose much more stringent limits. Based on lunar observations available in 1908,
       <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9" title="Henri Poincaré">
        Poincaré
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       established that h can be no greater than 10
       <sup>
        −18
       </sup>
       m²/kg. Subsequently, this bound has been greatly improved. Eckhardt
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       showed that lunar ranging data implies an upper bound of 10
       <sup>
        −22
       </sup>
       m²/kg, and Williams, et al.,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       have improved this to h = (3 ± 5)×10
       <sup>
        −22
       </sup>
       m²/kg. Note that the value is smaller than the uncertainty. The consequence of the negative results of those experiments (which are in good agreement with the predictions of general relativity) is, that every theory which contains shielding effects like
       <a href="/wiki/Le_Sage%27s_theory_of_gravitation" title="Le Sage's theory of gravitation">
        Le Sage's theory of gravitation
       </a>
       , must reduce those effects to an undetectable level. For a review of the current experimental limits on possible gravitational shielding, see the survey article by Bertolami, et al.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bert_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bert-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Also, for a discussion of recent observations during solar eclipses, see the paper by Unnikrishnan et al.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Majorana.27s_experiments_and_Russell.27s_criticism">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Majorana's_experiments_and_Russell's_criticism">
        Majorana's experiments and Russell's criticism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gravitational_shielding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Majorana's experiments and Russell's criticism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some shielding experiments were conducted in the early 20th century by
       <a href="/wiki/Quirino_Majorana" title="Quirino Majorana">
        Quirino Majorana
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majorana_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-majorana-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Majorana claimed to have measured positive shielding effects.
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Norris_Russell" title="Henry Norris Russell">
        Henry Norris Russell
       </a>
       's analysis of the tidal forces showed that Majorana's positive results had nothing to do with gravitational shielding.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To bring Majorana's experiments in accordance with the equivalence principle of General Relativity he proposed a model, in which the mass of a body is diminished by
       <i>
        the proximity
       </i>
       of another body, but he denied any connection between gravitational shielding and his proposal of mass variation. For another explanation of Majorana's experiments, see Coïsson et al.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       But Majorana's results couldn't be confirmed up to this day (see the section above) and Russell's mass variation theory, although meant as a modification of general relativity, is inconsistent with standard physics as well.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Minority_views">
        Minority views
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gravitational_shielding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Minority views">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The consensus view of the scientific community is that gravitational shielding does not exist, but there have been occasional investigations into this topic, such as the 1999 NASA-funded paper which reported negative results.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Podkletnov" title="Eugene Podkletnov">
        Eugene Podkletnov
       </a>
       claimed in two papers, one of which he later withdrew, that objects held above a magnetically-levitated, superconducting, rotating disc underwent a reduction of between 0.5 and 2% in weight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Theoreticians have attempted to reconcile Podkletnov's claims with
       <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">
        quantum gravity
       </a>
       theory.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, neither Podkletnov's claims, nor the similar claims of others, of "gravity reduction", "gravity shielding", or the like, have yet been successfully replicated, verified by independent review, or subjected to public demonstration.
      </p>
