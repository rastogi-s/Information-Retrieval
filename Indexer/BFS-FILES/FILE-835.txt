      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Estudios San Miguel
        </b>
       </i>
       (
       <b>
        San Miguel Studios
       </b>
       ) was an
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentine
       </a>
       film studio that was active in the 1940s and early 1950s. It flourished during the golden age of
       <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Argentina" title="Cinema of Argentina">
        Cinema of Argentina
       </a>
       , and at its peak was one of the major studios in
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       . Genres ranged from musical comedy to costume drama and gaucho thriller. Films included
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_guerra_gaucha" title="La guerra gaucha">
         La guerra gaucha
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        The Gaucho War
       </i>
       1942), co-produced with
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artistas_Argentinos_Asociados" title="Artistas Argentinos Asociados">
         Artistas Argentinos Asociados
        </a>
       </i>
       , and the comedy
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Juvenilia_(film)" title="Juvenilia (film)">
         Juvenilia
        </a>
       </i>
       (1943), both of which won several major awards. Eva Duarte, soon to become the first lady of Argentina as
       <a href="/wiki/Eva_Per%C3%B3n" title="Eva Perón">
        Eva Perón
       </a>
       , appeared in two of the studio's films in 1945. The studio became overextended financially and ceased production after 1952.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Estudios_San_Miguel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Estudios San Miguel
       </i>
       was founded and owned by
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Machinandiarena" title="Miguel Machinandiarena">
        Miguel Machinandiarena
       </a>
       (1899–1975).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBorrás200616_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorrás200616-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Machinandiarena was a
       <a href="/wiki/Basques" title="Basques">
        Basque
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">
        Navarre
       </a>
       who had emigrated to Argentina in 1915.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEElena200516_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEElena200516-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His family was very wealthy, had large real estate investments and controlled the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casino_de_Mar_del_Plata" title="Casino de Mar del Plata">
         Casino de Mar del Plata
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGilbert2011PT97_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGilbert2011PT97-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Miguel Machinandiarena founded the company in 1937 with his brothers Narciso and Silvestre and began to build the studios and laboratories in the town of
       <a href="/wiki/Bella_Vista,_Buenos_Aires" title="Bella Vista, Buenos Aires">
        Bella Vista
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAcademia_Nacional_de_la_Historia_Argentina2003260_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAcademia_Nacional_de_la_Historia_Argentina2003260-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The studio absorbed SIDE (
       <i>
        Sociedad Impresora de Discos Electrofónicos
       </i>
       ), which had pioneered the tango format with a trilogy of films, but failed to establish a solid business model.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEElenaDiaz_Lopez201241-42_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEElenaDiaz_Lopez201241-42-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1940
       <i>
        Estudios San Miguel
       </i>
       launched its first production,
       <i>
        Petróleo
       </i>
       , directed by
       <a href="/wiki/Arturo_S._Mom" title="Arturo S. Mom">
        Arturo S. Mom
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlazaola1989140_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlazaola1989140-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Machinandiarena was one of the great promoters of the Argentine film industry.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGirona_i_AlbuixecFernanda_Mancebo1995228_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGirona_i_AlbuixecFernanda_Mancebo1995228-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For a while the studio was one of the largest Argentine film companies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECirio201256_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECirio201256-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Italian director
       <a href="/wiki/Catrano_Catrani" title="Catrano Catrani">
        Catrano Catrani
       </a>
       , who emigrated to Argentina in 1937, became artistic director of the studio.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlazaola198948_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlazaola198948-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He and other directors at the studio were assisted by Catrani's wife, Vlasta Lah, who later directed
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Las_furias" title="Las furias">
         Las furias
        </a>
       </i>
       (1960) and
       <i>
        Las modelos
       </i>
       (1963).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlazaola1989119_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlazaola1989119-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The set designer and later director
       <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Pappier" title="Ralph Pappier">
        Ralph Pappier
       </a>
       designed the sets of
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_guerra_gaucha" title="La guerra gaucha">
         La guerra gaucha
        </a>
       </i>
       (1942),
       <i>
        En el viejo Buenos Aires
       </i>
       (1942) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Madame_Bovary_(1947_film)" title="Madame Bovary (1947 film)">
         Madame Bovary
        </a>
       </i>
       (1947). In 1944 Pappier created the first special effects department for the studio. Pappier and
       <a href="/wiki/Homero_Manzi" title="Homero Manzi">
        Homero Manzi
       </a>
       made
       <i>
        Pobre, me madre querida
       </i>
       in 1948.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlazaola1989156_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlazaola1989156-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Peruvian poet and author
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Mir%C3%B3" title="César Miró">
        César Miró
       </a>
       was a technical adviser at the studio from 1944 to 1953, when he moved to
       <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">
        Paramount Pictures
       </a>
       in Hollywood.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColl1974430_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColl1974430-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The future producer and director
       <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A9ctor_Olivera_(film_director)" title="Héctor Olivera (film director)">
        Hector Olivera
       </a>
       joined the studio as an assistant in 1947. Later he moved to
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artistas_Argentinos_Asociados" title="Artistas Argentinos Asociados">
         Artistas Argentinos Asociados
        </a>
       </i>
       and then became a co-founder of
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aries_Cinematogr%C3%A1fica_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aries Cinematográfica Argentina (page does not exist)">
         Aries Cinematográfica Argentina
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1956.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlazaola1989150_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlazaola1989150-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The scriptwriter, producer and director
       <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Carreras" title="Enrique Carreras">
        Enrique Carreras
       </a>
       joined the studio when he was very young. He later founded the
       <i>
        Productora Cinematográfica General Belgrano
       </i>
       in 1949 and directed his first film,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/El_mucamo_de_la_ni%C3%B1a" title="El mucamo de la niña">
         El mucamo de la niña
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1951.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlazaola198946_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlazaola198946-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The studio's last production was released in 1952. As of 31 December 1954 the studio had a debt of more than six million pesos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKriger200997_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKriger200997-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Noted_films">
        Noted films
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Enrique_Alvarez_Diosdado_-_Delia_Garc%C3%A9s_-_La_dama_duende_(1944).jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="307" data-file-width="429" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Enrique_Alvarez_Diosdado_-_Delia_Garc%C3%A9s_-_La_dama_duende_%281944%29.jpg/220px-Enrique_Alvarez_Diosdado_-_Delia_Garc%C3%A9s_-_La_dama_duende_%281944%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Enrique_Alvarez_Diosdado_-_Delia_Garc%C3%A9s_-_La_dama_duende_%281944%29.jpg/330px-Enrique_Alvarez_Diosdado_-_Delia_Garc%C3%A9s_-_La_dama_duende_%281944%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5d/Enrique_Alvarez_Diosdado_-_Delia_Garc%C3%A9s_-_La_dama_duende_%281944%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Enrique_Alvarez_Diosdado_-_Delia_Garc%C3%A9s_-_La_dama_duende_(1944).jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Diosdado" title="Enrique Diosdado">
          Enrique Diosdado
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Delia_Garc%C3%A9s" title="Delia Garcés">
          Delia Garcés
         </a>
         in
         <i>
          La dama duende
         </i>
         (1944)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_guerra_gaucha" title="La guerra gaucha">
         La guerra gaucha
        </a>
       </i>
       (1942) was an adaptation of a novel by
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Unamuno" title="Miguel de Unamuno">
        Miguel de Unamuno
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERicagnoBence200190_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERicagnoBence200190-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The partners at
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artistas_Argentinos_Asociados" title="Artistas Argentinos Asociados">
         Artistas Argentinos Asociados
        </a>
       </i>
       decided to make the film with their own funds. These proved insufficient and they had to partner with San Miguel Studios and undersell the exhibition rights for the movie earlier in some areas. These decisions allowed them to make the film with "a little less belt-tightening but without splurging".
       <b>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZolezzi200670_17-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZolezzi200670-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </b>
       The film was shot almost entirely within the
       <i>
        Estudios San Miguel
       </i>
       apart from two scenes in the Tigre delta in the
       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Río de la Plata">
        Río de la Plata
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERicagnoBence200190_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERicagnoBence200190-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The film won three Silver Condor awards, including
       <a href="/wiki/Silver_Condor_Award_for_Best_Film" title="Silver Condor Award for Best Film">
        Best Film
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELos_ganadores,_año_por_año_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELos_ganadores,_año_por_año-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Silver_Condor_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Silver Condor Award for Best Director">
        Best Director
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Demare" title="Lucas Demare">
        Lucas Demare
       </a>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOliveri199460_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOliveri199460-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Silver_Condor_Award_for_Best_Adapted_Screenplay" title="Silver Condor Award for Best Adapted Screenplay">
        Best Adapted Screenplay
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Ulyses_Petit_de_Murat" title="Ulyses Petit de Murat">
        Ulyses Petit de Murat
       </a>
       and Homero Manzior).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOliveri199460_19-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOliveri199460-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 1943 comedy-drama
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Juvenilia_(film)" title="Juvenilia (film)">
         Juvenilia
        </a>
       </i>
       was directed by
       <a href="/wiki/Augusto_C%C3%A9sar_Vatteone" title="Augusto César Vatteone">
        Augusto César Vatteone
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJuvenilia_–_Cinenacional.com_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJuvenilia_–_Cinenacional.com-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the
       <a href="/wiki/1944_Argentine_Film_Critics_Association_Awards" title="1944 Argentine Film Critics Association Awards">
        1944 Argentine Film Critics Association Awards
       </a>
       the film won the
       <a href="/wiki/Silver_Condor_Award_for_Best_Film" title="Silver Condor Award for Best Film">
        Silver Condor Award for Best Film
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELos_ganadores,_año_por_año_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELos_ganadores,_año_por_año-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Academy_of_Cinematography_Arts_and_Sciences_(1941%E2%80%9355)" title="Argentine Academy of Cinematography Arts and Sciences (1941–55)">
        Argentine Academy of Cinematography Arts and Sciences
       </a>
       gave the studio the "Best Picture" award for
       <i>
        Juvenilia
       </i>
       (1943),
       <i>
        La dama duende
       </i>
       (1945) and
       <i>
        Los isleros
       </i>
       (1951).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEARCHIVO_·_Premios_Anuales_1941_-_1953_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEARCHIVO_·_Premios_Anuales_1941_-_1953-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The studio had difficulty with the censors in 1944 over
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/El_fin_de_la_noche" title="El fin de la noche">
         El fin de la noche
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        End of the Night
       </i>
       ) which was anti-
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">
        Nazi
       </a>
       , while Argentina was neutral during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       (1939–45).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEContreras200388_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEContreras200388-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 1945 musical
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_cabalgata_del_circo" title="La cabalgata del circo">
         La cabalgata del circo
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        The Circus Cavalcade
       </i>
       ) was a vehicle for the female star of the moment,
       <a href="/wiki/Libertad_Lamarque" title="Libertad Lamarque">
        Libertad Lamarque
       </a>
       . Due to her performance, the film drew huge audiences. The film also included Eva Duarte, soon to become
       <a href="/wiki/Eva_Per%C3%B3n" title="Eva Perón">
        Eva Perón
       </a>
       as wife of the president of Argentina, in a minor role.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECoscia2012163_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoscia2012163-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Eva would arrive late at the studio, and would interrupt the shooting any time she was wanted on the phone, without the director daring to complain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorató2010PT156_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorató2010PT156-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, Eva's participation made it possible for Machinandiarena to obtain celluloid from the military government's Ministry of Communications.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECoscia2012163_23-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoscia2012163-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later in 1945 María Eva Duarte starred in
       <i>
        La Pródiga
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        The Prodigal
       </i>
       ). On 3 August 1946 Machinandiarena signed over all rights to this film to the president's wife.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGutierrez_Pechemiel200569_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGutierrez_Pechemiel200569-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1945 the studio produced
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_dama_duende" title="La dama duende">
         La dama duende
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        The Phantom Lady
       </i>
       ), produced by Machinandiarena and directed by
       <a href="/wiki/Luis_Saslavsky" title="Luis Saslavsky">
        Luis Saslavsky
       </a>
       , based on a 17th-century cloak-and-dagger comedy by
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Pedro Calderón de la Barca
       </a>
       . The director and the leading lady,
       <a href="/wiki/Delia_Garc%C3%A9s" title="Delia Garcés">
        Delia Garcés
       </a>
       , were Argentine. Otherwise the film was made by Spanish Republican refugees, including the script, decor, cinematography and principal actors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarnardRist201322_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarnardRist201322-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
