          H-alpha Emission
         </b>
         : In the simplified
         <b>

         <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">
          hydrogen atom
         </a>
         , the Balmer lines result from an electron jump between the second energy level closest to the nucleus, and those levels more distant. The

      <p>
       <b>
        H-alpha
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        Hα
       </b>
       ) is a specific deep-red visible
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
        spectral line
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Balmer_series" title="Balmer series">
        Balmer series
       </a>
       with a wavelength of 656.28
       <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">
        nm
       </a>
       ; it occurs when a
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       electron falls from its third to second lowest energy level. H-alpha light is important to
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomers
       </a>
       as it is emitted by many
       <a href="/wiki/Emission_nebula" title="Emission nebula">
        emission nebulae
       </a>
       and can be used to observe features in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Sun#Atmosphere" title="Sun">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
        solar prominences
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Balmer_series">
        Balmer series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=H-alpha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Balmer series">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to the
       <a href="/wiki/Bohr_model" title="Bohr model">
        Bohr model
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
        atom
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrons" title="Electrons">
        electrons
       </a>
       exist in
       <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">
        quantized
       </a>
       energy levels surrounding the atom's
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">
        nucleus
       </a>
       . These energy levels are described by the
       <a href="/wiki/Principal_quantum_number" title="Principal quantum number">
        principal quantum number
       </a>
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       = 1, 2, 3, ... . Electrons may only exist in these states, and may only transit between these states.
      </p>
      <p>
       The set of transitions from
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       ≥ 3 to
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       = 2 is called the
       <a href="/wiki/Balmer_series" title="Balmer series">
        Balmer series
       </a>
       and its members are named sequentially by Greek letters:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        = 3 to
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        = 2 is called Balmer-alpha or H-alpha,
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        = 4 to
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        = 2 is called Balmer-beta or H-beta,
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        = 5 to
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        = 2 is called Balmer-gamma or H-gamma, etc.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For the
       <a href="/wiki/Lyman_series" title="Lyman series">
        Lyman series
       </a>
       the naming convention is:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        = 2 to
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        = 1 is called Lyman-alpha,
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        = 3 to
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        = 1 is called Lyman-beta, etc.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <b>
        H-alpha
       </b>
       has a
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelength
       </a>
       of 656.281
       <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">
        nm
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cox2000_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cox2000-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is visible in the red part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        electromagnetic spectrum
       </a>
       , and is the easiest way for astronomers to trace the ionized hydrogen content of gas clouds. Since it takes nearly as much
       <a href="/wiki/Rydberg_constant#Rydberg_constant_for_hydrogen" title="Rydberg constant">
        energy to excite the hydrogen
       </a>
       atom's electron from
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       = 1 to
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       = 3 (12.1 eV, via the Rydberg formula) as it does to ionize the hydrogen atom (13.6 eV), ionization is far more probable than excitation to the
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       = 3 level. After ionization, the electron and proton recombine to form a new hydrogen atom. In the new atom, the electron may begin in any energy level, and subsequently cascades to the ground state (
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       = 1), emitting
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">
        photons
       </a>
       with each transition. Approximately half the time, this cascade will include the
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       = 3 to
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       = 2 transition and the atom will emit H-alpha light. Therefore, the H-alpha line occurs where hydrogen is being ionized.
      </p>
      <p>
       The H-alpha line saturates (self-absorbs) relatively easily because hydrogen is the primary component of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nebulae" title="Nebulae">
        nebulae
       </a>
       , so while it can indicate the shape and extent of the cloud, it cannot be used to accurately determine the cloud's mass. Instead, molecules such as
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
        carbon dioxide
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
        carbon monoxide
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">
        formaldehyde
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Acetonitrile" title="Acetonitrile">
        acetonitrile
       </a>
       are typically used to determine the mass of a cloud.
      </p>

      <p>
       A hydrogen-alpha filter is an
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_filter" title="Optical filter">
        optical filter
       </a>
       designed to transmit a narrow
       <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">
        bandwidth
       </a>
       of light generally centred on the H-alpha wavelength. They are characterized by a bandpass width that measures the width of the wavelength band that is transmitted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These filters can be
       <a href="/wiki/Dichroic_filter" title="Dichroic filter">
        dichroic filters
       </a>
       manufactured by multiple (~50) vacuum-deposited layers. These layers are selected to produce
       <a href="/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation)" title="Interference (wave propagation)">
        interference
       </a>
       effects that filter out any wavelengths except at the requisite band.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Taken in isolation, H-alpha dichroic filters are useful in
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophotography" title="Astrophotography">
        astrophotography
       </a>
       and for reducing the effects of
       <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">
        light pollution
       </a>
       . They do not have narrow enough bandwidth for observing the sun's atmosphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       For observing the sun, a much narrower band filter can be made from three parts: an "energy rejection filter" which is usually a piece of red glass that absorbs most of the unwanted wavelengths, a
       <a href="/wiki/Fabry%E2%80%93P%C3%A9rot_interferometer" title="Fabry–Pérot interferometer">
        Fabry–Pérot etalon
       </a>
       which transmits several wavelengths including one centred on the H-alpha emission line, and a "blocking filter" -a dichroic filter which transmits the H-alpha line while stopping those other wavelengths that passed through the etalon. This combination will pass only a narrow (&lt;0.1
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">
        nm
       </a>
       ) range of wavelengths of light centred on the H-alpha emission line.
      </p>
      <p>
       The physics of the etalon and the dichroic interference filters are essentially the same (relying on constructive/destructive interference of light reflecting between surfaces), but the implementation is different (a dichroic interference filter relies on the interference of internal reflections while the etalon has a relatively large air gap). Due to the high velocities sometimes associated with features visible in H-alpha light (such as fast moving prominences and ejections), solar H-alpha etalons can often be tuned (by tilting or changing the temperature) to cope with the associated
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">
        Doppler effect
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Commercially available H-alpha filters for amateur solar observing usually state bandwidths in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angstrom" title="Angstrom">
        Angstrom units
       </a>
       and are typically 0.7Å (0.07 nm). By using a second etalon, this can be reduced to 0.5Å leading to improved contrast in details observed on the sun's disc.
      </p>
      <p>
       An even more narrow band filter can be made using a
       <a href="/wiki/Lyot_filter" title="Lyot filter">
        Lyot filter
       </a>
       .
      </p>
