   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Solar phenomena
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Solar phenomena
       </b>
       are the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_phenomena" title="Natural phenomena">
        natural phenomena
       </a>
       occurring within the magnetically heated outer atmospheres in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       . These phenomena take many forms, including
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       , radio wave flux, energy bursts such as
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejection
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_eruption" title="Solar eruption">
        solar eruptions
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heliophysics_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-heliophysics-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       coronal heating and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">
        sunspots
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       These phenomena are generated by a helical
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_dynamo" title="Solar dynamo">
        dynamo
       </a>
       near the center of the Sun's mass that generates strong magnetic fields and a chaotic dynamo near the surface that generates smaller magnetic field fluctuations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The total sum of all solar fluctuations is referred to as solar variation. The collective effect of all solar variations within the Sun's gravitational field is referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">
        space weather
       </a>
       . A major weather component is the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       , a stream of
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       released from the Sun's upper atmosphere. It is responsible for the
       <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">
        aurora
       </a>
       , natural light displays in the sky in the
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">
        Arctic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       . Space weather disturbances can cause
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_solar_storms" title="List of solar storms">
        solar storms
       </a>
       on Earth, disrupting communications, as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        geomagnetic storms
       </a>
       in Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sudden_ionospheric_disturbance" title="Sudden ionospheric disturbance">
        sudden ionospheric disturbances
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
        ionosphere
       </a>
       . Variations in solar intensity also affect Earth's climate. These variations can explain events such as ice ages and the
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">
        Great Oxygenation Event
       </a>
       , while the Sun's future expansion into a
       <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">
        red giant
       </a>
       will likely end life on Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       Solar activity and related events have been recorded since the 8th century BCE.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonians" title="Babylonians">
        Babylonians
       </a>
       inscribed and possibly predicted
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_eclipses" title="Solar eclipses">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       , while the earliest extant report of sunspots dates back to the Chinese
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Changes" title="Book of Changes">
         Book of Changes
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <abbr title="circa">
        c.
       </abbr>
       <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
        800 BCE
       </span>
       . The first extant description of the solar corona was in 968, while the earliest sunspot drawing was in 1128 and a solar prominence was described in 1185 in the
       <a href="/wiki/Novgorod_First_Chronicle" title="Novgorod First Chronicle">
        Russian Chronicle of Novgorod
       </a>
       . The invention of the
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       allowed major advances in understanding, allowing the first detailed observations in the 1600s. Solar
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectroscopy
       </a>
       began in the 1800s, from which properties of the solar atmosphere could be determined, while the creation of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daguerreotypy" title="Daguerreotypy">
        daguerreotypy
       </a>
       led to the first solar photographs on 2 April 1845. Photography assisted in the study of solar prominences, granulation and spectroscopy. Early in the 20th century, interest in
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
        astrophysics
       </a>
       surged in America. A number of new observatories were built with solar telescopes around the world. The 1931 invention of the
       <a href="/wiki/Coronagraph" title="Coronagraph">
        coronagraph
       </a>
       allowed the corona to be studied in full daylight.
      </p>

      </p>

          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Sun_by_the_Atmospheric_Imaging_Assembly_of_NASA%27s_Solar_Dynamics_Observatory_-_20100819.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/False_color" title="False color">
          False-color image
         </a>
         of the Sun showing its turbulent surface. (credit:
         <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
          NASA
         </a>
         -
         <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
          SDO
         </a>
         )
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Sun is a
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       located at the center of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       . It is almost perfectly spherical and consists of hot
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic fields
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has a diameter of about 1,392,684 kilometres (865,374 mi),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arxiv1203_4898_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-arxiv1203_4898-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       around 109 times that of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , and its mass (1.989
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         30
        </span>
       </sup>
       kilograms, approximately 330,000 times that of Earth) accounts for some 99.86% of the total mass of the Solar System.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woolfson00_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Woolfson00-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Chemically, about three quarters of the Sun's mass consists of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       , while the rest is mostly
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       . The remaining 1.69% (equal to 5,600 times the mass of Earth) consists of heavier elements, including
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">
        neon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
        iron
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-basu2008_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-basu2008-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Sun formed about 4.567 billion
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-short_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-short-8">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Connelly2012_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Connelly2012-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       years ago from the gravitational collapse of a region within a large
       <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">
        molecular cloud
       </a>
       . Most of the matter gathered in the center, while the rest flattened into an orbiting disk that became
       <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">
        the balance of the Solar System
       </a>
       . The central mass became increasingly hot and dense, eventually initiating
       <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_fusion" title="Thermonuclear fusion">
        thermonuclear fusion
       </a>
       in its core.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Sun is a
       <a href="/wiki/G-type_main-sequence_star" title="G-type main-sequence star">
        G-type main-sequence star
       </a>
       (G2V) based on
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">
        spectral class
       </a>
       and it is informally designated as a
       <i>
        yellow dwarf
       </i>
       because its visible
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       is most intense in the yellow-green portion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       . It is actually white, but from the Earth's surface it appears yellow because of
       <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_sky_radiation" title="Diffuse sky radiation">
        atmospheric scattering
       </a>
       of blue light.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the spectral class label,
       <i>
        G2
       </i>
       indicates its
       <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">
        surface temperature
       </a>
       , of approximately 5778 K (5,505 °C (9,941 °F)) and
       <i>
        V
       </i>
       indicates that the Sun, like most stars, is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main-sequence" title="Main-sequence">
        main-sequence
       </a>
       star, and thus generates its energy via fusing hydrogen into helium. In its core, the Sun fuses about 620 million metric tons of hydrogen each second.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips1995-47_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Phillips1995-47-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Earth's mean distance from the Sun is approximately 1
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical unit
       </a>
       (about 150,000,000 km; 93,000,000 mi), though the distance varies as the Earth moves from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">
        perihelion
       </a>
       in January to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">
        aphelion
       </a>
       in July.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNO_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-USNO-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At this average distance,
       <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
        light
       </a>
       travels from the Sun to Earth in about 8 minutes, 19 seconds. The
       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
        energy
       </a>
       of this
       <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
        sunlight
       </a>
       supports almost all life
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
       on Earth by
       <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">
        photosynthesis
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simon2001_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Simon2001-15">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and drives Earth's climate and weather.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Portman1952_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Portman1952-16">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As recently as the 19th century scientists had little knowledge of the Sun's physical composition and source of energy. This understanding is still developing; a number of
       <a href="/wiki/Sun#Theoretical_problems" title="Sun">
        present-day anomalies
       </a>
       in the Sun's behavior remain unexplained. Sun.§
      </p>

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        Solar cycle
       </a>
      </div>

      <p>
       Many solar phenomena change periodically over an average interval of about 11 years. This solar cycle affects
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_irradiation" title="Solar irradiation">
        solar irradiation
       </a>
       and influences space weather, terrestrial
       <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">
        weather
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
        climate
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The solar cycle also modulates the flux of short-wavelength solar radiation, from
       <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
        ultraviolet
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-ray
       </a>
       and influences the frequency of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       and other solar eruptive phenomena.
      </p>

      <p>
       A coronal mass ejection (CME) is a massive burst of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       and magnetic fields rising above the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Near
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">
        solar maxima
       </a>
       , the Sun produces about three CMEs every day, whereas
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">
        solar minima
       </a>
       feature about one every five days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fox-18">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       CMEs, along with solar flares of other origin, can disrupt
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_transmission" title="Radio transmission">
        radio transmissions
       </a>
       and damage
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellites" title="Satellites">
        satellites
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_transmission_line" title="Electrical transmission line">
        electrical transmission line
       </a>
       facilities, resulting in potentially massive and long-lasting
       <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">
        power outages
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Coronal mass ejections often appear with other forms of solar activity, most notably
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       , but no causal relationship has been established. Most weak flares do not have CMEs; most powerful ones do. Most ejections originate from active regions on the Sun's surface, such as sunspot groupings associated with frequent flares. Other forms of solar activity frequently associcated with coronal mass ejections are eruptive prominences, coronal dimming, coronal waves and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moreton_waves" title="Moreton waves">
        Moreton waves
       </a>
       , also called solar tsunami.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        Magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       is responsible for CME and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">
        solar flares
       </a>
       . Magnetic reconnection is the name given to the rearrangement of magnetic field lines when two oppositely directed magnetic fields are brought together. This rearrangement is accompanied with a sudden release of energy stored in the original oppositely directed fields.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When a CME impacts the Earth's magnetosphere, it temporarily deforms the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       , changing the direction of compass needles and inducing large electrical ground currents in Earth itself; this is called a
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        geomagnetic storm
       </a>
       and it is a global phenomenon. CME impacts can induce
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       in Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere#Magnetic_tails" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetotail
       </a>
       (the midnight side of the magnetosphere); this launches protons and electrons downward toward Earth's atmosphere, where they form the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(phenomenon)" title="Aurora (phenomenon)">
        aurora
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diameter">
        Diameter
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diameter">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Data mostly from the Michelson Doppler Imager instrument on
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO
       </a>
       , show changes in solar diameter to be about 0.001%, much less than the effect of magnetic activity changes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Flares">
        Flares
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flares">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        Solar flare
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A solar flare is a sudden flash of brightness observed over the Sun's surface or the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_limb" title="Solar limb">
        solar limb
       </a>
       , which is interpreted as an energy release of up to 6 × 10
       <sup>
        25
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">
        joules
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
        energy
       </a>
       (about a sixth of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_E24_J" title="1 E24 J">
        total Sun's energy output each second
       </a>
       or 160 billion megatons of
       <a href="/wiki/TNT" title="TNT">
        TNT
       </a>
       equivalent, over 25,000 times more energy than released from the impact of
       <a href="/wiki/Comet_Shoemaker%E2%80%93Levy_9" title="Comet Shoemaker–Levy 9">
        Comet Shoemaker–Levy 9
       </a>
       with Jupiter). They may be followed by a
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejection
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp2005_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kopp2005-24">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The flare ejects clouds of electrons, ions and atoms through the corona into space. These clouds typically reach
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       a day or two after the event.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Similar phenomena in other stars are known as stellar flares.
      </p>
      <p>
       Solar flares strongly influence space weather near the Earth. They can produce streams of highly energetic particles in the solar wind, known as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_proton_event" title="Solar proton event">
        solar proton event
       </a>
       . These particles can impact the Earth's magnetosphere in the form of a
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        geomagnetic storm
       </a>
       and present
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       hazards to spacecraft and astronauts.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Irradiance">
        Irradiance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Irradiance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">
        Solar irradiance
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Irradiance is the power per unit area produced by the Sun in the form of electromagnetic radiation. Irradiance may be measured in space or at the Earth's surface after atmospheric absorption and scattering. Total solar irradiance (TSI), is a measure of the solar radiative power per unit area normal to the rays, incident on the Earth's upper atmosphere. The
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">
        solar constant
       </a>
       is a conventional measure of mean TSI at a distance of one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_Unit" title="Astronomical Unit">
        Astronomical Unit
       </a>
       (AU).
      </p>
      <p>
       Irradiance is a function of distance from the Sun, the solar cycle, and cross-cycle changes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boxwell_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Boxwell-26">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Irradiance on Earth is most intense at points directly facing (normal to) the Sun.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proton_event">
        Proton event
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Proton event">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_proton_event" title="Solar proton event">
        Solar proton event
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="1200" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/220px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/330px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/440px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Solar particles interact with Earth's
         <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
          magnetosphere
         </a>
         . Sizes not to scale.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A solar proton event (SPE), or "proton storm", occurs when particles (mostly protons) emitted by the Sun become accelerated either close to the Sun during a flare or in interplanetary space by CME shocks. The events can include other nuclei such as helium ions and
       <a href="/wiki/HZE_ions" title="HZE ions">
        HZE ions
       </a>
       . These particles cause multiple effects. They can penetrate the Earth's magnetic field and cause
       <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">
        ionization
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
        ionosphere
       </a>
       . The effect is similar to auroral events, except that protons rather than electrons are involved. Energetic protons are a significant radiation hazard to spacecraft and astronauts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA/TP-1999-209320_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA/TP-1999-209320-27">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Energetic
       <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">
        protons
       </a>
       can reach Earth within 30 minutes of a major flare's peak.
      </p>

        Prominences and filaments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

      <p>
       A prominence is a large, bright, gaseous feature extending outward from the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's surface, often in the shape of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_loops" title="Coronal loops">
        loop
       </a>
       . Prominences are anchored to the Sun's surface in the photosphere and extend outwards into the corona. While the corona consists of high temperature
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       , which do not emit much
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">
        visible light
       </a>
       , prominences contain much cooler plasma, similar in composition to that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Prominence plasma is typically a hundred times cooler and denser than coronal plasma. A prominence forms over timescales of about an earthly day and may persist for weeks or months. Some prominences break apart and from CMEs.
      </p>
      <p>
       A typical prominence extends over many thousands of kilometers; the largest on record was estimated at over 800,000 kilometres (500,000 mi) long
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-univtoday_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-univtoday-28">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       – roughly the solar radius.
      </p>
      <p>
       When a prominence is viewed against the Sun instead of space, it appears darker than the background. This formation is called a solar filament.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-univtoday_28-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-univtoday-28">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is possible for a projection to be both a filament and a prominence. Some prominences are so powerful that they eject matter at speeds ranging from 600 km/s to more than 1000 km/s. Other prominences form huge loops or arching columns of glowing gases over sunspots that can reach heights of hundreds of thousands of kilometers. Prominences may last for days or months.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sunspots">
        Sunspots
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sunspots">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">
        Sunspots
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        Sunspots
       </a>
       are relatively dark areas on the Sun's radiating 'surface' (
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       ) where intense magnetic activity inhibits convection and cools the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        Photosphere
       </a>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faculae" title="Faculae">
        Faculae
       </a>
       are slightly brighter areas that form around sunspot groups as the flow of energy to the photosphere is re-established and both the normal flow and the sunspot-blocked energy elevate the radiating 'surface' temperature. Scientists began speculating on possible relationships between sunspots and solar luminosity in the 17th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Luminosity decreases caused by sunspots (generally &lt; - 0.3%) are correlated with increases (generally &lt; + 0.05%) caused both by faculae that are associated with active regions as well as the magnetically active 'bright network'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willson812_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Willson812-32">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The net effect during periods of enhanced solar magnetic activity is increased radiant solar output because faculae are larger and persist longer than sunspots. Conversely, periods of lower solar magnetic activity and fewer sunspots (such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Maunder_Minimum" title="Maunder Minimum">
        Maunder Minimum
       </a>
       ) may correlate with times of lower irradiance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sunspot activity has been measured using the
       <a href="/wiki/Wolf_number" title="Wolf number">
        Wolf number
       </a>
       for about 300 years. This index (also known as the Zürich number) uses both the number of sunspots and the number of sunspot groups to compensate for measurement variations. A 2003 study found that sunspots had been more frequent since the 1940s than in the previous 1150 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sunspots usually appear as pairs with opposite magnetic polarity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Detailed observations reveal patterns, in yearly minima and maxima and in relative location. As each cycle proceeds, the latitude of spots declines, from 30-45° to around 7° after the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">
        solar maximum
       </a>
       . This latitudinal change follows
       <a href="/wiki/Sp%C3%B6rer%27s_law" title="Spörer's law">
        Spörer's law
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For a sunspot to be visible to the human eye it must be about 50,000 km in diameter, covering 2,000,000,000 square kilometres (770,000,000 sq mi) or 700 millionths of the visible area. Over recent cycles, approximately 100 sunspots or compact sunspot groups are visible from Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [c]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_Naked_Eye_36-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-BOM_Naked_Eye-36">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sunspots expand and contract as they move about and can travel at a few hundred meters per second when they first appear.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Wind">
        Wind
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wind">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        Solar wind
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Structure_of_the_magnetosphere-en.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="393" data-file-width="512" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Structure_of_the_magnetosphere-en.svg/330px-Structure_of_the_magnetosphere-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Structure_of_the_magnetosphere-en.svg/495px-Structure_of_the_magnetosphere-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Structure_of_the_magnetosphere-en.svg/660px-Structure_of_the_magnetosphere-en.svg.png 2x" width="330"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Structure_of_the_magnetosphere-en.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Schematic of
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         's magnetosphere. The solar wind flows from left to right.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Animati3.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="510" data-file-width="560" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Animati3.gif/220px-Animati3.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Animati3.gif/330px-Animati3.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Animati3.gif/440px-Animati3.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Animati3.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Simulation of Earth's magnetic field in interaction with (solar) interplanetar magnetic field that illustrates the dynamical changes of the global magnetic field in the course of a disturbance: a temporary compression of the magnetosphere by enhanced flow of the
         <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
          solar wind
         </a>
         is followed by a tailward stretching of the field lines.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The solar wind is a stream of plasma released from the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_atmosphere" title="Stellar atmosphere">
        upper atmosphere
       </a>
       . It consists of mostly
       <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">
        electrons
       </a>
       and protons with energies usually between 1.5 and 10
       <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">
        keV
       </a>
       . The stream of particles varies in density, temperature and speed over time and over solar longitude. These particles can escape the Sun's gravity because of their high energy.
      </p>
      <p>
       The solar wind is divided into the slow solar wind and the fast solar wind. The slow solar wind has a velocity of about 400 kilometres per second (250 mi/s), a temperature of 1.4–1.6
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         6
        </span>
       </sup>
       K and a composition that is a close match to the corona. The fast solar wind has a typical velocity of 750 km/s, a temperature of 8
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         5
        </span>
       </sup>
       K and nearly matches the photosphere's.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The slow solar wind is twice as dense and more variable in intensity than the fast solar wind. The slow wind has a more complex structure, with turbulent regions and large-scale organization.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kallenrode_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kallenrode-39">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Both the fast and slow solar wind can be interrupted by large, fast-moving bursts of plasma called interplanetary CMEs, or ICMEs. They cause shock waves in the thin plasma of the
       <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">
        heliosphere
       </a>
       , generating electromagnetic waves and accelerating particles (mostly protons and electrons) to form showers of
       <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">
        ionizing radiation
       </a>
       that precede the CME.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">
        Effects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Effects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Space_weather">
        Space weather
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Space weather">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">
        Space weather
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1173" data-file-width="1782" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg/250px-Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg/375px-Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg/500px-Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         An example of space weather:
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">
          Aurora australis
         </a>
         in the
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         's
         <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
          atmosphere
         </a>
         observed by
         <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery">
          Space Shuttle
          <i>
           Discovery
          </i>
         </a>
         , May 1991
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Space weather is the environmental condition within the Solar System, including the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       . It is studied especially surrounding the Earth, including conditions from the magnetosphere to the ionosphere and
       <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">
        thermosphere
       </a>
       . Space weather is distinct from terrestrial
       <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">
        weather
       </a>
       of the (
       <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">
        troposphere
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">
        stratosphere
       </a>
       ). The term was not used until the 1990s. Prior to that time, such phenomena were considered to be part of physics or
       <a href="/wiki/Aeronomy" title="Aeronomy">
        aeronomy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_storms">
        Solar storms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Solar storms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_solar_storms" title="List of solar storms">
        List of solar storms
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Solar storms are caused by disturbances on the Sun, most often
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_cloud" title="Coronal cloud">
        coronal clouds
       </a>
       associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flare
       </a>
       CMEs emanating from active sunspot regions, or less often from
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_hole" title="Coronal hole">
        coronal holes
       </a>
       . The Sun can produce intense
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        geomagnetic
       </a>
       and proton storms capable of causing
       <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">
        power outages
       </a>
       , disruption or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communications_blackouts" title="Communications blackouts">
        communications blackouts
       </a>
       (including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">
        GPS
       </a>
       systems) and temporary/permanent disabling of satellites and other spaceborne technology. Solar storms may be hazardous to high-latitude, high-altitude aviation and to
       <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">
        human spaceflight
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Geomagnetic storms cause aurorae.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The most significant known solar storm occurred in September 1859 and is known as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrington_event" title="Carrington event">
        Carrington event
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aurora">
        Aurora
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Aurora">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(phenomenon)" title="Aurora (phenomenon)">
        Aurora (phenomenon)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       An aurora is a natural light display in the sky, especially in the high latitude (
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">
        Arctic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       ) regions, in the form of that forms a large circle around the pole. They are caused by the collision of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       and charged magnetospheric particles with the high altitude atmosphere (
       <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">
        thermosphere
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Most auroras occur in a band known as the
       <i>
        auroral zone
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feldstein63_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-feldstein63-45">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feldstein86_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-feldstein86-46">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which is typically 3° to 6° wide in latitude and observed at 10° to 20° from the
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_pole" title="Geomagnetic pole">
        geomagnetic poles
       </a>
       at all longitudes, but often most vividly around the spring and autumn
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinoxes
       </a>
       . The charged particles and solar wind are directed into the atmosphere by the Earth's magnetosphere. A geomagnetic storm expands the auroral zone to lower latitudes.
      </p>
      <p>
       Auroras are associated with the solar wind. The Earth's magnetic field traps its particles, many of which travel toward the poles where they are accelerated toward Earth. Collisions between these ions and the atmosphere release energy in the form of auroras appearing in large circles around the poles. Auroras are more frequent and brighter during the solar cycle's intense phase when CMEs increase the intensity of the solar wind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geomagnetic_storm">
        Geomagnetic storm
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Geomagnetic storm">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        Geomagnetic storm
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A geomagnetic storm is a temporary disturbance of the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        Magnetosphere
       </a>
       caused by a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       shock wave and/or cloud of magnetic field that interacts with the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">
        Earth's magnetic field
       </a>
       . The increase in solar wind pressure compresses the magnetosphere and the solar wind's magnetic field interacts with the Earth’s magnetic field to transfer increased energy into the magnetosphere. Both interactions increase plasma movement through the magnetosphere (driven by increased electric fields) and an increase in electric current in the magnetosphere and ionosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The disturbance in the interplanetary medium that drives a storm may be due to a CME or a high speed stream (co-rotating interaction region or CIR)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of the solar wind originating from a region of weak magnetic field on the solar surface. The frequency of geomagnetic storms increases and decreases with the
       <a href="/wiki/Wolf_number" title="Wolf number">
        sunspot
       </a>
       cycle. CME driven storms are more common during the solar maximum of the solar cycle, while CIR-driven storms are more common during the solar minimum.
      </p>
      <p>
       Several space weather phenomena are associated with geomagnetic storms. These include Solar Energetic Particle (SEP) events,
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetically_induced_current" title="Geomagnetically induced current">
        geomagnetically induced currents
       </a>
       (GIC), ionospheric disturbances that cause radio and radar
       <a href="/wiki/Twinkling" title="Twinkling">
        scintillation
       </a>
       , disruption of compass navigation and auroral displays at much lower latitudes than normal. A
       <a href="/wiki/March_1989_geomagnetic_storm" title="March 1989 geomagnetic storm">
        1989 geomagnetic storm
       </a>
       energized
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetically_induced_current" title="Geomagnetically induced current">
        ground induced currents
       </a>
       that disrupted electric power distribution throughout most of the province of
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
        Quebec
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc.ca_50-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cbc.ca-50">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and caused aurorae as far south as
       <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
        Texas
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earth_dodges_magnetic_storm_51-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Earth_dodges_magnetic_storm-51">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sudden_ionospheric_disturbance">
        Sudden ionospheric disturbance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sudden ionospheric disturbance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Sudden_ionospheric_disturbance" title="Sudden ionospheric disturbance">
        Sudden ionospheric disturbance
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A sudden ionospheric disturbance (SID) is an abnormally high ionization/plasma density in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D_region" title="D region">
        D region
       </a>
       of the ionosphere caused by a solar flare. The SID results in a sudden increase in radio-wave absorption that is most severe in the upper
       <a href="/wiki/Medium_frequency" title="Medium frequency">
        medium frequency
       </a>
       (MF) and lower
       <a href="/wiki/High_frequency" title="High frequency">
        high frequency
       </a>
       (HF) ranges, and as a result often interrupts or interferes with
       <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">
        telecommunications
       </a>
       systems.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geomagnetically_induced_currents">
        Geomagnetically induced currents
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Geomagnetically induced currents">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geomagnetically_induced_currents" title="Geomagnetically induced currents">
        Geomagnetically induced currents
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Geomagnetically induced currents are a manifestation at ground level of space weather, which affect the normal operation of long electrical conductor systems. During space weather events, electric currents in the magnetosphere and ionosphere experience large variations, which manifest also in the Earth's magnetic field. These variations
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_induction" title="Electric induction">
        induce
       </a>
       currents (GIC) in earthly conductors.
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">
        Electric transmission grids
       </a>
       and buried
       <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">
        pipelines
       </a>
       are common examples of such conductor systems. GIC can cause problems such as increased
       <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">
        corrosion
       </a>
       of pipeline steel and damaged high-voltage power transformers.
      </p>

          </a>
         </div>
         Sunspot record (blue) with
         <sup>
          14
         </sup>
         C (inverted).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The production of
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">
        carbon-14
       </a>
       (radiocarbon:
       <sup>
        14
       </sup>
       C) is related to solar activity. Carbon-14 is produced in the upper atmosphere when cosmic ray bombardment of atmospheric nitrogen (
       <sup>
        14
       </sup>
       N) induces the Nitrogen to undergo
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%92%2B_decay" title="Β+ decay">
        β+ decay
       </a>
       , thus transforming into an unusual isotope of carbon with an atomic weight of 14 rather than the more common 12. Because galactic cosmic rays are partially excluded from the Solar System by the outward sweep of magnetic fields in the solar wind, increased solar activity reduces
       <sup>
        14
       </sup>
       C production.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
        <a href="#cite_note-53">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Atmospheric
       <sup>
        14
       </sup>
       C concentration is lower during solar maxima and higher during solar minima. By measuring the captured
       <sup>
        14
       </sup>
       C in wood and counting tree rings, production of radiocarbon relative to recent wood can be measured and dated. A reconstruction of the past 10,000 years shows that the
       <sup>
        14
       </sup>
       C production was much higher during the mid-
       <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">
        Holocene
       </a>
       7,000 years ago and decreased until 1,000 years ago. In addition to variations in solar activity, long term trends in carbon-14 production are influenced by changes in the Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_field" title="Geomagnetic field">
        geomagnetic field
       </a>
       and by changes in carbon cycling within the
       <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">
        biosphere
       </a>
       (particularly those associated with changes in the extent of vegetation between
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
        ice ages
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">
        Climate
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Climate">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_activity_and_climate" title="Solar activity and climate">
        Solar activity and climate
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       While solar activity has been a main driver of climate change over geologic time, its role in the warming that began in the twentieth century does not appear to have been significant.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
        <a href="#cite_note-54">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_history">
        Observation history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_phenomena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Observation history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_observation" title="Solar observation">
        Solar observation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Solar activity and related events have been regularly recorded since the time of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonians" title="Babylonians">
        Babylonians
       </a>
       . Early records described solar eclipses, the corona and sunspots.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Athanasius_Kircher_sunspots.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="799" data-file-width="616" height="376" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Athanasius_Kircher_sunspots.jpg/290px-Athanasius_Kircher_sunspots.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Athanasius_Kircher_sunspots.jpg/435px-Athanasius_Kircher_sunspots.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Athanasius_Kircher_sunspots.jpg/580px-Athanasius_Kircher_sunspots.jpg 2x" width="290"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Athanasius_Kircher_sunspots.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Illustration of sunspots drawn by 17th-century German Jesuit scholar
         <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_Kircher" title="Athanasius Kircher">
          Athanasius Kircher
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Soon after the invention of telescopes, in the early 1600s, astronomers began observing the sun.
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Harriot" title="Thomas Harriot">
        Thomas Harriot
       </a>
       was the first to observe sunspots, in 1610. Observers confirmed the less-frequent sunspots and aurorae during the Maunder minimum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_0_55-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_0-55">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Solar spectrometry began in 1817.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_1800_56-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_1800-56">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Wolf" title="Rudolf Wolf">
        Rudolf Wolf
       </a>
       gathered sunspot observations as far back as the 1755–1766 cycle. He established a relative sunspot number formulation (the
       <a href="/wiki/Wolf_number" title="Wolf number">
        Wolf or Zürich sunspot number
       </a>
       ) that became the standard measure. Around 1852, Sabine, Wolf, Gautier and von Lamont independently found a link between the solar cycle and geomagnetic activity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_1800_56-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_1800-56">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 2 April 1845,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Fizeau" title="Louis Fizeau">
        Fizeau
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Foucault" title="Léon Foucault">
        Foucault
       </a>
       first photographed the Sun. Photography assisted in the study of solar prominences,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_granulation" title="Solar granulation">
        granulation
       </a>
       , spectroscopy and solar eclipses.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_1800_56-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_1800-56">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 1 September 1859, Richard C. Carrington and separately R. Hodgson first observed a solar flare.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_1800_56-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_1800-56">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Carrington and
       <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Sp%C3%B6rer" title="Gustav Spörer">
        Gustav Spörer
       </a>
       discovered that the sun rotates at different rates at different latitudes, and that the outer layer must be fluid.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_1800_56-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_1800-56">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1907–08,
       <a href="/wiki/George_Ellery_Hale" title="George Ellery Hale">
        George Ellery Hale
       </a>
       uncovered the sun's magnetic cycle and the magnetic nature of sunspots. Hale and his colleagues later deduced Hale's polarity laws that described its magnetic field.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_1800_56-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_1800-56">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Bernard Lyot's 1931 invention of the
       <a href="/wiki/Coronagraph" title="Coronagraph">
        coronagraph
       </a>
       allowed the corona to be studied in full daylight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_1800_56-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_1800-56">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Sun was, until the 1990s, the only star whose surface had been resolved.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BURNS_57-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BURNS-57">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other major achievements included understanding of:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ground-based_Solar_Research_58-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ground-based_Solar_Research-58">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        X-ray-emitting loops (
        <i>
         e.g.
        </i>
        , by
        <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">
         Yohkoh
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Corona and solar wind (
        <i>
         e.g.
        </i>
        , by
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
         SoHO
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Variance of solar brightness with level of activity, and verification of this effect in other solar-type stars (
        <i>
         e.g.
        </i>
        , by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ACRIM" title="ACRIM">
         ACRIM
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        The intense
        <a href="/wiki/Fibril" title="Fibril">
         fibril
        </a>
        state of the magnetic fields at the visible surface of a star like the sun (
        <i>
         e.g.
        </i>
        , by
        <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">
         Hinode
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        The presence of magnetic fields of 0.5×10
        <sup>
         5
        </sup>
        to 1×10
        <sup>
         5
        </sup>
        gauss at the base of the conductive zone, presumably in some fibril form, inferred from the dynamics of rising azimuthal flux bundles.
       </li>
       <li>
        Low-level
        <a href="/wiki/Electron_neutrino" title="Electron neutrino">
         electron neutrino
        </a>
        emission from the Sun's core.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ground-based_Solar_Research_58-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Ground-based_Solar_Research-58">
          [55]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the later twentieth century, satellites began observing the Sun, providing many insights. For example, modulation of solar luminosity by magnetically active regions was confirmed by satellite measurements of total solar irradiance (TSI) by the ACRIM1 experiment on the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Maximum_Mission" title="Solar Maximum Mission">
        Solar Maximum Mission
       </a>
       (launched in 1980).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willson812_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Willson812-32">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
