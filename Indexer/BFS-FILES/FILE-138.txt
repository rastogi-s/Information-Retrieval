      <p>
       A total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       occurred on May 29, 1919. With the duration of totality at maximum eclipse (close to but slightly shorter than the longest duration of totality) of 6 minutes 51 seconds, it was the longest solar eclipses since May 27, 1416. A longer total solar eclipse occurred on
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_June_8,_1937" title="Solar eclipse of June 8, 1937">
        June 8, 1937
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was visible throughout most of
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South America
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       as a partial eclipse. Totality occurred through a narrow path across southeastern
       <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">
        Peru
       </a>
       , northern
       <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
        Chile
       </a>
       , central
       <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">
        Bolivia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       after sunrise, across the Atlantic Ocean and into south central Africa, covering southern
       <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">
        Liberia
       </a>
       , southern
       <a href="/wiki/French_West_Africa" title="French West Africa">
        French West Africa
       </a>
       (the part now belonging to
       <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">
        Ivory Coast
       </a>
       ), southwestern tip of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Gold_Coast" title="British Gold Coast">
        British Gold Coast
       </a>
       (now
       <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">
        Ghana
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="Príncipe">
        Príncipe
       </a>
       Island in
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="Portuguese São Tomé and Príncipe">
        Portuguese São Tomé and Príncipe
       </a>
       , southern
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Guinea" title="Spanish Guinea">
        Spanish Guinea
       </a>
       (now
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">
        Equatorial Guinea
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/French_Equatorial_Africa" title="French Equatorial Africa">
        French Equatorial Africa
       </a>
       (the parts now belonging to
       <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">
        Gabon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">
        R. Congo
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Libreville" title="Libreville">
        Libreville
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Congo" title="Belgian Congo">
        Belgian Congo
       </a>
       (now
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DR_Congo" title="DR Congo">
        DR Congo
       </a>
       ), northeastern
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Rhodesia" title="Northern Rhodesia">
        Northern Rhodesia
       </a>
       (now
       <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">
        Zambia
       </a>
       ), northern tip of
       <a href="/wiki/Nyasaland" title="Nyasaland">
        Nyasaland
       </a>
       (now
       <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">
        Malawi
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/German_East_Africa" title="German East Africa">
        German East Africa
       </a>
       (now belonging to
       <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">
        Tanzania
       </a>
       ) and northeastern
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique" title="Portuguese Mozambique">
        Portuguese Mozambique
       </a>
       (now
       <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">
        Mozambique
       </a>
       ), ending near sunset in eastern Africa.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">
        Observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_May_29,_1919&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#Deflection_of_light_by_the_Sun" title="Tests of general relativity">
        Tests of general relativity § Deflection of light by the Sun
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Experimentum_crucis" title="Experimentum crucis">
        Experimentum crucis
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Albert Einstein's prediction of the bending of light by the gravity of the Sun, one of the components of his
       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity#History" title="General relativity">
        general theory of relativity
       </a>
       , can be tested during a solar eclipse, when stars with apparent position near the sun become visible. Following an unsuccessful attempt to validate this prediction during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_June_8,_1918" title="Solar eclipse of June 8, 1918">
        Solar eclipse of June 8, 1918
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       two expeditions were made to measure positions of stars during this eclipse. The first was led by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Frank_Watson_Dyson" title="Sir Frank Watson Dyson">
        Sir Frank Watson Dyson
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Eddington" title="Sir Arthur Eddington">
        Sir Arthur Eddington
       </a>
       to the island of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principe" title="Principe">
        Principe
       </a>
       (off the west coast of Africa), the second by
       <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Claude_de_la_Cherois_Crommelin" title="Andrew Claude de la Cherois Crommelin">
        Andrew Claude de la Cherois Crommelin
       </a>
       and Charles Davidson to
       <a href="/wiki/Sobral,_Cear%C3%A1" title="Sobral, Ceará">
        Sobral
       </a>
       in Brazil.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Hyades_(star_cluster)" title="Hyades (star cluster)">
        stars
       </a>
       that both expeditions observed were in the constellation
       <a href="/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)" title="Taurus (constellation)">
        Taurus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection">

       </a>
       of the Moon's orbit.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saros_136">
        Saros 136
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_May_29,_1919&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Saros 136">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Saros_136" title="Solar Saros 136">
        Solar Saros 136
       </a>
       , repeating every 18 years, 11 days, contains 71 events. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saros_cycle" title="Saros cycle">
        series
       </a>
       started with partial solar eclipse on June 14, 1360, and reached a first annular eclipse on September 8, 1504. It was a hybrid event from November 22, 1612, through January 17, 1703, and total eclipses from January 27, 1721 through May 13, 2496. The series ends at member 71 as a partial eclipse on July 30, 2622, with the entire series lasting 1262 years. The longest eclipse occurred on June 20, 1955, with a maximum duration of totality at 7 minutes, 8 seconds.

        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
