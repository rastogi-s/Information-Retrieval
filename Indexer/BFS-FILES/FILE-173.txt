   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Besselian elements
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BesselianElementsForTotalEclipse3D-en.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="670" data-file-width="642" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/BesselianElementsForTotalEclipse3D-en.svg/260px-BesselianElementsForTotalEclipse3D-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/BesselianElementsForTotalEclipse3D-en.svg/390px-BesselianElementsForTotalEclipse3D-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/BesselianElementsForTotalEclipse3D-en.svg/520px-BesselianElementsForTotalEclipse3D-en.svg.png 2x" width="260"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BesselianElementsForTotalEclipse3D-en.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Outline of
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
          umbra and penumbra
         </a>
         (green) during total
         <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
          solar eclipse
         </a>
         on Earth's surface and in fundamental plane (red)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Besselian elements
       </b>
       are a set of values used to calculate and predict the local circumstances of
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occultations
       </a>
       for an observer on Earth. This method is particularly used for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_eclipses" title="Solar eclipses">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       , but is also applied for occultations of
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">
        transits
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Mercury" title="Transit of Mercury">
        Mercury
       </a>
       . In addition, for
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipses
       </a>
       a similar method is used, in which the shadow is cast on the Moon instead of the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seidelmann8.1_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Seidelmann8.1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For solar eclipses, the Besselian elements are used to calculate the path of the
       <a href="/wiki/Umbra,_penumbra_and_antumbra" title="Umbra, penumbra and antumbra">
        umbra and penumbra
       </a>
       on the Earth's surface, and hence the circumstances of the eclipse at a specific location. This method was developed in the 1820s by the German mathematician and astronomer,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Bessel" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Bessel">
        Friedrich Wilhelm Bessel
       </a>
       , and later improved by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Chauvenet" title="William Chauvenet">
        William Chauvenet
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The basic concept is that Besselian elements describe the movement of the shadow cast by the occulting body – for solar eclipses this is the shadow of the Moon – on a specifically chosen plane, called the
       <i>
        fundamental plane
       </i>
       . This is the geocentric, normal plane of the shadow axis. In other words, it is the plane through the Earth's center that is perpendicular to the line through the centers of the occulting and the occulted bodies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MuckeMeeus1992_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MuckeMeeus1992-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One advantage, among others, of choosing this plane is that the outline of the shadow on it is always a circle, and there is no
       <a href="/wiki/Perspective_projection_distortion" title="Perspective projection distortion">
        perspective distortion
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Comparatively few values are needed to accurately describe the movement of the shadow on the fundamental plane. Based on this, the next step is to
       <a href="/wiki/Graphical_projection" title="Graphical projection">
        project
       </a>
       the shadow cone on to the Earth's surface, taking into account the
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">
        figure of the Earth
       </a>
       , its
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
        rotation
       </a>
       , and the observer's
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
        elevation
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seidelmann8.32_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Seidelmann8.32-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
