   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Corona
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3489" data-file-width="3543" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg/220px-Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg/330px-Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg/440px-Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         During a total
         <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
          solar eclipse
         </a>
         , the Sun's corona and
         <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
          prominences
         </a>
         are visible to the
         <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">
          naked eye
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        corona
       </b>
       (Latin, '
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_(headgear)" title="Crown (headgear)">
        crown
       </a>
       ') is an aura of
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       that surrounds the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       and other
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       . The Sun's corona extends millions of kilometres into space and is most easily seen during a total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       , but it is also observable with a
       <a href="/wiki/Coronagraph" title="Coronagraph">
        coronagraph
       </a>
       . The word
       <i>
        corona
       </i>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       word meaning "crown", from the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       κορώνη (korōnè, “garland, wreath”).
      </p>
      <p>
       The high temperature of the Sun's corona gives it unusual
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectral
       </a>
       features, which led some in the 19th century to suggest that it contained a previously unknown element, "
       <a href="/wiki/Coronium" title="Coronium">
        coronium
       </a>
       ". Instead, these spectral features have since been explained by
       <a href="/wiki/Highly_charged_ion" title="Highly charged ion">
        highly ionized
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
        iron
       </a>
       (Fe-XIV, or Fe
       <sup>
        13+
       </sup>
       ).
       <a href="/wiki/Bengt_Edl%C3%A9n" title="Bengt Edlén">
        Bengt Edlén
       </a>
       , following the work of Grotrian (1939), first identified the coronal spectral lines in 1940 (observed since 1869) as transitions from low-lying metastable levels of the ground configuration of highly ionised metals (the green Fe-XIV line from Fe
       <sup>
        13+
       </sup>
       at 5303
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">
        Å
       </a>
       , but also the red Fe-X line from Fe
       <sup>
        9+
       </sup>
       at 6374 Å). These high stages of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionisation" title="Ionisation">
        ionisation
       </a>
       indicate a plasma temperature in excess of 1,000,000
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        kelvin
       </a>
       s,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aschwanden_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Aschwanden-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       much hotter than the surface of the Sun.
      </p>
      <p>
       Light from the corona comes from three primary sources, from the same volume of space. The K-corona (K for
       <i>
        kontinuierlich
       </i>
       , "continuous" in German) is created by sunlight
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scattering_(physics)" title="Scattering (physics)">
        scattering
       </a>
       off free
       <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">
        electrons
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_broadening" title="Doppler broadening">
        Doppler broadening
       </a>
       of the reflected photospheric
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_line" title="Absorption line">
        absorption lines
       </a>
       spreads them so greatly as to completely obscure them, giving the spectral appearance of a continuum with no absorption lines. The F-corona (F for
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_von_Fraunhofer" title="Joseph von Fraunhofer">
        Fraunhofer
       </a>
       ) is created by sunlight bouncing off dust particles, and is observable because its light contains the Fraunhofer absorption lines that are seen in raw sunlight; the F-corona extends to very high
       <a href="/wiki/Elongation_(astronomy)" title="Elongation (astronomy)">
        elongation
       </a>
       angles from the Sun, where it is called the
       <a href="/wiki/Zodiacal_light" title="Zodiacal light">
        zodiacal light
       </a>
       . The E-corona (E for emission) is due to spectral emission lines produced by ions that are present in the coronal plasma; it may be observed in broad or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbidden_line" title="Forbidden line">
        forbidden
       </a>
       or hot
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
        spectral emission lines
       </a>
       and is the main source of information about the corona's composition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corfield_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Corfield-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_features">
        Physical features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Twistedflux.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="367" data-file-width="541" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Twistedflux.png/220px-Twistedflux.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Twistedflux.png/330px-Twistedflux.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Twistedflux.png/440px-Twistedflux.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Twistedflux.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A drawing demonstrating the configuration of solar magnetic flux during the solar cycle
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The sun's corona is much hotter (by a factor from 150 to 450) than the visible surface of the Sun: the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       's average
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       is 5800
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        kelvin
       </a>
       s compared to the corona's one to three million kelvins. The corona is 10
       <sup>
        −12
       </sup>
       times as dense as the photosphere, and so produces about one-millionth as much visible light. The corona is separated from the photosphere by the relatively shallow
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       . The exact mechanism by which the corona is heated is still the subject of some debate, but likely possibilities include induction by the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">
        magnetohydrodynamic waves
       </a>
       from below. The outer edges of the Sun's corona are constantly being transported away due to open magnetic flux and hence generating the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The corona is not always evenly distributed across the surface of the sun. During periods of quiet, the corona is more or less confined to the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equatorial
       </a>
       regions, with
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_hole" title="Coronal hole">
        coronal holes
       </a>
       covering the
       <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">
        polar
       </a>
       regions. However, during the Sun's active periods, the corona is evenly distributed over the equatorial and polar regions, though it is most prominent in areas with
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspot
       </a>
       activity. The
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       spans approximately 11 years, from
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">
        solar minimum
       </a>
       to the following minimum. Since the solar magnetic field is continually wound up due to the faster rotation of mass at the sun's equator (
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_rotation" title="Differential rotation">
        differential rotation
       </a>
       ), sunspot activity will be more pronounced at
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">
        solar maximum
       </a>
       where the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       is more twisted. Associated with sunspots are
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_loop" title="Coronal loop">
        coronal loops
       </a>
       , loops of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">
        magnetic flux
       </a>
       , upwelling from the solar interior. The magnetic flux pushes the hotter
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       aside, exposing the cooler plasma below, thus creating the relatively dark sun spots.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the corona has been photographed at high resolution in the X-ray range of the spectrum by the satellite
       <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">
        Skylab
       </a>
       in 1973, and then later by
       <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">
        Yohkoh
       </a>
       and the other following space instruments, it has been seen that the structure of the corona is quite varied and complex: different zones have been immediately classified on the coronal disc.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The astronomers usually distinguish several regions,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibson_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gibson-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as described below.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Active_regions">
        Active regions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Active regions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <b>
        Active regions
       </b>
       are ensembles of loop structures connecting points of opposite magnetic polarity in the photosphere, the so-called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_loops" title="Coronal loops">
        coronal loops
       </a>
       . They generally distribute in two zones of activity, which are parallel to the solar equator. The average temperature is between two and four million kelvins, while the density goes from 10
       <sup>
        9
       </sup>
       to 10
       <sup>
        10
       </sup>
       particle per cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Prominence_(PSF).png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1452" data-file-width="3636" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Prominence_%28PSF%29.png/220px-Prominence_%28PSF%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Prominence_%28PSF%29.png/330px-Prominence_%28PSF%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Prominence_%28PSF%29.png/440px-Prominence_%28PSF%29.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Prominence_(PSF).png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Illustration depicting
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_prominences" title="Solar prominences">
          solar prominences
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
          sunspots
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Active regions involve all the phenomena directly linked to the magnetic field, which occur at different heights above the Sun's surface:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibson_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gibson-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspots
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Facula" title="Facula">
        faculae
       </a>
       , occur in the photosphere,
       <a href="/wiki/Spicule_(solar_physics)" title="Spicule (solar physics)">
        spicules
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%CE%B1" title="Hα">
        Hα
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
        filaments
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Plage_(astronomy)" title="Plage (astronomy)">
        plages
       </a>
       in the chromosphere,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_prominences" title="Solar prominences">
        prominences
       </a>
       in the chromosphere and transition region, and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        flares
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       happen in the corona and chromosphere. If flares are very violent, they can also perturb the photosphere and generate a
       <a href="/wiki/Moreton_wave" title="Moreton wave">
        Moreton wave
       </a>
       . On the contrary, quiescent prominences are large, cool dense structures which are observed as dark, "snake-like" Hα ribbons (appearing like filaments) on the solar disc. Their temperature is about 5000–8000 K, and so they are usually considered as chromospheric features.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2013, images from the
       <a href="/wiki/High_Resolution_Coronal_Imager" title="High Resolution Coronal Imager">
        High Resolution Coronal Imager
       </a>
       revealed never-before-seen "magnetic braids" of plasma within the outer layers of these active regions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Coronal_loops">
        Coronal loops
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coronal loops">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_loop" title="Coronal loop">
        Coronal loop
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Traceimage.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="768" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Traceimage.jpg/220px-Traceimage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Traceimage.jpg/330px-Traceimage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Traceimage.jpg/440px-Traceimage.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Traceimage.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         TRACE 171Å coronal loops
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_loop" title="Coronal loop">
        Coronal loops
       </a>
       are the basic structures of the magnetic solar corona. These loops are the closed-magnetic flux cousins of the open-magnetic flux that can be found in
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_hole" title="Coronal hole">
        coronal hole
       </a>
       (polar) regions and the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       . Loops of magnetic flux well-up from the solar body and fill with hot solar plasma.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Due to the heightened magnetic activity in these coronal loop regions, coronal loops can often be the precursor to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">
        solar flares
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       (CMEs).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Solar plasma that feed these structures is heated from under 6000 K to well over 10
       <sup>
        6
       </sup>
       K from the photosphere, through the transition region, and into the corona. Often, the solar plasma will fill these loops from one point and drain to another, called foot points (
       <a href="/wiki/Siphon" title="Siphon">
        siphon
       </a>
       flow due to a pressure difference,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or asymmetric flow due to some other driver).
      </p>
      <p>
       When the plasma rises from the foot points towards the loop top, as always occurs during the initial phase of a compact flare, it is defined as chromospheric
       <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">
        evaporation
       </a>
       . When the plasma rapidly cools and falls toward the photosphere, it is called chromospheric
       <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">
        condensation
       </a>
       . There may also be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symmetric" title="Symmetric">
        symmetric
       </a>
       flow from both loop foot points, causing a build-up of mass in the loop structure. The plasma may cool rapidly in this region (for a thermal instability), its dark
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
        filaments
       </a>
       obvious against the solar disk or
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
        prominences
       </a>
       off the
       <a href="/wiki/Limb_darkening" title="Limb darkening">
        Sun's limb
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Coronal loops may have lifetimes in the order of seconds (in the case of flare events), minutes, hours or days. Where there is a balance in loop energy sources and sinks, coronal loops can last for long periods of time and are known as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Steady_state" title="Steady state">
         steady state
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quiescent" title="wikt:quiescent">
         quiescent
        </a>
       </i>
       coronal loops. (
       <a href="/wiki/File:Energyfig.png" title="File:Energyfig.png">
        example
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Coronal loops are very important to our understanding of the current
       <i>
        coronal heating problem
       </i>
       . Coronal loops are highly radiating sources of plasma and are therefore easy to observe by instruments such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
         TRACE
        </a>
       </i>
       . An explanation of the coronal heating problem remains as these structures are being observed remotely, where many ambiguities are present (i.e. radiation contributions along the
       <a href="/wiki/Line-of-sight_propagation" title="Line-of-sight propagation">
        LOS
       </a>
       ).
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ" title="In-situ">
         In-situ
        </a>
       </i>
       measurements are required before a definitive answer can be had, but due to the high plasma temperatures in the corona,
       <i>
        in-situ
       </i>
       measurements are, at present, impossible. The next mission of the NASA
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Probe_Plus" title="Solar Probe Plus">
        Solar Probe Plus
       </a>
       will approach the Sun very closely allowing more direct observations.
      </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coronal_Hole_Magnetic_Field_Lines.svg">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Large-scale_structures">
        Large-scale structures
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Large-scale structures">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <b>
        Large-scale structures
       </b>
       are very long arcs which can cover over a quarter of the solar disk but contain plasma less dense than in the coronal loops of the active regions.
      </p>
      <p>
       They were first detected in the June 8, 1968 flare observation during a rocket flight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giacconi_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Giacconi-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The large-scale structure of the corona changes over the 11-year
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       and becomes particularly simple during the minimum period, when the magnetic field of the Sun is almost similar to a dipolar configuration (plus a quadrupolar component).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interconnections_of_active_regions">
        Interconnections of active regions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Interconnections of active regions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        interconnections of active regions
       </b>
       are arcs connecting zones of opposite magnetic field, of different active regions. Significant variations of these structures are often seen after a flare.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some other features of this kind are
       <a href="/wiki/Helmet_streamer" title="Helmet streamer">
        helmet streamers
       </a>
       —large cap-like coronal structures with long pointed peaks that usually overlie sunspots and active regions. Coronal streamers are considered as sources of the slow
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       <b>
        Filament cavities
       </b>
       are zones which look dark in the X-rays and are above the regions where
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%CE%B1" title="Hα">
        Hα
       </a>

       <i>
        coronal holes
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giacconi_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Giacconi-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       </a>
       and
       <span class="mwe-math-element">

         <math alttext="{\displaystyle T}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

       </span>
       the plasma temperature. It is evident from the equation that the plasma pressure lowers when the plasma temperature decreases with respect to the surrounding regions or when the zone of intense magnetic field empties. The same physical effect renders
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">

       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Bright_points">
        Bright points
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bright points">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <b>
        Bright points
       </b>
       are small active regions found on the solar disk. X-ray bright points were first detected on April 8, 1969 during a rocket flight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giacconi_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Giacconi-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The fraction of the solar surface covered by bright points varies with the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       . They are associated with small bipolar regions of the magnetic field. Their average temperature ranges from 1.1x10
       <sup>
        6
       </sup>
       K to 3.4x10
       <sup>
        6
       </sup>
       K. The variations in temperature are often correlated with changes in the X-ray emission.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Coronal_holes">
        Coronal holes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Coronal holes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_hole" title="Coronal hole">
        Coronal hole
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Coronal_hole" title="Coronal hole">
         Coronal holes
        </a>
       </b>
       are the Polar Regions which look dark in the X-rays since they do not emit much radiation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These are wide zones of the Sun where the magnetic field is unipolar and opens towards the interplanetary space. The high speed
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       arises mainly from these regions.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the UV images of the coronal holes, some small structures, similar to elongated bubbles, are often seen as they were suspended in the solar wind. These are the coronal
       <b>
        plumes
       </b>
       . More exactly, they are long thin streamers that project outward from the Sun's north and south poles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_quiet_Sun">
        The quiet Sun
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The quiet Sun">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The solar regions which are not part of active regions and coronal holes are commonly identified as the
       <b>
        quiet Sun
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The equatorial region has a faster rotation speed than the polar zones. The result of the Sun's differential rotation is that the active regions always arise in two bands parallel to the equator and their extension increases during the periods of maximum of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       , while they almost disappear during each minimum. Therefore, the quiet Sun always coincides with the equatorial zone and its surface is less active during the maximum of the solar cycle. Approaching the minimum of the solar cycle (also named butterfly cycle), the extension of the quiet Sun increases until it covers the whole disk surface excluding some bright points on the hemisphere and the poles, where there are the coronal holes.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Flares">
        Flares
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Flares">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">
        Solar flares
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magnificent_CME_Erupts_on_the_Sun_-_August_31.jpg">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Magnificent_CME_Erupts_on_the_Sun_-_August_31.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         On August 31, 2012 a long filament of solar material that had been hovering in the Sun's outer atmosphere, the corona, erupted at 4:36 p.m. EDT
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Flares take place in active regions and are characterized by a sudden increase of the radiative flux emitted from small regions of the corona. They are very complex phenomena, visible at different wavelengths; they involve several zones of the solar atmosphere and many physical effects, thermal and not thermal, and sometimes wide reconnections of the magnetic field lines with material expulsion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Flares are impulsive phenomena, of average duration of 15 minutes, and the most energetic events can last several hours. Flares produce a high and rapid increase of the density and temperature.
      </p>
      <p>
       An emission in white light is only seldom observed: usually, flares are only seen at extreme UV wavelengths and into the X-rays, typical of the chromospheric and coronal emission.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the corona, the morphology of flares, is described by observations in the UV, soft and hard X-rays, and in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%CE%B1" title="Hα">
        Hα
       </a>
       wavelengths, and is very complex. However, two kinds of basic structures can be distinguished:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Compact flares
        </b>
        , when each of the two arches where the event is happening maintains its morphology: only an increase of the emission is observed without significant structural variations. The emitted energy is of the order of 10
        <sup>
         22
        </sup>
        – 10
        <sup>
         23
        </sup>
        J.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Flares of long duration
        </b>
        , associated with eruptions of
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
         prominences
        </a>
        , transients in white light and
        <i>
         two-ribbon flares
        </i>
        :
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
        in this case the magnetic loops change their configuration during the event. The energies emitted during these flares are of such great proportion they can reach 10
        <sup>
         25
        </sup>
        J.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar-filament.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="478" data-file-width="669" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Solar-filament.gif/220px-Solar-filament.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Solar-filament.gif/330px-Solar-filament.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Solar-filament.gif/440px-Solar-filament.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar-filament.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Filament erupting during a solar flare, seen at EUV wavelengths (
         <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
          TRACE
         </a>
         )
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       As for temporal dynamics, three different phases are generally distinguished, whose duration are not comparable. The durations of those periods depend on the range of wavelengths used to observe the event:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         An initial impulsive phase
        </b>
        , whose duration is on the order of minutes, strong emissions of energy are often observed even in the microwaves, EUV wavelengths and in the hard X-ray frequencies.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         A maximum phase
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         A decay phase
        </b>
        , which can last several hours.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Sometimes also a phase preceding the flare can be observed, usually called as "pre-flare" phase.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transients">
        Transients
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transients">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        Coronal mass ejection
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Accompanying
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       or large
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
        solar prominences
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        "coronal transients"
       </b>
       (also called
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       ) are sometimes released. These are enormous loops of coronal material that travel outward from the Sun at over a million kilometers per hour, containing roughly 10 times the energy of the solar flare or prominence that accompanies them. Some larger ejections can propel hundreds of millions of tons of material into
       <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">
        space
       </a>
       at roughly 1.5 million kilometers an hour.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_coronae">
        Stellar coronae
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Stellar coronae">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Coronal stars are ubiquitous among the
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       in the cool half of the
       <a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung–Russell diagram">
        Hertzsprung–Russell diagram
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gudel_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gudel-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These coronae can be detected using
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_telescope" title="X-ray telescope">
        X-ray telescopes
       </a>
       . Some stellar coronae, particularly in young stars, are much more luminous than the Sun's. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/FK_Comae_Berenices" title="FK Comae Berenices">
        FK Comae Berenices
       </a>
       is the prototype for the
       <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">
        FK Com
       </a>
       class of
       <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">
        variable star
       </a>
       . These are giants of spectral types G and K with an unusually rapid rotation and signs of extreme activity. Their X-ray coronae are among the most luminous (
       <i>
        L
       </i>
       <sub>
        x
       </sub>
       ≥ 10
       <sup>
        32
       </sup>
       erg·s
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       or 10
       <sup>
        25
       </sup>
       W) and the hottest known with dominant temperatures up to 40 MK.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gudel_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gudel-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The astronomical observations planned with the
       <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Observatory" title="Einstein Observatory">
        Einstein Observatory
       </a>
       by Giuseppe Vaiana and his group
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaianaetal81_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Vaianaetal81-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       showed that F-, G-, K- and M-stars have chromospheres and often coronae much like our Sun. The
       <i>
        O-B stars
       </i>
       , which do not have surface convection zones, have a strong X-ray emission. However these stars do not have coronae, but the outer stellar envelopes emit this radiation during shocks due to thermal instabilities in rapidly moving gas blobs. Also A-stars do not have convection zones but they do not emit at the UV and X-ray wavelengths. Thus they appear to have neither chromospheres nor coronae.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physics_of_the_corona">
        Physics of the corona
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Physics of the corona">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:171879main_LimbFlareJan12_lg.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="712" data-file-width="1014" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/171879main_LimbFlareJan12_lg.jpg/220px-171879main_LimbFlareJan12_lg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/171879main_LimbFlareJan12_lg.jpg/330px-171879main_LimbFlareJan12_lg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/171879main_LimbFlareJan12_lg.jpg/440px-171879main_LimbFlareJan12_lg.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:171879main_LimbFlareJan12_lg.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         This image, taken by
         <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">
          Hinode
         </a>
         on 12 January 2007, reveals the filamentary nature of the corona.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The matter in the external part of the solar atmosphere is in the state of
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       , at very high temperature (a few million kelvins) and at very low density (of the order of 10
       <sup>
        15
       </sup>
       particles/m
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ). According to the definition of plasma, it is a quasi-neutral ensemble of particles which exhibits a collective behaviour.
      </p>
      <p>
       The composition is similar to that in the Sun's interior, mainly hydrogen, but with much greater ionization than that found in the photosphere. Heavier metals, such as iron, are partially ionized and have lost most of the external electrons. The ionization state of a chemical element depends strictly on the temperature and is regulated by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saha_equation" title="Saha equation">
        Saha equation
       </a>
       in the lowest atmosphere, but by collisional equilibrium in the optically-thin corona. Historically, the presence of the spectral lines emitted from highly ionized states of iron allowed determination of the high temperature of the coronal plasma, revealing that the corona is much hotter than the internal layers of the chromosphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       The corona behaves like a gas which is very hot but very light at the same time: the pressure in the corona is usually only 0.1 to 0.6 Pa in active regions, while on the Earth the atmospheric pressure is about 100 kPa, approximately a million times higher than on the solar surface. However it is not properly a gas, because it is made of charged particles, basically protons and electrons, moving at different velocities. Supposing that they have the same kinetic energy on average (for the
       <a href="/wiki/Equipartition_theorem" title="Equipartition theorem">
        equipartition theorem
       </a>
       ), electrons have a mass roughly 1800 times smaller than protons, therefore they acquire more velocity. Metal ions are always slower. This fact has relevant physical consequences either on radiative processes (that are very different from the photospheric radiative processes), or on thermal conduction. Furthermore, the presence of electric charges induces the generation of electric currents and high magnetic fields. Magnetohydrodynamic waves (MHD waves) can also propagate in this plasma,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeffrey_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jeffrey-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       even if it is not still clear how they can be transmitted or generated in the corona.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radiation">
        Radiation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Radiation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_radiative_losses" title="Coronal radiative losses">
        Coronal radiative losses
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The corona emits radiation mainly in the X-rays, observable only from space.
      </p>
      <p>
       The plasma is transparent to its own radiation and to that one coming from below, therefore we say that it is
       <b>
        optically-thin
       </b>
       . The gas, in fact, is very rarefied and the photon mean free-path overcomes by far all the other length-scales, including the typical sizes of the coronal features.
      </p>
      <p>
       Different processes of radiation take place in the emission, due to binary collisions between plasma particles, while the interactions with the photons, coming from below; are very rare. Because the emission is due to collisions between ions and electrons, the energy emitted from a unit volume in the time unit is proportional to the squared number of particles in a unit volume, or more exactly, to the product of the electron density and proton density.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_conduction">
        Thermal conduction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Thermal conduction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>

      <p>
       In the corona
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conduction" title="Thermal conduction">
        thermal conduction
       </a>
       occurs from the external hotter atmosphere towards the inner cooler layers. Responsible for the diffusion process of the heat are the electrons, which are much lighter than ions and move faster, as explained above.
      </p>
      <p>
       When there is a magnetic field the
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
        thermal conductivity
       </a>
       of the plasma becomes higher in the direction which is parallel to the field lines rather than in the perpendicular direction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitzer_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Spitzer-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A charged particle moving in the direction perpendicular to the magnetic field line is subject to the
       <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">
        Lorentz force
       </a>
       which is normal to the plane individuated by the velocity and the magnetic field. This force bends the path of the particle. In general, since particles also have a velocity component along the magnetic field line, the
       <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">
        Lorentz force
       </a>
       constrains them to bend and move along spirals around the field lines at the
       <a href="/wiki/Cyclotron" title="Cyclotron">
        cyclotron
       </a>
       frequency.
      </p>
      <p>
       If collisions between the particles are very frequent, they are scattered in every direction. This happens in the photosphere, where the plasma carries the magnetic field in its motion. In the corona, on the contrary, the mean free-path of the electrons is of the order of kilometres and even more, so each electron can do a helicoidal motion long before being scattered after a collision. Therefore, the heat transfer is enhanced along the magnetic field lines and inhibited in the perpendicular direction.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Coronal_seismology">
        Coronal seismology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Coronal seismology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_seismology" title="Coronal seismology">
        Coronal seismology
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Coronal seismology
       </b>
       is a new way of studying the
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       of the solar corona with the use of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">
        magnetohydrodynamic
       </a>
       (MHD) waves. Magnetohydrodynamics studies the
       <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">
        dynamics
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conduction" title="Electrical conduction">
        electrically conducting
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">
        fluids
       </a>
       —in this case the fluid is the coronal plasma. Philosophically, coronal seismology is similar to the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">
        seismology
       </a>
       , the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">
        helioseismology
       </a>
       , and MHD spectroscopy of laboratory plasma devices. In all these approaches, waves of various kinds are used to probe a medium. The potential of coronal seismology in the estimation of the coronal magnetic field, density
       <a href="/wiki/Scale_height" title="Scale height">
        scale height
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fine_structure" title="Fine structure">
        fine structure
       </a>
       and heating has been demonstrated by different research groups.
      </p>

      <p>
       The coronal heating problem in
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_physics" title="Solar physics">
        solar physics
       </a>
       relates to the question of why the temperature of the Sun's corona is millions of kelvins higher than that of the surface. The high temperatures require energy to be carried from the solar interior to the corona by non-thermal processes, because the
       <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">
        second law of thermodynamics
       </a>
       prevents heat from flowing directly from the solar photosphere (surface), which is at about 5800 K, to the much hotter corona at about 1 to 3
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_prefix" title="SI prefix">
        MK
       </a>
       (parts of the corona can even reach 10 MK).
      </p>
      <p>
       Between the photosphere and the corona, is the thin region through which the temperature increases known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_transition_region" title="Solar transition region">
        transition region
       </a>
       . It ranges from only tens to hundreds of kilometers thick. Energy cannot be transferred from the cooler photosphere to the corona by conventional heat transfer as this would violate the
       <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">
        second law of thermodynamics
       </a>
       . An analogy of this would be a light bulb raising the temperature of the air surrounding it to something greater than its glass surface. Hence, some other manner of energy transfer must be involved in the heating of the corona.
      </p>
      <p>
       The amount of power required to heat the solar corona can easily be calculated as the difference between
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_radiative_losses" title="Coronal radiative losses">
        coronal radiative losses
       </a>
       and heating by thermal conduction toward the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       through the transition region. It is about 1 kilowatt for every square meter of surface area on the Sun's chromosphere, or 1/40000 of the amount of light energy that escapes the Sun.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many coronal heating theories have been proposed,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but two theories have remained as the most likely candidates: wave heating and
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       (or
       <a href="/wiki/Nanoflares" title="Nanoflares">
        nanoflares
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Through most of the past 50 years, neither theory has been able to account for the extreme coronal temperatures.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2012, high resolution (&lt;0.2″)
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_X-ray" title="Soft X-ray">
        soft X-ray
       </a>
       imaging with the
       <a href="/wiki/High_Resolution_Coronal_Imager" title="High Resolution Coronal Imager">
        High Resolution Coronal Imager
       </a>
       aboard a
       <a href="/wiki/Sounding_rocket" title="Sounding rocket">
        sounding rocket
       </a>
       revealed tightly wound braids in the corona. It is hypothesized that the reconnection and unravelling of braids can act as primary sources of heating of the active solar corona to temperatures of up to 4 million kelvins. The main heat source in the quiescent corona (about 1.5 million kelvins) is assumed to originate from
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">
        MHD waves
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cirtain2013_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cirtain2013-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       mission
       <a href="/wiki/Parker_Solar_Probe" title="Parker Solar Probe">
        Parker Solar Probe
       </a>
       is intended to approach the Sun to a distance of approximately 9.5 solar radii to investigate coronal heating and the origin of the solar wind. It is scheduled to launch on July 31, 2018.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_heating_theory">
        Wave heating theory
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Wave heating theory">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The wave heating theory, proposed in 1949 by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evry_Schatzman" title="Evry Schatzman">
        Evry Schatzman
       </a>
       , proposes that waves carry energy from the solar interior to the solar chromosphere and corona. The Sun is made of
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       rather than ordinary gas, so it supports several types of waves analogous to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_waves" title="Sound waves">
        sound waves
       </a>
       in air. The most important types of wave are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magneto-acoustic_wave" title="Magneto-acoustic wave">
        magneto-acoustic waves
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Alfv%C3%A9n_wave" title="Alfvén wave">
        Alfvén waves
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Magneto-acoustic waves are sound waves that have been modified by the presence of a magnetic field, and Alfvén waves are similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Ultra_low_frequency" title="Ultra low frequency">
        ultra low frequency
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">
        radio waves
       </a>
       that have been modified by interaction with
       <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">
        matter
       </a>
       in the plasma. Both types of waves can be launched by the turbulence of
       <a href="/wiki/Granule_(solar_physics)" title="Granule (solar physics)">
        granulation
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_granulation" title="Super granulation">
        super granulation
       </a>
       at the solar photosphere, and both types of waves can carry energy for some distance through the solar atmosphere before turning into
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shock_waves" title="Shock waves">
        shock waves
       </a>
       that dissipate their energy as heat.
      </p>
      <p>
       One problem with wave heating is delivery of the heat to the appropriate place. Magneto-acoustic waves cannot carry sufficient energy upward through the chromosphere to the corona, both because of the low pressure present in the chromosphere and because they tend to be
       <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">
        reflected
       </a>
       back to the photosphere. Alfvén waves can carry enough energy, but do not dissipate that energy rapidly enough once they enter the corona. Waves in plasmas are notoriously difficult to understand and describe analytically, but computer simulations, carried out by Thomas Bogdan and colleagues in 2003, seem to show that Alfvén waves can transmute into other wave modes at the base of the corona, providing a pathway that can carry large amounts of energy from the photosphere through the chromosphere and transition region and finally into the corona where it dissipates it as heat.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another problem with wave heating has been the complete absence, until the late 1990s, of any direct evidence of waves propagating through the solar corona. The first direct observation of waves propagating into and through the solar corona was made in 1997 with the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
       </a>
       space-borne solar observatory, the first platform capable of observing the Sun in the
       <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">
        extreme ultraviolet
       </a>
       (EUV) for long periods of time with stable
       <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">
        photometry
       </a>
       . Those were magneto-acoustic waves with a frequency of about 1
       <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">
        millihertz
       </a>
       (mHz, corresponding to a 1,000 second wave period), that carry only about 10% of the energy required to heat the corona. Many observations exist of localized wave phenomena, such as Alfvén waves launched by solar flares, but those events are transient and cannot explain the uniform coronal heat.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is not yet known exactly how much wave energy is available to heat the corona. Results published in 2004 using data from the
       <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
        TRACE
       </a>
       spacecraft seem to indicate that there are waves in the solar atmosphere at frequencies as high as 100 mHz (10 second period). Measurements of the temperature of different
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">
        ions
       </a>
       in the solar wind with the UVCS instrument aboard
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO
       </a>
       give strong indirect evidence that there are waves at frequencies as high as 200 Hz, well into the range of human hearing. These waves are very difficult to detect under normal circumstances, but evidence collected during solar eclipses by teams from
       <a href="/wiki/Williams_College" title="Williams College">
        Williams College
       </a>
       suggest the presences of such waves in the 1–10 Hz range.
      </p>
      <p>
       Recently, Alfvénic motions have been found in the lower solar atmosphere
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and also in the quiet Sun, in coronal holes and in active regions using observations with AIA on board the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
        Solar Dynamics Observatory
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These Alfvénic oscillations have significant power, and seem to be connected to the chromospheric Alfvénic oscillations previously reported with the
       <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">
        Hinode
       </a>
       spacecraft .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Solar wind observations with the
       <a href="/wiki/WIND_(spacecraft)" title="WIND (spacecraft)">
        WIND (spacecraft)
       </a>
       have recently shown evidence to support theories of Alfvén-cyclotron dissipation, leading to local ion heating.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        Magnetic reconnection
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arcing_Active_Region.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="800" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Arcing_Active_Region.jpg/220px-Arcing_Active_Region.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Arcing_Active_Region.jpg/330px-Arcing_Active_Region.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Arcing_Active_Region.jpg/440px-Arcing_Active_Region.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arcing_Active_Region.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Arcing active region by
         <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
          Solar Dynamics Observatory
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       theory relies on the solar magnetic field to induce electric currents in the solar corona.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The currents then collapse suddenly, releasing energy as heat and wave energy in the corona. This process is called "reconnection" because of the peculiar way that magnetic fields behave in plasma (or any electrically conductive fluid such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">
        mercury
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">
        seawater
       </a>
       ). In a plasma,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_field_lines" title="Magnetic field lines">
        magnetic field lines
       </a>
       are normally tied to individual pieces of matter, so that the
       <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">
        topology
       </a>
       of the magnetic field remains the same: if a particular north and south
       <a href="/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies#Magnetic_poles" title="Poles of astronomical bodies">
        magnetic pole
       </a>
       are connected by a single field line, then even if the plasma is stirred or if the magnets are moved around, that field line will continue to connect those particular poles. The connection is maintained by electric currents that are induced in the plasma. Under certain conditions, the electric currents can collapse, allowing the magnetic field to "reconnect" to other magnetic poles and release heat and wave energy in the process.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        Magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       is hypothesized to be the mechanism behind solar flares, the largest explosions in our solar system. Furthermore, the surface of the Sun is covered with millions of small magnetized regions 50–1,000 km across. These small magnetic poles are buffeted and churned by the constant granulation. The magnetic field in the solar corona must undergo nearly constant reconnection to match the motion of this "magnetic carpet", so the energy released by the reconnection is a natural candidate for the coronal heat, perhaps as a series of "microflares" that individually provide very little energy but together account for the required energy.
      </p>
      <p>
       The idea that
       <a href="/wiki/Nanoflares" title="Nanoflares">
        nanoflares
       </a>
       might heat the corona was proposed by
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Parker" title="Eugene Parker">
        Eugene Parker
       </a>
       in the 1980s but is still controversial. In particular,
       <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
        ultraviolet
       </a>
       telescopes such as
       <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
        TRACE
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO
       </a>
       /EIT can observe individual micro-flares as small brightenings in extreme ultraviolet light,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but there seem to be too few of these small events to account for the energy released into the corona. The additional energy not accounted for could be made up by wave energy, or by gradual magnetic reconnection that releases energy more smoothly than micro-flares and therefore doesn't appear well in the
       <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
        TRACE
       </a>
       data. Variations on the micro-flare hypothesis use other mechanisms to stress the magnetic field or to release the energy, and are a subject of active research in 2005.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Spicules_.28type_II.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spicules_(type_II)">
        Spicules (type II)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Spicules (type II)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       For decades, researchers believed
       <a href="/wiki/Spicule_(solar_physics)" title="Spicule (solar physics)">
        spicules
       </a>
       could send heat into the corona. However, following observational research in the 1980s, it was found that spicule plasma did not reach coronal temperatures, and so the theory was discounted.
      </p>
      <p>
       As per studies performed in 2010 at the
       <i>
        National Center for Atmospheric Research
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">
        Colorado
       </a>
       , in collaboration with the
       <i>
        Lockheed Martin's Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory
       </i>
       (LMSAL) and the
       <i>
        Institute of Theoretical Astrophysics
       </i>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oslo" title="University of Oslo">
        University of Oslo
       </a>
       , a new class of spicules (TYPE II) discovered in 2007, which travel faster (up to 100 km/s) and have shorter lifespans, can account for the problem.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These jets insert heated plasma into the Sun's outer atmosphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thus, a much greater understanding of the Corona and improvement in the knowledge of the Sun's subtle influence on the Earth's upper atmosphere can be expected henceforth. The Atmospheric Imaging Assembly on NASA's recently launched Solar Dynamics Observatory and NASA's Focal Plane Package for the Solar Optical Telescope on the Japanese Hinode satellite which was used to test this hypothesis. The high spatial and temporal resolutions of the newer instruments reveal this coronal mass supply.
      </p>
      <p>
       These observations reveal a one-to-one connection between plasma that is heated to millions of degrees and the spicules that insert this plasma into the corona.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
