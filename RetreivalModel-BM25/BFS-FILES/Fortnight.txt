    Fortnight
   </h1>

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        fortnight
       </b>
       is a unit of time equal to 14
       <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">
        days
       </a>
       (2
       <a href="/wiki/Week" title="Week">
        weeks
       </a>
       ). The word derives from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language">
        Old English
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="ang" title="Old English language text" xml:lang="ang">
         <i>
          fēowertyne niht
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       , meaning "
       <i lang="ang-Latn" title="Old English-language transliteration" xml:lang="ang-Latn">
        fourteen nights
       </i>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some
       <a href="/wiki/Payroll#Frequency" title="Payroll">
        wages
       </a>
       and salaries are paid on a fortnightly basis;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       however in North America it is far more common to use the term
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/biweekly" title="wikt:biweekly">
         biweekly
        </a>
       </i>
       . Neither of these terms should be confused with
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/semimonthly" title="wikt:semimonthly">
         semimonthly
        </a>
       </i>
       , which divides a year into exactly 24 periods (12 months × 2), instead of the 26 (≈
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_8601#Week_dates" title="ISO 8601">
        52 weeks
       </a>
       ÷ 2) of fortnightly/biweekly.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">
        Astronomy
       </span>
      </h2>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         For more information see
         <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_cycle" title="Eclipse cycle">
          eclipse cycle
         </a>
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In astronomy, a fortnight is half a
       <a href="/wiki/Month#Types_of_months_in_astronomy" title="Month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       , equivalent to the
       <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_mean" title="Arithmetic mean">
        mean
       </a>
       time between a full moon and a new moon (and vice versa). This is equal to 14.77 days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-totality_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-totality-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       In many languages, there is no single word for a two-week period, and the equivalent terms "two weeks", "14 days", or "15 days" (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclusive_counting" title="Inclusive counting">
        counting inclusively
       </a>
       ) have to be used.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
         Celtic languages
        </a>
        : in
        <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
         Welsh
        </a>
        , the term
        <i>
         pythefnos
        </i>
        , meaning "15 nights", is used. This is in keeping with the Welsh term for a week, which is
        <i>
         wythnos
        </i>
        ("eight nights").
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
         Irish
        </a>
        , the term is
        <i>
         coicís
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Similarly in
        <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
         Greek
        </a>
        the term δεκαπενθήμερο (
        <i>
         dekapenthímero
        </i>
        ), meaning "15 days", is used.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">
         Hindu calendar
        </a>
        uses the
        <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
         Sanskrit
        </a>
        word "paksha", meaning one half of a
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">
         lunar month
        </a>
        , which is between 14 and 15 solar days.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
         Romance languages
        </a>
        there are the terms
        <i>
         quincena
        </i>
        (or
        <i>
         quince días
        </i>
        ) in
        <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
         Galician
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         quinzena
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         quinze dies
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
         Catalan
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
         Portuguese
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         quindicina
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
         Italian
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fr:quinze_jours" title="wikt:fr:quinze jours">
          quinze jours
         </a>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fr:quinzaine" title="wikt:fr:quinzaine">
          quinzaine
         </a>
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
        , and
        <i>
         chenzinǎ
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
         Romanian
        </a>
        , all meaning "a grouping of 15"; there are also the terms
        <i>
         bisettimanale
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
         Italian
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         bisemanal
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         bissemanal
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
         Portuguese
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         bisetmanal
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
         Catalan
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         bihebdomadaire
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
        , and
        <i>
         bisǎptǎmânal
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
         Romanian
        </a>
        , that literally mean "biweekly".
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">
         Semitic languages
        </a>
        have a special "doubling
        <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">
         suffix
        </a>
        ". When added at the end of the word for "week" it changes the meaning to "two weeks". In
        <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
         Hebrew
        </a>
        , the single-word
        <i>
         שבועיים
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         shvu′ayim
        </i>
        ) means exactly "two weeks". Also in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
         Arabic
        </a>
        , by adding the common
        <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)#Arabic" title="Dual (grammatical number)">
         dual suffix
        </a>
        to the word for "week",
        <i>
         أسبوع
        </i>
        , the form
        <i>
         أسبوعين
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         usbu′ayn
        </i>
        ), meaning "two weeks", is formed.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
         Slavic languages
        </a>
        : in
        <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
         Czech
        </a>
        the terms
        <i>
         čtrnáctidenní
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         dvoutýdenní
        </i>
        have the same meaning as "fortnight".
       </li>
      </ul>
