   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Video camera
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        video camera
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">
        camera
       </a>
       used for electronic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_picture" title="Motion picture">
        motion picture
       </a>
       acquisition (as opposed to a
       <a href="/wiki/Movie_camera" title="Movie camera">
        movie camera
       </a>
       , which records images on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filmstock" title="Filmstock">
        film
       </a>
       ), initially developed for the
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       industry but now common in other applications as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       The earliest video cameras were those of
       <a href="/wiki/John_Logie_Baird" title="John Logie Baird">
        John Logie Baird
       </a>
       , based on the mechanical
       <a href="/wiki/Nipkow_disk" title="Nipkow disk">
        Nipkow disk
       </a>
       and used in experimental broadcasts through the 1918s-1930s. All-electronic designs based on the
       <a href="/wiki/Video_camera_tube" title="Video camera tube">
        video camera tube
       </a>
       , such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladimir_Zworykin" title="Vladimir Zworykin">
        Vladimir Zworykin
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Iconoscope" title="Iconoscope">
        Iconoscope
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Philo_Farnsworth" title="Philo Farnsworth">
        Philo Farnsworth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Image_dissector" title="Image dissector">
        image dissector
       </a>
       , supplanted the Baird system by the 1930s. These remained in wide use until the 1980s, when cameras based on solid-state
       <a href="/wiki/Image_sensor" title="Image sensor">
        image sensors
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
        CCDs
       </a>
       (and later
       <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">
        CMOS
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Active_pixel_sensor" title="Active pixel sensor">
        active pixel sensors
       </a>
       ) eliminated common problems with tube technologies such as
       <a href="/wiki/Screen_burn-in" title="Screen burn-in">
        image burn-in
       </a>
       and made
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_video" title="Digital video">
        digital video
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Workflow" title="Workflow">
        workflow
       </a>
       practical. The transition to digital TV gave a boost to digital video cameras and by the 2010s, most video cameras were digital.
      </p>
      <p>
       With the advent of digital video capture, the distinction between professional video cameras and movie cameras has disappeared as the intermittent mechanism has become the same. Nowadays, mid-range cameras exclusively used for television and other work (except movies) are termed professional video cameras.
      </p>
      <p>
       Video cameras are used primarily in two modes. The first, characteristic of much early broadcasting, is
       <a href="/wiki/Live_television" title="Live television">
        live television
       </a>
       , where the camera feeds
       <a href="/wiki/Present" title="Present">
        real time
       </a>
       images directly to a screen for immediate observation. A few cameras still serve live television production, but most live connections are for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Security_camera" title="Security camera">
        security
       </a>
       , military/tactical, and industrial operations where surreptitious or remote viewing is required. In the second mode the images are recorded to a storage device for archiving or further processing; for many years,
       <a href="/wiki/Videotape" title="Videotape">
        videotape
       </a>
       was the primary format used for this purpose, but was gradually supplanted by
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_disc" title="Optical disc">
        optical disc
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_disk" title="Hard disk">
        hard disk
       </a>
       , and then
       <a href="/wiki/Flash_memory" title="Flash memory">
        flash memory
       </a>
       . Recorded video is used in television production, and more often
       <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">
        surveillance
       </a>
       and monitoring tasks in which unattended recording of a situation is required for later analysis.
      </p>

      <p>
       Modern video cameras have numerous designs and uses.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Professional_video_camera" title="Professional video camera">
         Professional video cameras
        </a>
        , such as those used in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_production" title="Television production">
         television production
        </a>
        , may be
        <a href="/wiki/Television_studio" title="Television studio">
         television studio
        </a>
        -based or mobile in the case of an
        <a href="/wiki/Electronic_field_production" title="Electronic field production">
         electronic field production
        </a>
        (EFP). Such cameras generally offer extremely fine-grained manual control for the camera operator, often to the exclusion of automated operation. They usually use three sensors to separately record red, green and blue.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Camcorder" title="Camcorder">
         Camcorders
        </a>
        combine a camera and a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VCR" title="VCR">
         VCR
        </a>
        or other recording device in one unit; these are mobile, and were widely used for television production,
        <a href="/wiki/Home_movies" title="Home movies">
         home movies
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_news_gathering" title="Electronic news gathering">
         electronic news gathering
        </a>
        (ENG) (including
        <a href="/wiki/Citizen_journalism" title="Citizen journalism">
         citizen journalism
        </a>
        ), and similar applications. Since the transition to digital video cameras, most cameras have in-built recording media and as such are also camcorders.
        <a href="/wiki/Action_camera" title="Action camera">
         Action camera's
        </a>
        often have 360° recording capabilities.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Closed-circuit_television" title="Closed-circuit television">
         Closed-circuit television
        </a>
        (CCTV) generally uses
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan_tilt_zoom_camera" title="Pan tilt zoom camera">
         pan tilt zoom cameras
        </a>
        (PTZ), for security, surveillance, and/or monitoring purposes. Such cameras are designed to be small, easily hidden, and able to operate unattended; those used in industrial or scientific settings are often meant for use in environments that are normally inaccessible or uncomfortable for humans, and are therefore hardened for such hostile environments (e.g.
        <a href="/wiki/Radiation_hardening" title="Radiation hardening">
         radiation
        </a>
        , high heat, or toxic chemical exposure).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Webcam" title="Webcam">
         Webcams
        </a>
        are video cameras which
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Streaming_video" title="Streaming video">
         stream
        </a>
        a live video feed to a computer.
       </li>
       <li>
        Camera phones have video cameras that are incorporated into
        <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">
         mobile phones
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Special camera systems are used for scientific research, e.g. on board a
        <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
         satellite
        </a>
        or a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spaceprobe" title="Spaceprobe">
         spaceprobe
        </a>
        , in
        <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">
         artificial intelligence
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">
         robotics
        </a>
        research, and in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical" title="Medical">
         medical
        </a>
        use. Such cameras are often tuned for non-visible radiation for
        <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
         infrared
        </a>
        (for
        <a href="/wiki/Night_vision" title="Night vision">
         night vision
        </a>
        and heat sensing) or
        <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
         X-ray
        </a>
        (for medical and
        <a href="/wiki/Speckle_imaging" title="Speckle imaging">
         video astronomy
        </a>
        use).
       </li>
      </ul>
