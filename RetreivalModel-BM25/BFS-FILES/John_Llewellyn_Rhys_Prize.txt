      <p>
       The
       <b>
        John Llewellyn Rhys Prize
       </b>
       was a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_prize" title="Literary prize">
        literary prize
       </a>
       awarded annually for the best work of literature (fiction, non-fiction, poetry, drama) by an author from the
       <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
        Commonwealth
       </a>
       aged 35 or under, written in English and published in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-booktrusthome_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-booktrusthome-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Established in 1942, it was the second oldest literary award in the UK.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flood_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-flood-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Since 2011 the award has been suspended due to funding problems. The last award was in 2010.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flood_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-flood-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Llewellyn_Rhys_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The prize was initiated in 1942 by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jane_Oliver&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jane Oliver (page does not exist)">
        Jane Oliver
       </a>
       in memory of her husband John Llewellyn Rhys, a young author who was killed on 5 August 1940 while serving as a
       <a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">
        bomber
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">
        pilot
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">
        Royal Air Force
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       From 1987 to 2003, the prize was funded by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Mail_on_Sunday" title="The Mail on Sunday">
         Mail on Sunday
        </a>
       </i>
       . The newspaper withdrew in 2003, after the 2002 prize was awarded to Mary Laven. Subsequently, the prize was sponsored by
       <a href="/wiki/Booktrust" title="Booktrust">
        Booktrust
       </a>
       , an independent educational charity, but in June 2011 the award was suspended due to funding problems.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flood_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-flood-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Booktrust said that it "strongly" intended to bring the award "back with a bang as soon as possible" as it looked for outside funding sources.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flood_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-flood-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2010, the winner received £5,000, while the runners-up each received £500.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flood_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-flood-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
