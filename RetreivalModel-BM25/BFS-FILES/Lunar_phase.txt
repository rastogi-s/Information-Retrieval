      <p>
       The
       <b>
        lunar phase
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        phase of the Moon
       </b>
       is the shape of the directly
       <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
        sunlit
       </a>
       portion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       as viewed from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       . The lunar phases gradually and cyclically change over the period of a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Synodic_month" title="Lunar month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       (about 29.53 days), as the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbital
       </a>
       positions of the Moon around Earth and of Earth around the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       shift.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon's rotation is
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       by Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       ; therefore, most of the same lunar side always faces Earth. This
       <a href="/wiki/Near_side_of_the_Moon" title="Near side of the Moon">
        near side
       </a>
       is variously sunlit, depending on the position of the Moon in
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        its orbit
       </a>
       . Thus, the sunlit portion of this face can vary from 0% (at
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       ) to 100% (at
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       ). The
       <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)#Lunar_terminator" title="Terminator (solar)">
        lunar terminator
       </a>
       is the boundary between the illuminated and darkened hemispheres.
      </p>
      <p>
       Each of the four "intermediate" lunar phases (see below) is around 7.4
       <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">
        days
       </a>
       , but this
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        varies slightly
       </a>
       due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">
        elliptical
       </a>
       shape of the Moon's orbit. Aside from some
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_craters" title="Lunar craters">
        craters
       </a>
       near the lunar poles, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Shoemaker_(lunar_crater)" title="Shoemaker (lunar crater)">
        Shoemaker
       </a>
       , all parts of the Moon see around 14.77 days of
       <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">
        daylight
       </a>
       , followed by 14.77 days of "
       <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">
        night
       </a>
       ". (The
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        side of the Moon
       </a>
       facing away from Earth is sometimes called the "dark side of the Moon", although that is a
       <a href="/wiki/Misnomer" title="Misnomer">
        misnomer
       </a>
       .)
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phases_of_the_Moon">
        Phases of the Moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_phase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phases of the Moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">
        Western culture
       </a>
       , the four
       <i>
        principal phases
       </i>
       of the Moon are
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       , first quarter,
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       , and third quarter (also known as last quarter). These are the instances when the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">
        ecliptic longitude
       </a>
       and the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       longitude differ by 0°, 90°, 180°, and 270°, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Each of these phases occur at slightly different times when viewed from different points on Earth. During the intervals between principal phases, the Moon's apparent shape is either
       <a href="/wiki/Crescent" title="Crescent">
        crescent
       </a>
       or
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gibbous" title="wikt:gibbous">
        gibbous
       </a>
       . These shapes, and the periods when the Moon shows them, are called the
       <i>
        intermediate phases
       </i>
       and last one-quarter of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_month" title="Synodic month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       , or 7.38 days, on average. However, their durations
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        vary slightly
       </a>
       because the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        Moon's orbit
       </a>
       is rather
       <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">
        elliptical
       </a>
       , so the satellite's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_speed" title="Orbital speed">
        orbital speed
       </a>
       is not constant. The descriptor
       <i>
        waxing
       </i>
       is used for an intermediate phase when the Moon's apparent shape is thickening, from new to full moon, and
       <i>
        waning
       </i>
       when the shape is thinning.
      </p>

       Non-Western cultures may use a different number of lunar phases; for example, traditional
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawaiian_culture" title="Hawaiian culture">
        Hawaiian culture
       </a>
       has a total of 30 different phases (one per day).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">

         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Waxing_and_waning">
        Waxing and waning
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_phase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Waxing and waning">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When the Sun and Moon are
       <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy)" title="Conjunction (astronomy)">
        aligned on the same side
       </a>
       of the Earth, the Moon is "new", and the side of the Moon facing Earth is not illuminated by the Sun. As the Moon
       <i>
        waxes
       </i>
       (the amount of illuminated surface as seen from Earth is increasing), the lunar phases progress through new moon, crescent moon, first-quarter moon,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gibbous" title="wikt:gibbous">
        gibbous
       </a>
       moon, and full moon. The Moon is then said to
       <i>
        wane
       </i>
       as it passes through the gibbous moon, third-quarter moon, crescent moon, and back to new moon. The terms
       <i>
        old moon
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        new moon
       </i>
       are not interchangeable. The "old moon" is a waning sliver (which eventually becomes undetectable to the naked eye) until the moment it aligns with the Sun and begins to wax, at which point it becomes new again.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Half moon
       </i>
       is often used to mean the first- and third-quarter moons, while the term
       <i>
        quarter
       </i>
       refers to the extent of the Moon's cycle around the Earth, not its shape.
      </p>
      <p>
       When an illuminated hemisphere is viewed from a certain angle, the portion of the illuminated area that is visible will have a two-dimensional shape as defined by the intersection of an
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        ellipse
       </a>
       and circle (in which the ellipse's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_axis" title="Major axis">
        major axis
       </a>
       coincides with the circle's diameter). If the half-ellipse is convex with respect to the half-circle, then the shape will be gibbous (bulging outwards),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       whereas if the half-ellipse is concave with respect to the half-circle, then the shape will be a
       <a href="/wiki/Crescent" title="Crescent">
        crescent
       </a>
       . When a crescent moon occurs, the phenomenon of
       <a href="/wiki/Earthlight_(astronomy)" title="Earthlight (astronomy)">
        earthshine
       </a>
       may be apparent, where the night side of the Moon dimly reflects indirect sunlight reflected from Earth.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orientation_by_latitude">
        Orientation by latitude
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_phase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orientation by latitude">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
        Northern Hemisphere
       </a>
       , if the left (east) side of the Moon is dark, then the bright part is thickening, and the Moon is described as
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wax" title="wikt:wax">
        waxing
       </a>
       (shifting toward full moon). If the right (west) side of the Moon is dark, then the bright part is thinning, and the Moon is described as waning (past full and shifting toward new moon). Assuming that the viewer is in the Northern Hemisphere, the right side of the Moon is the part that is always waxing. (That is, if the right side is dark, the Moon is becoming darker; if the right side is lit, the Moon is getting brighter.)
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">
        Southern Hemisphere
       </a>
       , the Moon is observed from a perspective inverted, or rotated 180°, to that of the Northern and to all of the images in this article, so that the opposite sides appear to wax or wane.
      </p>
      <p>
       Closer to the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_terminator" title="Lunar terminator">
        lunar terminator
       </a>
       will appear horizontal during the morning and evening. Since the above descriptions of the lunar phases only apply at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_latitude" title="Middle latitude">
        middle
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_regions_of_Earth" title="Polar regions of Earth">
        high latitudes
       </a>
       , observers moving towards the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">
        tropics
       </a>
       from northern or southern latitudes will see the Moon rotated anti-clockwise or clockwise with respect to the images in this article.
      </p>
      <p>
       The lunar crescent can open upward or downward, with the "horns" of the crescent pointing up or down, respectively. When the Sun appears above the Moon in the sky, the crescent opens downward; when the Moon is above the Sun,
       <a href="/wiki/Wet_moon" title="Wet moon">
        the crescent opens upward
       </a>
       . The crescent Moon is most clearly and brightly visible when the Sun is below the horizon, which implies that the Moon must be above the Sun, and the crescent must open upward. This is therefore the orientation in which the crescent Moon is most often seen from the tropics. The waxing and waning crescents look very similar. The waxing crescent appears in the western sky in the evening, and the waning crescent in the eastern sky in the morning.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Earthshine">
        Earthshine
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_phase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Earthshine">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Earthlight_(astronomy)" title="Earthlight (astronomy)">
        Earthlight (astronomy)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       When the Moon as seen from Earth is a narrow crescent, Earth as viewed from the Moon is almost fully lit by the Sun. Often, the dark side of the Moon is dimly illuminated by indirect sunlight reflected from Earth, but is bright enough to be easily visible from Earth. This phenomenon is called
       <a href="/wiki/Earthlight_(astronomy)" title="Earthlight (astronomy)">
        earthshine
       </a>
       and sometimes picturesquely described as "the old moon in the new moon's arms" or, as pictured here, "the new moon in the old moon's arms".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Calendar">
        Calendar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_phase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Calendar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">
        Lunar calendar
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_phase_calendar_May2005.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="465" data-file-width="545" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Moon_phase_calendar_May2005.jpg/220px-Moon_phase_calendar_May2005.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Moon_phase_calendar_May2005.jpg/330px-Moon_phase_calendar_May2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Moon_phase_calendar_May2005.jpg/440px-Moon_phase_calendar_May2005.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_phase_calendar_May2005.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         May–June 2005 calendar of lunar phases
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian calendar
       </a>
       month, which is ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         12
        </sub>
       </span>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">
        tropical year
       </a>
       , is about 30.44 days, while the cycle of lunar phases (the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period#Synodic_period" title="Orbital period">
        synodic period
       </a>
       ) repeats every
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Synodic_month" title="Lunar month">
        29.53 days
       </a>
       on average. Therefore, the timing of the lunar phases shifts by an average of almost one day for each successive month. (A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_year" title="Lunar year">
        lunar year
       </a>
       lasts about 354 days.)
      </p>
      <p>
       Photographing the Moon's phase everyday for a month (starting in the evening after
       <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">
        sunset
       </a>
       , and repeating roughly 24 hours and 50 minutes later, and ending in the morning before
       <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">
        sunrise
       </a>
       ) and arranging the series of photos on a calendar would create a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composite_image" title="Composite image">
        composite image
       </a>
       like the example calendar (May 8 – June 6, 2005) shown on the left. May 20 is blank because a picture would be taken before midnight on May 19 and the next after midnight on May 21.
      </p>
      <p>
       Similarly, on a calendar listing moonrise or moonset times, some days will appear to be skipped. When moonrise precedes
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight" title="Midnight">
        midnight
       </a>
       one night, the next moonrise will follow midnight on the next night (so too with moonset). The "skipped day" is just a feature of the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prograde_motion" title="Prograde motion">
        eastward movement
       </a>
       in relation to the Sun, which at most latitudes, causes the Moon to rise later each day. The Moon follows a predictable orbit every month.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Calculating_phase">
        Calculating phase
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_phase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Calculating phase">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_Crescent_Moon_over_the_VLTI.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="848" data-file-width="1280" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/A_Crescent_Moon_over_the_VLTI.jpg/220px-A_Crescent_Moon_over_the_VLTI.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/A_Crescent_Moon_over_the_VLTI.jpg/330px-A_Crescent_Moon_over_the_VLTI.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/A_Crescent_Moon_over_the_VLTI.jpg/440px-A_Crescent_Moon_over_the_VLTI.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_Crescent_Moon_over_the_VLTI.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A Crescent Moon over the
         <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">
          VLT
         </a>
         .
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
          <a href="#cite_note-5">
           [4]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Each of the 4 lunar phases lasts approximately 7 days (~7.4 days), but varies slightly due to lunar
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
        apogee
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
        perigee
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The number of days counted from the time of the New Moon is the Moon's "age". Each complete cycle of phases is called a "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">
        lunation
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The approximate age of the moon, and hence the approximate phase, can be calculated for any date by calculating the number of days since a known new moon (such as January 1, 1900 or August 11, 1999) and reducing this
       <a href="/wiki/Modulo_operation" title="Modulo operation">
        modulo
       </a>
       29.530588853 (the length of a synodic month). The difference between two dates can be calculated by subtracting the
       <a href="/wiki/Julian_day" title="Julian day">
        Julian Day Number
       </a>
       of one from that of the other, or there are simpler formulae giving (for instance) the number of days since December 31, 1899. However, this calculation assumes a perfectly circular orbit and makes no allowance for the time of day at which the new moon happened, therefore may be incorrect by several hours (it also becomes less accurate the larger the difference between the required date and the reference date); it is accurate enough to use in a novelty clock application showing moon phase, but specialist usage taking account of lunar apogee and perigee requires a more elaborate calculation.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_parallax">
        Effect of parallax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_phase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effect of parallax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Earth subtends an angle of about two degrees, when seen from the Moon. This means that an observer on Earth who sees the Moon when it is close to the eastern horizon sees it from an angle that is about two degrees different from the line of sight of an observer who sees the Moon on the western horizon. The Moon moves about 12 degrees around its orbit per day, so, if these observers were stationary, they would see the phases of the Moon at times that differ by about one-sixth of a day, or four hours. But in reality the observers are on the surface of the rotating Earth, so someone who sees the Moon on the eastern horizon at one moment sees it on the western horizon about 12 hours later. This adds an oscillation to the apparent progression of the lunar phases. They appear to occur more slowly when the Moon is high in the sky than when it is below the horizon. The Moon appears to move jerkily, and the phases do the same. The amplitude of this oscillation is never more than about four hours, which is a small fraction of a month. It does not have any obvious effect on the appearance of the Moon. However, it does affect accurate calculations of the times of lunar phases.
      </p>

         The
         <i>
          lunar phase
         </i>
         depends on the Moon's position in orbit around the Earth and the Earth's position in orbit around the sun. This animation (
         <i>
          not to scale
         </i>
         ) looks down on Earth from the north pole of the ecliptic.
        </div>
       </div>

      <p>
       It might be expected that once every month, when the Moon passes between Earth and the Sun during a new moon, its shadow would fall on Earth causing a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       , but this does not happen every month. Nor is it true that during every full moon, the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_shadow" title="Earth's shadow">
        Earth's shadow
       </a>
       falls on the Moon, causing a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       . Solar and lunar eclipses are not observed
       <i>
        every
       </i>
       month because the plane of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon%27s_orbit" title="Moon's orbit">
        Moon's orbit
       </a>
       around the Earth is tilted by about 5° with respect to the plane of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">
        Earth's orbit
       </a>
       around the Sun (the plane of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       ). Thus, when new and full moons occur, the Moon usually lies to the north or south of a direct line through the Earth and Sun. Although an
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipse
       </a>
       can only occur when the Moon is either new (solar) or full (lunar), it must also be positioned very near the intersection of Earth's orbital plane about the Sun and the Moon's orbital plane about the Earth (that is, at one of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">
        its nodes
       </a>
       ). This happens about twice per year, and so there are between four and seven eclipses in a calendar year. Most of these eclipses are partial; total eclipses of the Moon or Sun are less frequent.
      </p>
