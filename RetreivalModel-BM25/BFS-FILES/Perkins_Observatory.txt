      <p>
       <b>
        Perkins Observatory
       </b>
       is an astronomical
       <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">
        observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Delaware,_Ohio" title="Delaware, Ohio">
        Delaware, Ohio
       </a>
       . It is owned and operated by
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Wesleyan_University" title="Ohio Wesleyan University">
        Ohio Wesleyan University
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">
        Early history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Perkins_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The observatory is named for
       <a href="/wiki/Hiram_Perkins" title="Hiram Perkins">
        Hiram Perkins
       </a>
       , a professor of
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
        mathematics
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Wesleyan_University" title="Ohio Wesleyan University">
        Ohio Wesleyan University
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Delaware,_Ohio" title="Delaware, Ohio">
        Delaware, Ohio
       </a>
       from 1857 to 1907. A devoutly religious
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist" title="Methodist">
        Methodist
       </a>
       and a man of deep convictions, he was also known as an uncompromising and demanding instructor.
      </p>
      <p>
       Perkins graduated from Ohio Wesleyan in 1857, just nine years after the university was founded. He was immediately offered a position on the faculty. Shortly thereafter he married Caroline Barkdull, a graduate from OWU’s Women’s College.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1861 Perkins temporarily left OWU when the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
        American Civil War
       </a>
       began. He intended to enlist in the
       <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">
        Union Army
       </a>
       , but was deemed physically unfit for service. (At 6’4” tall and 97 pounds, his students referred to him as “the human skeleton.”) Perkins then returned to his family
       <a href="/wiki/Pig_farming" title="Pig farming">
        hog farm
       </a>
       and worked to help feed the troops. (
       <a href="/wiki/Salt_pork" title="Salt pork">
        Salt pork
       </a>
       was a staple military food at the time.) Applying his mathematical skills to the science of pork production, by war’s end he had amassed an impressive (for the time) fortune. After the war Perkins returned to his university teaching position and lived a very frugal life on his small salary. Meanwhile, his shrewd business investments caused his fortune to multiply considerably.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1896 Professor Perkins donated the funds necessary to build the first of two observatories to bear his name. It is located on West William Street in
       <a href="/wiki/Delaware,_Ohio" title="Delaware, Ohio">
        Delaware, Ohio
       </a>
       right next to Hiram and Caroline’s former residence. This original “Perkins Astronomical Observatory” later had its name changed to “the Student Observatory” when the second Perkins Observatory was built a quarter century later.
      </p>
      <p>
       Perkins’s marriage never produced children. His older sister never married. Therefore, toward the end of his life Perkins realized he had no living relatives to whom to leave his fortune. Retiring in 1907, Professor Perkins applied himself to the creation of “an astronomical observatory of importance.” It was his desire that this second observatory be a place where cutting-edge research could be conducted. It took 15 years to find an appropriate location and secure the necessary funding (Perkins himself provided about $250,000 of the approximately $350,000 budget).
      </p>
      <p>
       Construction began in 1923 with the frail 90-year-old professor as Guest of Honor at the groundbreaking ceremony. Within a year, however, both Hiram and Caroline Perkins had died. Neither saw the completion of the new observatory.
      </p>
      <p>
       The building and telescope mount were completed in less than two years. The work was done by the Warner and Swasey Company of Cleveland, Ohio. (Warner and Swasey also built other observatories and telescopes, including
       <a href="/wiki/Yerkes_Observatory" title="Yerkes Observatory">
        Yerkes Observatory
       </a>
       near
       <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">
        Chicago
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodore_Jacobsen_Observatory" title="Theodore Jacobsen Observatory">
        Theodore Jacobsen Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle,_Washington" title="Seattle, Washington">
        Seattle, Washington
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/McCormick_Observatory" title="McCormick Observatory">
        McCormick Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Charlottesville,_Virginia" title="Charlottesville, Virginia">
        Charlottesville, Virginia
       </a>
       , and (of course) the
       <a href="/wiki/Warner_and_Swasey_Observatory" title="Warner and Swasey Observatory">
        Warner and Swasey Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">
        Cleveland, Ohio
       </a>
       .) The building included a lecture room, library, office space, walk-in vault, small bedroom for visiting astronomers, and spacious work rooms and metal fabrication shops.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, Professor Perkins had stipulated that the telescope mirror be cast in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       . At this time no U.S. companies had experience in
       <a href="/wiki/Casting" title="Casting">
        casting
       </a>
       such a large mirror, so the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Standards" title="National Bureau of Standards">
        National Bureau of Standards
       </a>
       agreed to take on the project. It can be argued that casting of this mirror launched the optical glass industry in the United States.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first four attempts to cast the mirror were unsuccessful. The fifth attempt, using a different technique, created a 69-inch (1.8 m) blank (somewhat larger than originally intended). Three years of grinding and polishing followed. When installed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope_mount" title="Telescope mount">
        telescope mount
       </a>
       in 1931, it was the third largest mirror in the world. (Prior to installation of the 69-inch (1.8 m) mirror, the observatory used a 60-inch (1.5 m) mirror on loan from
       <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
        Harvard University
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="OSU_Era">
        OSU Era
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Perkins_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: OSU Era">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Early on it became apparent that the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Wesleyan_University" title="Ohio Wesleyan University">
        Ohio Wesleyan University
       </a>
       simply did not have the staff or expertise necessary to operate one of the world’s most important observatories. In 1935 a cooperative agreement was reached with the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">
        Ohio State University
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">
        Columbus, Ohio
       </a>
       to staff and operate Perkins Observatory. For the next 63 years, for most practical purposes, the observatory belonged to OSU.
      </p>
      <p>
       The following three decades were Golden Years for Perkins Observatory. The remote skies were dark (if somewhat cloudy). Famous astronomers from around the world traveled to central Ohio to use the large telescope. Important meetings of professional and amateur astronomers were hosted on site. The library collection grew to include many rare volumes.
      </p>
      <p>
       Astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Philip_Childs_Keenan" title="Philip Childs Keenan">
        Philip C. Keenan
       </a>
       spent most of his professional life as an astronomer working at Perkins Observatory. (He was employed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">
        Ohio State University
       </a>
       , not
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_Wesleyan" title="Ohio Wesleyan">
        Ohio Wesleyan
       </a>
       .) Using the 69-inch (1.8 m) telescope he spent almost 20 years taking spectrographic plates of vast areas of the night sky. In collaboration with
       <a href="/wiki/William_Wilson_Morgan" title="William Wilson Morgan">
        William Wilson Morgan
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Yerkes_Observatory" title="Yerkes Observatory">
        Yerkes Observatory
       </a>
       , Dr. Keenan helped to create the M-K System of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_Classification" title="Stellar Classification">
        Stellar Classification
       </a>
       . (“M” is for Morgan and “K” is for Keenan.) This is the most common
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">
        stellar classification
       </a>
       system used by astronomers today.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1932 the Acting Director of the Observatory Nikolai T. Bobrovnikov began publication of a small in-house magazine known as “The Telescope.” At first this quarterly dealt primarily with research and current events related to Perkins Observatory, but in following issues it expanded its coverage of topics somewhat. In 1941 it merged with another small astronomy magazine known as “The Sky” to create “
       <a href="/wiki/Sky_%26_Telescope" title="Sky &amp; Telescope">
        Sky &amp; Telescope
       </a>
       Magazine.”
      </p>
      <p>
       Another stipulation in Hiram Perkins’ endowment was that observing sessions be open to the public at least once a month.
      </p>
      <p>
       The radio telescope known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Ear" title="Big Ear">
        Big Ear
       </a>
       was built on Perkins Observatory property and operated from 1963 to 1998. It was famous in part for its work on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SETI" title="SETI">
        SETI
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WOW!_Signal" title="WOW! Signal">
        WOW! Signal
       </a>
       detected in 1977. This instrument was built and operated by the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">
        Ohio State University
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Perkins_-_The_Difficult_Years">
        Perkins - The Difficult Years
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Perkins_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Perkins - The Difficult Years">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Central Ohio is not, as it turns out, a particularly good location for an astronomical research telescope. The low elevation and generally cloudy weather conspired to frustrate most astronomers using the 69-inch (1.8 m) telescope. More troublesome, the cities of Columbus, Ohio to the south and Delaware, Ohio to the north were both growing. Light pollution therefore became increasingly problematic. So in 1961 the Perkins Telescope was moved to
       <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Observatory" title="Lowell Observatory">
        Lowell Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Flagstaff,_Arizona" title="Flagstaff, Arizona">
        Flagstaff, Arizona
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Lowell is the largest private observatory (non-university or government affiliated) in the world. Through the end of the 20th century, the Perkins Telescope was the observatory’s largest instrument. Observing time on this instrument was shared between Lowell astronomers and the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">
        Ohio State University
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The 69-inch (1.8 m) telescope at Perkins was immediately replaced with a 32-inch (810 mm)
       <a href="/wiki/Cassegrain_reflector" title="Cassegrain reflector">
        cassegrain reflector
       </a>
       telescope. It was donated by Michael R. Schottland, an entrepreneur from Martinsville, Virginia. At that time it was the largest privately owned telescope in the United States. Currently it is one of the three largest telescopes in Ohio.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1964, three years after the Perkins Telescope arrived at
       <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Observatory" title="Lowell Observatory">
        Lowell Observatory
       </a>
       , the 69-inch (1.8 m) mirror was replaced. A new 72-inch (1.8 m) mirror made of a more modern material was installed in the old mount. The old 69-inch (1.8 m) mirror was loaned to
       <a href="/wiki/COSI_Columbus" title="COSI Columbus">
        COSI
       </a>
       in Columbus as an exhibit.
      </p>
      <p>
       Part of the arrangement between OSU, OWU, and
       <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Observatory" title="Lowell Observatory">
        Lowell Observatory
       </a>
       called for Ohio State to continue to fund operations at Perkins Observatory. This included maintaining the building, the library, and the monthly public programs. However, over the following decades, without a research instrument on site, interest in Perkins Observatory within the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">
        Ohio State University
       </a>
       Astronomy Department declined. The on-site staff shrank until there was just a part-time secretary and a building superintendent who had only a small maintenance budget. Few of the observatory’s Directors (employees of OSU) spent much time at the observatory. There was not even enough money in the budget to maintain subscriptions to prominent astronomy magazines for the Observatory’s library.
      </p>
      <p>
       For some years a small dome detached and separated from the main observatory building housed a 0.6-meter f1.8 Schmidt telescope. In 1990 this instrument was moved to Lowell Observatory and extensively refurbished using funding from NASA. In 1993 this new instrument came online as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LONEOS" title="LONEOS">
        LONEOS
       </a>
       system to detect
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-Earth_asteroids" title="Near-Earth asteroids">
        near-Earth asteroids
       </a>
       . However, at the time Perkins Observatory received no compensation for use of this instrument.
      </p>
      <p>
       At
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Wesleyan_University" title="Ohio Wesleyan University">
        Ohio Wesleyan University
       </a>
       , unfortunately, interest in Perkins Observatory also waned. Little concern was shown toward maintaining the historic property. An endowment fund of approximately $90,000 left by Hiram Perkins in his will (intended to fund observatory operations and the Director’s salary) disappeared somewhere into the OWU’s general endowment fund. Worst of all, most of the land surrounding the observatory was sold to developers. Of a sizable initial plot, by 1990 only 16 acres (65,000 m
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ) remained. (A golf course was built on the sold off property, which later also swallowed up the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Ear" title="Big Ear">
        Big Ear
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">
        radio telescope
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rebirth_and_Reinvention">
        Rebirth and Reinvention
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Perkins_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rebirth and Reinvention">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the 1990s, Tom Burns, a member of the Columbus Astronomical Society and professor of English at the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Wesleyan_University" title="Ohio Wesleyan University">
        Ohio Wesleyan University
       </a>
       , became Director of Perkins Observatory. He greatly expanded the Observatory’s public programs and visibility in the central Ohio area.
      </p>
      <p>
       A collaborative and mutually beneficial relationship was also established with the Columbus Astronomical Society. (In exchange for monthly meeting space and observatory access the CAS provides volunteer assistance with the many
       <a href="/wiki/Public_observatory" title="Public observatory">
        public
       </a>
       programs.)
      </p>
      <p>
       Major repairs to the observatory dome were undertaken, thanks to the profits made from the sale of eclipse viewing glasses for the 1994
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       . Unused office and storage space was converted into exhibit rooms, a children’s play area and a small gift shop. In September 1999 the original 69-inch (1.8 m) telescope mirror was retrieved from
       <a href="/wiki/COSI_Columbus" title="COSI Columbus">
        COSI
       </a>
       (where it had been sitting in a closet for over a decade) and placed on display.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1998 the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">
        Ohio State University
       </a>
       formally ended its relationship with
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Wesleyan_University" title="Ohio Wesleyan University">
        Ohio Wesleyan University
       </a>
       and Perkins Observatory. OSU withdrew from the 1935 agreement so it could apply its financial resources to purchasing time on the
       <a href="/wiki/Large_Binocular_Telescope" title="Large Binocular Telescope">
        Large Binocular Telescope
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Graham" title="Mount Graham">
        Mount Graham
       </a>
       . The 72-inch (1.8 m) telescope was sold outright to
       <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Observatory" title="Lowell Observatory">
        Lowell Observatory
       </a>
       by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_Wesleyan" title="Ohio Wesleyan">
        Ohio Wesleyan
       </a>
       . (The proceeds of this sale went into the Perkins Observatory Endowment Fund.) Staff members who were technically Ohio State University employees started receiving their paychecks from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OWU" title="OWU">
        OWU
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Current_Use">
        Current Use
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Perkins_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Current Use">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Currently, regular observing programs are held almost every Friday and Saturday night throughout the year. Programs are held on other evenings and during the day by special appointment. A monthly lecture series detailing various current topics in astronomy is ongoing. Occasional special events (like telescope fairs, celebrity guest lecturers, and viewings of unusual astronomical events) are also sponsored and organized by Perkins. (Thousands of people visited the observatory to see comet
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hale-Bopp" title="Hale-Bopp">
        Hale-Bopp
       </a>
       in 1997. Each time a notable
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       is visible from Central Ohio, several thousand pairs of eclipse glasses are distributed and educational school programs scheduled.
       <a href="/wiki/Ed_Krupp" title="Ed Krupp">
        Ed Krupp
       </a>
       , Director of
       <a href="/wiki/Griffith_Observatory" title="Griffith Observatory">
        Griffith Observatory
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Dobson_(astronomer)" title="John Dobson (astronomer)">
        John Dobson
       </a>
       , inventor of the
       <a href="/wiki/Dobsonian_telescope" title="Dobsonian telescope">
        Dobsonian telescope
       </a>
       , have both visited and lectured at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_Wesleyan" title="Ohio Wesleyan">
        Ohio Wesleyan
       </a>
       thanks to Perkins sponsorship.)
      </p>
      <p>
       Perkins Observatory is now the most visible and most reliable source of information related to astronomy and space exploration in central
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">
        Ohio
       </a>
       . Television stations, newspapers, other local science museums, and members of the general public rely on Professor Burns and his staff to answer questions, provide perspective, make media appearances and dispel astronomical misconceptions.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">
        Challenges
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Perkins_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Challenges">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Perkins Observatory faces many challenges as it begins the 21st Century.
      </p>
      <p>
       Like many public institutions without government support, Perkins faces funding limitations. Although the
       <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Wesleyan_University" title="Ohio Wesleyan University">
        Ohio Wesleyan University
       </a>
       is very supportive, it cannot by itself provide adequate monies for staff, program expansion, or maintenance on the historic building. (An endowment fund has been established, and donations are accepted.)
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the observatory was constructed 1923–1931, it experiences many maintenance challenges typical of older, historic buildings. Costly repairs and heating and cooling expenses eat into the limited budget. Also,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handicapped_accessibility" title="Handicapped accessibility">
        handicapped accessibility
       </a>
       was not a concern in the 1920s. Retrofitting the building for public use while keeping its unique architectural identity has proved to be difficult, costly, and time-consuming.
      </p>
      <p>
       The most troublesome challenge that Perkins Observatory must now deal with is the increasing effect of
       <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">
        light pollution
       </a>
       . The city of Delaware is expanding from the north while Columbus expands from the south. Although
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lighting_ordinances" title="Lighting ordinances">
        lighting ordinances
       </a>
       do exist which cover the surrounding area, enforcing compliance is a constant struggle. Observatory staff recognizes that the day is coming when observation of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_sky_objects" title="Deep sky objects">
        deep sky objects
       </a>
       will no longer be possible from the site.
      </p>
      <p>
       The observatory now faces new challenges in October 2009 as part of the neighboring golf course plans to develop box stores and apartments next to the observatory. This light pollution will certainly disable the observatory. Volunteers and staff are looking to the Delaware and Columbus residents to voice their opinion to the Delaware zoning commission to keep this from happening and to preserve what view of the heavens the observatory has now.
      </p>
