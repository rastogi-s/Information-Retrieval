      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lilith">
        Lilith
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_objects_proposed_in_religion,_astrology,_ufology_and_pseudoscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lilith">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       This article is about the astrological Earth moon. For the asteroid, see
       <a href="/wiki/1181_Lilith" title="1181 Lilith">
        1181 Lilith
       </a>
       . For the scientific hypothesis of Earth's second moon, see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Other_moons_of_Earth" title="Other moons of Earth">
        Other moons of Earth
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Lilith
       </b>
       is a hypothetical second moon of Earth, supposedly about the same mass as the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , proposed in 1918 by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrologer" title="Astrologer">
        astrologer
       </a>
       Walter Gorn Old, who called himself
       <a href="/wiki/Sepharial" title="Sepharial">
        Sepharial
       </a>
       . Sepharial applied the name
       <a href="/wiki/Lilith" title="Lilith">
        Lilith
       </a>
       from medieval Jewish legend, where she is described as the first wife of Adam.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sepharial claimed that Lilith was the same second moon that scientist
       <a href="/wiki/Georg_Waltemath" title="Georg Waltemath">
        Georg Waltemath
       </a>
       claimed to have discovered twenty years earlier.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambridge1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cambridge1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlyter_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schlyter-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sepharial also claimed to be the first person in history to observe Waltemath's moon as it crossed the sun, and rationalized that it was too dark to be otherwise visually detected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sepharial_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sepharial-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sepharial's comments ignored the fact that Waltemath's proposed moon had already been discredited by the
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_community" title="Scientific community">
        scientific community
       </a>
       at the turn of the century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are many readily apparent holes in the arguments supporting Lilith's existence, and the existence of this
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">
        astronomical object
       </a>
       is believed only by
       <a href="/wiki/Fringe_science" title="Fringe science">
        fringe
       </a>
       groups.
      </p>
      <p>
       In present-day
       <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">
        astrology
       </a>
       , the name Lilith is usually given to a point on the horoscope that represents the direction of the actual moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
        apogee
       </a>
       , unrelated to the hypothetical second moon. When considered as a point, this Lilith is sometimes defined as the second focus of the ellipse described by the Moon's orbit; the earth is the first focus, and the apogee lies in the same direction. It takes 8 years and 10 months to complete its circuit around the zodiac.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Planets_proposed_by_L._Ron_Hubbard">
        Planets proposed by L. Ron Hubbard
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_objects_proposed_in_religion,_astrology,_ufology_and_pseudoscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Planets proposed by L. Ron Hubbard">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/L._Ron_Hubbard" title="L. Ron Hubbard">
        L. Ron Hubbard
       </a>
       , the founder of
       <a href="/wiki/Scientology" title="Scientology">
        Scientology
       </a>
       , proposed as part of his cosmology a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_Confederacy" title="Galactic Confederacy">
        Galactic Confederacy
       </a>
       which consisted of 26 stars and 76 planets including Earth, which was then known as "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teegeeack#Teegeeack" title="Teegeeack">
        Teegeeack
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One planet in the Scientology doctrine is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Helatrobus" title="Helatrobus">
        Helatrobus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ummo">
        Ummo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_objects_proposed_in_religion,_astrology,_ufology_and_pseudoscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ummo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <b>
        Ummo
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Ummoism
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is a series of decades-long claims that
       <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">
        aliens
       </a>
       from the planet Ummo were communicating with people on the Earth. Most Ummo information was in the form of detailed documents and letters sent to various esoteric groups or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unidentified_Flying_Object" title="Unidentified Flying Object">
        UFO
       </a>
       enthusiasts. The Ummo affair was subject to much mainstream attention in France and Spain during the 1960s through the 1970s, and a degree of interest remains regarding the subject. General consensus is that Ummoism was an elaborate
       <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">
        hoax
       </a>
       . The culprit (or culprits) is unknown, but a José Luis Jordán Peña has claimed responsibility for instigating Ummoism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, there are still a few small groups of devotees, such as "a strange Bolivian cult called the Daughters of Ummo".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Vall%C3%A9e" title="Jacques Vallée">
        Jacques Vallée
       </a>
       has said that the author(s) of the Ummo documents might be a real-world analogue of the fictional creators of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borges" title="Borges">
        Borges
       </a>
       ' "
       <a href="/wiki/Tl%C3%B6n,_Uqbar,_Orbis_Tertius" title="Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius">
        Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Historian
       <a href="/wiki/Mike_Dash" title="Mike Dash">
        Mike Dash
       </a>
       writes that Ummoism began on February 6, 1966, in
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       . On that day, Jordán Peña claimed to have had a
       <a href="/wiki/Close_encounter" title="Close encounter">
        close encounter of the first kind
       </a>
       when he saw "an enormous circular object with three legs and, on its underside, a curious symbol: three vertical lines joined by a horizontal bar. The two exterior lines curved outward at the edges, which made the
       <a href="/wiki/Pictogram" title="Pictogram">
        pictogram
       </a>
       resemble the
       <a href="/wiki/Alchemical_symbol" title="Alchemical symbol">
        alchemical sign
       </a>
       for the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       ." (Dash, 299)
      </p>
      <p>
       Peña's report generated a fair amount of excitement, but it was only the beginning. Not long afterwards, a Madrid author of a UFO book received several photographs in an anonymous mailing. The photos were of a craft similar to the one reported by Peña, and bearing the same symbol. Within a few weeks, "a leading Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Contactee" title="Contactee">
        contactee
       </a>
       named Fernando Sesma Manzano became involved when he began receiving lengthy, typewritten documents which purported to come from a spacefaring race called the Ummites." (Dash, 299) Within the year, various persons (mostly in Madrid) received about 150 Ummite documents, totaling over 1000 pages. Every page of Ummite documents was stamped with the same symbol of three linked lines. New Ummite documents would continue surfacing for many subsequent years. Many others have received Ummo letters, including French scientist
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Petit" title="Jean-Pierre Petit">
        Jean-Pierre Petit
       </a>
       , a researcher at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNRS" title="CNRS">
        CNRS
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In June 2002, a scientist with the
       <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">
        pseudonym
       </a>
       Jean Pollion released, in French, his book
       <i>
        Ummo, de vrais extraterrestres
       </i>
       , or,
       <i>
        Ummo, real extraterrestrials
       </i>
       in which he analyses the "Ummite" thoughts and language. He shows that the Ummo language is different from any other language we know in that it is a "functional" language. One of the astonishing properties of this language, according to the author, is that it works without a dictionary. One must only know 18 symbols, that Pollion has named "soncepts", which if combined make up a functional description of the thing or situation that the creator of the "word" is trying to convey. Currently, more than 1300 pages of those letters have been registered, but it is possible that many other letters exist. In a 1988 letter, reference is made to the existence of 3850 pages, copies of which have been sent to several individuals, representing perhaps up to 160,000 pages of total Ummo documents. The true identity of the authors of those reports remains unknown.
      </p>
      <p>
       Dash notes that "few ufologists outside Spain took Ummoism seriously—the photographic evidence was highly suspect, and, while the Ummite letters were more sophisticated than most contactee communication, there was nothing in them that could not have originated on Earth." Still, Dash allows that, whatever their origins, "considerable effort had gone into the supposed hoax." (Dash, 299)
      </p>
      <p>
       Many scientific subjects are described in detail in the letters, including
       <a href="/wiki/Network_theory" title="Network theory">
        network theory
       </a>
       (or
       <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">
        graph theory
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
        astrophysics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">
        cosmology
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Unified_field_theory" title="Unified field theory">
        unified field theory
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">
        biology
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">
        evolution
       </a>
       . Some of this information is thought to be dubious
       <a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">
        pseudoscience
       </a>
       , but much of it is scientifically accurate. However,
       <a href="/wiki/Jerome_Clark" title="Jerome Clark">
        Jerome Clark
       </a>
       (Clark, 1993) notes that Jacques Vallée argued that the scientific content of the Ummo letters was knowledgeable but unremarkable, and compared the scientific references to a well-researched
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       novel—plausible in the 1960s, but dated by the standards of the 1990s. Controversy arose about one particular assertion of the Ummites. In 1965, they wrote they were coming from a planet orbiting
       <a href="/wiki/Wolf_424" title="Wolf 424">
        Wolf 424
       </a>
       , adding this star is 3.68502
       <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">
        light-years
       </a>
       of the Sun. This was consistent with the distance estimated in 1938, but after some additional measurements, the estimate was revised to 14.3 light-years. Fernando Sesma asked then the Ummites about this apparent mistake. The Ummites replied in another letter the same year that the first measurement is the real distance measured in the "three-dimensional framework" while the second is "the apparent distance traveled by light".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Planets_proposed_by_Zecharia_Sitchin">
        Planets proposed by Zecharia Sitchin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_objects_proposed_in_religion,_astrology,_ufology_and_pseudoscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Planets proposed by Zecharia Sitchin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Zecharia_Sitchin" title="Zecharia Sitchin">
        Zecharia Sitchin
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The work of Zecharia Sitchin has garnered much attention among
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ufologists" title="Ufologists">
        ufologists
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_astronaut_theories" title="Ancient astronaut theories">
        ancient astronaut theorists
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theorist" title="Conspiracy theorist">
        conspiracy theorists
       </a>
       . He claimed to have uncovered, through his retranslations of
       <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">
        Sumerian
       </a>
       texts, evidence that the human race was visited by a group of extraterrestrials from a distant planet in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       . Part of his theory lay in an astronomical interpretation of the Babylonian
       <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">
        creation myth
       </a>
       , the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enuma_Elish" title="Enuma Elish">
         Enuma Elish
        </a>
       </i>
       , in which he replaced the names of gods with hypothetical planets. However, since the principal evidence for Sitchin's claims lay in his own personally derived etymologies and not on any scholarly agreed interpretations, academics consider it pseudoscience and
       <a href="/wiki/Pseudohistory" title="Pseudohistory">
        pseudohistory
       </a>
       , if they know it at all.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sitchin's theory proposes the planets Tiamat and Nibiru. Tiamat supposedly existed between
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       . He postulated that it was a thriving world in a very different
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">
        planetary system
       </a>
       , with jungles and oceans, whose orbit was disrupted by the arrival of a large planet or very small star (less than twenty times the size of Jupiter) which passed through the planetary system between 65 million and four billion years ago. The new orbits caused Tiamat to collide with one of the moons of this object, which is known as Nibiru. The debris from this collision are thought by the theory's proponents to have variously formed the
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">
        asteroid belt
       </a>
       , the Moon, and the current inclination of the planet Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sitchin claims that the
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">
        Babylonians
       </a>
       associated
       <a href="/wiki/Nibiru_(Babylonian_astronomy)" title="Nibiru (Babylonian astronomy)">
        Nibiru
       </a>
       with the god
       <a href="/wiki/Marduk" title="Marduk">
        Marduk
       </a>
       . The word is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">
        Akkadian
       </a>
       and the meaning is uncertain. Sitchin hypothesized it as a planet in a highly
       <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">
        elliptic orbit
       </a>
       around the Sun, with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">
        perihelion
       </a>
       passage some 3,600 years ago and assumed
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       of about 3,750 years; he also claimed it was the home of a technologically advanced human-like alien race, the
       <a href="/wiki/Anunnaki" title="Anunnaki">
        Anunnaki
       </a>
       , who apparently visited Earth in search of gold. These beings eventually created humanity by genetically crossing themselves with extant primates, and thus became the first gods.
      </p>
      <p>
       Beginning in 1995, websites such as ZetaTalk have claimed that Nibiru or "
       <a href="/wiki/Nibiru_cataclysm" title="Nibiru cataclysm">
        Planet X
       </a>
       " is a
       <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">
        brown dwarf
       </a>
       currently within our planetary system, soon to pass relatively close to Earth. Sitchin disagreed with the timing of passage.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sitchin also postulated that
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       began life as
       <a href="/wiki/Gaga_(god)" title="Gaga (god)">
        Gaga
       </a>
       , a satellite of Saturn which, due to gravitational disruption caused by Nibiru's passing, was flung into orbit beyond Neptune.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Serpo">
        Serpo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_objects_proposed_in_religion,_astrology,_ufology_and_pseudoscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Serpo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Betty_and_Barney_Hill" title="Betty and Barney Hill">
        Betty and Barney Hill
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grey_alien" title="Grey alien">
        Grey alien
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Project Serpo
       </b>
       is an alleged
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top_secret" title="Top secret">
        top-secret
       </a>
       exchange program between the United States government and an alien planet called
       <i>
        Serpo
       </i>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Reticuli" title="Zeta Reticuli">
        Zeta Reticuli
       </a>
       star system. Details of the alleged exchange program have appeared in several UFO conspiracy stories, including one incident in 1983— in which a man identifying himself as USAF Sergeant Richard C. Doty contacted investigative journalist
       <a href="/wiki/Linda_Moulton_Howe" title="Linda Moulton Howe">
        Linda Moulton Howe
       </a>
       claiming to be able to supply her Air Force records of the exchange for her HBO documentary
       <i>
        The ET Factor
       </i>
       only to pull out without providing any evidence to substantiate his story— and one incident in 2005 when a series of emails were sent to a UFO discussion group run by Victor Martinez claiming that the project was real.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mysteriesmagazine15_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-mysteriesmagazine15-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some variations on the conspiracy story state that the name Serpo is the nickname of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">
        extrasolar planet
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mysteriesmagazine15_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-mysteriesmagazine15-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other versions state that it is a mispronunciation of either
       <i>
        Serponia
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Seinu
       </i>
       by US authorities involved in the project.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first mention of a 'Project Serpo' was in a UFO email list maintained by enthusiast Victor Martinez. Various versions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theory" title="Conspiracy theory">
        conspiracy theory
       </a>
       circulated, and were later detailed on a website. According to the most common version of the story, an alien survived a crash near
       <a href="/wiki/Roswell,_New_Mexico" title="Roswell, New Mexico">
        Roswell
       </a>
       in the later 1940s (see
       <a href="/wiki/Roswell_UFO_incident" title="Roswell UFO incident">
        Roswell UFO incident
       </a>
       ). This alien was detained but treated well by American military forces, contacted its home planet and eventually repatriated. The story continues by claiming that this led to the establishment of some sort of relationship between the American government and the people of its home world – said to be a planet of the binary star system
       <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Reticuli" title="Zeta Reticuli">
        Zeta Reticuli
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mysteriesmagazine15_21-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-mysteriesmagazine15-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Zeta Reticuli has a history in ufology (including the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Betty_and_Barney_Hill_abduction" title="Betty and Barney Hill abduction">
        Betty and Barney Hill abduction
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Bob_Lazar" title="Bob Lazar">
        Bob Lazar
       </a>
       story), having been claimed as the home system of an alien race called the
       <a href="/wiki/Grey_alien" title="Grey alien">
        Greys
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The story finally claims that twelve American military personnel visited the planet between 1965 and 1978 and that all of the party have since died, from 'after effects of high radiation levels from the
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">
        two suns
       </a>
       '.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mysteriesmagazine15_21-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-mysteriesmagazine15-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another version of the story says that "Eight (8) Team Members returned on the seven (7) month trek home. Team Member #308 (Team Pilot #2) died of a pulmonary embolism en route to SERPO on the 9-month journey; 11 arrived safely. One (1) died on the planet – and both of their bodies were returned to Earth – while two (2) others decided to remain on the ALIEN homeworld of SERPO." (
       <a class="external free" href="http://www.serpo.org/release36.php" rel="nofollow">
        http://www.serpo.org/release36.php
       </a>
       ) One criticism of Project Serpo stems from the lack of veracity of one of its alleged witnesses,
       <a href="/wiki/Sergeant" title="Sergeant">
        Sergeant
       </a>
       Richard Doty. Doty has been involved in other alleged UFO-related activities (see
       <a href="/wiki/Majestic_12" title="Majestic 12">
        Majestic 12
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bennewitz" title="Paul Bennewitz">
        Paul Bennewitz
       </a>
       ), and thus is a discredited source (or a purposeful provider of disinformation).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mysteriesmagazine15_21-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-mysteriesmagazine15-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additionally, there is no
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_evidence" title="Physical evidence">
        physical evidence
       </a>
       supporting the project's existence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mysteriesmagazine15_21-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-mysteriesmagazine15-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to Tim Swartz of
       <i>
        Mysteries Magazine
       </i>
       , Doty, who promised evidence to Moulton Howe before backing out, has been involved in circulating several other UFO conspiracy stories.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mysteriesmagazine15_21-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-mysteriesmagazine15-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Swartz also expressed that the details of Project Serpo have varied considerably with different accounts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mysteriesmagazine15_21-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-mysteriesmagazine15-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has been alleged that the entire series of posts were designed to be
       <a href="/wiki/Viral_marketing" title="Viral marketing">
        viral marketing
       </a>
       for a new book by Doty.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Bill Ryan, a chief proponent of publicizing the Project Serpo claims, announced on March 5, 2007 that he was stepping down from his role as webmaster for the Serpo material. Ryan nevertheless maintains his belief that an extraterrestrial exchange program did occur – although he states that the Serpo releases definitely contained disinformation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-serpo.org-final_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-serpo.org-final-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
