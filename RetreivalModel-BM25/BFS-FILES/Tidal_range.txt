   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Tidal range
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        tidal range
       </b>
       is the vertical difference between the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_tide" title="High tide">
        high tide
       </a>
       and the succeeding
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_tide" title="Low tide">
        low tide
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        Tides
       </a>
       are the rise and fall of sea levels caused by the combined effects of the gravitational forces exerted by the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       and the rotation of the Earth. The tidal range is not constant but changes depending on the locations of the sun and the moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       The most extreme tidal range occurs during
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_tide" title="Spring tide">
        spring tides
       </a>
       , when the gravitational forces of both the Sun and Moon are aligned (
       <a href="/wiki/Syzygy_(astronomy)" title="Syzygy (astronomy)">
        syzygy
       </a>
       ), reinforcing each other in the same direction ([new moon]]) or in opposite directions (
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       ). During
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neap_tide" title="Neap tide">
        neap tides
       </a>
       , when the Moon and Sun's gravitational force vectors act in quadrature (making a right angle to the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's orbit), the difference between high and low tides is smaller. Neap tides occur during the first and last quarters of the moon's phases. The largest annual tidal range can be expected around the time of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinox
       </a>
       , if coincidental with a spring tide.
      </p>
      <p>
       Tidal data for coastal areas is published by the national hydrographic service of the country concerned.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Tidal data is based on astronomical phenomena and is predictable. Storm force winds blowing from a steady direction for a prolonged time interval combined with low barometric pressure can increase the tidal range, particularly in narrow bays. Such weather-related effects on the tide, which can cause ranges in excess of predicted values and can cause localized flooding, are not calculable in advance.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
        Geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_range&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The typical tidal range in the open ocean is about 0.6 metres (2 feet).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Closer to the coast, this range is much greater. Coastal tidal ranges vary globally and can differ anywhere from near zero to over 11 metres (38 feet).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The exact range depends on the volume of water adjacent to the coast, and the geography of the basin the water sits in. Larger bodies of water have higher ranges, and the geography can act as a
       <a href="/wiki/Funnel" title="Funnel">
        funnel
       </a>
       amplifying or dispersing the tide.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA2_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NOAA2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The world's largest tidal range of 16.3 metres (53.5 feet) occurs in
       <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Fundy" title="Bay of Fundy">
        Bay of Fundy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAAOcean_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NOAAOcean-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       regularly experiences tidal ranges up to 15 metres between
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">
        Wales
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Severn_Estuary" title="Severn Estuary">
        Severn Estuary
       </a>
       . The top 50 locations with the largest tidal ranges worldwide are listed by the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">
        National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some of the smallest tidal ranges occur in the
       <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">
        Mediterranean
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">
        Baltic
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">
        Caribbean Seas
       </a>
       . A point within a tidal system where the tidal range is almost zero is called an
       <a href="/wiki/Amphidromic_point" title="Amphidromic point">
        amphidromic point
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_range&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The tidal range has been classified
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Micromareal
        </b>
        , when the tidal range is lower than 2 metres.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Mesomareal
        </b>
        , when the tidal range is between 2 metres and 4 metres.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Macromareal
        </b>
        , when the tidal range is higher than 4 metres.
       </li>
      </ul>
