       </i>
       ;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Persian_language" title="Old Persian language">
        Old Persian
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="peo" xml:lang="peo">
        Sparda
       </span>
       ) was an ancient city at the location of modern
       <i>
        Sart
       </i>
       (Sartmahmut before 19 October 2005) in
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkey
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Manisa_Province" title="Manisa Province">
        Manisa Province
       </a>
       . Sardis was the capital of the ancient kingdom of
       <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">
        Lydia
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhodes_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-rhodes-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       one of the important cities of the
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
        Persian Empire
       </a>
       , the seat of a
       <a href="/wiki/Proconsul" title="Proconsul">
        proconsul
       </a>
       under the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       , and the metropolis of the province Lydia in later Roman and
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
        Byzantine
       </a>
       times. As one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Seven_churches_of_Asia" title="Seven churches of Asia">
        Seven churches of Asia
       </a>
       , it was addressed by
       <a href="/wiki/John_of_Patmos" title="John of Patmos">
        John
       </a>
       , the author of the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">
        Book of Revelation
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
        Bible
       </a>
       , in terms which seem to imply that its population was notoriously soft and fainthearted. Its importance was due first to its military strength, secondly to its situation on an important highway leading from the interior to the
       <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">
        Aegean
       </a>
       coast, and thirdly to its commanding the wide and fertile plain of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gediz_River" title="Gediz River">
        Hermus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
        Geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sardis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Sardis was situated in the middle of
       <a href="/wiki/Gediz_River" title="Gediz River">
        Hermus
       </a>
       valley, at the foot of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Tmolus" title="Mount Tmolus">
        Mount Tmolus
       </a>
       , a steep and lofty spur which formed the citadel. It was about 4 kilometres (2.5 mi) south of the Hermus. Today, the site is located by the present day village of
       <i>
        Sart
       </i>
       , near
       <a href="/wiki/Salihli" title="Salihli">
        Salihli
       </a>
       in the Manisa province of Turkey, close to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ankara" title="Ankara">
        Ankara
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir" title="İzmir">
        İzmir
       </a>
       highway (approximately 72 kilometres (45 mi) from
       <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir" title="İzmir">
        İzmir
       </a>
       ). The part of remains including the bath-gymnasium complex, synagogue and Byzantine shops is open to visitors year-round.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sardis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Lydia_(satrapy)" title="Lydia (satrapy)">
        Lydia (satrapy)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Greek historian and father of history, Herodotus, notes that the city was founded by the sons of Hercules, the Heraclides.The earliest reference to Sardis is in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Persians" title="The Persians">
         The Persians
        </a>
       </i>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">
        Aeschylus
       </a>
       (472 BC); in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">
         Iliad
        </a>
       </i>
       , the name Hyde seems to be given to the city of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maeonian" title="Maeonian">
        Maeonian
       </a>
       (i.e.
       <a href="/wiki/Lydians" title="Lydians">
        Lydian
       </a>
       ) chiefs and in later times Hyde was said to be the older name of Sardis, or the name of its
       <a href="/wiki/Citadel" title="Citadel">
        citadel
       </a>
       . It is, however, more probable that Sardis was not the original capital of the Maeonians, but that it became so amid the changes which produced the powerful
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lydian_empire" title="Lydian empire">
        Lydian empire
       </a>
       of the 8th century BC.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Lydia_ancient_times-en.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="277" data-file-width="400" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Map_of_Lydia_ancient_times-en.svg/220px-Map_of_Lydia_ancient_times-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Map_of_Lydia_ancient_times-en.svg/330px-Map_of_Lydia_ancient_times-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Map_of_Lydia_ancient_times-en.svg/440px-Map_of_Lydia_ancient_times-en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Lydia_ancient_times-en.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Map of Sardis and Other Cities within the Lydian Empire
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The city was captured by the
       <a href="/wiki/Cimmerians" title="Cimmerians">
        Cimmerians
       </a>
       in the 7th century BC, by the
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
        Persians
       </a>
       in the 6th, by the
       <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">
        Athenians
       </a>
       in the 5th, and by
       <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_III_the_Great" title="Antiochus III the Great">
        Antiochus III the Great
       </a>
       at the end of the 3rd century BC. In the Persian era, Sardis was conquered by
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">
        Cyrus the Great
       </a>
       and formed the end station for the Persian
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Road" title="Royal Road">
        Royal Road
       </a>
       which began in
       <a href="/wiki/Persepolis" title="Persepolis">
        Persepolis
       </a>
       , capital of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">
        Persia
       </a>
       . During the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Revolt" title="Ionian Revolt">
        Ionian Revolt
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athenians" title="Athenians">
        Athenians
       </a>
       burnt down the city. Sardis remained under Persian domination until it surrendered to
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
        Alexander the Great
       </a>
       in 334 BC.
      </p>
      <p>
       The early Lydian kingdom was very advanced in the industrial arts and Sardis was the chief seat of its manufactures. The most important of these trades was the manufacture and dyeing of delicate woolen stuffs and carpets. The stream
       <a href="/wiki/Pactolus" title="Pactolus">
        Pactolus
       </a>
       which flowed through the market-place "carried golden sands" in early antiquity, which was in reality gold dust out of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Tmolus" title="Mount Tmolus">
        Mount Tmolus
       </a>
       . It was during the reign of King
       <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">
        Croesus
       </a>
       that the
       <a href="/wiki/Metallurgist" title="Metallurgist">
        metallurgists
       </a>
       of Sardis discovered the secret of separating
       <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">
        gold
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">
        silver
       </a>
       , thereby producing both metals of a purity never known before.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This was an economic revolution, for while gold nuggets panned or mined were used as currency, their purity was always suspect and a hindrance to trade. Such nuggets or coinage were naturally occurring alloys of gold and silver known as
       <a href="/wiki/Electrum" title="Electrum">
        electrum
       </a>
       and one could never know how much of it was gold and how much was silver. Sardis now could mint nearly pure silver and gold coins, the value of which could be — and was — trusted throughout the known world. This revolution made Sardis rich and
       <a href="/wiki/Croesus" title="Croesus">
        Croesus
       </a>
       ' name synonymous with wealth itself. For this reason, Sardis is famed in history as the place where modern
       <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">
        currency
       </a>
       was invented.
      </p>
      <p>
       Disaster came to the great city under the reign of the emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">
        Tiberius
       </a>
       , when in
       <a href="/wiki/AD_17_Lydia_earthquake" title="AD 17 Lydia earthquake">
        AD 17, Sardis was destroyed by an earthquake
       </a>
       , but it was rebuilt with the help of ten million
       <a href="/wiki/Sestertius" title="Sestertius">
        sesterces
       </a>
       from the Emperor and exempted from paying taxes for five years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was one of the great cities of western
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">
        Asia Minor
       </a>
       until the later
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">
        Byzantine
       </a>
       period.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SardisByzantineShops1February2003.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/SardisByzantineShops1February2003.JPG/220px-SardisByzantineShops1February2003.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/SardisByzantineShops1February2003.JPG/330px-SardisByzantineShops1February2003.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/SardisByzantineShops1February2003.JPG/440px-SardisByzantineShops1February2003.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SardisByzantineShops1February2003.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Remains of the Greek
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">
          Byzantine
         </a>
         shops in Sardis
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Later, trade and the organization of commerce continued to be sources of great wealth. After
       <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">
        Constantinople
       </a>
       became the capital of the East, a new road system grew up connecting the provinces with the capital. Sardis then lay rather apart from the great lines of communication and lost some of its importance. It still, however, retained its titular supremacy and continued to be the seat of the
       <a href="/wiki/See_of_Sardis" title="See of Sardis">
        metropolitan bishop
       </a>
       of the province of Lydia, formed in AD 295. It was enumerated as third, after
       <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">
        Ephesus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Smyrna" title="Smyrna">
        Smyrna
       </a>
       , in the list of cities of the Thracesion
       <a href="/wiki/Theme_(Byzantine_district)" title="Theme (Byzantine district)">
        thema
       </a>
       given by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_Porphyrogenitus" title="Constantine Porphyrogenitus">
        Constantine Porphyrogenitus
       </a>
       in the 10th century. However, over the next four centuries it was in the shadow of the provinces of Magnesia-upon-Sipylum and Philadelphia, which retained their importance in the region.
      </p>
      <p>
       After 1071, the Hermus valley began to suffer from the inroads of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turks" title="Seljuk Turks">
        Seljuk Turks
       </a>
       but the Byzantine general
       <a href="/wiki/John_Doukas_(megas_doux)" title="John Doukas (megas doux)">
        John Doukas
       </a>
       reconquered the city in 1097, the successes of the general Philokales in 1118 relieved the district from later Turkish pressure and the ability of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comnenus" title="Comnenus">
        Comneni
       </a>
       dynasty together with the gradual decay of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seljuk_Sultanate_of_Rum" title="Seljuk Sultanate of Rum">
        Seljuk Sultanate of Rum
       </a>
       meant that it remained under Byzantine dominion. When
       <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">
        Constantinople
       </a>
       was taken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">
        Venetians
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">
        Franks
       </a>
       in 1204 Sardis came under the rule of the Byzantine
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_of_Nicea" title="Empire of Nicea">
        Empire of Nicea
       </a>
       . However once the Byzantines retook Constantinople in 1261, Sardis with the entire
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">
        Asia Minor
       </a>
       was neglected and the region eventually fell under the control of Ghazi (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghazw" title="Ghazw">
        Ghazw
       </a>
       ) emirs, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cayster_river" title="Cayster river">
        Cayster
       </a>
       valleys and a fort on the citadel of Sardis was handed over to them by treaty in 1306. The city continued its decline until its capture (and probable destruction) by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turco-Mongol" title="Turco-Mongol">
        Turco-Mongol
       </a>
       warlord
       <a href="/wiki/Timur" title="Timur">
        Timur
       </a>
       in 1402.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_expeditions">
        Archaeological expeditions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sardis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Archaeological expeditions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_churches_at_Sardis" title="Byzantine churches at Sardis">
        Byzantine churches at Sardis
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Temple_of_Artemis_Sardis_Turkey4.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Temple_of_Artemis_Sardis_Turkey4.jpg/220px-Temple_of_Artemis_Sardis_Turkey4.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Temple_of_Artemis_Sardis_Turkey4.jpg/330px-Temple_of_Artemis_Sardis_Turkey4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Temple_of_Artemis_Sardis_Turkey4.jpg/440px-Temple_of_Artemis_Sardis_Turkey4.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Temple_of_Artemis_Sardis_Turkey4.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The Greek Temple of Artemis at Sardis
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       By the 19th century, Sardis was in ruins, showing construction chiefly of the Roman period. Early excavators included the British explorer
       <a href="/wiki/George_Dennis_(explorer)" title="George Dennis (explorer)">
        George Dennis
       </a>
       , who uncovered an enormous marble head of
       <a href="/wiki/Faustina_the_Elder" title="Faustina the Elder">
        Faustina the Elder
       </a>
       , wife of the Roman Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Antoninus_Pius" title="Antoninus Pius">
        Antoninus Pius
       </a>
       . Found in the precinct of the Temple of
       <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">
        Artemis
       </a>
       , it probably formed part of a pair of colossal statues devoted to the Imperial couple. The 1.76 metre high head is now kept at the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">
        British Museum
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first large scale archaeological expedition in Sardis was directed by a
       <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
        Princeton University
       </a>
       team led by
       <a href="/wiki/Howard_Crosby_Butler" title="Howard Crosby Butler">
        Howard Crosby Butler
       </a>
       between years 1910–1914, unearthing a temple to
       <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">
        Artemis
       </a>
       , and more than a thousand Lydian tombs. The excavation campaign was halted by
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
        World War I
       </a>
       , followed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_War_of_Independence" title="Turkish War of Independence">
        Turkish War of Independence
       </a>
       , though it briefly resumed in 1922. Some surviving artifacts from the Butler excavation were added to the collection of the
       <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">
        Metropolitan Museum of Art
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
        New York
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A new expedition known as the Archaeological Exploration of Sardis was founded in 1958 by G.M.A. Hanfmann, professor in the Department of Fine Arts at
       <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
        Harvard University
       </a>
       , and by Henry Detweiler, dean of the Architecture School at
       <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">
        Cornell University
       </a>
       . Hanfmann excavated widely in the city and the region, excavating and restoring the major Roman bath-gymnasium complex, the synagogue, late Roman houses and shops, a Lydian industrial area for processing electrum into pure gold and silver, Lydian occupation areas, and tumulus tombs at Bin Tepe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From 1976 until 2007, the excavation was directed by Crawford H. Greenewalt, jr., professor in the Department of Classics at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">
        University of California, Berkeley
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since 2008, the excavation has been under the directorship of Nicholas Cahill, professor at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">
        University of Wisconsin–Madison
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The laws governing archaeological expeditions in Turkey ensure that all archaeological artifacts remain in Turkey. Some of the important finds from the site of Sardis are housed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_Museum_of_Manisa" title="Archaeological Museum of Manisa">
        Archaeological Museum of Manisa
       </a>
       , including Late Roman mosaics and sculpture, a helmet from the mid-6th century BC, and pottery from various periods.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sardis_synagogue">
        Sardis synagogue
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sardis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sardis synagogue">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Sardis_Synagogue" title="Sardis Synagogue">
        Sardis Synagogue
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sardis_Synagogue,_late_3rd_century_AD,_Sardis,_Lydia,_Turkey_(19331773400).jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3252" data-file-width="4903" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Sardis_Synagogue%2C_late_3rd_century_AD%2C_Sardis%2C_Lydia%2C_Turkey_%2819331773400%29.jpg/200px-Sardis_Synagogue%2C_late_3rd_century_AD%2C_Sardis%2C_Lydia%2C_Turkey_%2819331773400%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Sardis_Synagogue%2C_late_3rd_century_AD%2C_Sardis%2C_Lydia%2C_Turkey_%2819331773400%29.jpg/300px-Sardis_Synagogue%2C_late_3rd_century_AD%2C_Sardis%2C_Lydia%2C_Turkey_%2819331773400%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Sardis_Synagogue%2C_late_3rd_century_AD%2C_Sardis%2C_Lydia%2C_Turkey_%2819331773400%29.jpg/400px-Sardis_Synagogue%2C_late_3rd_century_AD%2C_Sardis%2C_Lydia%2C_Turkey_%2819331773400%29.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sardis_Synagogue,_late_3rd_century_AD,_Sardis,_Lydia,_Turkey_(19331773400).jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Sardis_Synagogue" title="Sardis Synagogue">
          Sardis Synagogue
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Since 1958, both
       <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
        Harvard
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">
        Cornell Universities
       </a>
       have sponsored annual archeological expeditions to Sardis. These excavations unearthed perhaps the most impressive synagogue in the western diaspora yet discovered from antiquity, yielding over eighty Greek and seven Hebrew inscriptions as well as numerous mosaic floors. (For evidence in the east, see
       <a href="/wiki/Dura-Europos_synagogue" title="Dura-Europos synagogue">
        Dura Europos
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">
        Syria
       </a>
       .) The discovery of the Sardis synagogue has reversed previous assumptions about Judaism in the later Roman empire. Along with the discovery of the
       <a href="/wiki/God-fearer" title="God-fearer">
        godfearers
       </a>
       /theosebeis inscription from
       <a href="/wiki/Aphrodisias" title="Aphrodisias">
        Aphrodisias
       </a>
       , it provides indisputable evidence for the continued presence of Jewish communities in Asia Minor and their integration into general Roman life at a time when many scholars previously assumed that Christianity had eclipsed Judaism.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The synagogue was a section of a large bath-gymnasium complex, that was in use for about 450 – 500 years. In the beginning, middle of the 2nd century AD, the rooms the synagogue is situated in were used as changing rooms or resting rooms.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sardis and the Hebrew
       <a href="/wiki/Sepharad" title="Sepharad">
        Sepharad
       </a>
       may have been one and the same.
      </p>
