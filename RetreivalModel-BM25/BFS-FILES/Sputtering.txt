   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Sputtering
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Sputtering
       </b>
       is a process whereby
       <a href="/wiki/Particle" title="Particle">
        particles
       </a>
       are ejected from a solid target material due to bombardment of the target by energetic
       <a href="/wiki/Particle" title="Particle">
        particles
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Behrisch1981_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Behrisch1981-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       particularly gas
       <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
        ions
       </a>
       in a laboratory. It only happens when the kinetic energy of the incoming particles is much higher than conventional thermal energies (
       <a href="/wiki/Inequality_(mathematics)" title="Inequality (mathematics)">
        ≫
       </a>
       1
       <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">
        eV
       </a>
       ). This process can lead, during prolonged ion or plasma bombardment of a material, to significant erosion of materials, and can thus be harmful. On the other hand, it is commonly used for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin-film" title="Thin-film">
        thin-film
       </a>
       deposition,
       <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">
        etching
       </a>
       and analytical techniques. Sputtering is done either using DC voltage (DC sputtering) or using AC voltage (RF sputtering). In DC sputtering, voltage is set from 3-5 kV and in RF sputtering, power supply is set at 14 MHz. Due to the application of an alternating current, the ions inside the plasma oscillate resulting in an increases in the levels of plasma.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physics_of_sputtering">
        Physics of sputtering
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputtering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physics of sputtering">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Physical sputtering is driven by the momentum exchange between the ions and atoms in the target materials, due to collisions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Behrisch1981_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Behrisch1981-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sigmund1987_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sigmund1987-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Behrisch2007_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Behrisch2007-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Linearcollisioncascadesput.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="454" data-file-width="567" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Linearcollisioncascadesput.png/220px-Linearcollisioncascadesput.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Linearcollisioncascadesput.png/330px-Linearcollisioncascadesput.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Linearcollisioncascadesput.png/440px-Linearcollisioncascadesput.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Linearcollisioncascadesput.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Sputtering from a linear collision cascade. The thick line illustrates the position of the surface, and the thinner lines the ballistic movement paths of the atoms from beginning until they stop in the material. The purple circle is the incoming ion. Red, blue, green and yellow circles illustrate primary, secondary, tertiary and quaternary recoils, respectively. Two of the atoms happen to move out from the sample, i.e. they are sputtered.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The incident ions set off
       <a href="/wiki/Collision_cascade" title="Collision cascade">
        collision cascades
       </a>
       in the target. When such cascades recoil and reach the target surface with an energy greater than the surface binding energy, an atom would be ejected, and this process is known as sputtering. If the target is thin on an atomic scale, the collision cascade can reach the back side of the target and atoms can escape the surface binding energy "in transmission". The average number of atoms ejected from the target per incident ion is called the sputter yield and depends on the ion incident angle, the energy of the ion, the masses of the ion and target atoms, and the surface
       <a href="/wiki/Binding_energy" title="Binding energy">
        binding energy
       </a>
       of atoms in the target. For a crystalline target the orientation of the crystal axes with respect to the target surface is relevant.
      </p>
      <p>
       The primary particles for the sputtering process can be supplied in a number of ways: for example by a
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       , an ion source, an accelerator or by a radioactive material emitting alpha particles.
      </p>
      <p>
       A model for describing sputtering in the cascade regime for amorphous flat targets is Thompson's analytical model.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Thompson-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An algorithm that simulates sputtering based on a quantum mechanical treatment including electrons stripping at high energy is implemented in the program
       <a href="/wiki/Stopping_and_Range_of_Ions_in_Matter" title="Stopping and Range of Ions in Matter">
        TRIM
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziegler1984_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ziegler1984-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A different mechanism of physical sputtering is
       <i>
        heat spike sputtering
       </i>
       . This may occur when the solid is dense enough, and then the incoming ion heavy enough, that the collisions occur very close to each other. Then the binary collision approximation is no longer valid, but rather the collisional process should be understood as a many-body process. The dense collisions induce a
       <a href="/wiki/Collision_cascade#Heat_spikes_(thermal_spikes)" title="Collision cascade">
        heat spike
       </a>
       (also called thermal spike), which essentially melts the crystal locally. If the molten zone is close enough to a surface, large numbers of atoms may sputter due to flow of liquid to the surface and/or microexplosions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ghaly1994_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ghaly1994-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Heat spike sputtering is most important for heavy ions (e.g. Xe or Au or cluster ions) with energies in the keV–MeV range bombarding dense but soft metals with a low melting point (Ag, Au, Pb, etc.). The heat spike sputtering often increases nonlinearly with energy, and can for small cluster ions lead to dramatic sputtering yields per cluster of the order of 10,000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bouneau1982_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bouneau1982-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For animations of such a process see "Re: Displacement Cascade 1" in the
       <a href="#External_links">
        external links
       </a>
       section.
      </p>
      <p>
       Physical sputtering has a well-defined minimum energy threshold equal to or larger than the ion energy at which the maximum energy transfer of the ion to a sample atom equals the binding energy of a surface atom. This threshold typically is somewhere in the range 10–100 eV.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Preferential sputtering
       </i>
       can occur at the start when a multicomponent solid target is bombarded and there is no solid state diffusion. If the energy transfer is more efficient to one of the target components, or it is less strongly bound to the solid, it will sputter more efficiently than the other. If in an AB alloy the component A is sputtered preferentially, the surface of the solid will, during prolonged bombardment, become enriched in the B component, thereby increasing the probability that B is sputtered such that the composition of the sputtered material will ultimately return to AB.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_sputtering">
        Electronic sputtering
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputtering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electronic sputtering">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        electronic sputtering
       </i>
       can mean either sputtering induced by energetic electrons (for example in a transmission electron microscope), or sputtering due to very high-energy or highly charged heavy ions that lose energy to the solid, mostly by electronic
       <a href="/wiki/Stopping_power_(particle_radiation)" title="Stopping power (particle radiation)">
        stopping power
       </a>
       , where the electronic excitations cause sputtering.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schenkel1997_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schenkel1997-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Electronic sputtering produces high sputtering yields from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insulator_(electrical)" title="Insulator (electrical)">
        insulators
       </a>
       , as the electronic excitations that cause sputtering are not immediately quenched, as they would be in a conductor. One example of this is Jupiter's ice-covered moon
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       , where a MeV sulfur ion from Jupiter's magnetosphere can eject up to 10,000 H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O molecules.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_sputtering">
        Potential sputtering
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputtering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Potential sputtering">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sputterer.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Sputterer.JPG/220px-Sputterer.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Sputterer.JPG/330px-Sputterer.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Sputterer.JPG/440px-Sputterer.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sputterer.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A commercial sputtering system
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the case of multiple charged projectile ions a particular form of electronic sputtering can take place that has been termed
       <i>
        potential sputtering
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neidhart1995_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Neidhart1995-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sporn1997_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sporn1997-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In these cases the potential energy stored in multiply charged ions (i.e., the energy necessary to produce an ion of this charge state from its neutral atom) is liberated when the ions recombine during impact on a solid surface (formation of
       <a href="/wiki/Hollow_atoms" title="Hollow atoms">
        hollow atoms
       </a>
       ). This sputtering process is characterized by a strong dependence of the observed sputtering yields on the charge state of the impinging ion and can already take place at ion impact energies well below the physical sputtering threshold. Potential sputtering has only been observed for certain target species
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aumayr2004_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Aumayr2004-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and requires a minimum potential energy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayderer1999_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hayderer1999-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etching_and_chemical_sputtering">
        Etching and chemical sputtering
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputtering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etching and chemical sputtering">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Removing atoms by sputtering with an inert gas is called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ion_milling_machine" title="Ion milling machine">
         ion milling
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ion etching
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Sputtering can also play a role in
       <a href="/wiki/Reactive-ion_etching" title="Reactive-ion etching">
        reactive-ion etching
       </a>
       (RIE), a plasma process carried out with chemically active ions and radicals, for which the sputtering yield may be enhanced significantly compared to pure physical sputtering. Reactive ions are frequently used in
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_ion_mass_spectrometry" title="Secondary ion mass spectrometry">
        secondary ion mass spectrometry
       </a>
       (SIMS) equipment to enhance the sputter rates. The mechanisms causing the sputtering enhancement are not always well understood, although the case of fluorine etching of Si has been modeled well theoretically.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schoolcraft1991_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schoolcraft1991-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sputtering observed to occur below the threshold energy of physical sputtering is also often called chemical sputtering.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Behrisch1981_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Behrisch1981-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Behrisch2007_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Behrisch2007-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mechanisms behind such sputtering are not always well understood, and may be hard to distinguish from chemical
       <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">
        etching
       </a>
       . At elevated temperatures, chemical sputtering of carbon can be understood to be due to the incoming ions weakening bonds in the sample, which then desorb by thermal activation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Küppers1995_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Küppers1995-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The hydrogen-induced sputtering of carbon-based materials observed at low temperatures has been explained by H ions entering between C-C bonds and thus breaking them, a mechanism dubbed
       <i>
        swift chemical sputtering
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salonen2001_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Salonen2001-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_and_phenomena">
        Applications and phenomena
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputtering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications and phenomena">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Film_deposition">
        Film deposition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputtering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Film deposition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Sputter_deposition" title="Sputter deposition">
        Sputter deposition
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Sputter_deposition" title="Sputter deposition">
        Sputter deposition
       </a>
       is a method of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_film_deposition" title="Thin film deposition">
        depositing
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Thin_film" title="Thin film">
        thin films
       </a>
       by sputtering that involves eroding material from a "target" source onto a "substrate" e.g. a silicon
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wafer_(semiconductor)" title="Wafer (semiconductor)">
        wafer
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Resputtering" title="Resputtering">
        Resputtering
       </a>
       , in contrast, involves re-emission of the deposited material, e.g. SiO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       during the deposition also by ion bombardment.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sputtered atoms are ejected into the gas phase but are not in their
       <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">
        thermodynamic equilibrium
       </a>
       state, and tend to deposit on all surfaces in the vacuum chamber. A substrate (such as a wafer) placed in the chamber will be coated with a thin film. Sputtering deposition usually uses an
       <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">
        argon
       </a>
       plasma because argon, a noble gas, will not react with the target material.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etching">
        Etching
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputtering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Etching">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the semiconductor industry sputtering is used to etch the target. Sputter etching is chosen in cases where a high degree of etching
       <a href="/wiki/Anisotropy" title="Anisotropy">
        anisotropy
       </a>
       is needed and selectivity is not a concern. One major drawback of this technique is wafer damage.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="For_analysis">
        For analysis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputtering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: For analysis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Another application of sputtering is to etch away the target material. One such example occurs in
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_ion_mass_spectrometry" title="Secondary ion mass spectrometry">
        secondary ion mass spectrometry
       </a>
       (SIMS), where the target sample is sputtered at a constant rate. As the target is sputtered, the concentration and identity of sputtered atoms are measured using
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">
        mass spectrometry
       </a>
       . In this way the composition of the target material can be determined and even extremely low concentrations (20 µg/kg) of impurities detected. Furthermore, because the sputtering continually etches deeper into the sample, concentration profiles as a function of depth can be measured.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_space">
        In space
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sputtering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In space">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Sputtering is one of the forms of space weathering, a process that changes the physical and chemical properties of airless bodies, such as asteroids and the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . On icy moons, especially
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       , sputtering of photolyzed water from the surface leads to net loss of hydrogen and accumulation of oxygen-rich materials that may be important for life. Sputtering is also one of the possible ways that
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       has lost most of its
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       and that
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       continually replenishes its tenuous surface-bounded
       <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">
        exosphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
