      <p>
       <b>
        Pareidolia
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/ær/: 'arr' in 'marry'">
            ær
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         parr-i-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          DOH
         </span>
         -lee-ə
        </i>
       </a>
       ) is a psychological phenomenon in which the mind responds to a
       <a href="/wiki/Stimulation" title="Stimulation">
        stimulus
       </a>
       , usually an image or a sound, by perceiving a familiar pattern where none exists.
      </p>
      <p>
       Common examples are perceived images of animals, faces, or objects in cloud formations, the
       <a href="/wiki/Man_in_the_Moon" title="Man in the Moon">
        Man in the Moon
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_rabbit" title="Moon rabbit">
        Moon rabbit
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hidden_message" title="Hidden message">
        hidden messages
       </a>
       within recorded music played in reverse or at higher- or lower-than-normal speeds, and hearing indistinct voices in random noise such as that produced by air conditioners or fans.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The word derives from the Greek words
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/para-" title="wikt:para-">
         para
        </a>
       </i>
       (παρά, "beside, alongside, instead [of]" — in this context meaning something faulty or wrong) and the noun
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B5%E1%BC%B4%CE%B4%CF%89%CE%BB%CE%BF%CE%BD" title="wikt:εἴδωλον">
        <i>
         eidōlon
        </i>
       </a>
       (εἴδωλον "image, form, shape" — the diminutive of
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_Forms" title="Theory of Forms">
         eidos
        </a>
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Explanations">
        Explanations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Explanations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emojione_BW_1F611.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Emojione_BW_1F611.svg/220px-Emojione_BW_1F611.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Emojione_BW_1F611.svg/330px-Emojione_BW_1F611.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Emojione_BW_1F611.svg/440px-Emojione_BW_1F611.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emojione_BW_1F611.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A simple collection of lines can be quickly perceived as a face, and even be interpreted as expressing a particular emotion
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Pareidolia can cause people to interpret random images, or patterns of light and shadow, as faces.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A 2009
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetoencephalography" title="Magnetoencephalography">
        magnetoencephalography
       </a>
       study found that objects perceived as faces evoke an early (165
       <abbr title="millseconds">
        ms
       </abbr>
       ) activation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Fusiform_face_area" title="Fusiform face area">
        fusiform face area
       </a>
       at a time and location similar to that evoked by faces, whereas other common objects do not evoke such activation. This activation is similar to a slightly faster time (130 ms) that is seen for images of real faces. The authors suggest that face perception evoked by face-like objects is a relatively early process, and not a late cognitive reinterpretation phenomenon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19218867_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pmid19218867-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Functional_magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Functional magnetic resonance imaging">
        functional magnetic resonance imaging
       </a>
       (fMRI) study in 2011 similarly showed that repeated presentation of novel visual shapes that were interpreted as meaningful led to decreased fMRI responses for real objects. These results indicate that the interpretation of ambiguous stimuli depends upon processes similar to those elicited by known objects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22079921_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pmid22079921-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       These studies help to explain why people identify a few circles and a line as a "face" so quickly and without hesitation.
       <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">
        Cognitive processes
       </a>
       are activated by the "face-like" object, which alert the observer to both the emotional state and
       <a href="/wiki/Identity_(philosophy)" title="Identity (philosophy)">
        identity
       </a>
       of the subject, even before the conscious mind begins to process or even receive the information. A "stick figure face", despite its simplicity, can convey mood information, and be drawn to indicate emotions such as happiness or anger. This robust and subtle capability is hypothesized to be the result of eons of natural selection favoring people most able to quickly identify the mental state, for example, of threatening people, thus providing the individual an opportunity to flee or attack pre-emptively. In other words, processing this information subcortically — therefore subconsciously — before it is passed on to the rest of the brain for detailed processing accelerates judgment and decision making when a fast reaction is needed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recog_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-recog-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This ability, though highly specialized for the processing and
       <a href="/wiki/Emotion_recognition" title="Emotion recognition">
        recognition of
        <i>
         human
        </i>
        emotions
       </a>
       , also functions to determine the demeanor of wildlife.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dog_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dog-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Pareidolia can be considered a subcategory of
       <a href="/wiki/Apophenia" title="Apophenia">
        Apophenia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mimetoliths">
        Mimetoliths
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mimetoliths">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Rocks may come to mimic recognizable forms through the random processes of formation, weathering and erosion. Most often, the size scale of the rock is larger than the object it resembles, such as a cliff profile resembling a human face. Well-meaning people with a new interest in fossils can pick up
       <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">
        chert
       </a>
       nodules,
       <a href="/wiki/Concretion" title="Concretion">
        concretions
       </a>
       or pebbles resembling bones, skulls, turtle shells, dinosaur eggs, etc., in both size and shape.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the late 1970s and early 1980s, Japanese researcher
       <a href="/wiki/Chonosuke_Okamura" title="Chonosuke Okamura">
        Chonosuke Okamura
       </a>
       self-published a series of reports titled
       <i>
        Original Report of the Okamura Fossil Laboratory
       </i>
       , in which he described tiny inclusions in polished
       <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">
        limestone
       </a>
       from the
       <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">
        Silurian
       </a>
       period (425
       <a href="/wiki/Year#Mya" title="Year">
        mya
       </a>
       ) as being preserved
       <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">
        fossil
       </a>
       remains of tiny humans, gorillas, dogs, dragons, dinosaurs and other organisms, all of them only millimeters long, leading him to claim, "There have been no changes in the bodies of mankind since the Silurian period... except for a growth in stature from 3.5 mm to 1,700 mm."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Okamura's research earned him an
       <a href="/wiki/Ig_Nobel_Prize" title="Ig Nobel Prize">
        Ig Nobel Prize
       </a>
       (a parody of the Nobel Prizes) in
       <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">
        biodiversity
       </a>
       in 1996.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Projective_tests">
        Projective tests
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Projective tests">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Rorschach_test" title="Rorschach test">
        Rorschach test
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Rorschach_test" title="Rorschach test">
        Rorschach inkblot test
       </a>
       uses pareidolia in an attempt to gain insight into a person's mental state. The Rorschach is a projective test, as it intentionally elicits the thoughts or feelings of respondents that are "projected" onto the ambiguous inkblot images. Projection in this instance is a form of "directed pareidolia".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zusne_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-zusne-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Art">
        Art
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Art">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Giuseppe_Arcimboldo_-_The_Jurist_-_WGA00837.jpg">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">

         <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Arcimboldo" title="Giuseppe Arcimboldo">

      <p>
       In his notebooks,
       <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">
        Leonardo da Vinci
       </a>
       wrote of pareidolia as a device for painters, writing, "If you look at any walls spotted with various stains or with a mixture of different kinds of stones, if you are about to invent some scene you will be able to see in it a resemblance to various different landscapes adorned with mountains, rivers, rocks, trees, plains, wide valleys, and various groups of hills. You will also be able to see divers combats and figures in quick movement, and strange expressions of faces, and outlandish costumes, and an infinite number of things which you can then reduce into separate and well conceived forms."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Religious">
        Religious
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religious">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Perceptions_of_religious_imagery_in_natural_phenomena" title="Perceptions of religious imagery in natural phenomena">
        Perceptions of religious imagery in natural phenomena
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       There have been many instances of perceptions of religious imagery and themes, especially the faces of religious figures, in ordinary phenomena. Many involve images of
       <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
        Jesus
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zusne_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-zusne-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Mary,_mother_of_Jesus" title="Mary, mother of Jesus">
        Virgin Mary
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the word
       <a href="/wiki/Allah" title="Allah">
        Allah
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or other religious phenomena: in September 2007 in
       <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">
        Singapore
       </a>
       , for example, a
       <a href="/wiki/Callus_(cell_biology)" title="Callus (cell biology)">
        callus
       </a>
       on a tree resembled a
       <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">
        monkey
       </a>
       , leading believers to pay homage to the "Monkey god" (either
       <a href="/wiki/Sun_Wukong" title="Sun Wukong">
        Sun Wukong
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Hanuman" title="Hanuman">
        Hanuman
       </a>
       ) in the
       <a href="/wiki/Monkey_tree_phenomenon" title="Monkey tree phenomenon">
        monkey tree phenomenon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ng_Hui_Hui_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ng_Hui_Hui-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Publicity surrounding sightings of religious figures and other surprising images in ordinary objects has spawned a market for such items on online auctions like
       <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">
        eBay
       </a>
       . One famous instance was a grilled cheese sandwich with the face of the Virgin Mary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_vision">
        Computer vision
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Computer vision">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aurelia-aurita-3-0049_(cropped).jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="448" data-file-width="610" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Aurelia-aurita-3-0049_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Aurelia-aurita-3-0049_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Aurelia-aurita-3-0049_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Aurelia-aurita-3-0049_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Aurelia-aurita-3-0049_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Aurelia-aurita-3-0049_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aurelia-aurita-3-0049_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Given an image of jellyfish swimming, the
         <a href="/wiki/DeepDream" title="DeepDream">
          DeepDream
         </a>
         program can be encouraged to "see" the faces and limbs of dogs
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Pareidolia also arises in
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_vision" title="Computer vision">
        computer vision
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       specifically in
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_vision#Recognition" title="Computer vision">
        image recognition
       </a>
       programs, which can spuriously detect
       <a href="/wiki/Feature_(machine_learning)" title="Feature (machine learning)">
        features
       </a>
       . In the case of an
       <a href="/wiki/Artificial_neural_network" title="Artificial neural network">
        artificial neural network
       </a>
       , higher-level features correspond to more recognizable features, and enhancing these features brings out what the computer sees. These reflect the training set of images that the network has "seen" previously.
      </p>
      <p>
       Striking visuals can be produced in this way, notably in the
       <a href="/wiki/DeepDream" title="DeepDream">
        DeepDream
       </a>
       software, which falsely detects and then exaggerates features such as eyes and faces in any image.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Speech">
        Speech
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Speech">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1971
       <a href="/wiki/Konstant%C4%ABns_Raudive" title="Konstantīns Raudive">
        Konstantīns Raudive
       </a>
       wrote
       <i>
        Breakthrough
       </i>
       , detailing what he believed was the discovery of
       <a href="/wiki/Electronic_voice_phenomenon" title="Electronic voice phenomenon">
        electronic voice phenomena
       </a>
       (EVP). EVP has been described as auditory pareidolia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zusne_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-zusne-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Allegations of
       <a href="/wiki/Backmasking" title="Backmasking">
        backmasking
       </a>
       in popular music, in which a listener claims a message has been recorded backward onto a track meant to be played forward, have also been described as auditory pareidolia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zusne_11-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-zusne-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1995, the psychologist
       <a href="/wiki/Diana_Deutsch" title="Diana Deutsch">
        Diana Deutsch
       </a>
       invented an algorithm for producing phantom words and phrases with the sounds coming from two stereo loudspeakers, with one to the listener's left and the other to his right. Each loudspeaker produces a phrase consisting of two words or syllables. The same sequence is presented repeatedly through both loudspeakers; however, they are offset in time so that one when the first sound (word or syllable) is coming from the speaker on the left, the second sound is coming from the speaker on the right, and vice versa. After listening for a while, phantom words and phrases suddenly emerge, and these often appear to reflect what is on the listener's mind, and they transform perceptually into different words and phrases as the sequence continues.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_phenomena">
        Related phenomena
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareidolia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Related phenomena">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Various European ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">
        divination
       </a>
       practices involved the interpretation of shadows cast by objects. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Molybdomancy" title="Molybdomancy">
        molybdomancy
       </a>
       , a random shape produced by pouring molten tin into cold water is interpreted by the shadow it casts in candlelight.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Shadow_person" title="Shadow person">
        shadow person
       </a>
       (also known as a shadow figure, shadow being or black mass) is often attributed to pareidolia. It is the perception of a patch of shadow as a living, humanoid figure, particularly as interpreted by believers in the
       <a href="/wiki/Paranormal" title="Paranormal">
        paranormal
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">
        supernatural
       </a>
       as the presence of a spirit or other entity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Idiot's_Guide_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Idiot's_Guide-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Pareidolia is also what some skeptics believe causes people to believe that they have seen
       <a href="/wiki/Ghost#Scientific_view" title="Ghost">
        ghosts
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
