      <p>
       A
       <b>
        flood myth
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        deluge myth
       </b>
       is a narrative in which a great
       <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">
        flood
       </a>
       , usually sent by a
       <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">
        deity
       </a>
       or deities, destroys
       <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">
        civilization
       </a>
       , often in an act of
       <a href="/wiki/Divine_retribution" title="Divine retribution">
        divine retribution
       </a>
       . Parallels are often drawn between the flood waters of these
       <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">
        myths
       </a>
       and the primeval waters found in certain
       <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">
        creation myths
       </a>
       , as the flood waters are described as a measure for the cleansing of humanity, in preparation for rebirth. Most flood myths also contain a
       <a href="/wiki/Culture_hero" title="Culture hero">
        culture hero
       </a>
       , who "represents the human craving for life".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_flood_myths" title="List of flood myths">
        flood myth motif
       </a>
       is found among many cultures as seen in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopotamian" title="Mesopotamian">
        Mesopotamian
       </a>
       flood stories,
       <a href="/wiki/Deucalion" title="Deucalion">
        Deucalion
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrrha" title="Pyrrha">
        Pyrrha
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Genesis_flood_narrative" title="Genesis flood narrative">
        Genesis flood narrative
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sraddhadeva_Manu" title="Sraddhadeva Manu">
        Manu
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">
        Hinduism
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Flood_(China)" title="Great Flood (China)">
        Gun-Yu
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">
        Chinese mythology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bergelmir" title="Bergelmir">
        Bergelmir
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">
        Norse mythology
       </a>
       , in the lore of the
       <a href="/wiki/K%27iche%27_people" title="K'iche' people">
        K'iche'
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Maya_peoples" title="Maya peoples">
        Maya peoples
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">
        Mesoamerica
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lac_Courte_Oreilles_Band_of_Ojibwe" title="Lac Courte Oreilles Band of Ojibwe">
        Lac Courte Oreilles Ojibwa
       </a>
       tribe of Native Americans in North America, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muisca_people" title="Muisca people">
        Muisca
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ca%C3%B1ari" title="Cañari">
        Cañari Confederation
       </a>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South America
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians" title="Aboriginal Australians">
        Aboriginal tribes
       </a>
       in southern Australia.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mythologies">
        Mythologies
       </span>
      </h2>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

      <p>
       The Mesopotamian flood stories concern the epics of
       <a href="/wiki/Ziusudra" title="Ziusudra">
        Ziusudra
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gilgamesh_flood_myth" title="Gilgamesh flood myth">
        Gilgamesh
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atrahasis" title="Atrahasis">
        Atrahasis
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_King_List" title="Sumerian King List">
        Sumerian King List
       </a>
       relies on the flood motif to divide its history into preflood (
       <a href="/wiki/Antediluvian" title="Antediluvian">
        antediluvian
       </a>
       ) and postflood periods. The preflood kings had enormous lifespans, whereas postflood lifespans were much reduced. The
       <a href="/wiki/Ziusudra#Sumerian_flood_myth" title="Ziusudra">
        Sumerian flood myth
       </a>
       found in the Deluge tablet was the epic of Ziusudra, who heard the gods' plan to destroy humanity, in response to which he constructed a vessel that delivered him from great waters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBandstra200961,_62_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBandstra200961,_62-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the Atrahasis version, the flood is a river flood.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">
        Genesis
       </a>
       mythology of the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">
        Hebrew Bible
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yahweh" title="Yahweh">
        Yahweh
       </a>
       decides to flood the earth because of the depth of the sinful state of mankind. Righteous
       <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">
        Noah
       </a>
       is given instructions to build
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noah%27s_ark" title="Noah's ark">
        an ark
       </a>
       . When the ark is completed, Noah, his family, and representatives of all the animals of the earth are called upon to enter the ark. When the destructive flood begins, all life outside of the ark perishes. After the waters recede, all those aboard the ark disembark and have Yahweh's promise that he will never judge the earth with a flood again. He causes a
       <a href="/wiki/Rainbows_in_mythology" title="Rainbows in mythology">
        rainbow
       </a>
       to form as the sign of this promise.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 19th century,
       <a href="/wiki/Assyriology" title="Assyriology">
        Assyriologist
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Smith_(assyriologist)" title="George Smith (assyriologist)">
        George Smith
       </a>
       translated the
       <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">
        Babylonian
       </a>
       account of a great flood. Further discoveries produced several versions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">
        Mesopotamian
       </a>
       flood myth, with the account closest to that in Genesis found in a 700 BC Babylonian copy of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">
         Epic of Gilgamesh
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cline_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cline-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         20
        </span>
       </sup>
       In this work, the hero
       <a href="/wiki/Gilgamesh" title="Gilgamesh">
        Gilgamesh
       </a>
       meets the immortal man
       <a href="/wiki/Utnapishtim" title="Utnapishtim">
        Utnapishtim
       </a>
       , and the latter describes how the god
       <a href="/wiki/Enki" title="Enki">
        Ea
       </a>
       instructed him to build a huge vessel in anticipation of a deity-created flood that would destroy the world. The vessel would save Utnapishtim, his family, his friends, and the animals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Hindu_mythology" title="Hindu mythology">
        Hindu mythology
       </a>
       , texts such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satapatha_Brahmana" title="Satapatha Brahmana">
        Satapatha Brahmana
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Puranas" title="Puranas">
        Puranas
       </a>
       contain the story of a great flood,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HinduArk_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HinduArk-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       wherein the
       <a href="/wiki/Matsya" title="Matsya">
        Matsya
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Avatar" title="Avatar">
        Avatar
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">
        Vishnu
       </a>
       warns the first man,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sraddhadeva_Manu" title="Sraddhadeva Manu">
        Manu
       </a>
       , of the impending flood, and also advises him to build a giant boat.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
        Plato
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue)" title="Timaeus (dialogue)">
         Timaeus
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Timaeus_of_Locri" title="Timaeus of Locri">
        Timaeus
       </a>
       says that because the Bronze race of Humans had been making wars constantly Zeus was angered and decided to punish humanity by a flood.
       <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">
        Prometheus
       </a>
       the Titan knew of this and told the secret to
       <a href="/wiki/Deucalion" title="Deucalion">
        Deucalion
       </a>
       , advising him to build an ark in order to be saved. After nine nights and days, the water started receding and the ark was landed at
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Parnassus" title="Mount Parnassus">
        Mount Parnassus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Claims_of_historicity">
        Claims of historicity
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Outburst_flood" title="Outburst flood">
        Outburst flood
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In ancient Mesopotamia, the
       <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_King_List" title="Sumerian King List">
        Sumerian King List
       </a>
       reads
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        After kingship came down from heaven .... the kingship was taken to Shuruppak. In Shuruppak, Ubara-Tutu became king; he ruled for 5 sars and 1 ner. In 5 cities 8 kings; they ruled for 241,200 years. Then the flood swept over.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       <span id="Sumerian_floods">
       </span>
       Excavations in Iraq have revealed evidence of localized flooding at
       <a href="/wiki/Shuruppak" title="Shuruppak">
        Shuruppak
       </a>
       (modern Tell Fara, Iraq) and various other Sumerian cities. A layer of riverine sediments, radiocarbon dated to about 2900 BC, interrupts the continuity of settlement, extending as far north as the city of Kish, which took over hegemony after the flood. Polychrome pottery from the
       <a href="/wiki/Jemdet_Nasr" title="Jemdet Nasr">
        Jemdet Nasr
       </a>
       period (3000–2900 BC) was discovered immediately below the Shuruppak flood stratum. Other sites, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Ur" title="Ur">
        Ur
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kish_(Sumer)" title="Kish (Sumer)">
        Kish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Uruk" title="Uruk">
        Uruk
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lagash" title="Lagash">
        Lagash
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ninevah" title="Ninevah">
        Ninevah
       </a>
       , all present evidence of flooding. However, this evidence comes from different time periods.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Geologically, the Shuruppak flood coincides with the
       <a href="/wiki/5.9_kiloyear_event" title="5.9 kiloyear event">
        5.9 kiloyear event
       </a>
       at the end of the
       <a href="/wiki/Older_Peron" title="Older Peron">
        Older Peron
       </a>
       . It would seem to have been a localised event caused through the damming of the Kurun through the spread of dunes, flooding into the Tigris, and simultaneous heavy rainfall in the Nineveh region, spilling across into the Euphrates. In
       <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
        Israel
       </a>
       , there is no such evidence of a widespread flood.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBandstra200962_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBandstra200962-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Given the similarities in the Mesopotamian flood story and the Biblical account, it would seem that they have a common origin in the memories of the Shuruppak account.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="359" data-file-width="526" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png/200px-Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png/300px-Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png/400px-Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Earth's
         <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">
          sea level
         </a>
         rose dramatically in the millennia after the
         <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">
          Last Glacial Maximum
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Floods in the wake of the
       <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">
        last glacial period
       </a>
       may have inspired myths that survive to this day.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has been postulated that the deluge myth in North America may be based on a sudden rise in sea levels caused by the rapid draining of prehistoric
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Agassiz" title="Lake Agassiz">
        Lake Agassiz
       </a>
       at the end of the last Ice Age, about 8,400 years ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The geography of the Mesopotamian area was considerably changed by the filling of the
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">
        Persian Gulf
       </a>
       after sea waters rose following the last glacial period. Global sea levels were about 120 m (390 ft) lower around 18,000
       <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">
        BP
       </a>
       and rose until 8,000 BP when they reached current levels, which are now an average 40 m (130 ft) above the floor of the Gulf, which was a huge (800 km × 200 km (500 mi × 120 mi)) low-lying and fertile region in Mesopotamia, in which human habitation is thought to have been strong around the Gulf Oasis for 100,000 years. A sudden increase in settlements above the present water level is recorded at around 7,500
       <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">
        BP
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Adrienne_Mayor" title="Adrienne Mayor">
        Adrienne Mayor
       </a>
       promoted the
       <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">
        hypothesis
       </a>
       that global flood stories were inspired by ancient observations of seashells and fish
       <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">
        fossils
       </a>
       in inland and mountain areas. The ancient Greeks, Egyptians, and Romans all documented the discovery of such remains in these locations; the Greeks hypothesized that Earth had been covered by water on several occasions, citing the seashells and fish fossils found on mountain tops as evidence of this history.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another hypothesis is that a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">
        meteor
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comet
       </a>
       crashed into the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">
        Indian Ocean
       </a>
       around 3000–2800 BC, created the 30-kilometre (19 mi) undersea
       <a href="/wiki/Burckle_Crater" title="Burckle Crater">
        Burckle Crater
       </a>
       , and generated a giant
       <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">
        tsunami
       </a>
       that flooded coastal lands.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the late 17th century, there were famous speculations accounting for the
       <a href="/wiki/Genesis_flood_narrative" title="Genesis flood narrative">
        Genesis flood
       </a>
       by natural causes.
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Burnet" title="Thomas Burnet">
        Thomas Burnet
       </a>
       ’s
       <i>
        Telluris Theoria Sacra
       </i>
       (Sacred Theory of the Earth) had water rising from the hollow earth.
       <a href="/wiki/William_Whiston" title="William Whiston">
        William Whiston
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_New_Theory_of_the_Earth" title="A New Theory of the Earth">
         A New Theory of the Earth
        </a>
       </i>
       postulated that major changes in the earth’s history could be attributed to the action of comets.
      </p>
      <p>
       Speculation regarding the
       <a href="/wiki/Deucalion" title="Deucalion">
        Deucalion
       </a>
       myth has also been introduced, whereby a large
       <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">
        tsunami
       </a>
       in the Mediterranean Sea, caused by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thera_eruption" title="Thera eruption">
        Thera eruption
       </a>
       (with an approximate geological date of 1630–1600 BC), is the myth's historical basis. Although the tsunami hit the South
       <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">
        Aegean Sea
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">
        Crete
       </a>
       , it did not affect cities in the mainland of Greece, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mycenae" title="Mycenae">
        Mycenae
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Athens" title="History of Athens">
        Athens
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Thebes,_Greece" title="Thebes, Greece">
        Thebes
       </a>
       , which continued to prosper, indicating that it had a local rather than a regionwide effect.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the latest, and quite controversial, hypotheses of long term flooding is the
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea_deluge_hypothesis" title="Black Sea deluge hypothesis">
        Black Sea deluge hypothesis
       </a>
       , which argues for a catastrophic deluge about 5600 BC from the
       <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">
        Mediterranean Sea
       </a>
       into the
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">
        Black Sea
       </a>
       . This has been the subject of considerable discussion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A world-wide deluge, such as described in Genesis, is incompatible with modern scientific understanding of
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">
        natural history
       </a>
       , especially
       <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
        geology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">
        paleontology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To compare: some of the largest tsunamis in history, resulting from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicxulub_impact" title="Chicxulub impact">
        Chicxulub impact
       </a>
       , 66 million years ago, were thought to have affected roughly the entire
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       (or nearly all of the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">
        Western Hemisphere
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
