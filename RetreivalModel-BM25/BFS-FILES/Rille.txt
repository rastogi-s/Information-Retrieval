      <p>
       <b>
        Rille
       </b>
       (German for 'groove') is typically used to describe any of the long, narrow depressions in the surface of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       that resemble
       <a href="/wiki/Channel_(geography)" title="Channel (geography)">
        channels
       </a>
       . The Latin term is
       <i>
        rima
       </i>
       , plural
       <i>
        rimae
       </i>
       . Typically a rille can be up to several kilometers wide and hundreds of kilometers in length. However, the term has also been used loosely to describe similar structures on a number of planets in the Solar System, including Mars, Venus, and on a number of moons. All bear a structural resemblance to each other.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Structures">
        Structures
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structures">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Three types of rille are found on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        lunar
       </a>
       surface:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Sinuous rilles
        </b>
        <a href="/wiki/Meander" title="Meander">
         meander
        </a>
        in a curved path like a mature river, and are commonly thought to be the remains of collapsed
        <a href="/wiki/Lava_tube" title="Lava tube">
         lava tubes
        </a>
        or extinct
        <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
         lava
        </a>
        flows. They usually begin at an extinct
        <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
         volcano
        </a>
        , then meander and sometimes split as they are followed across the surface.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vallis_Schr%C3%B6teri" title="Vallis Schröteri">
         Vallis Schröteri
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
         Oceanus Procellarum
        </a>
        is the largest sinuous rille, and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rima_Hadley" title="Rima Hadley">
         Rima Hadley
        </a>
        is the only one visited by humans, on the
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
         Apollo 15
        </a>
        mission. Another prominent example is
        <a href="/wiki/Herigonius_(crater)" title="Herigonius (crater)">
         Rima Herigonius
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Arcuate
        </b>
        rilles have a smooth curve and are found on the edges of the dark
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
         lunar maria
        </a>
        . They are believed to have formed when the
        <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
         lava
        </a>
        flows that created a
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
         mare
        </a>
        cooled, contracted, and sank. These are found all over the moon, examples can be seen near the south-western border of
        <a href="/wiki/Mare_Tranquillitatis" title="Mare Tranquillitatis">
         Mare Tranquillitatis
        </a>
        and on the south-eastern border of
        <a href="/wiki/Mare_Humorum" title="Mare Humorum">
         Mare Humorum
        </a>
        .
        <a href="/wiki/Sulpicius_Gallus_(crater)" title="Sulpicius Gallus (crater)">
         Rima Sulpicius Gallus
        </a>
        is a clear example in southwestern
        <a href="/wiki/Mare_Serenitatis" title="Mare Serenitatis">
         Mare Serenitatis
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Straight rilles
        </b>
        follow long, linear paths and are believed to be
        <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">
         grabens
        </a>
        , sections of the crust that have sunk between two parallel
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_fault" title="Geologic fault">
         faults
        </a>
        . These can be readily identified when they pass through
        <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
         craters
        </a>
        or mountain ranges.
        <a href="/wiki/Vallis_Alpes" title="Vallis Alpes">
         Vallis Alpes
        </a>
        is by far the largest graben rille, indeed it is regarded as too large to be called a rille and is itself bisected by a sinuous rille;
        <a href="/wiki/Rima_Ariadaeus" title="Rima Ariadaeus">
         Rima Ariadaeus
        </a>
        , west of
        <a href="/wiki/Mare_Tranquillitatis" title="Mare Tranquillitatis">
         Mare Tranquillitatis
        </a>
        , is a clearer example.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Rilles which show more than one structure are termed hybrid rilles.
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rima_Hyginus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rima Hyginus (page does not exist)">
        Rima Hyginus
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Sinus_Medii" title="Sinus Medii">
        Sinus Medii
       </a>
       is an example, initially formed through a fault and subsequently subject to volcanic activity.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">
        Formation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Precise formation mechanisms of rilles have yet to be determined. It is likely that different types formed by different processes. Common features shared by lunar rilles and similar structures on other bodies suggest that common causative mechanisms operate widely in the solar system. Leading theories include
       <a href="/wiki/Lava_channel" title="Lava channel">
        lava channels
       </a>
       , collapsed lava tubes, near-surface dike intrusion, nuée ardente (pyroclastic cloud), subsidence of lava-covered basin and crater floors, and tectonic extension. On-site examination would be necessary to clarify exact methods.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sinuous_rilles">
        Sinuous rilles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sinuous rilles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       According to NASA, the origin of lunar sinuous rilles remains controversial.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history.nasa_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-history.nasa-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley_Rille" title="Hadley Rille">
        Hadley Rille
       </a>
       is a 1.5 km wide and over 300 m deep sinuous rille. It is thought to be a giant conduit that carried lava from an eruptive vent far to the south. Topographic information obtained from the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       photographs supports this possibility; however, many puzzles about the rille remain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history.nasa_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-history.nasa-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
