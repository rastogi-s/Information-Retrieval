   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Gamma (eclipse)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SolarEclipseGamma.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="200" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/SolarEclipseGamma.svg/220px-SolarEclipseGamma.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/SolarEclipseGamma.svg/330px-SolarEclipseGamma.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/SolarEclipseGamma.svg/440px-SolarEclipseGamma.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SolarEclipseGamma.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Gamma of a central total eclipse. This illustrates solar eclipse gamma: The red line shows the least distance from the center of the Earth, in this case approximately 75% of radius of the Earth. Because the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
          umbra
         </a>
         passes north of the Earth's center, gamma in this example is +0.75.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Gamma
       </b>
       (denoted as
       <a href="/wiki/Gamma" title="Gamma">
        γ
       </a>
       ) of an
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipse
       </a>
       describes how centrally the
       <a href="/wiki/Shadow" title="Shadow">
        shadow
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       strikes the other body. This distance, measured at the moment when the axis of the shadow cone passes closest to the center of the Earth or Moon, is stated as a fraction of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">
        equatorial radius
       </a>
       of the Earth or Moon.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sign">
        Sign
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gamma_(eclipse)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sign">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The sign of gamma defines, for a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       , if the axis of the shadow passes north or south of the center of the Earth; a positive value means north. The Earth is defined as that half which is exposed to the Sun (this changes with the seasons and is not related directly to the Earth's poles or equator; thus, the Earth's center is wherever the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Subsolar_point" title="Subsolar point">
        directly overhead
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       For a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       , it defines whether the axis of the Earth's shadow passes north or south of the Moon; a positive value means south.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Limiting_cases_for_solar_eclipses_on_the_earth">
        Limiting cases for solar eclipses on the earth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gamma_(eclipse)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Limiting cases for solar eclipses on the earth">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Absolute_value" title="Absolute value">
        absolute value
       </a>
       of gamma (denoted as
       <span class="texhtml">
        |
        <i>
         γ
        </i>
        |
       </span>
       ) allows us to distinguish different kinds of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_eclipses" title="Solar eclipses">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       from the earth:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       If the Earth were a sphere, the limit for a central eclipse would be 1.0, but because of the
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">
        oblateness of the Earth
       </a>
       (which causes the distance between the Earth's north and south poles to be slightly shorter than if the Earth were perfectly spherical), it is 0.9972.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        If
        <span class="texhtml">
         |
         <i>
          γ
         </i>
         |
        </span>
        is 0, the axis of the shadow cone is exactly between the northern and southern halves of the sunlit side of the Earth when it passes over the center.
       </li>
       <li>
        If
        <span class="texhtml">
         |
         <i>
          γ
         </i>
         |
        </span>
        is lower than 0.9972, the eclipse is
        <b>
         central
        </b>
        . The axis of the shadow cone strikes the Earth and there are locations on Earth, where the Moon can be seen central in front of the Sun.
        <i>
         Central eclipses
        </i>
        can be
        <i>
         total
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         annular
        </i>
        (if the tip of the umbra only barely reaches the surface of the Earth, the type can change during the eclipse from annular to total and/or vice versa; this is called a
        <i>
         hybrid
        </i>
        eclipse).
       </li>
       <li>
        If
        <span class="texhtml">
         |
         <i>
          γ
         </i>
         |
        </span>
        is between 0.9972 and 1.0260, the axis of the shadow cone misses Earth, but, because the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
         umbra
        </a>
        or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antumbra" title="Antumbra">
         antumbra
        </a>
        has a nonzero width, part of the umbra or antumbra may touch down in the polar regions of the Earth. This is called a
        <b>
         non-central
        </b>
        total or annular eclipse.
       </li>
       <li>
        If
        <span class="texhtml">
         |
         <i>
          γ
         </i>
         |
        </span>
        is between 0.9972 and 1.0260 and the special circumstances mentioned above do not occur, or if
        <span class="texhtml">
         |
         <i>
          γ
         </i>
         |
        </span>
        is greater than 1.0260 but less than approximately 1.55, the eclipse is
        <b>
         partial
        </b>
        ; the Earth traverses only the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penumbra" title="Penumbra">
         penumbra
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        If
        <span class="texhtml">
         |
         <i>
          γ
         </i>
         |
        </span>
        exceeds approximately 1.55, the shadow cone misses the Earth completely, and
        <b>
         no eclipse
        </b>
        occurs.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_April_29,_2014" title="Solar eclipse of April 29, 2014">
        Solar eclipse of April 29, 2014
       </a>
       , with a gamma of 1.0001, is an example of the special case of a non-central annular eclipse. The axis of the shadow cone barely missed Earth's south pole. Thus, no central line could be specified for the zone of annular visibility.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
