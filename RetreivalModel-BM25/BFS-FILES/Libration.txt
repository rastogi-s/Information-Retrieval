      <p>
       In astronomy,
       <b>
        libration
       </b>
       is a perceived
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oscillating" title="Oscillating">
        oscillating
       </a>
       motion of
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbiting
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">
        bodies
       </a>
       relative to each other, notably including the motion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       relative to
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , or of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_trojan#Orbits" title="Jupiter trojan">
        trojan asteroids
       </a>
       relative to
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       . Lunar libration is distinct from the slight changes in the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_size" title="Apparent size">
        apparent size
       </a>
       viewed from Earth. Although this appearance can also be described as an oscillating motion, it is caused by actual changes in the physical
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(astronomy)" title="Lunar distance (astronomy)">
        distance
       </a>
       of the Moon because of its
       <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">
        elliptic orbit
       </a>
       around Earth. Lunar libration is caused by three phenomena detailed below.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_libration">
        Lunar libration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Libration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lunar libration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Moon keeps one
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hemisphere" title="wikt:hemisphere">
        hemisphere
       </a>
       of itself facing the Earth, due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal locking
       </a>
       . Therefore, humans' first view of the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side of the Moon
       </a>
       resulted from
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_the_Moon" title="Exploration of the Moon">
        lunar exploration
       </a>
       on October 7, 1959. However, this simple picture is only approximately true: over time, slightly
       <i>
        more
       </i>
       than half (about 59%) of the Moon's surface is seen from Earth due to libration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Libration is manifested as a slow rocking back and forth of the Moon as viewed from Earth, permitting an observer to see slightly different halves of the surface at different times.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are three types of lunar libration:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Libration in
         <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
          longitude
         </a>
        </i>
        results from the
        <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
         eccentricity
        </a>
        of the Moon's orbit around Earth; the Moon's rotation sometimes leads and sometimes lags its orbital position.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Libration in
         <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
          latitude
         </a>
        </i>
        results from a slight inclination (about 6.7 degrees) between the Moon's
        <a href="/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis" title="Rotation around a fixed axis">
         axis of rotation
        </a>
        and the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_normal" title="Surface normal">
         normal
        </a>
        to the
        <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">
         plane
        </a>
        of its
        <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
         orbit
        </a>
        around Earth. Its origin is analogous to how the
        <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">
         seasons
        </a>
        arise from Earth's revolution about the Sun.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_motion" title="Diurnal motion">
          Diurnal
         </a>
         libration
        </i>
        is a small daily oscillation due to the Earth's rotation, which carries an observer first to one side and then to the other side of the straight line joining Earth's and the Moon's centers, allowing the observer to look first around one side of the Moon and then around the other—because the observer is on the surface of the Earth, not at its center.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trojan_libration">
        Trojan libration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Libration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trojan libration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit" title="Horseshoe orbit">
        Horseshoe orbit
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1772
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange" title="Lagrange">
        Lagrange
       </a>
       's analyses determined that small bodies can stably share the same orbit as a planet if they remain near
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrange points
       </a>
       , which are 60° ahead of or behind the planet in its orbit. Such ‘
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_(astronomy)" title="Trojan (astronomy)">
        trojan asteroids
       </a>
       ’ have been found co-orbiting with Earth,
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       . Trojan asteroids associated with Earth are difficult to observe in the
       <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">
        visible spectrum
       </a>
       , as their libration paths are such that they would be visible primarily in the daylight sky. In 2010, however, using
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       observation techniques, the asteroid
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2010_TK7" title="2010 TK7">
         2010 TK
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           7
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       was found to be a trojan companion of the Earth; it librates around the leading Lagrange point,
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
       </a>
       , in a stable orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
