   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Antoine Caron
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Antoine Caron
       </b>
       (1521–1599) was a
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        French
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Master_craftsman" title="Master craftsman">
        master
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">
        glassmaker
       </a>
       , illustrator,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Mannerist" title="Northern Mannerist">
        Northern Mannerist
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">
        painter
       </a>
       and a product of the
       <a href="/wiki/School_of_Fontainebleau" title="School of Fontainebleau">
        School of Fontainebleau
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       He is one of the few French painters of his time who had a pronounced artistic personality.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His work reflects the refined, although highly unstable, atmosphere at the court of the
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Valois" title="House of Valois">
        House of Valois
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Wars_of_Religion" title="French Wars of Religion">
        French Wars of Religion
       </a>
       of 1560 to 1598.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Life">
        Life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_Caron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Caron was born in
       <a href="/wiki/Beauvais" title="Beauvais">
        Beauvais
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       He began painting in his teens doing frescos for a number of churches. Between 1540 and 1550 he worked under
       <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Primaticcio" title="Francesco Primaticcio">
        Primaticcio
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_dell%27Abbate" title="Niccolò dell'Abbate">
        Niccolò dell'Abbate
       </a>
       at the School of Fontainebleau. In 1561, he was appointed the
       <a href="/wiki/Court_painter" title="Court painter">
        court painter
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Catherine_de%27_Medici" title="Catherine de' Medici">
        Catherine de' Medici
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_France" title="Henry II of France">
        Henry II of France
       </a>
       . As court painter he also had the duties of organizing the court pageants. In this way he was involved in organizing the ceremony and
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_entry" title="Royal entry">
        royal entry
       </a>
       for the coronation of
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_IX_of_France" title="Charles IX of France">
        Charles IX
       </a>
       in Paris and the wedding of
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">
        Henry IV of France
       </a>
       with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marguerite_de_Valois" title="Marguerite de Valois">
        Marguerite de Valois
       </a>
       . Some of his surviving illustrations are from these pageants.
      </p>
      <p>
       His drawings of festivities at the court of Charles IX are likely sources for the depiction of the court in the
       <a href="/wiki/Valois_Tapestries" title="Valois Tapestries">
        Valois Tapestries
       </a>
       . He died in Paris in 1599.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Art">
        Art
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_Caron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Art">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Lion_of_Barbarossa_by_Antoine_Caron_1562.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2066" data-file-width="2803" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/The_Lion_of_Barbarossa_by_Antoine_Caron_1562.jpg/220px-The_Lion_of_Barbarossa_by_Antoine_Caron_1562.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/The_Lion_of_Barbarossa_by_Antoine_Caron_1562.jpg/330px-The_Lion_of_Barbarossa_by_Antoine_Caron_1562.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/The_Lion_of_Barbarossa_by_Antoine_Caron_1562.jpg/440px-The_Lion_of_Barbarossa_by_Antoine_Caron_1562.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Lion_of_Barbarossa_by_Antoine_Caron_1562.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          The Lion of
          <a href="/wiki/Hayreddin_Barbarossa" title="Hayreddin Barbarossa">
           Barbarossa
          </a>
         </i>
         by Antoine Caron, circa 1562, thought to depict the lion given to
         <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">
          Francis I
         </a>
         during the
         <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_embassy_to_France_(1533)" title="Ottoman embassy to France (1533)">
          Ottoman embassy to France (1533)
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Not many of Caron’s works survive, but they include historical and allegorical subjects, court ceremonies, astrological scenes, and his massacres, done in the mid-1560s. An example is his only signed and dated painting,
       <i>
        Massacres under the Triumvirate
       </i>
       (1566) which hangs in the
       <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">
        Louvre
       </a>
       . Caron used bright colors and incorporated unusual architectural forms.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He often placed his human figures almost insignificantly on grand stages, as did his mentor dell'Abbate. His figures tend to be elongated, even in portraits such as
       <i>
        Portrait of a Lady
       </i>
       (1577).
      </p>
      <p>
       Many works attributed to him are also attributed to others. As there is minimal documentation of French painting in that era, this is not unusual. Because Caron is relatively well known, his name is likely to be attached to paintings similar to his known works.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In some cases, such painting are now ascribed "to the workshop of Antoine Caron", for example,
       <i>
        The Submission of Milan to Francis I in 1515
       </i>
       (c.1570).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milan_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Milan-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
