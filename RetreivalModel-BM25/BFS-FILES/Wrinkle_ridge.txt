      <p>
       A
       <b>
        wrinkle ridge
       </b>
       is a type of feature commonly found on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_maria" title="Lunar maria">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       . These features are low, sinuous
       <a href="/wiki/Ridge" title="Ridge">
        ridges
       </a>
       formed on the mare surface that can extend for up to several hundred kilometers. Wrinkle ridges are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic">
        tectonic
       </a>
       features created when the
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basaltic
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
        lava
       </a>
       cooled and contracted. They frequently outline ring structures buried within the mare, follow circular patterns outlining the mare, or intersect protruding peaks. They are sometimes called
       <i>
        veins
       </i>
       due to their resemblance to the
       <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">
        veins
       </a>
       that protrude from beneath the skin. These are found near craters.
      </p>
      <p>
       Wrinkle ridges are named with the Latin designation dorsum (plural dorsa). The standard
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nomenclature" title="Planetary nomenclature">
        nomenclature
       </a>
       uses the names of people to identify wrinkle ridges on the Moon. Thus the Dorsa Burnet are named for
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Burnet" title="Thomas Burnet">
        Thomas Burnet
       </a>
       , and the Dorsum Owen is named after
       <a href="/wiki/George_Owen_of_Henllys" title="George Owen of Henllys">
        George Owen of Henllys
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       Wrinkle ridges can also be found on
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       , for example in
       <a href="/wiki/Chryse_Planitia" title="Chryse Planitia">
        Chryse Planitia
       </a>
       , as well as on several of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroids" title="Asteroids">
        asteroids
       </a>
       that have been visited by spacecraft, as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       , and a couple of moons of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . Although several hypotheses have been advanced as causes of Martian wrinkle ridges, today they are generally considered to be of tectonic origin. They involve folding and faulting.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are evidence of compressional stresses in Mars’ crust. The stress created a crack (fault) where one side was pushed on top of the other side, also known as a thrust fault.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
