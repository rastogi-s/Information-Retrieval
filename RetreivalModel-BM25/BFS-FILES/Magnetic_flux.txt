      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
        physics
       </a>
       , specifically
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">
        electromagnetism
       </a>
       , the
       <b>
        magnetic flux
       </b>
       (often denoted
       <span class="texhtml">
        Φ
       </span>
       or
       <span class="texhtml">
        Φ
        <sub>
         <i>
          <b>
           B
          </b>
         </i>
        </sub>
       </span>
       ) through a surface is the surface
       <a href="/wiki/Integral" title="Integral">
        integral
       </a>
       of the normal component of the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       <b>
        B
       </b>
       passing through that surface. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">
        SI
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_of_measurement" title="Unit of measurement">
        unit
       </a>
       of magnetic flux is the
       <a href="/wiki/Weber_(unit)" title="Weber (unit)">
        weber
       </a>
       (Wb) (in derived units: volt-seconds), and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centimetre_gram_second_system_of_units" title="Centimetre gram second system of units">
        CGS
       </a>
       unit is the
       <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_(unit)" title="Maxwell (unit)">
        maxwell
       </a>
       . Magnetic flux is usually measured with a fluxmeter, which contains measuring coils and
       <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">
        electronics
       </a>
       , that evaluates the change of
       <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">
        voltage
       </a>
       in the measuring coils to calculate the magnetic flux.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tmulti tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:254px;max-width:254px">
        <div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:252px;max-width:252px">

          </a>
         </div>
         <div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">
          The magnetic flux through a surface—when the magnetic field is variable—relies on splitting the surface into small surface elements, over which the magnetic field can be considered to be locally constant. The total flux is then a formal summation of these surface elements (see
          <a href="/wiki/Surface_integral" title="Surface integral">
           surface integration
          </a>
          ).
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:252px;max-width:252px">
         <div class="thumbimage">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Surface_normal.png">
           <img alt="" data-file-height="241" data-file-width="499" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Surface_normal.png/250px-Surface_normal.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Surface_normal.png/375px-Surface_normal.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f6/Surface_normal.png 2x" width="250"/>
          </a>
         </div>
         <div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">
          Each point on a surface is associated with a direction, called the
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_normal" title="Surface normal">
           surface normal
          </a>
          ; the magnetic flux through a point is then the component of the magnetic field along this direction.
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The magnetic interaction is described in terms of a
       <a href="/wiki/Vector_field" title="Vector field">
        vector field
       </a>
       , where each point in space (and time) is associated with a vector that determines what force a moving charge would experience at that point (see
       <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">
        Lorentz force
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since a vector field is quite difficult to visualize at first, in elementary physics one may instead visualize this field with
       <a href="/wiki/Field_line" title="Field line">
        field lines
       </a>
       . The magnetic flux through some surface, in this simplified picture, is proportional to the number of field lines passing through that surface (in some contexts, the flux may be defined to be precisely the number of field lines passing through that surface; although technically misleading, this distinction is not important). Note that the magnetic flux is the
       <i>
        net
       </i>
       number of field lines passing through that surface; that is, the number passing through in one direction minus the number passing through in the other direction (see below for deciding in which direction the field lines carry a positive sign and in which they carry a negative sign).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>

      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Gauss%27s_law_for_magnetism" title="Gauss's law for magnetism">
        Gauss's law for magnetism
       </a>
       , which is one of the four
       <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell's equations">
        Maxwell's equations
       </a>
       , states that the total magnetic flux through a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed_surface" title="Closed surface">
        closed surface
       </a>
       is equal to zero. (A "closed surface" is a surface that completely encloses a volume(s) with no holes.) This law is a consequence of the empirical observation that
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_monopole" title="Magnetic monopole">
        magnetic monopoles
       </a>
       have never been found.
      </p>

         <b>

         <b>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed_surface" title="Closed surface">

       </a>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_surface" title="Open surface">
        open surface
       </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">
         electric charge
        </a>
        inside the surface
        <i>
         S
        </i>
        ,
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         ε
        </i>
        <sub>
         0
        </sub>
        is the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_constant" title="Electric constant">
         electric constant
        </a>
        (a universal constant, also called the "
        <a href="/wiki/Permittivity" title="Permittivity">
         permittivity
        </a>
        of free space").
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Note that the
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_flux" title="Electric flux">
        flux of
        <b>
         E
        </b>
       </a>
       through a closed surface is
       <i>
        not
       </i>
       always zero; this indicates the presence of "electric monopoles", that is, free positive or negative
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">
        charges
       </a>
       .
