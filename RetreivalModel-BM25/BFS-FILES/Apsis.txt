      <p>
       An
       <b>
        apsis
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>

       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">

       </span>

       <b>
        apsides
       </b>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p

           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">

            d

           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>

            z

          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       , Greek: ἁψῖδες) is an extreme point in an object's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       . The word comes via Latin from Greek and is
       <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
        cognate
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Apse" title="Apse">
        apse
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For
       <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">
        elliptic orbits
       </a>
       about a larger body, there are two apsides, named with the prefixes
       <i>
        peri-
       </i>
       (from περί
       <i>
        (peri)
       </i>
       , meaning 'near') and
       <i>
        ap-
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        apo-
       </i>
       (from ἀπ(ό)
       <i>
        (ap(ó))
       </i>
       , meaning 'away from') added to a reference to the thing being orbited.

         <a href="#cite_note-nasaglossary-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        For any orbit around a center of mass, there are the terms
        <b>
         periapsis
        </b>
        and
        <b>
         apoapsis
        </b>
        (or
        <i>
         apapsis
        </i>
        ).
        <b>
         Pericenter
        </b>
        and
        <b>
         apocenter
        </b>
        are equivalent alternatives.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A straight line connecting the periapsis and apoapsis is the
       <i>
        line of apsides
       </i>
       . This is the major axis of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        ellipse
       </a>
       , its greatest diameter. For a two-body system the
       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
        center of mass
       </a>
       of the system lies on this line at one of the two
       <a href="/wiki/Conic_section" title="Conic section">
        foci
       </a>
       of the ellipse. When one body is sufficiently larger than the other it may be taken to be at this focus. However whether or not this is the case, both bodies are in
       <a href="/wiki/Similarity_(geometry)" title="Similarity (geometry)">
        similar
       </a>
       elliptical orbits each having one focus at the system's
       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
        center of mass
       </a>
       , with their respective lines of apsides being of length inversely proportional to their masses. Historically, in
       <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_model" title="Geocentric model">
        geocentric systems
       </a>
       , apsides were measured from the center of the Earth. However, in the case of the Moon, the center of mass of the Earth–Moon system, or
       <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">
        Earth–Moon barycenter
       </a>
       , as the common focus of both the Moon's and Earth's orbits about each other, is about 75% of the way from Earth's center to its surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_mechanics" title="Orbital mechanics">
        orbital mechanics
       </a>
       , the apsis technically refers to the distance measured between the
       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
        centers of mass
       </a>
       of the central and orbiting body. However, in the case of
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       , the family of terms are commonly used to refer to the orbital
       <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">
        altitude
       </a>
       of the spacecraft from the surface of the central body (assuming a constant, standard reference radius).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
        Terminology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The words "pericenter" and "apocenter" are often seen, although periapsis/apoapsis are preferred in technical usage.
      </p>
      <p>
       Various related terms are used for other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">
        celestial objects
       </a>
       . The '-gee', '-helion', '-astron' and '-galacticon' forms are frequently used in the astronomical literature when referring to the Earth, Sun, stars and the Galactic Center respectively. The suffix '-jove' is occasionally used for Jupiter, while '-saturnium' has very rarely been used in the last 50 years for Saturn. The '-gee' form is commonly used as a generic 'closest approach to planet' term instead of specifically applying to the Earth. During the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       , the terms
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pericynthion" title="Pericynthion">
         pericynthion
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        apocynthion
       </i>
       (referencing Cynthia, an alternative name for the Greek Moon goddess
       <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">
        Artemis
       </a>
       ) were used when referring to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Regarding black holes, the term
       <i>
        peri/apomelasma
       </i>
       (from a Greek root) was used by physicist
       <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_A._Landis" title="Geoffrey A. Landis">
        Geoffrey A. Landis
       </a>
       in 1998,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       before
       <i>
        peri/aponigricon
       </i>
       (from Latin) appeared in the scientific literature in 2002,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as well as
       <i>
        peri/apobothron
       </i>
       (from Greek
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Bothros" title="Bothros">
         bothros
        </a>
       </i>
       , meaning hole or pit).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Perihelion_and_aphelion_of_the_Earth">
        Perihelion and aphelion of the Earth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Perihelion and aphelion of the Earth">

         ]
        </span>
       </span>

      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">

      <p>
       For the orbit of the Earth around the Sun, the time of apsis is often expressed in terms of a time relative to seasons, since this determines the contribution of the elliptical orbit to seasonal variations. The variation of the seasons is primarily controlled by the annual cycle of the elevation angle of the Sun, which is a result of the tilt of the axis of the Earth measured from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_of_the_ecliptic" title="Plane of the ecliptic">
        plane of the ecliptic
       </a>
       . The Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       and other orbital elements are not constant, but vary slowly due to the perturbing effects of the planets and other objects in the solar system. See
       <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">
        Milankovitch cycles
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       On a very long time scale, the dates of the perihelion and of the aphelion progress through the seasons, and they make one complete cycle in 22,000 to 26,000 years. There is a corresponding movement of the position of the stars as seen from Earth that is called the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">
        apsidal precession
       </a>
       . (This is closely related to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axial_precession_(astronomy)" title="Axial precession (astronomy)">
        precession of the axis
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       Currently, the Earth reaches perihelion in early January, approximately 14 days after the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/December_Solstice" title="December Solstice">
        December Solstice
       </a>
       . At perihelion, the Earth's center is about
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6999983290000000000♠
        </span>
        0.983
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         29
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical units
       </a>
       (AU) or 147,098,070 km (91,402,500 mi) from the Sun's center.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Earth reaches aphelion currently in early July, approximately 14 days after the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/June_Solstice" title="June Solstice">
        June Solstice
       </a>
       . The aphelion distance between the Earth's and Sun's centers is currently about
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7011152097651119397♠
        </span>
        1.016
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         71
        </span>
        AU
       </span>
       or 152,097,700 km (94,509,100 mi).
      </p>
      <p>
       In the short term, the dates of perihelion and aphelion can vary up to 2 days from one year to another.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This significant variation is due to the presence of the Moon: while the Earth–Moon barycenter is moving on a stable orbit around the Sun, the position of the Earth's center which is on average about 4,700 kilometres (2,900 mi) from the barycenter, could be shifted in any direction from it – and this affects the timing of the actual closest approach between the Sun's and the Earth's centers (which in turn defines the timing of perihelion in a given year).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Astronomers commonly express the timing of perihelion relative to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernal_equinox" title="Vernal equinox">
        vernal equinox
       </a>
       not in terms of days and hours, but rather as an angle of orbital displacement, the so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude_of_the_periapsis" title="Longitude of the periapsis">
        longitude of the periapsis
       </a>
       (also called longitude of the pericenter). For the orbit of the Earth, this is called the
       <i>
        longitude of perihelion
       </i>
       , and in 2000 it was about 282.895°; by the year 2010, this had advanced by a small fraction of a degree to about 283.067°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
