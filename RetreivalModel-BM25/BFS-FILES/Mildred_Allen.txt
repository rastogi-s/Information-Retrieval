      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
        Biography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mildred_Allen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">
        Early life and education
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mildred_Allen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and education">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Mildred Allen was born in
       <a href="/wiki/Sharon,_Massachusetts" title="Sharon, Massachusetts">
        Sharon, Massachusetts
       </a>
       to MIT professor C. Frank Allen and Caroline Hadley Allen. She had one younger sister, Margaret Allen Anderson.
      </p>
      <p>
       Allen graduated from
       <a href="/wiki/Vassar_College" title="Vassar College">
        Vassar College
       </a>
       in 1916 with
       <a href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa" title="Phi Beta Kappa">
        Phi Beta Kappa
       </a>
       honors. She completed her doctoral studies in physics in 1922 at
       <a href="/wiki/Clark_University" title="Clark University">
        Clark University
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Gordon_Webster" title="Arthur Gordon Webster">
        Arthur Gordon Webster
       </a>
       , with thesis research done at
       <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">
        Massachusetts Institute of Technology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
        Career
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mildred_Allen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       During the 1920s and early 1930s, Allen taught at
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Holyoke_College" title="Mount Holyoke College">
        Mount Holyoke
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Wellesley_College" title="Wellesley College">
        Wellesley
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oberlin_College" title="Oberlin College">
        Oberlin Colleges
       </a>
       and undertook post-doctoral work at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">
        University of Chicago
       </a>
       and at
       <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">
        Yale University
       </a>
       . She began working with
       <a href="/wiki/William_Francis_Gray_Swann" title="William Francis Gray Swann">
        William Francis Gray Swann
       </a>
       at Yale and continued work under his direction with the Bartol Research Foundation between 1927 and 1930. She also did research at
       <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
        Harvard University
       </a>
       before becoming a professor at Mount Holyoke, where she taught until her retirement in 1959.
      </p>
      <p>
       For nearly 20 years, starting in the early 1960s, Allen collaborated with
       <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Saxl" title="Erwin Saxl">
        Erwin Saxl
       </a>
       , an industrial physicist living in Harvard, Massachusetts, on experiments with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torsion_pendulum" title="Torsion pendulum">
        torsion pendulum
       </a>
       . Allen and Saxl reported anomalous changes in the period of a torsion pendulum during a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       in 1970 and hypothesized that “gravitational theory needs to be modified”.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Their measurements, and
       <a href="/wiki/Allais_effect" title="Allais effect">
        similar anomalies
       </a>
       earlier observed by
       <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Allais" title="Maurice Allais">
        Allais
       </a>
       using a paraconical pendulum, have not been accepted by the physics community as in need of unconventional explanation, and subsequent experiments have not succeeded in reproducing the results.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
