      <p>
       A
       <b>
        degree
       </b>
       (in full, a
       <b>
        degree of arc
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        arc degree
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        arcdegree
       </b>
       ), usually denoted by
       <b>
        °
       </b>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Degree_symbol" title="Degree symbol">
        degree symbol
       </a>
       ), is a measurement of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">
        plane
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">
        angle
       </a>
       , defined so that a
       <a href="/wiki/Turn_(geometry)" title="Turn (geometry)">
        full rotation
       </a>
       is 360 degrees.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is not an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_unit" title="SI unit">
        SI unit
       </a>
       , as the SI unit of angular measure is the
       <a href="/wiki/Radian" title="Radian">
        radian
       </a>
       , but it is mentioned in the SI brochure as an
       <a href="/wiki/Non-SI_units_mentioned_in_the_SI" title="Non-SI units mentioned in the SI">
        accepted unit
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because a full rotation equals 2
       <span class="texhtml">
        π
       </span>
       radians, one degree is equivalent to
       <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;">
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">
         <span class="texhtml">
          π
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="visualhide">
         /
        </span>
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">
         180
        </span>
       </span>
       radians.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Degree_(angle)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decans" title="Decans">
        Decans
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Equilateral_chord_with_length_equal_to_radius.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="900" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Equilateral_chord_with_length_equal_to_radius.svg/220px-Equilateral_chord_with_length_equal_to_radius.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Equilateral_chord_with_length_equal_to_radius.svg/330px-Equilateral_chord_with_length_equal_to_radius.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Equilateral_chord_with_length_equal_to_radius.svg/440px-Equilateral_chord_with_length_equal_to_radius.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Equilateral_chord_with_length_equal_to_radius.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A circle with an equilateral
         <a href="/wiki/Chord_(geometry)" title="Chord (geometry)">
          chord
         </a>
         (red). One sixtieth of this arc is a degree. Six such chords complete the circle.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The original motivation for choosing the degree as a unit of rotations and angles is unknown. One theory states that it is related to the fact that 360 is approximately the number of days in a year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ancient
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomers" title="Astronomers">
        astronomers
       </a>
       noticed that the sun, which follows through the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       path over the course of the year, seems to advance in its path by approximately one degree each day. Some ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">
        calendars
       </a>
       , such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_calendar" title="Iranian calendar">
        Persian calendar
       </a>
       , used 360 days for a year. The use of a calendar with 360 days may be related to the use of
       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">
        sexagesimal
       </a>
       numbers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another theory is that the Babylonians subdivided the circle using the angle of an
       <a href="/wiki/Equilateral_triangle" title="Equilateral triangle">
        equilateral triangle
       </a>
       as the basic unit and further subdivided the latter into 60 parts following their
       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">
        sexagesimal
       </a>
       numeric system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_trigonometry" title="History of trigonometry">
        earliest trigonometry
       </a>
       , used by the
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy" title="Babylonian astronomy">
        Babylonian astronomers
       </a>
       and their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_astronomy" title="Greek astronomy">
        Greek
       </a>
       successors, was based on
       <a href="/wiki/Chord_(geometry)" title="Chord (geometry)">
        chords
       </a>
       of a circle. A chord of length equal to the radius made a natural base quantity. One sixtieth of this, using their standard
       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">
        sexagesimal
       </a>
       divisions, was a degree.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samos" title="Aristarchus of Samos">
        Aristarchus of Samos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">
        Hipparchus
       </a>
       seem to have been among the first
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_astronomy" title="Greek astronomy">
        Greek scientists
       </a>
       to exploit Babylonian astronomical knowledge and techniques systematically.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Timocharis" title="Timocharis">
        Timocharis
       </a>
       , Aristarchus,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aristillus" title="Aristillus">
        Aristillus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">
        Archimedes
       </a>
       , and Hipparchus were the first Greeks known to divide the circle in 360 degrees of 60
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_minute" title="Arc minute">
        arc minutes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
        Eratosthenes
       </a>
       used a simpler
       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">
        sexagesimal
       </a>
       system dividing a circle into 60 parts.
      </p>
      <p>
       The division of the circle into 360 parts also occurred in ancient
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       , as evidenced in the
       <a href="/wiki/Rigveda" title="Rigveda">
        Rigveda
       </a>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Twelve spokes, one wheel, navels three.
        <br/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Who can comprehend this?
        <br/>
        On it are placed together
        <br/>
        three hundred and sixty like pegs.
        <br/>
        They shake not in the least.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         <a href="/wiki/Dirghatamas" title="Dirghatamas">
          Dirghatamas
         </a>
         , Rigveda 1.164.48
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Another motivation for choosing the number 360 may have been that it is
       <a href="/wiki/Highly_composite_number" title="Highly composite number">
        readily divisible
       </a>
       : 360 has 24
       <a href="/wiki/Divisor" title="Divisor">
        divisors
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       making it one of only 7 numbers such that no number less than twice as much has more divisors (sequence
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a class="extiw" href="//oeis.org/A072938" title="oeis:A072938">
         A072938
        </a>
       </span>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences" title="On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences">
        OEIS
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Furthermore, it is divisible by every number from 1 to 10 except 7.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [note 2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This property has many useful applications, such as dividing the world into 24
       <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">
        time zones
       </a>
       , each of which is nominally 15° of
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       , to correlate with the established
       <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">
        24-hour
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">
        day
       </a>
       convention.
      </p>
      <p>
       Finally, it may be the case that more than one of these factors has come into play. According to that theory, the number is approximately 365 because of the apparent movement of the sun against the celestial sphere and that it was rounded to 360 for some of the mathematical reasons cited above.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Subdivisions">
        Subdivisions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Degree_(angle)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subdivisions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For many practical purposes, a degree is a small enough angle that whole degrees provide sufficient precision. When this is not the case, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       or for
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">
        geographic coordinates
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       ), degree measurements may be written using
       <a href="/wiki/Decimal_degrees" title="Decimal degrees">
        decimal degrees
       </a>
       , with the degree symbol behind the decimals; for example, 40.1875°.
      </p>
      <p>
       Alternatively, the traditional
       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">
        sexagesimal
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">
        unit
       </a>
       subdivisions can be used. One degree is divided into 60
       <i>
        minutes (of arc)
       </i>
       , and one minute into 60
       <i>
        seconds (of arc)
       </i>
       . Use of degrees-minutes-seconds is also called DMS notation. These subdivisions, also called the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcminute" title="Arcminute">
         arcminute
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">
         arcsecond
        </a>
       </i>
       , are respectively represented by a single and double
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_(symbol)" title="Prime (symbol)">
        prime
       </a>
       . For example,
       <span class="nowrap">
        40.1875° = 40° 11′ 15″
       </span>
       , or, using
       <a href="/wiki/Quotation_mark" title="Quotation mark">
        quotation mark
       </a>
       characters,
       <span class="nowrap">
        40° 11' 15"
       </span>
       . Additional precision can be provided using decimals for the arcseconds component.
      </p>
      <p>
       The older system of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_(angle)" title="Third (angle)">
        thirds
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourth_(angle)" title="Fourth (angle)">
        fourths
       </a>
       , etc., which continues the sexagesimal unit subdivision, was used by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Kashi" title="Al-Kashi">
        al-Kashi
       </a>
       and other ancient astronomers, but is rarely used today. These subdivisions were denoted
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       by writing the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_numeral" title="Roman numeral">
        Roman numeral
       </a>
       for the number of sixtieths in superscript: 1
       <sup>
        I
       </sup>
       for a "
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_(symbol)" title="Prime (symbol)">
        prime
       </a>
       " (minute of arc), 1
       <sup>
        II
       </sup>
       for a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_prime" title="Double prime">
        second
       </a>
       , 1
       <sup>
        III
       </sup>
       for a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple_prime" title="Triple prime">
        third
       </a>
       , 1
       <sup>
        IV
       </sup>
       for a
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quarter_prime&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quarter prime (page does not exist)">
        fourth
       </a>
       , etc. Hence the modern symbols for the minute and second of arc, and the word "second" also refer to this system.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_units">
        Alternative units
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Degree_(angle)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alternative units">

        </span>

      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Angle#Measuring_angles" title="Angle">
        Measuring angles
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Degree-Radian_Conversion.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="700" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Degree-Radian_Conversion.svg/300px-Degree-Radian_Conversion.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Degree-Radian_Conversion.svg/450px-Degree-Radian_Conversion.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Degree-Radian_Conversion.svg/600px-Degree-Radian_Conversion.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>

         <div class="magnify">

         A chart to convert between degrees and radians

      <p>
       In most
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
        mathematical
       </a>
       work beyond practical geometry, angles are typically measured in
       <a href="/wiki/Radian" title="Radian">
        radians
       </a>
       rather than degrees. This is for a variety of reasons; for example, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonometric_function" title="Trigonometric function">
        trigonometric functions
       </a>
       have simpler and more "natural" properties when their arguments are expressed in radians. These considerations outweigh the convenient divisibility of the number 360. One complete
       <a href="/wiki/Turn_(geometry)" title="Turn (geometry)">
        turn
       </a>
       (360°) is equal to 2
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">
         <span class="texhtml">
          π
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       radians, so 180° is equal to
       <span class="texhtml">
        π
       </span>
       radians, or equivalently, the degree is a
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_constant" title="Mathematical constant">
        mathematical constant
       </a>
       : 1° = ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         <span class="texhtml">
          π
         </span>
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         180
        </sub>
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Turn_(geometry)" title="Turn (geometry)">
        turn
       </a>
       (or revolution, full circle, full rotation, cycle) is used in technology and science. One turn is equal to 360°.
      </p>
      <p>
       With the invention of the
       <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">
        metric system
       </a>
       , based on powers of ten, there was an attempt to replace degrees by decimal "degrees"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [note 3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       called
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grad_(angle)" title="Grad (angle)">
         grad
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gon_(angle)" title="Gon (angle)">
         gon
        </a>
       </i>
       , where the number in a right angle is equal to 100 gon with 400 gon in a full circle (1° = ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         10
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         9
        </sub>
       </span>
       gon). Although that idea was abandoned by Napoleon, grades continued to be used in several fields and many
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_calculator" title="Scientific calculator">
        scientific calculators
       </a>
       support them. Decigrades (​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         4,000
        </sub>
       </span>
       ) were used with French artillery sights in World War I.
      </p>
      <p>
       An
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_mil" title="Angular mil">
        angular mil
       </a>
       , which is most used in military applications, has at least three specific variants, ranging from ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         6,400
        </sub>
       </span>
       to ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         6,000
        </sub>
       </span>
       . It is approximately equal to one
       <a href="/wiki/Milliradian" title="Milliradian">
        milliradian
       </a>
       (
       <abbr title="circa">
        c.
       </abbr>
       ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         6,283
        </sub>
       </span>
       ). A mil measuring ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         6,000
        </sub>
       </span>
       of a revolution originated in the imperial Russian army, where an equilateral chord was divided into tenths to give a circle of 600 units. This may be seen on a lining plane (an early device for aiming
       <a href="/wiki/Indirect_fire" title="Indirect fire">
        indirect fire
       </a>
       artillery) dating from about 1900 in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg">
        St. Petersburg
       </a>
       Museum of Artillery.
      </p>
