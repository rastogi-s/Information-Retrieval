   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Thermal conduction
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Thermal conduction
       </b>
       is the transfer of heat (
       <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">
        internal energy
       </a>
       ) by microscopic collisions of particles and movement of electrons within a body. The microscopically colliding objects, that include molecules, atoms, and electrons, transfer disorganized microscopic kinetic and potential energy, jointly known as internal energy. Conduction takes place in all phases of
       <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">
        matter
       </a>
       , including solids, liquids, gases and waves. The rate at which energy is conducted as heat between two bodies is a function of the temperature difference (
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature_gradient" title="Temperature gradient">
        temperature gradient
       </a>
       ) between the two bodies and the properties of the conductive medium through which the heat is transferred. Thermal conduction was originally called diffusion. Conduction: transfer of heat via direct contact.
      </p>
      <p>
       Heat spontaneously flows from a hotter to a colder body. For example, heat is conducted from the hotplate of an electric stove to the bottom of a saucepan in contact with it. In the absence of an external driving energy source to the contrary, within a body or between bodies,
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       differences decay over time, and
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_equilibrium" title="Thermal equilibrium">
        thermal equilibrium
       </a>
       is approached, temperature becoming more uniform.
      </p>
      <p>
       In conduction, the heat flow is within and through the body itself. In contrast, in heat transfer by
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">
        thermal radiation
       </a>
       , the transfer is often between bodies, which may be separated spatially. Also possible is transfer of heat by a combination of conduction and thermal radiation. In convection, internal energy is carried between bodies by a moving material carrier. In solids, conduction is mediated by the combination of vibrations and collisions of molecules, of propagation and collisions of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonons" title="Phonons">
        phonons
       </a>
       , and of diffusion and collisions of
       <a href="/wiki/Free_electron_model" title="Free electron model">
        free electrons
       </a>
       . In gases and liquids, conduction is due to the collisions and
       <a href="/wiki/Molecular_diffusion" title="Molecular diffusion">
        diffusion
       </a>
       of molecules during their random motion.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">
        Photons
       </a>
       in this context do not collide with one another, and so heat transport by
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
        electromagnetic radiation
       </a>
       is conceptually distinct from heat conduction by microscopic diffusion and collisions of material particles and phonons. But the distinction is often not easily observed, unless the material is semi-transparent.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the engineering sciences, heat transfer includes the processes of
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">
        thermal radiation
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
        convection
       </a>
       , and sometimes mass transfer. Usually, more than one of these processes occurs in a given situation. The conventional symbol for the material property,
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
        thermal conductivity
       </a>
       , is
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
         <math alttext="{\displaystyle k}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
          <semantics>
           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
             <mi>
              k
             </mi>
            </mstyle>
           </mrow>
           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
            {\displaystyle k}
           </annotation>
          </semantics>
         </math>
        </span>
        <img alt="k" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.211ex; height:2.176ex;"/>
       </span>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_equation" title="Heat equation">
        Heat equation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       On a microscopic scale, conduction occurs within a body considered as being stationary; this means that the kinetic and potential energies of the bulk motion of the body are separately accounted for.
       <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">
        Internal energy
       </a>
       diffuses as rapidly moving or vibrating atoms and
       <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
        molecules
       </a>
       interact with neighbouring particles, transferring some of their microscopic kinetic and potential energies, these quantities being defined relative to the bulk of the body considered as being stationary. Heat is transferred by conduction when adjacent atoms or molecules collide, or as several
       <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">
        electrons
       </a>
       move backwards and forwards from atom to atom in a disorganized way so as not to form a macroscopic electric current, or as photons collide and scatter. Conduction is the most significant means of heat transfer within a solid or between solid objects in
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_contact" title="Thermal contact">
        thermal contact
       </a>
       . Conduction is greater in solids because the network of relatively close fixed spatial relationships between atoms helps to transfer energy between them by vibration.
      </p>
      <p>
       Fluids (and especially gases) are less conductive. This is due to the large distance between atoms in a gas: fewer collisions between atoms means less conduction. At low densities, the conductivity of gases can be derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases" title="Kinetic theory of gases">
        kinetic theory
       </a>
       using rigid, non-interacting spheres and refined using
       <a href="/wiki/Chapman%E2%80%93Enskog_theory" title="Chapman–Enskog theory">
        Chapman-Enskog
       </a>
       kinetic theory of gases.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The conductivity of gases increases with temperature. Conductivity increases with increasing pressure from vacuum up to a critical point that the density of the gas is such that molecules of the gas may be expected to collide with each other before they transfer heat from one surface to another. After this point, conductivity increases only slightly with increasing pressure and density.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_contact_conductance" title="Thermal contact conductance">
        Thermal contact conductance
       </a>
       is the study of heat conduction between solid bodies in contact. A temperature drop is often observed at the interface between the two surfaces in contact. This phenomenon is said to be a result of a thermal contact resistance existing between the contacting surfaces.
       <a href="/wiki/Interfacial_thermal_resistance" title="Interfacial thermal resistance">
        Interfacial thermal resistance
       </a>
       is a measure of an interface's resistance to thermal flow. This thermal resistance differs from contact resistance, as it exists even at atomically perfect interfaces. Understanding the thermal resistance at the interface between two materials is of primary significance in the study of its thermal properties. Interfaces often contribute significantly to the observed properties of the materials.
      </p>
      <p>
       The inter-molecular transfer of energy could be primarily by elastic impact, as in fluids, or by free electron diffusion, as in metals, or
       <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">
        phonon vibration
       </a>
       , as in insulators. In
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">
        insulators
       </a>
       , the heat flux is carried almost entirely by
       <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">
        phonon
       </a>
       vibrations.
      </p>
      <p>
       Metals (e.g., copper, platinum, gold, etc.) are usually good
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_conduction" title="Heat conduction">
        conductors
       </a>
       of thermal energy. This is due to the way that metals bond chemically:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallic_bond" title="Metallic bond">
        metallic bonds
       </a>
       (as opposed to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalent_bonds" title="Covalent bonds">
        covalent
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bonds" title="Ionic bonds">
        ionic bonds
       </a>
       ) have free-moving electrons that transfer thermal energy rapidly through the metal. The
       <i>
        electron fluid
       </i>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">
        conductive
       </a>
       metallic solid conducts most of the heat flux through the solid. Phonon flux is still present, but carries less of the energy. Electrons also conduct
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">
        electric current
       </a>
       through conductive solids, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
        thermal
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">
        electrical conductivities
       </a>
       of most metals have about the same ratio. A good electrical conductor, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">
        copper
       </a>
       , also conducts heat well.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoelectricity" title="Thermoelectricity">
        Thermoelectricity
       </a>
       is caused by the interaction of heat flux and electric current. Heat conduction within a solid is directly analogous to
       <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">
        diffusion
       </a>
       of particles within a fluid, in the situation where there are no fluid currents.
      </p>
      <p>
       To quantify the ease with which a particular medium conducts, engineers employ the
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
        thermal conductivity
       </a>
       , also known as the conductivity constant or conduction coefficient,
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
        thermal conductivity
       </a>
       ,

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Steady-state_conduction">
        Steady-state conduction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Steady-state conduction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Steady state conduction is the form of conduction that happens when the temperature difference(s) driving the conduction are constant, so that (after an equilibration time), the spatial distribution of temperatures (temperature field) in the conducting object does not change any further. Thus, all partial derivatives of temperature
       <i>
        with respect to space
       </i>
       may either be zero or have nonzero values, but all derivatives of temperature at any point
       <i>
        with respect to time
       </i>
       are uniformly zero. In steady state conduction, the amount of heat entering any region of an object is equal to amount of heat coming out (if this were not so, the temperature would be rising or falling, as thermal energy was tapped or trapped in a region).
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, a bar may be cold at one end and hot at the other, but after a state of steady state conduction is reached, the spatial gradient of temperatures along the bar does not change any further, as time proceeds. Instead, the temperature at any given section of the rod remains constant, and this temperature varies linearly in space, along the direction of heat transfer.
      </p>
      <p>
       In steady state conduction, all the laws of direct current electrical conduction can be applied to "heat currents". In such cases, it is possible to take "thermal resistances" as the analog to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance" title="Electrical resistance">
        electrical resistances
       </a>
       . In such cases, temperature plays the role of voltage, and heat transferred per unit time (heat power) is the analog of electric current. Steady state systems can be modelled by networks of such thermal resistances in series and in parallel, in exact analogy to electrical networks of resistors. See
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lumped_capacitance_model#Thermal_purely_resistive_circuits" title="Lumped capacitance model">
        purely resistive thermal circuits
       </a>
       for an example of such a network.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transient_conduction">
        Transient conduction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transient conduction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_equation" title="Heat equation">
        Heat equation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       During any period in which temperatures changes
       <i>
        in time
       </i>
       at any place within an object, the mode of thermal energy flow is termed
       <i>
        transient conduction.
       </i>
       Another term is "non steady-state" conduction, referring to time-dependence of temperature fields in an object. Non-steady-state situations appear after an imposed change in temperature at a boundary of an object. They may also occur with temperature changes inside an object, as a result of a new source or sink of heat suddenly introduced within an object, causing temperatures near the source or sink to change in time.
      </p>
      <p>
       When a new perturbation of temperature of this type happens, temperatures within the system change in time toward a new equilibrium with the new conditions, provided that these do not change. After equilibrium, heat flow into the system once again equals the heat flow out, and temperatures at each point inside the system no longer change. Once this happens, transient conduction is ended, although steady-state conduction may continue if heat flow continues.
      </p>
      <p>
       If changes in external temperatures or internal heat generation changes are too rapid for the equilibrium of temperatures in space to take place, then the system never reaches a state of unchanging temperature distribution in time, and the system remains in a transient state.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of a new source of heat "turning on" within an object, causing transient conduction, is an engine starting in an automobile. In this case, the transient thermal conduction phase for the entire machine is over, and the steady state phase appears, as soon as the engine reaches steady-state
       <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">
        operating temperature
       </a>
       . In this state of steady-state equilibrium, temperatures vary greatly from the engine cylinders to other parts of the automobile, but at no point in space within the automobile does temperature increase or decrease. After establishing this state, the transient conduction phase of heat transfer is over.
      </p>
      <p>
       New external conditions also cause this process: for example the copper bar in the example steady-state conduction experiences transient conduction as soon as one end is subjected to a different temperature from the other. Over time, the field of temperatures inside the bar reach a new steady-state, in which a constant temperature gradient along the bar is finally set up, and this gradient then stays constant in space. Typically, such a new steady state gradient is approached exponentially with time after a new temperature-or-heat source or sink, has been introduced. When a "transient conduction" phase is over, heat flow may still continue at high power, so long as temperatures do not change.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of transient conduction that does not end with steady-state conduction, but rather no conduction, occurs when a hot copper ball is dropped into oil at a low temperature. Here, the temperature field within the object begins to change as a function of time, as the heat is removed from the metal, and the interest lies in analyzing this spatial change of temperature within the object over time, until all gradients disappear entirely (the ball has reached the same temperature as the oil). Mathematically, this condition is also approached exponentially; in theory it takes infinite time, but in practice it is over, for all intents and purposes, in a much shorter period. At the end of this process with no heat sink but the internal parts of the ball (which are finite), there is no steady state heat conduction to reach. Such a state never occurs in this situation, but rather the end of the process is when there is no heat conduction at all.
      </p>
      <p>
       The analysis of non steady-state conduction systems is more complex than that of steady-state systems. If the conducting body has a simple shape, then exact analytical mathematical expressions and solutions may be possible (see
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_equation" title="Heat equation">
        heat equation
       </a>
       for the analytical approach).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, most often, because of complicated shapes with varying
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
        thermal conductivities
       </a>
       within the shape (i.e., most complex objects, mechanisms or machines in engineering) often the application of approximate theories is required, and/or numerical analysis by computer. One popular graphical method involves the use of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heisler_Chart" title="Heisler Chart">
        Heisler Charts
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Occasionally, transient conduction problems may be considerably simplified if regions of the object being heated or cooled can be identified, for which
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
        thermal conductivity
       </a>
       is very much greater than that for heat paths leading into the region. In this case, the region with high conductivity can often be treated in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lumped_capacitance_model" title="Lumped capacitance model">
        lumped capacitance model
       </a>
       , as a "lump" of material with a simple thermal capacitance consisting of its aggregate
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">
        heat capacity
       </a>
       . Such regions warm or cool, but show no significant temperature
       <i>
        variation
       </i>
       across their extent, during the process (as compared to the rest of the system). This is due to their far higher conductance. During transient conduction, therefore, the temperature across their conductive regions changes uniformly in space, and as a simple exponential in time. An example of such systems are those that follow
       <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_cooling" title="Newton's law of cooling">
        Newton's law of cooling
       </a>
       during transient cooling (or the reverse during heating). The equivalent thermal circuit consists of a simple capacitor in series with a resistor. In such cases, the remainder of the system with high thermal resistance (comparatively low conductivity) plays the role of the resistor in the circuit.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relativistic_conduction">
        Relativistic conduction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relativistic conduction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The theory of
       <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_heat_conduction" title="Relativistic heat conduction">
        relativistic heat conduction
       </a>
       is a model that is compatible with the theory of special relativity. For most of the last century, it was recognized that the Fourier equation is in contradiction with the theory of relativity because it admits an infinite speed of propagation of heat signals. For example, according to the Fourier equation, a pulse of heat at the origin would be felt at infinity instantaneously. The speed of information propagation is faster than the speed of light in vacuum, which is physically inadmissible within the framework of relativity.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_conduction">
        Quantum conduction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Quantum conduction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Second_sound" title="Second sound">
        Second sound
       </a>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanical" title="Quantum mechanical">
        quantum mechanical
       </a>
       phenomenon in which
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
        heat transfer
       </a>
       occurs by
       <a href="/wiki/Wave_equation" title="Wave equation">
        wave
       </a>
       -like motion, rather than by the more usual mechanism of
       <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">
        diffusion
       </a>
       . Heat takes the place of pressure in normal sound waves. This leads to a very high
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
        thermal conductivity
       </a>
       . It is known as "second sound" because the wave motion of heat is similar to the propagation of sound in air.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Fourier.27s_law">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fourier's_law">
        Fourier's law
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fourier's law">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The law of heat conduction, also known as
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">
        Fourier
       </a>
       's law, states that the time rate of
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
        heat transfer
       </a>
       through a material is
       <a href="/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics)" title="Proportionality (mathematics)">
        proportional
       </a>
       to the negative
       <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">
        gradient
       </a>
       in the temperature and to the area, at right angles to that gradient, through which the heat flows. We can state this law in two equivalent forms: the integral form, in which we look at the amount of energy flowing into or out of a body as a whole, and the differential form, in which we look at the flow rates or
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_flux" title="Heat flux">
        fluxes
       </a>
       of energy locally.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_cooling" title="Newton's law of cooling">
        Newton's law of cooling
       </a>
       is a discrete analog of Fourier's law, while
       <a href="/wiki/Ohm%27s_law" title="Ohm's law">
        Ohm's law
       </a>
       is the electrical analogue of Fourier's law.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Metal_quenching">
        Metal quenching
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Metal quenching">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Metal
       <a href="/wiki/Quenching" title="Quenching">
        quenching
       </a>
       is a transient heat transfer process in terms of the
       <a href="/wiki/Isothermal_transformation_diagram" title="Isothermal transformation diagram">
        Time Temperature Transformation
       </a>
       (TTT). It is possible to manipulate the cooling process to adjust the phase of a suitable material. For example, appropriate quenching of steel can convert a desirable proportion of its content of
       <a href="/wiki/Austenite" title="Austenite">
        austenite
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Martensite" title="Martensite">
        martensite
       </a>
       , creating a very tough product. To achieve this, it is necessary to quench at the "nose" (or
       <a href="/wiki/Eutectic_system" title="Eutectic system">
        eutectic
       </a>
       ) of the TTT diagram. Since materials differ in their
       <a href="/wiki/Biot_number" title="Biot number">
        Biot numbers
       </a>
       , the time it takes for the material to quench, or the
       <a href="/wiki/Fourier_number" title="Fourier number">
        Fourier number
       </a>
       , varies in practice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AsthanaKumar2006_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AsthanaKumar2006-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In steel, the quenching temperature range is generally from 600 °C to 200 °C. To control the quenching time and to select suitable quenching media, it is necessary to determine the Fourier number from the desired quenching time, the relative temperature drop, and the relevant Biot number. Usually, the correct figures are read from a standard
       <a href="/wiki/Nomogram" title="Nomogram">
        nomogram
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="Where is this nomogram published? (May 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       By calculating the heat transfer coefficient from this Biot number, one can find a liquid medium suitable for the application.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Totten2002_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Totten2002-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Zeroth_law_of_thermodynamics">
        Zeroth law of thermodynamics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Zeroth law of thermodynamics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One statement of the so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Zeroth_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Zeroth law of thermodynamics">
        zeroth law of thermodynamics
       </a>
       is directly focused on the idea of conduction of heat. Bailyn (1994) writes that "... the zeroth law may be stated:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          All diathermal walls are equivalent."
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
           <a href="#cite_note-7">
            [7]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system#boundary" title="Thermodynamic system">
        diathermal wall
       </a>
       is a physical connection between two bodies that allows the passage of heat between them. Bailyn is referring to diathermal walls that exclusively connect two bodies, especially conductive walls.
      </p>
      <p>
       This statement of the 'zeroth law' belongs to an idealized theoretical discourse, and actual physical walls may have peculiarities that do not conform to its generality.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, the material of the wall must not undergo a phase transition, such as evaporation or fusion, at the temperature at which it must conduct heat. But when only thermal equilibrium is considered and time is not urgent, so that the conductivity of the material does not matter too much, one suitable heat conductor is as good as another. Conversely, another aspect of the zeroth law is that, subject again to suitable restrictions, a given diathermal wall is indifferent to the nature of the heat bath to which it is connected. For example, the glass bulb of a thermometer acts as a diathermal wall whether exposed to a gas or to a liquid, provided they do not corrode or melt it.
      </p>
      <p>
       These indifferences are amongst the defining characteristics of
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
        heat transfer
       </a>
       . In a sense, they are
       <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_(physics)" title="Symmetry (physics)">
        symmetries
       </a>
       of heat transfer.
      </p>

      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_Conductivity_Analyzer">
        Thermal Conductivity Analyzer
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Thermal Conductivity Analyzer">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Thermal conduction property of any gas under standard conditions of pressure and temperature is a fixed quantity. This property of a known reference gas or known reference gas mixtures can, therefore, be used for certain sensory applications, such as the thermal conductivity analyzer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The working of this instrument is by principle based on the Wheatstone Bridge containing four filaments whose resistances are matched. Whenever a certain gas is passed over such network of filaments, their resistance changes due to the altered thermal conductivity of the filaments and thereby changing the net voltage output from the Wheatstone Bridge. This voltage output will be correlated with the database to identify the gas sample.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_sensor">
        Gas sensor
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Gas sensor">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The principle of thermal conductivity of gases can also be used to measure the concentration of a gas in a binary mixture of gases.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Working:
       </b>
       If same gas is present around the all the Wheatstone Bridge filaments, then the same temperature is maintained in all the filaments and hence same resistances are also maintained; resulting in a balanced Wheatstone bridge. However, If dissimilar gas sample (or gas mixture) is passed over one set of two filaments and the reference gas on the other set of two filaments, then the Wheatstone bridge becomes unbalanced. And the resulting net voltage output of the circuit will be correlated with the database to identify the constituents of the sample gas.
      </p>
      <p>
       Using this technique many unknown gas samples can be identified by comparing their thermal conductivity with other reference gas of known thermal conductivity.The most commonly used reference gas is Nitrogen; as the thermal conductivity of most common gases (except Hydrogen and Helium) are similar to that of Nitrogen.
      </p>
