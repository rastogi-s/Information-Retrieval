        Joseph Ritter von Fraunhofer
       </b>

         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">

       <small>
        German:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">
         [ˈfraʊnˌhoːfɐ]

       </span>
       ; 6 March 1787 – 7 June 1826) was a
       <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">
        Bavarian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">
        physicist
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Achromatic_telescope" title="Achromatic telescope">
        achromatic telescope
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Objective_lens" title="Objective lens">
        objective lenses
       </a>
       , invented the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscope" title="Spectroscope">
        spectroscope
       </a>
       , and developed
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction_grating" title="Diffraction grating">
        diffraction grating
       </a>
       . He also discovered and studied the dark absorption lines in the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       of the sun now known as
       <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines" title="Fraunhofer lines">
        Fraunhofer lines
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The German research organization
       <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">
        Fraunhofer Society
       </a>
       is named after him and is Europe´s biggest Society for the Advancement of Applied Research.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
        Biography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_von_Fraunhofer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Joseph Fraunhofer was born in
       <a href="/wiki/Straubing" title="Straubing">
        Straubing
       </a>
       , in the
       <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">
        Electorate of Bavaria
       </a>
       , to Franz Xaver Fraunhofer and Maria Anna Frohlich.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was orphaned at the age of 11 and started working as an apprentice to a harsh glassmaker named Philipp Anton Weichelsberger
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . In 1801, the workshop in which he was working collapsed, and he was buried in the rubble. The rescue operation was led by
       <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I_Joseph_of_Bavaria" title="Maximilian I Joseph of Bavaria">
        Prince-Elector Maximilian Joseph
       </a>
       . The prince entered Fraunhofer's life, providing him with books and forcing his employer to allow the young Fraunhofer time to study
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Joseph Utzschneider was also at the site of the disaster, and would also become a benefactor to Fraunhofer. With the money given to him by the prince upon his rescue and the support he received from Utzschneider, Fraunhofer was able to continue his education alongside his practical training.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1806, Utzschneider and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_von_Reichenbach" title="Georg von Reichenbach">
        Georg von Reichenbach
       </a>
       brought Fraunhofer into their Institute at
       <a href="/wiki/Benediktbeuern_Abbey" title="Benediktbeuern Abbey">
        Benediktbeuern
       </a>
       , a secularised
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">
        Benedictine
       </a>
       monastery devoted to glassmaking. There he discovered how to make the fine optical glass and invented precise methods for measuring
       <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">
        optical dispersion
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It was at the Institute that Fraunhofer met Pierre Louis Guinand, a Swiss glass technician, who instructed Fraunhofer in glassmaking at Utzschneider's behest.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plicht_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-plicht-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By 1809, the mechanical part of the Optical Institute was chiefly under Fraunhofer's direction, and Fraunhofer became one of the members of the firm that same year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amer_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-amer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1814, Guinand left the firm, as did Reichenbach. Guinad would later became a partner with Fraunhofer in the firm,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plicht_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-plicht-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the name was changed to Utzschneider-und-Fraunhofer. During 1818, Fraunhofer became the director of the Optical Institute. Due to the fine optical instruments developed by Fraunhofer, Bavaria overtook England as the center of the optics industry. Even the likes of
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">
        Michael Faraday
       </a>
       were unable to produce glass that could rival Fraunhofer
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       His illustrious career eventually earned him an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_doctorate" title="Honorary doctorate">
        honorary doctorate
       </a>
       from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Erlangen" title="University of Erlangen">
        University of Erlangen
       </a>
       in 1822. In 1824, Fraunhofer was appointed an
       <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Merit_of_the_Bavarian_Crown" title="Order of Merit of the Bavarian Crown">
        Knight of the Order of Merit of the Bavarian Crown
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I_Joseph_of_Bavaria" title="Maximilian I Joseph of Bavaria">
        King Maximilian I
       </a>
       , through which he was raised into personal nobility (with the title "Ritter von", i.e. knight). The same year, he was also made an honorary citizen of
       <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">
        Munich
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Like many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glassmaker" title="Glassmaker">
        glassmakers
       </a>
       of his era, he was poisoned by
       <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">
        heavy metal
       </a>
       vapors, resulting in his premature death. Fraunhofer died in 1826 at the age of 39. His most valuable glassmaking recipes are thought to have gone to the grave with him
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Invention_and_scientific_research">
        Invention and scientific research
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_von_Fraunhofer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Invention and scientific research">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One of the most difficult operations of practical optics during the time period of Fraunhofer's life was accurately
       <a href="/wiki/Polishing" title="Polishing">
        polishing
       </a>
       the spherical surfaces of large
       <a href="/wiki/Objective_(optics)" title="Objective (optics)">
        object glasses
       </a>
       . Fraunhofer invented the machine
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words">
          <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2018)">
           which?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       which rendered the surface more accurately than traditional
       <a href="/wiki/Grinding_(abrasive_cutting)" title="Grinding (abrasive cutting)">
        grinding
       </a>
       . He also invented other grinding and polishing machines and introduced many improvements into the manufacture of the different kinds of glass used for optical instruments, which he always found to be injured by flaws and irregularities of various sorts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amer_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-amer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1811, he constructed a new kind of
       <a href="/wiki/Glass_production" title="Glass production">
        furnace
       </a>
       , and during his second melting session when he melted a large quantity of glass, he found that he could produce
       <a href="/wiki/Flint_glass" title="Flint glass">
        flint glass
       </a>
       , which, when taken from the bottom of a vessel containing roughly 224 pounds of glass, had the same
       <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">
        refractive
       </a>
       power as glass taken from the surface. He found that the English
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_glass_(optics)" title="Crown glass (optics)">
        crown glass
       </a>
       and the German table glass both contained defects which tended to cause irregular refraction. In the thicker and larger glasses, there would be even more of such defects, so that in larger telescopes this kind of glass would not be fit for object glasses. Fraunhofer, therefore, made his own crown glass.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amer_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-amer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was thought that the accurate determination of power for a given medium to refract rays of light and separate the different colors which they contain was impeded due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">
        colors
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       having no precise limits, making it difficult to accurately measure the angle of refraction. To debunk this, Fraunhofer performed a series of experiments for the purpose of producing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic_light" title="Monochromatic light">
        homogeneous light
       </a>
       artificially, and unable to effect his object in a direct way, he did so by means of lamps and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prism_(optics)" title="Prism (optics)">
        prisms
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amer_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-amer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fraunhofer_spectroscope.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1325" data-file-width="1685" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Fraunhofer_spectroscope.JPG/220px-Fraunhofer_spectroscope.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Fraunhofer_spectroscope.JPG/330px-Fraunhofer_spectroscope.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Fraunhofer_spectroscope.JPG/440px-Fraunhofer_spectroscope.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fraunhofer_spectroscope.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Fraunhofer demonstrating the spectroscope.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Thus in 1814, Fraunhofer invented the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscope" title="Spectroscope">
        spectroscope
       </a>
       . In the course of his experiments, he discovered a bright fixed line which appears in the orange color of the spectrum when it is produced by the light of
       <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">
        fire
       </a>
       . This line enabled him afterward to determine the absolute power of refraction in different substances. Experiments to ascertain whether the solar spectrum contained the same bright line in orange as the line produced by the orange of fire light led him to the discovery of 574 dark fixed lines in the solar spectrum. Today, millions of such fixed absorption lines are now known.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amer_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-amer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       These dark fixed lines were later shown to be atomic absorption lines, as explained by
       <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">
        Kirchhoff
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bunsen" title="Robert Bunsen">
        Bunsen
       </a>
       in 1859.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These lines are still called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines" title="Fraunhofer lines">
         Fraunhofer lines
        </a>
       </i>
       in his honor; his discovery had gone far beyond the half-dozen apparent divisions in the solar spectrum that had previously been noted by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">
        Wollaston
       </a>
       in 1802.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Fraunhofer also developed a
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction_grating" title="Diffraction grating">
        diffraction grating
       </a>
       in 1821, which occurred after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Gregory_(astronomer_and_mathematician)" title="James Gregory (astronomer and mathematician)">
        James Gregory
       </a>
       discovered the principles of diffraction grating and after American astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/David_Rittenhouse" title="David Rittenhouse">
        David Rittenhouse
       </a>
       invented the first man-made diffraction grating in 1785.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Fraunhofer found out that the spectra of
       <a href="/wiki/Sirius" title="Sirius">
        Sirius
       </a>
       and other first-magnitude stars differed from the sun and from each other, thus founding
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">
        stellar spectroscopy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ultimately, however, his primary passion was still practical optics, once noting that "In all my experiments I could, owing to lack of time, pay attention to only those matters which appeared to have a bearing upon practical optics".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Telescopes_and_optical_instruments">
        Telescopes and optical instruments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_von_Fraunhofer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Telescopes and optical instruments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Fraunhofer produced various optical instruments for his firm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plicht_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-plicht-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This included the Fraunhofer Dorpat Refractor used by Struve (delivered 1824 to
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tartu_Old_Observatory" title="University of Tartu Old Observatory">
        Dorpat Observatory
       </a>
       ), and the Bessel
       <a href="/wiki/Heliometer" title="Heliometer">
        Heliometer
       </a>
       (delivered posthumously), which were both used to collect data for
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_parallax" title="Stellar parallax">
        stellar parallax
       </a>
       . The firm's successor, Merz und Mahler, made a telescope for the New Berlin Observatory, which confirmed the existence of the major planet
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       . Possibly the last telescope
       <a href="/wiki/Objective_(optics)#Telescope" title="Objective (optics)">
        objective
       </a>
       made by Fraunhofer was supplied for a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transit_telescope" title="Transit telescope">
        transit telescope
       </a>
       at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_Observatory,_Edinburgh" title="City Observatory, Edinburgh">
        City Observatory, Edinburgh
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edinburgh_city_obs_web_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Edinburgh_city_obs_web-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the telescope itself being completed by
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Georg_Repsold" title="Johann Georg Repsold">
        Repsold
       </a>
       of Hamburg after Fraunhofer's death.
      </p>

         <span class="reference-text">
          Fraunhofer (1814-1815),
          <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=2-AAAAAAYAAJ&amp;pg=PA230#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
           pages 220-221
          </a>
          :
          <i>
           Original
          </i>
          : Ich habe auch mit derselben Vorrichtung Versuche mit dem Lichte einiger Fixsterne erster Grösse gemachte. Da aber das Licht dieser Sterne noch vielmal schwächer ist, als das der Venus, so ist natürlich auch die Helligkeit des Farbenbildes vielmal geringer. Demohngeachtet habe ich, ohne Täuschung, im Farbenbilde vom Lichte des Sirius drey breite Streifen gesehen, die mit jenen vom Sonnenlichte keine Aehnlichkeit zu haben scheinen; einer dieser Streifen ist im Grünen, und zwey im Blauen. Auch im Farbenbilde vom Lichte anderer Fixsterne erster Grösse erkennt man Streifen; doch scheinen diese Sterne, in Beziehung auf die Streifen, unter sich verschieden zu seyn.
          <i>
           Translation
          </i>
          : With the same device [i.e., spectroscope], I've also made some experiments on the light of some stars of the first magnitude. Since the light of these stars is many times weaker than that of Venus, so naturally, the brightness of the spectrum is also many times less. Notwithstanding, I have seen -- without any illusion -- three broad stripes in the spectrum of Sirius, which seem to have no similarity to those of sunlight; one of these stripes is in the green, and two in the blue. Also, in the spectrum of the light of other fixed stars of the first magnitude, one detects stripes; yet these stars, in regard to the stripes, seem to differ among themselves.
