       <b>
        Parmenides of Elea
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">

           <span title="'z' in 'zoom'">

          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">

           </span>
           <span title="'v' in 'vie'">

           </span>

          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text" xml:lang="grc">
        Παρμενίδης ὁ Ἐλεάτης
       </span>
       ; fl. late sixth or early fifth century BC) was a
       <a href="/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy" title="Pre-Socratic philosophy">
        pre-Socratic
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Greek
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">
        philosopher
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Velia" title="Velia">
        Elea
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">
        Magna Graecia
       </a>
       (Greater Greece, included Southern Italy). He was the founder of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleatic_school" title="Eleatic school">
        Eleatic school
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">
        philosophy
       </a>
       . The single known work of Parmenides is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poem" title="Poem">
        poem
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        On Nature
       </i>
       , which has survived only in fragmentary form. In this poem, Parmenides describes two views of
       <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">
        reality
       </a>
       . In "the way of truth" (a part of the poem), he explains how reality (coined as "what-is") is one,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Change_(mathematics)" title="Change (mathematics)">
        change
       </a>
       is impossible, and
       <a href="/wiki/Existence" title="Existence">
        existence
       </a>
       is timeless, uniform, necessary, and unchanging. In "the way of opinion", he explains the world of appearances, in which one's sensory faculties lead to conceptions which are false and deceitful. He has been considered to be the founder of
       <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">
        metaphysics
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">
        ontology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Palmer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Parmenides was born in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_colony" title="Greek colony">
        Greek colony
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Velia" title="Velia">
        Elea
       </a>
       (now
       <a href="/wiki/Ascea" title="Ascea">
        Ascea
       </a>
       ), which, according to
       <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
        Herodotus
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       had been founded shortly before 535 BC. He was descended from a wealthy and illustrious family.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       His dates are uncertain; according to
       <a href="/wiki/Diogenes_La%C3%ABrtius" title="Diogenes Laërtius">
        Diogenes Laërtius
       </a>
       , he flourished just before 500 BC,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which would put his year of birth near 540 BC, but
       <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
        Plato
       </a>
       has him visiting
       <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">
        Athens
       </a>
       at the age of 65, when
       <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">
        Socrates
       </a>
       was a young man, c. 450 BC,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which, if true, suggests a year of birth of c. 515 BC. He was said to have been a pupil of
       <a href="/wiki/Xenophanes" title="Xenophanes">
        Xenophanes
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and regardless of whether they actually knew each other, Xenophanes' philosophy is the most obvious influence on Parmenides.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Diogenes Laërtius also describes Parmenides as a disciple of "Ameinias, son of Diochaites, the
       <a href="/wiki/Pythagoreanism" title="Pythagoreanism">
        Pythagorean
       </a>
       "; but there are no obvious Pythagorean elements in his thought.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, according to
       <a href="/wiki/William_Smith_(lexicographer)" title="William Smith (lexicographer)">
        Sir William Smith
       </a>
       , in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dictionary_of_Greek_and_Roman_Biography_and_Mythology" title="Dictionary of Greek and Roman Biography and Mythology">
         Dictionary of Greek and Roman Biography and Mythology
        </a>
       </i>
       (1870):
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Others content themselves with reckoning Parmenides as well as
        <a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Elea" title="Zeno of Elea">
         Zeno
        </a>
        as belonging to the Pythagorean school, or with speaking of a
        <i>
         Parmenidean life
        </i>
        , in the same way as a
        <i>
         Pythagorean life
        </i>
        is spoken of; and even the censorious Timon allows Parmenides to have been a high-minded man; while Plato speaks of him with veneration, and Aristotle and others give him an unqualified preference over the rest of the Eleatics.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
        Career
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmenides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The first
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hero_cult" title="Hero cult">
        hero cult
       </a>
       of a philosopher we know of was Parmenides' dedication of a
       <a href="/wiki/Heroon" title="Heroon">
        heroon
       </a>
       to his teacher Ameinias in Elea.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Parmenides was the founder of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_of_Elea" title="School of Elea">
        School of Elea
       </a>
       , which also included
       <a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Elea" title="Zeno of Elea">
        Zeno of Elea
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Melissus_of_Samos" title="Melissus of Samos">
        Melissus of Samos
       </a>
       . Of his life in Elea, it was said that he had written the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laws" title="Laws">
        laws
       </a>
       of the city.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His most important pupil was
       <a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Elea" title="Zeno of Elea">
        Zeno
       </a>
       , who according to Plato was 25 years his junior, and was regarded as his
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eromenos" title="Eromenos">
         eromenos
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Parmenides had a large influence on Plato, who not only named a dialogue,
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parmenides_(Plato)" title="Parmenides (Plato)">
         Parmenides
        </a>
       </i>
       , after him, but always wrote about him with veneration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Thought">
        Thought
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmenides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thought">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       William Smith also wrote in
       <i>
        Dictionary of Greek and Roman Biography and Mythology
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        On the former reason is our guide; on the latter the eye that does not catch the object and re-echoing hearing. On the former path we convince ourselves that the existent neither has come into being, nor is perishable, and is entirely of one sort, without change and limit, neither past nor future, entirely included in the present. For it is as impossible that it can become and grow out of the existent, as that it could do so out of the non-existent; since the latter, non-existence, is absolutely inconceivable, and the former cannot precede itself; and every coming into existence presupposes a non-existence. By similar arguments divisibility, motion or change, as also infinity, are shut out from the absolutely existent, and the latter is represented as shut up in itself, so that it may be compared to a well-rounded ball; while thought is appropriated to it as its only positive definition. Thought and that which is thought of (Object) coinciding; the corresponding passages of Plato, Aristotle, Theophrastus, and others, which authenticate this view of his theory.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_11-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Smith-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Parmenides_cosmology">
        Parmenides cosmology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmenides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parmenides cosmology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_Earth" title="Spherical Earth">
        Spherical Earth
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">
        Cosmology
       </a>
       originally comprised the greater part of his poem, him explaining the world’s origins and operations. John Palmer notes "Parmenides’ distinction among the principal modes of being and his derivation of the attributes that must belong to what must be, simply as such, qualify him to be seen as the founder of metaphysics or ontology as a domain of inquiry distinct from theology."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Palmer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some idea of the sphericity of the Earth seems to have been known to both Parmenides and
       <a href="/wiki/Empedocles" title="Empedocles">
        Empedocles
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kahn53_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kahn53-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Parmenides also outlined the phases of the moon, highlighted in a rhymed translation by
       <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">
        Karl Popper
       </a>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <div class="poem">
        <p>
         Bright in the night with the gift of his light,
         <br/>
         Round the earth she is erring,
         <br/>
         Evermore letting her gaze
         <br/>
         Turn towards Helios' rays
        </p>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Smith stated:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Of the
        <a href="/wiki/Cosmogony" title="Cosmogony">
         cosmogony
        </a>
        of Parmenides, which was carried out very much in detail, we possess only a few fragments and notices, which are difficult to understand, according to which, with an approach to the doctrines of the
        <i>
         Pythagoreans
        </i>
        , he conceived the spherical mundane system, surrounded by a circle of the pure light (Olympus, Uranus); in the centre of this mundane system the solid earth, and between the two the circle of the milkyway, of the morning or evening star, of the sun, the planets, and the moon; which circle he regarded as a mixture of the two primordial elements.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The fragments read:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palmer_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Palmer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        You will know the aether’s nature, and in the aether all the/ signs, and the unseen works of the pure torch/ of the brilliant sun, and from whence they came to be,/ and you will learn the wandering works of the round-eyed moon/ and its nature, and you will know too the surrounding heaven,/ both whence it grew and how Necessity directing it bound it/ to furnish the limits of the stars. (Fr. 10)
       </p>
       <p>
        …how the earth and sun and moon/ and the shared aether and the heavenly milk and Olympos/ outermost and the hot might of the stars began/ to come to be. (Fr. 11)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="On_Nature">
        <i>
         On Nature
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmenides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: On Nature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Parmenides is one of the most significant of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Socratic" title="Pre-Socratic">
        pre-Socratic
       </a>
       philosophers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His single known work, a poem conventionally titled
       <i>
        On Nature
       </i>
       , has survived only in fragments. Approximately 160 verses remain today from an original total that was probably near 800.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The poem was originally divided into three parts:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        A
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proem" title="Proem">
         proem
        </a>
        (Greek: προοίμιον), which introduced the entire work,
       </li>
       <li>
        A section known as "The Way of Truth" (
        <i>
         aletheia
        </i>
        , ἀλήθεια), and
       </li>
       <li>
        A section known as "The Way of Appearance/Opinion" (
        <i>
         doxa
        </i>
        , δόξα).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The proem is a narrative sequence in which the narrator travels "beyond the beaten paths of mortal men" to receive a revelation from an unnamed goddess (generally thought to be
       <a href="/wiki/Persephone" title="Persephone">
        Persephone
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Dike_(mythology)" title="Dike (mythology)">
        Dikē
       </a>
       ) on the nature of reality.
       <i>
        Aletheia
       </i>
       , an estimated 90% of which has survived, and
       <i>
        doxa
       </i>
       , most of which no longer exists, are then presented as the spoken revelation of the goddess without any accompanying narrative.
      </p>
      <p>
       Parmenides attempted to distinguish between the unity of nature and its variety, insisting in the
       <i>
        Way of Truth
       </i>
       upon the reality of its unity, which is therefore the object of knowledge, and upon the unreality of its variety, which is therefore the object, not of knowledge, but of opinion. In the
       <i>
        Way of Opinion
       </i>
       he propounded a theory of the world of seeming and its development, pointing out, however, that, in accordance with the principles already laid down, these cosmological speculations do not pretend to anything more than mere appearance.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proem">
        Proem
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmenides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Proem">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the proem, Parmenides describes the journey of the poet, escorted by maidens ("the daughters of the Sun made haste to escort me, having left the halls of Night for the light"),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       from the ordinary daytime world to a strange destination, outside our human paths.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Carried in a whirling
       <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">
        chariot
       </a>
       , and attended by the daughters of
       <a href="/wiki/Helios" title="Helios">
        Helios
       </a>
       the Sun, the man reaches a temple sacred to an unnamed goddess (variously identified by the commentators as
       <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">
        Nature
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sophia_(wisdom)" title="Sophia (wisdom)">
        Wisdom
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ananke_(mythology)" title="Ananke (mythology)">
        Necessity
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Themis" title="Themis">
        Themis
       </a>
       ), by whom the rest of the poem is spoken. The goddess resides in a well-known mythological space: where Night and Day have their meeting place. Its essential character is that here all opposites are undivided, or one.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He must learn all things, she tells him – both truth, which is certain, and human opinions, which are uncertain – for though one cannot rely on human opinions, they represent an aspect of the whole truth.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Way_of_Truth">
        The Way of Truth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmenides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Way of Truth">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:160px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sanzio_01_Parmenides.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="372" data-file-width="158" height="372" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/20/Sanzio_01_Parmenides.jpg" width="158"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sanzio_01_Parmenides.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Parmenides. Detail from
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/The_School_of_Athens" title="The School of Athens">
           The School of Athens
          </a>
         </i>
         by
         <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">
          Raphael
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The section known as "the way of truth" discusses that which is real and contrasts with the argument in the section called "the way of opinion," which discusses that which is illusory. Under the "way of truth," Parmenides stated that there are two ways of inquiry: that it
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Being" title="Being">
         is
        </a>
       </i>
       , on the one side, and that it
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Meontology" title="Meontology">
         is not
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       on the other side. He said that the latter argument is never feasible because there is no thing that can
       <i>
        not be
       </i>
       : "For never shall this prevail, that things that are not
       <b>
        are
       </b>
       ." (B 7.1)
      </p>
      <p>
       There are extremely delicate issues here. In the original Greek the two ways are simply named "that Is" (ὅπως ἐστίν) and "that Not-Is" (ὡς οὐκ ἐστίν) (B 2.3 and 2.5) without the "it" inserted in our English translation. In ancient Greek, which, like many languages in the world, does not always require the presence of a subject for a verb, "is" functions as a grammatically complete sentence. Much debate has been focused on where and what the subject is. The simplest explanation as to why there is no subject here is that Parmenides wishes to express the simple, bare fact of existence in his mystical experience without the ordinary distinctions, just as the Latin "pluit" and the Greek
       <i>
        huei
       </i>
       (ὕει "rains") mean "it rains"; there is no subject for these impersonal verbs because they express the simple fact of raining without specifying what is doing the raining. This is, for instance,
       <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Fr%C3%A4nkel" title="Hermann Fränkel">
        Hermann Fränkel
       </a>
       's thesis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many scholars still reject this explanation and have produced more complex metaphysical explanations. Since existence is an immediately intuited fact, non-existence is the wrong path because a thing cannot disappear, just as something cannot originate from nothing. In such mystical experience (
       <i>
        unio mystica
       </i>
       ), however, the distinction between subject and object disappears along with the distinctions between objects, in addition to the fact that if nothing cannot be, it cannot be the object of thought either:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Thinking and the thought that it is are the same; for you will not find thinking apart from what is, in relation to which it is uttered. (B 8.34–36)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        For to be aware and to be are the same. (B 3)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        It is necessary to speak and to think what is; for being is, but nothing is not. (B 6.1–2)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Helplessness guides the wandering thought in their breasts; they are carried along deaf and blind alike, dazed, beasts without judgment, convinced that to be and not to be are the same and not the same, and that the road of all things is a backward-turning one. (B 6.5–9)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Thus, he concluded that "Is" could not have "come into being" because "
       <a href="/wiki/Nothing_comes_from_nothing" title="Nothing comes from nothing">
        nothing comes from nothing
       </a>
       ". Existence is necessarily eternal. That which truly is [x], has always been [x], and was never becoming [x]; that which is becoming [x] was never nothing (Not-[x]), but will never actually be. Parmenides was not struggling to formulate the laws of
       <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_mass" title="Conservation of mass">
        conservation of mass
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">
        conservation of energy
       </a>
       ; he was struggling with the metaphysics of change, which is still a relevant philosophical topic today. Moreover, he argued that movement was impossible because it requires moving into "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Void_(philosophy)" title="The Void (philosophy)">
        the void
       </a>
       ", and Parmenides identified "the void" with nothing, and therefore (by definition) it does not exist. That which does exist is
       <i>
        The Parmenidean One
       </i>
       , which is timeless, uniform, and unchanging:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        How could what is perish? How could it have come to be? For if it came into being, it is not; nor is it if ever it is going to be. Thus coming into being is extinguished, and destruction unknown. (B 8.20–22)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Nor was [it] once, nor will [it] be, since [it] is, now, all together, / One, continuous; for what coming-to-be of it will you seek? / In what way, whence, did [it] grow? Neither from what-is-not shall I allow / You to say or think; for it is not to be said or thought / That [it] is not. And what need could have impelled it to grow / Later or sooner, if it began from nothing? Thus [it] must either be completely or not at all. (B 8.5–11)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        [What exists] is now, all at once, one and continuous... Nor is it divisible, since it is all alike; nor is there any more or less of it in one place which might prevent it from holding together, but all is full of what is. (B 8.5–6, 8.22–24)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        And it is all one to me / Where I am to begin; for I shall return there again. (B 5)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Perception_vs._Logos">
        Perception vs. Logos
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmenides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Perception vs. Logos">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Parmenides claimed that there is no truth in the opinions of the mortals. Genesis-and-destruction, as Parmenides emphasizes, is a false opinion, because to be means to be completely, once and for all. What exists can in no way not exist.
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        For this view, that That Which Is Not exists, can never predominate. You must debar your thought from this way of search, nor let ordinary experience in its variety force you along this way, (namely, that of allowing) the eye, sightless as it is, and the ear, full of sound, and the tongue, to rule; but (you must) judge by means of the Reason (
        <a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">
         Logos
        </a>
        ) the much-contested proof which is expounded by me. (B 7.1–8.2)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h3>
       <span id="The_Way_of_Opinion_.28doxa.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Way_of_Opinion_(doxa)">
        The Way of Opinion (
        <i>
         doxa
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmenides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Way of Opinion (doxa)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       After the exposition of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Arche" title="Arche">
         arche
        </a>
       </i>
       (ἀρχή), i.e. the origin, the necessary part of reality that is understood through reason or logos (
       <i>
        that [it] Is
       </i>
       ), in the next section,
       <i>
        the Way of Appearance/Opinion/Seeming
       </i>
       , Parmenides proceeds to explain the structure of the becoming cosmos (which is an illusion, of course) that comes from this origin.
      </p>
      <p>
       The structure of the cosmos is a fundamental binary principle that governs the manifestations of all the particulars: "the aether fire of flame" (B 8.56), which is gentle, mild, soft, thin and clear, and self-identical, and the other is "ignorant night", body thick and heavy.
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        The mortals lay down and decided well to name two forms (i.e. the flaming light and obscure darkness of night), out of which it is necessary not to make one, and in this they are led astray. (B 8.53–4)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The structure of the cosmos then generated is recollected by
       <a href="/wiki/Aetius_(philosopher)" title="Aetius (philosopher)">
        Aetius
       </a>
       (II, 7, 1):
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        For Parmenides says that there are circular bands wound round one upon the other, one made of the rare, the other of the dense; and others between these mixed of light and darkness. What surrounds them all is solid like a wall. Beneath it is a fiery band, and what is in the very middle of them all is solid, around which again is a fiery band. The most central of the mixed bands is for them all the origin and cause of motion and becoming, which he also calls steering goddess and keyholder and Justice and Necessity. The air has been separated off from the earth, vapourized by its more violent condensation, and the
        <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
         sun
        </a>
        and the circle of the
        <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">
         Milky Way
        </a>
        are exhalations of fire. The
        <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
         moon
        </a>
        is a mixture of both earth and fire. The
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Aether_(classical_element)" title="Aether (classical element)">
          aether
         </a>
        </i>
        lies around above all else, and beneath it is ranged that fiery part which we call
        <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">
         heaven
        </a>
        , beneath which are the regions around the earth.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
         <a href="#cite_note-25">
          [25]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations_of_Parmenides">
        Interpretations of Parmenides
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmenides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Interpretations of Parmenides">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The traditional interpretation of Parmenides' work is that he argued that the every-day
       <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">
        perception
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">
        reality
       </a>
       of the physical world (as described in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Doxa" title="Doxa">
         doxa
        </a>
       </i>
       ) is mistaken, and that the reality of the world is 'One Being' (as described in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Aletheia" title="Aletheia">
         aletheia
        </a>
       </i>
       ): an unchanging, ungenerated, indestructible whole. Under the
       <i>
        Way of Opinion
       </i>
       , Parmenides set out a contrasting but more conventional view of the world, thereby becoming an early exponent of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dualism_(philosophy_of_mind)" title="Dualism (philosophy of mind)">
        duality
       </a>
       of appearance and reality. For him and his pupils, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenomena" title="Phenomena">
        phenomena
       </a>
       of movement and change are simply appearances of a changeless,
       <a href="/wiki/Eternity" title="Eternity">
        eternal
       </a>
       reality. This interpretation could settle because of various wrong translations of the fragments. For example, it is not at all clear that Parmenides refuted that which we call perception. The verb
       <i>
        noein
       </i>
       , used frequently by Parmenides, could better be translated as 'to be aware of' than as 'to think'. Furthermore, it is hard to believe that 'being' is only within our heads, according to Parmenides.
      </p>
      <p>
       Parmenides' philosophy is presented in the form of poetry. The philosophy he argued was, he says, given to him by a goddess, though the "mythological" details in Parmenides' poem do not bear any close correspondence to anything known from traditional Greek mythology:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Welcome, youth, who come attended by immortal charioteers and mares which bear you on your journey to our dwelling. For it is no evil fate that has set you to travel on this road, far from the beaten paths of men, but right and justice. It is meet that you learn all things — both the unshakable heart of well-rounded truth and the opinions of mortals in which there is not true belief. (B 1.24–30)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       It is with respect to this religious/mystical context that recent generations of scholars such as Alexander P. Mourelatos, Charles H. Kahn, and the controversial
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kingsley_(scholar)" title="Peter Kingsley (scholar)">
        Peter Kingsley
       </a>
       have begun to call parts of the traditional, rational logical/philosophical interpretation of Parmenides into question (Kingsley in particular stating that Parmenides practiced
       <a href="/wiki/Iatromantis" title="Iatromantis">
        iatromancy
       </a>
       ). It has been claimed that previous scholars placed too little emphasis on the apocalyptic context in which Parmenides frames his revelation. As a result, traditional interpretations have put Parmenidean philosophy into a more modern, metaphysical context to which it is not necessarily well suited, which has led to misunderstanding of the true meaning and intention of Parmenides' message. The obscurity and fragmentary state of the text, however, renders almost every claim that can be made about Parmenides extremely contentious, and the traditional interpretation has by no means been abandoned.
      </p>
      <p>
       Parmenides' considerable influence on the thinking of
       <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
        Plato
       </a>
       is undeniable, and in this respect Parmenides has influenced the whole history of
       <a href="/wiki/Western_philosophy" title="Western philosophy">
        Western philosophy
       </a>
       , and is often seen as its grandfather. Even Plato himself, in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sophist_(dialogue)" title="Sophist (dialogue)">
         Sophist
        </a>
       </i>
       , refers to the work of "our Father Parmenides" as something to be taken very seriously and treated with respect. In the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parmenides_(Plato)" title="Parmenides (Plato)">
         Parmenides
        </a>
       </i>
       , the Eleatic philosopher, which may well be Parmenides himself, and
       <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">
        Socrates
       </a>
       argue about
       <a href="/wiki/Dialectic" title="Dialectic">
        dialectic
       </a>
       . In the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theaetetus_(Plato)" title="Theaetetus (Plato)">
         Theaetetus
        </a>
       </i>
       , Socrates says that Parmenides alone among the wise (
       <a href="/wiki/Protagoras" title="Protagoras">
        Protagoras
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus" title="Heraclitus">
        Heraclitus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Empedocles" title="Empedocles">
        Empedocles
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epicharmus" title="Epicharmus">
        Epicharmus
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">
        Homer
       </a>
       ) denied that everything is change and motion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Parmenides is credited with a great deal of influence as the author of an "Eleatic challenge" that determined the course of subsequent philosophers' enquiries. For example, the ideas of
       <a href="/wiki/Empedocles" title="Empedocles">
        Empedocles
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Anaxagoras" title="Anaxagoras">
        Anaxagoras
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Leucippus" title="Leucippus">
        Leucippus
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">
        Democritus
       </a>
       have been seen as in response to Parmenides' arguments and conclusions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Parmenides' influence on philosophy reaches up till present times. The Italian philosopher
       <a href="/wiki/Emanuele_Severino" title="Emanuele Severino">
        Emanuele Severino
       </a>
       has founded his extended philosophical investigations on the words of Parmenides. His philosophy is sometimes called Neo Parmenideism, and can be understood as an attempt to build a bridge between the poem on truth and the poem on opinion.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_the_development_of_science">
        Influence on the development of science
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmenides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Influence on the development of science">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Parmenides made the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontological" title="Ontological">
        ontological
       </a>
       argument against nothingness,
       <i>
        essentially denying the possible existence of a void
       </i>
       . According to
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       , this led
       <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">
        Democritus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Leucippus" title="Leucippus">
        Leucippus
       </a>
       , and many other physicists,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Physics-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to propose the
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_theory" title="Atomic theory">
        atomic theory
       </a>
       , which supposes that everything in the universe is either atoms or voids, specifically to contradict Parmenides' argument.
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       himself reasoned, in opposition to atomism, that in a complete vacuum, motion would encounter no resistance, and "no one could say why a thing once set in motion should stop anywhere; for why should it stop here rather than here? So that a thing will either be at rest or must be moved ad infinitum, unless something more powerful get in its way."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Physics-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       See also
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Horror_vacui_(physics)" title="Horror vacui (physics)">
         horror vacui
        </a>
        .
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger" title="Erwin Schrödinger">
        Erwin Schrödinger
       </a>
       identified Parmenides'
       <a href="/wiki/Monad_(philosophy)" title="Monad (philosophy)">
        monad
       </a>
       of the "Way of Truth" as being the conscious self in "Nature and the Greeks".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The scientific implications of this view have been discussed by scientist
       <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Hyman" title="Anthony Hyman">
        Anthony Hyman
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A shadow of Parmenides' ideas can be seen in the physical concept of
       <a href="/wiki/Eternalism_(philosophy_of_time)" title="Eternalism (philosophy of time)">
        Block time
       </a>
       , which considers existence to consist of past, present, and future, and the flow of time to be illusory. In his critique of this idea,
       <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">
        Karl Popper
       </a>
       called
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Einstein
       </a>
       "Parmenides".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, Popper did write:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        So what was really new in Parmenides was his axiomatic-deductive method, which
        <a href="/wiki/Leucippus" title="Leucippus">
         Leucippus
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">
         Democritus
        </a>
        turned into a hypothetical-deductive method, and thus made part of scientific methodology.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
         <a href="#cite_note-31">
          [31]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
