      <p>
       <b>
        STEREO
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        Solar Terrestrial Relations Observatory
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       observation mission.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Two nearly identical spacecraft were launched in 2006 into orbits around the Sun that cause them to respectively pull farther ahead of and fall gradually behind the Earth. This enables
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoscopic" title="Stereoscopic">
        stereoscopic
       </a>
       imaging of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       and solar phenomena, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       The two STEREO spacecraft were launched at 00:52 UTC on October 26, 2006, from Launch Pad 17B at the
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">
        Cape Canaveral Air Force Station
       </a>
       in Florida on a
       <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">
        Delta II
       </a>
       7925-10L launcher into highly
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        elliptical
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_orbit" title="Geocentric orbit">
        geocentric orbits
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        apogee
       </a>
       reached the Moon's orbit. On December 15, 2006, on the fifth orbit, the pair swung by the Moon for a
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">
        gravity assist
       </a>
       . Because the two spacecraft were in slightly different orbits, the "ahead" (A) spacecraft was ejected to a
       <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">
        heliocentric orbit
       </a>
       inside Earth's orbit while the "behind" (B) spacecraft remained temporarily in a high Earth orbit. The B spacecraft encountered the Moon again on the same orbital revolution on January 21, 2007, being ejected from Earth orbit in the opposite direction from spacecraft A. Spacecraft B entered a heliocentric orbit outside the Earth's orbit. Spacecraft A will take 347 days to complete one revolution of the Sun and Spacecraft B will take 387 days. The A spacecraft/Sun/Earth angle will increase at 21.650 degree/year. The B spacecraft/Sun/Earth angle will change −21.999 degrees per year. Given that the length of Earth's orbit is around 940 million kilometres, both craft have an average speed, in a rotating geocentric frame of reference in which the sun is always in the same direction, of about 1.8 km/s, but the speed varies considerably depending on how close they are to their respective aphelion or perihelion (as well as on the position of Earth). Their current locations are shown
       <a class="external text" href="http://stereo.gsfc.nasa.gov/where.shtml" rel="nofollow">
        here
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Over time, the STEREO spacecraft will continue to separate from each other at a combined rate of approximately 44 degrees per year. There are no
       <i>
        final
       </i>
       positions for the spacecraft. They achieved 90 degrees separation on January 24, 2009, a condition known as
       <a href="/wiki/Quadrature_(astronomy)" title="Quadrature (astronomy)">
        quadrature
       </a>
       . This is of interest because the mass ejections seen from the side on the limb by one spacecraft can potentially be observed by the
       <i>
        in situ
       </i>
       particle experiments of the other spacecraft. As they passed through Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrangian points
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         5
        </sub>
       </a>
       , in late 2009, they searched for
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_(astronomy)" title="Trojan (astronomy)">
        Lagrangian (trojan) asteroids
       </a>
       . On February 6, 2011, the two spacecraft were exactly 180 degrees apart from each other, allowing the entire Sun to be seen at once for the first time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Even as the angle increases, the addition of an Earth-based view,
       <i>
        e.g.
       </i>
       , from the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
        Solar Dynamics Observatory
       </a>
       , will still provide full-Sun observations for several years. In 2015, contact was lost for several months when the STEREO spacecraft passed behind the Sun. They will then start to approach Earth again, with closest approach sometime in 2023. They will not be recaptured into Earth orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20151211_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20151211-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_of_contact">
        Loss of contact
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=STEREO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Loss of contact">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On October 1, 2014, contact was lost with STEREO-B during a planned reset to test the craft's automation, in anticipation of the aforementioned solar "conjunction" period. The team originally thought the spacecraft had begun to spin, decreasing the amount of power that could be generated by the solar panels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20151211_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20151211-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       NASA used its
       <a href="/wiki/NASA_Deep_Space_Network" title="NASA Deep Space Network">
        Deep Space Network
       </a>
       , first weekly and later monthly, to try to re-establish communications.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20151211_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20151211-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After a silence of 22 months, contact was regained at 22:27 UTC on August 21, 2016, when the Deep Space Network established a lock on STEREO-B for 2.4 hours.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20160822_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20160822-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stereonews20161023_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-stereonews20161023-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Later analysis of the received telemetry concluded that the spacecraft was in an uncontrolled spin of about three degrees per second; this is too rapid to be immediately corrected using its
       <a href="/wiki/Reaction_wheel" title="Reaction wheel">
        reaction wheels
       </a>
       , which would become oversaturated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stereonews20161023_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-stereonews20161023-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Engineers will work to develop software to fix the spacecraft, but once its computer is powered on there will only be about two minutes to upload the fix before STEREO-B enters failure mode again.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businside20160823_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-businside20160823-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Further, while the spacecraft was power positive at the time of contact, its orientation will drift and power levels will fall.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stereonews20161023_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-stereonews20161023-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As of 11 October 2016
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=STEREO&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , recovery attempts have been reduced until the spacecraft returns to a favorable orbit. Six attempts at communication between September 27 and October 9 failed, and a carrier wave has not been detected since September 23.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stereonews20161023_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-stereonews20161023-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_benefits">
        Mission benefits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=STEREO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mission benefits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The principal benefit of the mission is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoscopic" title="Stereoscopic">
        stereoscopic
       </a>
       images of the Sun. In other words, because the satellites are at different points along the Earth's orbit, but distant from the Earth, they can photograph parts of the Sun that are not visible from the Earth. This permits NASA scientists to directly monitor the far side of the Sun, instead of inferring the activity on the far side from data that can be gleaned from Earth's view of the Sun. The STEREO satellites principally monitor the far side for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejections" title="Coronal mass ejections">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       — massive bursts of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       , solar
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       , and magnetic fields that are sometimes ejected into space.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBC_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CBC-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the radiation from coronal mass ejections, or CMEs, can disrupt Earth's communications, airlines, power grids, and satellites, more accurate forecasting of CMEs has the potential to provide greater warning to operators of these services.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBC_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-CBC-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Before STEREO, the detection of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspots
       </a>
       that are associated with CMEs on the far side of the Sun was only possible using
       <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">
        helioseismology
       </a>
       , which only provides low-resolution maps of the activity on the far side of the Sun. Since the Sun rotates every 25 days, detail on the far side was invisible to Earth for days at a time before STEREO. The period that the Sun's far side was previously invisible was a principal reason for the STEREO mission.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       STEREO program scientist
       <a href="/wiki/Madhulika_Guhathakurta" title="Madhulika Guhathakurta">
        Madhulika Guhathakurta
       </a>
       expects "great advances" in theoretical
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_physics" title="Solar physics">
        solar physics
       </a>
       and space weather forecasting with the advent of constant 360-degree views of the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       STEREO's observations are already being incorporated into forecasts of solar activity for airlines, power companies, satellite operators, and others.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       STEREO has also been used to discover 122
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eclipsing_binaries#Eclipsing_binaries" title="Eclipsing binaries">
        eclipsing binaries
       </a>
       and study hundreds more
       <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">
        variable stars
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variable_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-variable-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       STEREO can look at the same star for up to 20 days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variable_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-variable-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On July 23, 2012, STEREO-A was in the path of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_2012" title="Solar storm of 2012">
        Solar storm of 2012
       </a>
       which was similar in strength to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrington_Event" title="Carrington Event">
        Carrington Event
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-July2012CME_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-July2012CME-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its instrumentation was able to collect and relay a significant amount of data about the event. STEREO-A was not harmed by the solar storm.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Science_instrumentation">
        Science instrumentation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=STEREO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Science instrumentation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stereo_mission.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="373" data-file-width="392" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Stereo_mission.jpg/220px-Stereo_mission.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Stereo_mission.jpg/330px-Stereo_mission.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d7/Stereo_mission.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stereo_mission.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Instrument locations on STEREO
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Each of the spacecraft carries cameras, particle experiments and radio detectors in four instrument packages:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Sun Earth Connection Coronal and Heliospheric Investigation (SECCHI)
        </b>
        has five cameras: an extreme ultraviolet imager (EUVI) and two white-light
        <a href="/wiki/Coronagraph" title="Coronagraph">
         coronagraphs
        </a>
        (COR1 and COR2). These three telescopes are collectively known as the Sun Centered Instrument Package or SCIP, and image the solar disk and the inner and outer
        <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
         corona
        </a>
        . Two additional telescopes,
        <a href="/wiki/Heliospheric_imager" title="Heliospheric imager">
         heliospheric imagers
        </a>
        (called the HI1 and HI2) image the space between Sun and Earth. The purpose of SECCHI is to study the 3-D evolution of
        <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
         coronal mass ejections
        </a>
        through their full journey from the Sun's surface through the corona and interplanetary medium to their impact at Earth.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inst_14-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-inst-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         In-situ Measurements of Particles and CME Transients (IMPACT)
        </b>
        will study
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles" title="Solar energetic particles">
         energetic particles
        </a>
        , the three-dimensional distribution of solar wind electrons and interplanetary magnetic field.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inst_14-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-inst-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         PLAsma and SupraThermal Ion Composition (PLASTIC)
        </b>
        will study the plasma characteristics of
        <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">
         protons
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">
         alpha particles
        </a>
        and
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Heavy_ion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Heavy ion (page does not exist)">
         heavy ions
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inst_14-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-inst-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         STEREO/WAVES (SWAVES)
        </b>
        is a radio burst tracker that will study radio disturbances traveling from the Sun to the orbit of Earth.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inst_14-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-inst-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_subsystems">
        Spacecraft subsystems
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=STEREO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spacecraft subsystems">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Each STEREO spacecraft had a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dry_mass" title="Dry mass">
        dry mass
       </a>
       of 547 kg (1,206 lb) and a launch mass of 619 kg (1,364 lb). In their stowed configuration, each had a length, width and height of 2.0 × 1.2 × 1.1 m (6.67 × 4.00 × 3.75 ft). Upon solar array deployment, its width increased to 6.5 m (21.24 ft).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsfc-stereo1_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gsfc-stereo1-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsfc-stereo2_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gsfc-stereo2-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       With all of its instrument booms and antennae deployed, its dimensions are 7.5 × 8.7 × 5.9 m (24.5 × 28.6 × 19.2 ft).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jhuapl-stereo1_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jhuapl-stereo1-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The solar panels can produce an average of 596 watts of power, and the spacecraft consumes an average of 475 watts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsfc-stereo1_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-gsfc-stereo1-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsfc-stereo2_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-gsfc-stereo2-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The STEREO spacecraft are 3-axis stabilized, and each has a primary and backup
       <a href="/wiki/Miniature_inertial_measurement_unit" title="Miniature inertial measurement unit">
        miniature inertial measurement unit
       </a>
       (MIMU) provided by
       <a href="/wiki/Honeywell" title="Honeywell">
        Honeywell
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These measure changes to a spacecraft's attitude, and each MIMU contains three
       <a href="/wiki/Ring_laser_gyroscope" title="Ring laser gyroscope">
        ring laser gyroscopes
       </a>
       to detect angular changes. Additional attitude information is provided by the
       <a href="/wiki/Star_tracker" title="Star tracker">
        star tracker
       </a>
       and the SECCHI Guide Telescope.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Driesman2008_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Driesman2008-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       STEREO's onboard computer systems are based on the Integrated Electronics Module (IEM), a device that combines core
       <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">
        avionics
       </a>
       in a single box. Each single-string spacecraft carries two CPUs, one for command-and-data handling and one for guidance and control. Both are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_hardened" title="Radiation hardened">
        radiation hardened
       </a>
       25
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz">
        megahertz
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/IBM_RAD6000" title="IBM RAD6000">
        IBM RAD6000
       </a>
       processors, based on
       <a href="/wiki/POWER1" title="POWER1">
        POWER1
       </a>
       CPUs (predecessor of the PowerPC chip found in older
       <a href="/wiki/Macintosh" title="Macintosh">
        Macintoshes
       </a>
       ). The computers, slow by current
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">
        personal computer
       </a>
       standards, are typical for the radiation requirements needed on the STEREO mission.
      </p>
      <p>
       STEREO also carries Actel
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FPGA" title="FPGA">
        FPGAs
       </a>
       that use
       <a href="/wiki/Triple_modular_redundancy" title="Triple modular redundancy">
        triple modular redundancy
       </a>
       for radiation hardening. The FPGAs hold the
       <a href="/wiki/Minimal_instruction_set_computer" title="Minimal instruction set computer">
        P24 MISC
       </a>
       and CPU24
       <a href="/wiki/Soft_microprocessor" title="Soft microprocessor">
        soft microprocessors
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mewaldt2008_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mewaldt2008-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For data storage, each spacecraft carries a
       <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_drive" title="Solid-state drive">
        solid state
       </a>
       recorder able to store up to one
       <a href="/wiki/Gigabyte" title="Gigabyte">
        gigabyte
       </a>
       each. Its main processor collects and stores on the recorder images and other data from STEREO's instruments, which can then be sent back to Earth. The spacecraft have an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-band" title="X-band">
        X-band
       </a>
       downlink capacity of between 427 and 750
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilobit_per_second" title="Kilobit per second">
        kbps
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsfc-stereo1_17-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-gsfc-stereo1-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsfc-stereo2_18-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-gsfc-stereo2-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
