      <p>
       <b>
        Gemini 12
       </b>
       (officially
       <b>
        Gemini XII
       </b>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-titans_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-titans-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       was a 1966
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_spaceflight" title="Manned spaceflight">
        manned spaceflight
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">
        Project Gemini
       </a>
       . It was the 10th and final manned Gemini flight, the 18th manned American spaceflight, and the 26th spaceflight of all time, including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-15" title="X-15">
        X-15
       </a>
       flights over 100 kilometers (54 nmi). Commanded by
       <a href="/wiki/Gemini_7" title="Gemini 7">
        Gemini VII
       </a>
       veteran
       <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">
        James A. Lovell
       </a>
       , the flight featured three periods of
       <a href="/wiki/Extravehicular_activity" title="Extravehicular activity">
        extravehicular activity
       </a>
       (EVA) by rookie
       <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
        Edwin "Buzz" Aldrin
       </a>
       , lasting a total of 5 hours and 30 minutes. It also achieved the fifth
       <a href="/wiki/Space_rendezvous" title="Space rendezvous">
        rendezvous
       </a>
       and fourth
       <a href="/wiki/Docking_and_berthing_of_spacecraft" title="Docking and berthing of spacecraft">
        docking
       </a>
       with an
       <a href="/wiki/Agena_target_vehicle" title="Agena target vehicle">
        Agena target vehicle
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Gemini XII marked a successful conclusion of the Gemini program, achieving the last of its goals by successfully demonstrating that astronauts can effectively work outside of spacecraft. This was instrumental in paving the way for the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       to achieve its goal of landing a man on the Moon by the end of the 1960s.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">
        Launch
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gemini_12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Launch">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Liftoff of the Atlas/Agena Target Vehicle occurred at 2:07:59 PM EST, and of the Gemini/Titan spacecraft at 3:46:33 PM EST, on November 11.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mission_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-mission-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       All launch vehicle systems performed nominally during powered flight, but at staging there was a recurrence of the first stage oxidizer tank rupture first seen on
       <a href="/wiki/Gemini_10" title="Gemini 10">
        Gemini 10
       </a>
       's launch. On Gemini 12, the fuel tank appeared to have also ruptured as a white cloud was seen emitting from the spent stage along with the orange
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_tetroxide" title="Nitrogen tetroxide">
        nitrogen tetroxide
       </a>
       . Another episode of "Green Man" also occurred at SECO, referring to pitch gyrations caused by pressure buildup in the second stage protective skirt.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives">
        Objectives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S66-63387_PR.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2665" data-file-width="2623" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/S66-63387_PR.jpg/220px-S66-63387_PR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/S66-63387_PR.jpg/330px-S66-63387_PR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/S66-63387_PR.jpg/440px-S66-63387_PR.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>

          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S66-63387_PR.jpg" title="Enlarge">

         The Gemini 12 spacecraft and the Agena, photographed at 15 feet apart during docking
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gemini_12_tethered_stationkeeping.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="7060" data-file-width="7307" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Gemini_12_tethered_stationkeeping.jpg/220px-Gemini_12_tethered_stationkeeping.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Gemini_12_tethered_stationkeeping.jpg/330px-Gemini_12_tethered_stationkeeping.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Gemini_12_tethered_stationkeeping.jpg/440px-Gemini_12_tethered_stationkeeping.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gemini_12_tethered_stationkeeping.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Gemini 12 tethered stationkeeping
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       At the completion of the previous Gemini flight, the program still had not demonstrated that an astronaut could work easily and efficiently outside the spacecraft. In preparation for Gemini XII new, improved restraints were added to the outside of the capsule, and a new technique—underwater training—was introduced, which would become a staple of future space-walk simulation. Aldrin's two-hour, 20-minute tethered space-walk, during which he photographed star fields, retrieved a micrometeorite collector and did other chores, at last demonstrated the feasibility of extravehicular activity. Two more stand-up EVAs also went smoothly, as did the by-now routine rendezvous and docking with an Agena which was done "manually" using the onboard computer and charts when a rendezvous radar failed. The climb to a higher orbit, however, was canceled because of a problem with the
       <a href="/wiki/Agena_target_vehicle" title="Agena target vehicle">
        Agena
       </a>
       booster.
      </p>
      <p>
       During orbital injection, the GATV engine experienced a drop in turbopump speed lasting about 2.5 seconds. After this, pump performance returned to normal. Telemetry data indicated erratic pump speeds, but engine performance did not reflect this. Ground controllers decided not to risk the planned orbital boost maneuver since the exact reason for the pump slowdown was unclear. Following Gemini 12's reentry and during the GATV's 63rd orbit, they attempted to fire the propulsion system, but a stuck fuel valve prevented engine start from occurring.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was suspected that a turbopump bearing failure caused the anomalous conditions during orbital injection, followed by heating and melting of pump components. The inability of ground controllers to start the engine during the 63rd orbit was possibly due to melted or loose debris blocking the fuel valve and preventing its operation. The telemetry data falsely reporting erratic pump speed was concluded to be debris being knocked around and affecting the data probes.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many documentaries
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:AUDIENCE" title="Wikipedia:AUDIENCE">
          <span title="Wikipedia:AUDIENCE">
           example needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       afterward largely credit the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">
        spacewalk
       </a>
       innovations, including the
       <a href="/wiki/Neutral_buoyancy_simulation_as_a_training_aid" title="Neutral buoyancy simulation as a training aid">
        underwater training
       </a>
       , to Aldrin himself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Gemini 12 was designed to perform rendezvous and docking with the Agena target vehicle, to conduct three
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">
        extra-vehicular activity (EVA)
       </a>
       operations, to conduct a tethered stationkeeping exercise, to perform docked maneuvers using the Agena propulsion system to change orbit, and demonstrate an automatic reentry.
      </p>

      <p>
       When Gemini 12 was being planned, one of the possibilities raised was the potential for the flight to be run in conjunction with the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">
        first Apollo mission
       </a>
       , which had been tentatively scheduled for the last quarter of 1966.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By May 1966, delays in making Apollo ready for flight just by itself, and the extra time needed to incorporate compatibility with the Gemini, made that impractical.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This became moot when slippage in readiness of the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">
        Apollo spacecraft
       </a>
       caused the last-quarter 1966 target date to be missed, and the Apollo mission was rescheduled for February 21, 1967.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SP4029_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SP4029-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The 14 scientific experiments were (1) frog egg growth under zero-g, (2) synoptic terrain photography, (3) synoptic weather photography, (4) nuclear emulsions, (5) airglow horizon photography, (6) UV astronomical photography, and (7) dim sky photography. Two micrometeorite collection experiments, as well as three space phenomena photography experiments, were not fully completed.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reentry">
        Reentry
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gemini_12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reentry">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The capsule was controlled on reentry by computer and splashed down 4.8 kilometers from its target. The crew were taken aboard the aircraft carrier
       <a href="/wiki/USS_Wasp_(CV-18)" title="USS Wasp (CV-18)">
        USS
        <i>
         Wasp
        </i>
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Gemini 12 mission was supported by the following U.S. Department of Defense resources; 9,775 personnel, 65 aircraft and 12 ships.
      </p>
      <p>
       Postflight medical examination disclosed no unusual conditions in either astronaut. Both were slightly exhausted and dehydrated due to problems with the spacecraft's water supply system forcing them to reduce their fluid intake on the last day of the mission and Lovell had a mild case of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinkeye" title="Pinkeye">
        pinkeye
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Insignia">
        Insignia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gemini_12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Insignia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gemini_12_Flown_Fliteline_Gold-Plated_Sterling_Silver_Medallion.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2124" data-file-width="4206" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Gemini_12_Flown_Fliteline_Gold-Plated_Sterling_Silver_Medallion.jpg/220px-Gemini_12_Flown_Fliteline_Gold-Plated_Sterling_Silver_Medallion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Gemini_12_Flown_Fliteline_Gold-Plated_Sterling_Silver_Medallion.jpg/330px-Gemini_12_Flown_Fliteline_Gold-Plated_Sterling_Silver_Medallion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Gemini_12_Flown_Fliteline_Gold-Plated_Sterling_Silver_Medallion.jpg/440px-Gemini_12_Flown_Fliteline_Gold-Plated_Sterling_Silver_Medallion.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gemini_12_Flown_Fliteline_Gold-Plated_Sterling_Silver_Medallion.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Gemini 12 space-flown
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_space-flown_Robbins_medallions_of_the_Apollo_missions#Gemini_mission_space-flown_Fliteline_medallions" title="NASA space-flown Robbins medallions of the Apollo missions">
          Fliteline Medallion
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The patch's unique orange and black colors are a link to the flight's original scheduled date close to Halloween. The Roman numeral XII is located at the 12 o'clock position on the face of a clock, with the Gemini spacecraft pointing to it like the hour hand of a clock. This represents the position of Gemini 12 as the last flight of the Gemini program. With the Apollo project following this last flight of the Gemini program, the ultimate objective—the Moon—is symbolized by the crescent on the left.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_location">
        Spacecraft location
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gemini_12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Spacecraft location">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       After several years at the
       <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Transport_and_Technology" title="Museum of Transport and Technology">
        Museum of Transport and Technology
       </a>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">
        Auckland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       , the spacecraft was returned to the United States. It is now on display at the
       <a href="/wiki/Adler_Planetarium" title="Adler Planetarium">
        Adler Planetarium
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">
        Chicago, Illinois
       </a>
       . Lovell and Aldrin were reunited with the spacecraft November 9, 2006 during the opening for Adler's "Shoot for the Moon" exhibit, almost 40 years after the mission launched.
      </p>
