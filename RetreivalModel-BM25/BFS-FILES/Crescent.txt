       A
       <b>
        crescent
       </b>
       shape (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">

       ,
       <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
        British English
       </a>
       also
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">
            ɛ
           </span>
           <span title="'z' in 'zoom'">
            z
           </span>
           <span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">
            ən
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">

           </span>
          </span>

         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">
        symbol
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Emblem" title="Emblem">
        emblem
       </a>
       used to represent the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        lunar phase
       </a>
       in the first quarter (the "sickle moon"), or by extension a symbol representing the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       itself.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is used as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrological_symbol" title="Astrological symbol">
        astrological symbol
       </a>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_(astrology)" title="Moon (astrology)">
        the Moon
       </a>
       , and hence as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alchemical" title="Alchemical">
        alchemical
       </a>
       symbol for
       <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">
        silver
       </a>
       . It was also the emblem of
       <a href="/wiki/Diana_(mythology)" title="Diana (mythology)">
        Diana
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">
        Artemis
       </a>
       , and hence represented
       <a href="/wiki/Virginity" title="Virginity">
        virginity
       </a>
       . In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">
        Roman Catholic
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marian_veneration" title="Marian veneration">
        Marian veneration
       </a>
       , it is associated with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" title="Virgin Mary">
        Virgin Mary
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       From its use as roof
       <a href="/wiki/Finial" title="Finial">
        finial
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_era" title="Ottoman era">
        Ottoman era
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">
        mosques
       </a>
       , it has also become associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">
        Islam
       </a>
       , and the crescent was introduced as
       <a href="/wiki/Religious_symbolism_in_the_United_States_military#Muslim" title="Religious symbolism in the United States military">
        chaplain badge
       </a>
       for Muslim chaplains in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_military" title="US military">
        US military
       </a>
       in 1993.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USchaplains_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-USchaplains-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism">
        Symbolism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crescent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Symbolism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tmulti tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:448px;max-width:448px">
        <div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:242px;max-width:242px">
         <div class="thumbimage">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nuremberg_chronicles_f_76r_3.png">
           <img alt="" data-file-height="323" data-file-width="342" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Nuremberg_chronicles_f_76r_3.png/240px-Nuremberg_chronicles_f_76r_3.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b2/Nuremberg_chronicles_f_76r_3.png 1.5x" width="240"/>
          </a>
         </div>
         <div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">
          Sun and Moon with faces (1493 woodcut)
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:202px;max-width:202px">

          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marriage_Sol_Moon_-_Rosarium_Philosophorum_Griemiller02.jpg">

          </a>
         </div>
         <div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">
          <i>
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hierosgamos" title="Hierosgamos">

           </a>
          </i>
          of Sun and Moon in a 16th-century alchemical manuscript
         </div>

        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The crescent symbol is primarily used to represent the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , not necessarily in a particular
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        lunar phase
       </a>
       . When used to represent a waxing or waning lunar phase, "crescent" or "increscent" refers to the waxing first quarter, while the symbol representing the waning final quarter is called "decrescent".
      </p>
      <p>
       The crescent symbol was long used as a symbol of the Moon in
       <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">
        astrology
       </a>
       , and by extension of
       <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">
        Silver
       </a>
       (as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrology_and_alchemy" title="Astrology and alchemy">
        corresponding metal
       </a>
       ) in
       <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">
        alchemy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The astrological use of the symbol is attested in early Greek papyri containing
       <a href="/wiki/Horoscope" title="Horoscope">
        horoscopes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the 2nd-century
       <i>
        Bianchini's
        <a href="/wiki/Planisphere" title="Planisphere">
         planisphere
        </a>
       </i>
       , the personification of the Moon is shown with a crescent attached to her headdress.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Its ancient association with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ishtar" title="Ishtar">
        Ishtar/Astarte
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Diana_(mythology)" title="Diana (mythology)">
        Diana
       </a>
       is preserved in the Moon (as symbolised by a crescent) representing the
       <a href="/wiki/Femininity" title="Femininity">
        female principle
       </a>
       (as juxtaposed with the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       representing the
       <a href="/wiki/Masculinity" title="Masculinity">
        male principle
       </a>
       ), and (Artemis-Diana being a virgin goddess) especially
       <a href="/wiki/Virginity" title="Virginity">
        virginity
       </a>
       and female
       <a href="/wiki/Chastity" title="Chastity">
        chastity
       </a>
       . In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">
        Roman Catholic
       </a>
       tradition, the crescent entered
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marian_iconography" title="Marian iconography">
        Marian iconography
       </a>
       , by the association of Mary with the
       <a href="/wiki/Woman_of_the_Apocalypse" title="Woman of the Apocalypse">
        Woman of the Apocalypse
       </a>
       (described with "the moon under her feet, and on her head a crown of twelve stars" in
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">
        Revelation
       </a>
       ) The most well known representation of Mary as the Woman of the Apocalypse is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_of_Guadalupe" title="Virgin of Guadalupe">
        Virgin of Guadalupe
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div style="clear:both;">
      </div>
      <p>
       The symbol of the Crescent is an emblem of Islam. It denotes divine authority, increase, resurrection and, with a star, it denotes paradise.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Shape">

      <p>
       The crescent shape consists of a
       <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">
        circular
       </a>
       disk with a segment of another circle removed from its edge, so that what remains is a shape enclosed by two circular arcs of different diameters which intersect at two points (usually in such a manner that the enclosed shape does not include the center of the original circle). As such, it belongs to the class of figures known as
       <a href="/wiki/Lune_(geometry)" title="Lune (geometry)">
        lune
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planar_geometry" title="Planar geometry">
        planar geometry
       </a>
       . The tapering towards the points of intersection of the two arcs are known as the "horns" of the crescent. The classical crescent shape has its horns pointing upward (and is often worn as horns when worn as a crown or diadem, e.g. in depictions of the lunar goddess, or in the headdress of Persian kings, etc.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The word
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/crescent" title="wikt:crescent">
         crescent
        </a>
       </i>
       is derived etymologically from the present participle of the Latin verb
       <i>
        crescere
       </i>
       "to grow", technically denoting the waxing moon (
       <i>
        luna crescens
       </i>
       ). As seen from the northern hemisphere, the waxing Moon tends to appear with its horns pointing towards the left, and conversely the waning Moon with its horns pointing towards the right; the English word
       <i>
        crescent
       </i>
       may however refer to the shape regardless of its orientation, except for the technical language of
       <a href="/wiki/Blazon" title="Blazon">
        blazoning
       </a>
       used in
       <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">
        heraldry
       </a>
       , where the word "increscent" refers to a crescent shape with its horns to the left, and "decrescent" refers to one with its horns to the right, while the word "crescent" on its own denotes a crescent shape with horns pointing upward.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The shape of the lit side of a spherical body (most notably the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       ) that appears to be less than half illuminated by the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       as seen by the viewer appears in a different shape from what is generally termed a crescent in planar geometry: Assuming the
       <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">
        terminator
       </a>
       lies on a
       <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">
        great circle
       </a>
       , The crescent Moon will actually apperar as the figure bounded by a half-
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        ellipse
       </a>
       and a half-circle, with the major axis of the ellipse coinciding with a diameter of the semicircle.
      </p>
      <p>
       Unicode encodes a crescent (increscent) at U+263D (☽) and a decrescent at U+263E (☾). The
       <a href="/wiki/Miscellaneous_Symbols_and_Pictographs" title="Miscellaneous Symbols and Pictographs">
        Miscellaneous Symbols and Pictographs
       </a>
       block provides variants with faces: FIRST QUARTER MOON WITH FACE at U+1F31B (🌛) and LAST QUARTER MOON WITH FACE at U+1F31C (🌜).
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_Antiquity">
        Classical Antiquity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crescent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Classical Antiquity">
         edit

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the iconography of the
       <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">
        Hellenistic period
       </a>
       , the crescent became the symbol of
       <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">
        Artemis
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Diana_(mythology)" title="Diana (mythology)">
        Diana
       </a>
       , the virgin hunter goddess associated with the Moon. Numerous depictions show Artemis-Diana wearing the crescent Moon as part of her headdress. The related symbol of the
       <a href="/wiki/Star_and_crescent" title="Star and crescent">
        star and crescent
       </a>
       was the emblem of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_I_of_Pontus" title="Mithridates I of Pontus">
        Mithradates
       </a>
       dynasty in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus" title="Kingdom of Pontus">
        Kingdom of Pontus
       </a>
       and was also used as the emblem of
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">
        Byzantium
       </a>
       .
      </p>

        </a>

         <i>

         </i>

         </a>
         , 15th century)
        </div>

      <p>
       The crescent remained in use as an emblem in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sassanid_Persia" title="Sassanid Persia">
        Sassanid Persia
       </a>
       , used as a
       <a href="/wiki/Mah" title="Mah">
        Zoroastrian
       </a>
       regal or astrological symbol. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">
        Crusades
       </a>
       it came to be associated with the
       <a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">
        Orient
       </a>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
        Byzantine Empire
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">
        Levant
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Outremer" title="Outremer">
        Outremer
       </a>
       in general) and was widely used (often alongside a
       <a href="/wiki/Star_and_crescent" title="Star and crescent">
        star
       </a>
       ) in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusader_seals" title="Crusader seals">
        Crusader seals
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusader_coins" title="Crusader coins">
        coins
       </a>
       . It was used as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heraldic_charge" title="Heraldic charge">
        heraldic charge
       </a>
       by the later 13th century.
       <a href="/wiki/Anna_Notaras" title="Anna Notaras">
        Anna Notaras
       </a>
       , daughter of the last Megas Doux of the Byzantine Empire
       <a href="/wiki/Loukas_Notaras" title="Loukas Notaras">
        Loukas Notaras
       </a>
       , after the fall of Constantinople and her emigration to Italy, made a seal with her coat of arms which included
       <i>
        Two Lions holding above the crescent a cross or a sword
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From its use in Sassanid Persia, the crescent also found its way into Islamic iconography after the
       <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Persia" title="Muslim conquest of Persia">
        Muslim conquest of Persia
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Umar" title="Umar">
        Umar
       </a>
       is said to have two hung two crescent-shaped ornaments captured from the Sassanid capital
       <a href="/wiki/Ctesiphon" title="Ctesiphon">
        Ctesiphon
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kaaba" title="Kaaba">
        Kaaba
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The crescent appears to have been adopted as an emblem on
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_flags" title="Islamic flags">
        military flags
       </a>
       by the Islamic armies from at least the 13th century, although the scholarly consensus holds that the widespread use of the crescent in Islam develops later, during the 14th to 15th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The use of such flags is reflected in the 14th-century
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libro_del_Conoscimiento" title="Libro del Conoscimiento">
         Libro del Conoscimiento
        </a>
       </i>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_Atlas" title="Catalan Atlas">
        Catalan Atlas
       </a>
       . Examples include the flags attributed to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gabes" title="Gabes">
        Gabes
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Tlemcen" title="Kingdom of Tlemcen">
        Tlemcen
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hafsid_dynasty" title="Hafsid dynasty">
        Tunis
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Buda" title="Buda">
        Buda
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">
        Nubia
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Dongola" title="Dongola">
        Dongola
       </a>
       (documented by
       <a href="/wiki/Angelino_Dulcert" title="Angelino Dulcert">
        Angelino Dulcert
       </a>
       in 1339) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate_(Cairo)" title="Mamluk Sultanate (Cairo)">
        Mamluks of Egypt
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Roman Catholic fashion of depicting
       <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(art)" title="Madonna (art)">
        Madonna
       </a>
       standing or sitting on a crescent develops in the 15th century.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_and_modern">
        Early modern and modern
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crescent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early modern and modern">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">

      <p>
       The goddess
       <a href="/wiki/Diana_(mythology)" title="Diana (mythology)">
        Diana
       </a>
       was associated with the Moon in classical mythology. In reference to this, feminine jewelry representing crescents, especially diadems, became popular in the early modern period. The
       <a href="/wiki/Tarot" title="Tarot">
        Tarot
       </a>
       card of the "Popess" also wears a crescent on her head.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gr%C3%BCnenberg" title="Conrad Grünenberg">
        Conrad Grünenberg
       </a>
       in his
       <i>
        Pilgrimage to the Holy Land
       </i>
       (1486) consistently depicts cities in the Holy Land with crescent finials.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Flags with crescents appear to have been used on Ottoman vessels since at least the 16th century. Prints depicting the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lepanto" title="Battle of Lepanto">
        Battle of Lepanto
       </a>
       (1571), including the print by Agostino Barberigo of Rome made just a few weeks after the battle,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the Martino Rota of Venice in the following year, show the Ottoman vessels displaying flags with one or several crescents in various orientations (as do the monumental paintings commissioned later based on these prints). Rota also shows numerous crescent finials, both on ships and on fortresses depicted in the background, as well as some finials with stars or suns radiant, and in some cases a sun radiant combined with a crescent in the star-and-crescent configuration. The official adoption of
       <a href="/wiki/Star_and_crescent" title="Star and crescent">
        star and crescent
       </a>
       as the Ottoman state symbol started during the reign of
       <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_III" title="Mustafa III">
        Mustafa III
       </a>
       (1757–1774) and its use became well-established during
       <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Hamid_I" title="Abdul Hamid I">
        Abdul Hamid I
       </a>
       (1774–1789) and
       <a href="/wiki/Selim_III" title="Selim III">
        Selim III
       </a>
       (1789–1807) periods. A
       <i>
        <span lang="tr" title="Turkish language text" xml:lang="tr">
         buyruldu
        </span>
       </i>
       (decree) from 1793 states that the ships in the Ottoman navy have that flag.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islamencyclopedia_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-islamencyclopedia-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Muhammad Ali, who became Pasha of
       <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">
        Egypt
       </a>
       in 1805, introduced the first
       <a href="/wiki/National_flag" title="National flag">
        national flag
       </a>
       of Egypt, red with three white crescents, each accompanied by a white star. The association of the crescent with
       <a href="/wiki/Flags_of_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Flags of the Ottoman Empire">
        the Ottoman Empire
       </a>
       appears to have resulted in a gradual association of the crescent shape with
       <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">
        Islam
       </a>
       in the 20th century. A
       <a href="/wiki/International_Red_Cross_and_Red_Crescent_Movement#The_Red_Crescent" title="International Red Cross and Red Crescent Movement">
        Red Crescent
       </a>
       appears to have been used as a replacement of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Cross" title="Red Cross">
        Red Cross
       </a>
       as early as in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%9378)" title="Russo-Turkish War (1877–78)">
        Russo-Turkish War
       </a>
       of 1877/8, and it was officially adopted in 1929. While some Islamic organisations since the 1970s have embraced the crescent as their logo or emblem (e.g.
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crescent_International" title="Crescent International">
         Crescent International
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine, established 1980), Muslim publications tend to emphasize that the interpretation of the crescent, historically used on the banners of Muslim armies, as a "religious symbol" of Islam was an error made by the "Christians of Europe".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The identification of the crescent as an "Islamic symbol" is mentioned by
       <a href="/wiki/James_Hastings" title="James Hastings">
        James Hastings
       </a>
       as a "common error" to which "even approved writers on Oriental subjects" are prone as early as 1928.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Heraldry">
        Heraldry
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crescent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Heraldry">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Star_and_crescent" title="Star and crescent">
        Star and crescent
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The crescent has been used as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heraldic_charge" title="Heraldic charge">
        heraldic charge
       </a>
       since the 13th century. In heraldic terminology, the term "crescent" when used alone refers to a crescent with the horns pointing upward. A crescent with the horns pointing left (
       <i>
        dexter
       </i>
       ) is called "a crescent increscent" (or simply "an increscent"), and when the horns are pointing right (
       <i>
        sinister
       </i>
       ), it is called "a crescent decrescent" (or "a decrescent"). A crescent with horns pointing down is called "a crescent reversed". Two crescents with horns pointing away from each other are called "addorsed".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox-Davies_209_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fox-Davies_209-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Siebmachers_Wappenbuch" title="Siebmachers Wappenbuch">
         Siebmachers Wappenbuch
        </a>
       </i>
       (1605) has 48 coats of arms with one or more crescents, for example:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Azure, a crescent moon argent pierced by an arrow fesswise Or all between in chief three mullets of six points and in base two mullets of six points argent
        </i>
        (von Hagen, p. 176);
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Azure, an increscent and a decrescent addorsed Or
        </i>
        (von Stoternheim, p. 146);
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Per pale Or and sable, a crescent moon and in chief three mullets of six points counterchanged
        </i>
        (von Bodenstein, p. 182).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In English heraldry, the crescent is used as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Difference_(heraldry)" title="Difference (heraldry)">
        difference
       </a>
       denoting a second son.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox-Davies_209_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fox-Davies_209-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

           </a>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="gallerytext">

           Three examples of coats of arms with crescents from the

           </a>
           (c. 1270): No. 118: Willem FitzLel (Sable
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sem%C3%A9" title="Semé">

           </a>
           and three crescents argent); no. 120: John Peche (Gules, a crescent or, on a chief argent two mullets gules); no. 128: Rauf de Stopeham (Argent, two (of three) crescents and a canton gules).

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_use">
        Contemporary use
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crescent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Contemporary use">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Star_and_crescent#Contemporary_use" title="Star and crescent">
        Star_and_crescent § Contemporary_use
       </a>
      </div>
      <table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <img alt="" class="noviewer" data-file-height="1376" data-file-width="1024" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Commons-logo.svg/30px-Commons-logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Commons-logo.svg/45px-Commons-logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Commons-logo.svg/59px-Commons-logo.svg.png 2x" width="30"/>
        </td>
        <td class="mbox-text plainlist">
         Wikimedia Commons has media related to
         <i>
          <b>
           <a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Flags_with_crescents" title="commons:Category:Flags with crescents">
            Flags with crescents
           </a>
          </b>
         </i>
         .
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Maldives.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Flag_of_Maldives.svg/220px-Flag_of_Maldives.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Flag_of_Maldives.svg/330px-Flag_of_Maldives.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Flag_of_Maldives.svg/440px-Flag_of_Maldives.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Maldives.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Maldives" title="Flag of the Maldives">
          Flag of the Maldives
         </a>
         (1965)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The crescent remains in use as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrological_symbol" title="Astrological symbol">
        astrological symbol
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_symbol" title="Astronomical symbol">
        astronomical symbol
       </a>
       representing the Moon. Use of a standalone crescent in flags is less common than the
       <a href="/wiki/Star_and_crescent" title="Star and crescent">
        star and crescent
       </a>
       combination. Crescents without stars are found in the
       <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_South_Carolina" title="Flag of South Carolina">
        South Carolina state flag
       </a>
       (1861), the
       <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Maldives" title="Flag of the Maldives">
        flag of the Maldives
       </a>
       (1965), the
       <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation" title="Flag of the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation">
        flag of the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation
       </a>
       (1981)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the flag of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sudan_Liberation_Movement/Army" title="Sudan Liberation Movement/Army">
        Sudan Liberation Movement/Army
       </a>
       (c. 2002).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">
        New Orleans
       </a>
       is nicknamed
       <i>
        The Crescent City
       </i>
       , and a crescent, or crescent and star, is used to represent the city in official emblems.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_New_Orleans_Police_Department_Badge_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_New_Orleans_Police_Department_Badge-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Crescents, often with faces, are found on numerous modern municipal coats of arms in Europe, e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B6nnigheim" title="Bönnigheim">
        Bönnigheim
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dettighofen,_Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Dettighofen, Baden-Württemberg">
        Dettighofen
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dogern" title="Dogern">
        Dogern
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jesenwang" title="Jesenwang">
        Jesenwang
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Karst%C3%A4dt,_Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="Karstädt, Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">
        Karstädt
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Angelbachtal" title="Angelbachtal">
        Michelfeld (Angelbachtal)
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Waldbronn" title="Waldbronn">
        Waldbronn
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Boswil" title="Boswil">
        Boswil
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A4ttlikon" title="Dättlikon">
        Dättlikon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Neerach" title="Neerach">
        Neerach
       </a>
       (from the 16th-century
       <a href="/wiki/Neuamt" title="Neuamt">
        Neuamt
       </a>
       coat of arms);
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Katzenthal" title="Katzenthal">
        Katzenthal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mortcerf" title="Mortcerf">
        Mortcerf
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">
        Malta
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Qormi" title="Qormi">
        Qormi
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
        Sweden
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Trosa_Municipality" title="Trosa Municipality">
        Trosa
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The crescent printed on military ration boxes is the US Department of Defense symbol for subsistence items. The symbol is used on packaged foodstuffs but not on fresh produce or on items intended for resale.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Since 1993, the crescent has also been in use as
       <a href="/wiki/Religious_symbolism_in_the_United_States_military#Muslim" title="Religious symbolism in the United States military">
        chaplain badge
       </a>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_the_United_States" title="Islam in the United States">
        Muslim
       </a>
       chaplains in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_military" title="US military">
        US military
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USchaplains_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-USchaplains-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

         ]

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

        Crescent (disambiguation)
       </a>

      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2441" data-file-width="1987" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg/220px-Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg/330px-Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg/440px-Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_fertile_crescent.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">
          Fertile Crescent
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        crescent
       </i>
       may also refer to objects with a shape reminiscent of the crescent shape, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Crescent_(architecture)" title="Crescent (architecture)">
        houses forming an arc
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Crescent_(solitaire)" title="Crescent (solitaire)">
        a type of solitaire game
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Crescent_Nebula" title="Crescent Nebula">
        Crescent Nebula
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glomerular_crescent" title="Glomerular crescent">
        glomerular crescent
       </a>
       (crescent shaped scar of the
       <a href="/wiki/Glomerulus" title="Glomerulus">
        glomeruli
       </a>
       of the kidney).,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urliROCKET_Learning_Module:_Glomerular_Pathology,_Case_I_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-urliROCKET_Learning_Module:_Glomerular_Pathology,_Case_I-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlRenal_Pathology_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-urlRenal_Pathology-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">
        Fertile Crescent
       </a>
       (the fertile area of land between
       <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">
        Mesopotamia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">
        Egypt
       </a>
       roughly forming a crescent shape), and the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Croissant" title="Croissant">
         croissant
        </a>
       </i>
       (the French form of the word) for the crescent-shaped pastry.
      </p>
