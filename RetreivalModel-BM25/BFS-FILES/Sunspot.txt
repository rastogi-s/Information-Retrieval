      <p>
       <b>
        Sunspots
       </b>
       are temporary
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_event" title="Celestial event">
        phenomena
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       that appear as spots darker than the surrounding areas. They are regions of reduced surface temperature caused by concentrations of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">
        magnetic field flux
       </a>
       that inhibit
       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
        convection
       </a>
       . Sunspots usually appear in pairs of opposite magnetic
       <a href="/wiki/Polarity_(physics)" title="Polarity (physics)">
        polarity
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Their number varies according to the approximately 11-year
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Individual sunspots or groups of sunspots may last anywhere from a few days to a few months, but eventually decay. Sunspots expand and contract as they move across the surface of the Sun, with diameters ranging from 16 km (10 mi)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to 160,000 km (100,000 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The larger variety are visible from Earth without the aid of a
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They may travel at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relative_speed" title="Relative speed">
        relative speeds
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">
        proper motions
       </a>
       , of a few hundred meters per second when they first emerge.
      </p>
      <p>
       Indicating intense magnetic activity, sunspots accompany secondary phenomena such as
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_loop" title="Coronal loop">
        coronal loops
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
        prominences
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        reconnection
       </a>
       events. Most
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       originate in magnetically active regions around visible sunspot groupings. Similar phenomena indirectly observed on
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       other than the Sun are commonly called
       <a href="/wiki/Starspot" title="Starspot">
        starspots
       </a>
       , and both light and dark spots have been measured.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strassmeier_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-strassmeier-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Although they are at temperatures of roughly 3,000–4,500 K (2,700–4,200 °C), the contrast with the surrounding material at about 5,780 K (5,500 °C) leaves sunspots clearly visible as dark spots. This is because the luminance (which is essentially "brightness" in visible light) of a heated
       <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">
        black body
       </a>
       (closely approximated by the photosphere) at these temperatures varies extremely with temperature—considerably more so than the (temperature to the fourth power) variation in the total
       <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">
        black-body radiation
       </a>
       at all wavelengths (see
       <a href="/wiki/Stefan%E2%80%93Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan–Boltzmann law">
        Stefan–Boltzmann law
       </a>
       ). Isolated from the surrounding photosphere a sunspot would be brighter than the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sunspots have two parts: the central
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/umbra" title="wikt:umbra">
        umbra
       </a>
       , which is the darkest part, where the magnetic field is approximately vertical (
       <a href="/wiki/Normal_(geometry)" title="Normal (geometry)">
        normal
       </a>
       to the Sun's surface) and the surrounding
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/penumbra" title="wikt:penumbra">
        penumbra
       </a>
       , which is lighter, where the magnetic field is more inclined.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lifecycle">
        Lifecycle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunspot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lifecycle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Any given appearance of a sunspot may last anywhere from a few days to a few months, though groups of sunspots and their active regions tend to last weeks or months, but all do eventually decay and disappear. Sunspots expand and contract as they move across the surface of the Sun, with diameters ranging from 16 km (10 mi) to 160,000 km (100,000 mi).
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the details of sunspot generation are still a matter of research, it appears that sunspots are the visible counterparts of
       <a href="/wiki/Flux_tube" title="Flux tube">
        magnetic flux tubes
       </a>
       in the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">
        convective zone
       </a>
       that get "wound up" by
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_rotation" title="Differential rotation">
        differential rotation
       </a>
       . If the stress on the tubes reaches a certain limit, they curl up and puncture the Sun's surface. Convection is inhibited at the puncture points; the energy flux from the Sun's interior decreases, and with it, surface temperature.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Wilson_effect" title="Wilson effect">
        Wilson effect
       </a>
       implies that sunspots are depressions on the Sun's surface. Observations using the
       <a href="/wiki/Zeeman_effect" title="Zeeman effect">
        Zeeman effect
       </a>
       show that prototypical sunspots come in pairs with opposite magnetic polarity. From cycle to cycle, the polarities of leading and trailing (with respect to the solar rotation) sunspots change from north/south to south/north and back. Sunspots usually appear in groups.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_pressure" title="Magnetic pressure">
        Magnetic pressure
       </a>
       should tend to remove field concentrations, causing the sunspots to disperse, but sunspot lifetimes are measured in days to weeks. In 2001, observations from the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
       </a>
       (SOHO) using sound waves traveling below the photosphere (local
       <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">
        helioseismology
       </a>
       ) were used to develop a three-dimensional image of the internal structure below sunspots; these observations show that a powerful downdraft underneath each sunspot, forms a rotating
       <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">
        vortex
       </a>
       that sustains the concentrated magnetic field.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_cycle">
        Solar cycle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunspot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar cycle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        Solar cycle
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif">
         <img alt="Point chart showing sunspot area as percent of the total area at various latitudes, above grouped bar chart showing average daily sunspot area as % of visible hemisphere." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="2150" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif/300px-Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif/450px-Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif/600px-Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Butterfly diagram showing paired
         <a href="/wiki/Sp%C3%B6rer%27s_law" title="Spörer's law">
          Spörer's law
         </a>
         behavior
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Sunspot activity cycles are about every eleven years, with some variation in length. Over the solar cycle, sunspot populations rise quickly and then fall more slowly. The point of highest sunspot activity during a cycle is known as solar maximum, and the point of lowest activity as solar minimum. This period is also observed in most other
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_phenomena" title="Solar phenomena">
        solar activity
       </a>
       and is linked to a variation in the solar magnetic field that changes polarity with this period.
      </p>
      <p>
       Early in the cycle, sunspots appear in the higher latitudes and then move towards the equator as the cycle approaches maximum, following
       <a href="/wiki/Sp%C3%B6rer%27s_law" title="Spörer's law">
        Spörer's law
       </a>
       . Spots from two adjacent cycles can co-exist for some time. Spots from adjacent cycles can be distinguished by direction of their magnetic field.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Wolf_number" title="Wolf number">
        The Wolf number
       </a>
       sunspot index counts the average number of sunspots and groups of sunspots during specific intervals. The 11-year solar cycles are numbered sequentially, starting with the observations made in the 1750s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-testtest1_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-testtest1-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/George_Ellery_Hale" title="George Ellery Hale">
        George Ellery Hale
       </a>
       first linked magnetic fields and sunspots in 1908.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doi10.1086/141602_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-doi10.1086/141602-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hale suggested that the sunspot cycle period is 22 years, covering two periods of increased and decreased sunspot numbers, accompanied by polar reversals of the solar magnetic
       <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">
        dipole
       </a>
       field.
       <a href="/wiki/Horace_W._Babcock" title="Horace W. Babcock">
        Horace W. Babcock
       </a>
       later proposed a qualitative model for the dynamics of the solar outer layers. The
       <a href="/wiki/Babcock_Model" title="Babcock Model">
        Babcock Model
       </a>
       explains that magnetic fields cause the behavior described by Spörer's law, as well as other effects, which are twisted by the Sun's rotation.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Longer-period_trends">
        Longer-period trends
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunspot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Longer-period trends">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        Solar cycle
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Sunspot number also changes over long periods. For example, from 1900 to the 1960s, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_maxima" title="Solar maxima">
        solar maxima
       </a>
       trend of sunspot count was upwards; for the following decades it diminished.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the Sun was last as active as this period over 8,000 years ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15510145_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pmid15510145-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sunspots number is correlated with the intensity of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">
        solar radiation
       </a>
       over the period since 1979, when satellite measurements became available. The variation caused by the sunspot cycle to solar output is relatively small, on the order of 0.1% of the solar constant (a peak-to-trough range of 1.3 W·m
       <sup>
        −2
       </sup>
       compared with 1366 W·m
       <sup>
        −2
       </sup>
       for the average solar constant).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCCtarWG1244_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IPCCtarWG1244-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AIPsolar_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AIPsolar-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_observation">
        Modern observation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_observatory" title="Solar observatory">
        Solar observatory
       </a>
      </div>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">

          </a>
         </div>

         <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Solar_Telescope" title="Swedish Solar Telescope">

         </a>

      <p>
       Sunspots are observed with land-based and Earth-orbiting
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_telescope" title="Solar telescope">
        solar telescopes
       </a>
       . These telescopes use filtration and projection techniques for direct observation, in addition to various types of filtered cameras. Specialized tools such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscope" title="Spectroscope">
        spectroscopes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrohelioscope" title="Spectrohelioscope">
        spectrohelioscopes
       </a>
       are used to examine sunspots and sunspot areas. Artificial eclipses allow viewing of the circumference of the Sun as sunspots rotate through the horizon.
      </p>

        </a>

       Since looking directly at the Sun with the
       <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">
        naked eye
       </a>
       permanently damages
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_vision" title="Human vision">
        human vision
       </a>
       , amateur observation of sunspots is generally conducted using projected images, or directly through protective
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_filter" title="Astronomical filter">
        filters
       </a>
       . Small sections of very dark
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_filter" title="Optical filter">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Eyepiece" title="Eyepiece">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/H-alpha#Filter" title="H-alpha">
        hydrogen-alpha
       </a>
       narrow bandpass filters and
       <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_deposition" title="Vacuum deposition">
        aluminum-coated
       </a>
       glass
       <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">
        attenuation
       </a>
       filters (which have the appearance of mirrors due to their extremely high
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_density" title="Optical density">
        optical density
       </a>
       ) on the front of a telescope provide safe observation through the eyepiece.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Application">
        Application
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunspot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Application">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Due to its link to other kinds of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">
        solar activity
       </a>
       , sunspot occurrence can be used to help predict
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">
        space weather
       </a>
       , the state of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
        ionosphere
       </a>
       , and hence the conditions of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short-wave" title="Short-wave">
        short-wave
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">
        radio propagation
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_communication" title="Satellite communication">
        satellite communications
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_activity_and_climate" title="Solar activity and climate">
        Solar activity
       </a>
       (and the solar cycle) have been implicated in
       <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">
        global warming
       </a>
       , originally the role of the
       <a href="/wiki/Maunder_Minimum" title="Maunder Minimum">
        Maunder Minimum
       </a>
       of sunspot occurrence in the
       <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">
        Little Ice Age
       </a>
       in European winter climate.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MM_PAPER_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MM_PAPER-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sunspots themselves, in terms of the magnitude of their radiant-energy deficit, have a weak effect on terrestrial climate
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in a direct sense. On longer time scales, such as the solar cycle, other magnetic phenomena (
       <a href="/wiki/Facula" title="Facula">
        faculae
       </a>
       and the chromospheric network) correlate with sunspot occurrence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Starspot">
        Starspot
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunspot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Starspot">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1947, G. E. Kron proposed that
       <a href="/wiki/Starspot" title="Starspot">
        starspots
       </a>
       were the reason for periodic changes in brightness on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_dwarfs" title="Red dwarfs">
        red dwarfs
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strassmeier_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-strassmeier-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since the mid-1990s, starspot observations have been made using increasingly powerful techniques yielding more and more detail:
       <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">
        photometry
       </a>
       showed starspot growth and decay and showed cyclic behavior similar to the Sun's;
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectroscopy
       </a>
       examined the structure of starspot regions by analyzing variations in spectral line splitting due to the Zeeman effect;
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_imaging" title="Doppler imaging">
        Doppler imaging
       </a>
       showed differential rotation of spots for several stars and distributions different from the Sun's; spectral line analysis measured the temperature range of spots and the stellar surfaces. For example, in 1999, Strassmeier reported the largest cool starspot ever seen rotating the giant
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification#Class_K" title="Stellar classification">
        K0
       </a>
       star XX Triangulum (HD 12545) with a temperature of 3,500 K (3,230 °C), together with a warm spot of 4,800 K (4,530 °C).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strassmeier_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-strassmeier-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
