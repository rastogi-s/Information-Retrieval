      <p>
       <b>
        Moon rock
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        lunar rock
       </b>
       is rock that is found on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Earth's moon
       </a>
       , or lunar material collected during the course of human
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">
        exploration of the Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">
        Sources
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Moon rocks on Earth come from three sources: those collected by the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       manned lunar landings from 1969 to 1972; samples returned by three
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">
        Luna programme
       </a>
       unmanned probes in the 1970s; and rocks that were ejected naturally from the lunar surface by
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        cratering
       </a>
       events and subsequently fell to Earth as
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_meteorite" title="Lunar meteorite">
        lunar meteorites
       </a>
       . During the six Apollo landing missions, 2,415 samples were collected weighing 839.87 pounds (380.96 kg). Three Luna spacecraft returned with 11.5 ounces (330 g) of samples. More than 300 lunar meteorites have been collected on Earth,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeteorBulletin_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MeteorBulletin-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       representing more than 30 different meteorite fall events (none witnessed), with a total mass of over 419 pounds (190 kg).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some were discovered by scientific teams searching for meteorites in
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/ANSMET" title="ANSMET">
        ANSMET
       </a>
       , with most of the remainder discovered by collectors in the desert regions of northern
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">
        Oman
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dating">
        Dating
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dating">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Rocks from the Moon have been measured by
       <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">
        radiometric dating
       </a>
       techniques. They range in age from about 3.16 billion years old for the
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basaltic
       </a>
       samples derived from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_maria" title="Lunar maria">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       , up to about 4.44 billion years old for rocks derived from the highlands.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Based on the age-dating technique of "crater counting," the youngest basaltic eruptions are believed to have occurred about 1.2 billion years ago,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but scientists do not possess samples of these lavas. In contrast, the
       <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">
        oldest ages of rocks from the Earth
       </a>
       are between 3.8 and 4.28 billion years old.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
        Composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Moon" title="Geology of the Moon">
        Geology of the Moon
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Moon rocks fall into two main categories: those found in the lunar highlands (terrae), and those in the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        maria
       </a>
       . The terrae consist dominantly of
       <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">
        mafic
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutonic" title="Plutonic">
        plutonic
       </a>
       rocks. Regolith
       <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">
        breccias
       </a>
       with similar
       <a href="/wiki/Protolith" title="Protolith">
        protoliths
       </a>
       are also common. Mare basalts come in three distinct series in direct relation to their titanium content:
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-Ti_basalt" title="High-Ti basalt">
         high-Ti basalts
        </a>
        , low-Ti basalts
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        Very Low-Ti (VLT) basalts
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Almost all lunar rocks are depleted in
       <a href="/wiki/Volatiles" title="Volatiles">
        volatiles
       </a>
       and are completely lacking in
       <a href="/wiki/Mineral_hydration" title="Mineral hydration">
        hydrated minerals
       </a>
       common in Earth rocks. In some regards, lunar rocks are closely related to Earth's rocks in their isotopic composition of the element
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       . The Apollo moon rocks were collected using a variety of tools, including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hammers" title="Hammers">
        hammers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rake_(tool)" title="Rake (tool)">
        rakes
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scoop_(tool)" title="Scoop (tool)">
        scoops
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tongs" title="Tongs">
        tongs
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Core_sample" title="Core sample">
        core tubes
       </a>
       . Most were photographed prior to collection to record the condition in which they were found. They were placed inside sample bags and then a
       <i>
        Special Environmental Sample Container
       </i>
       for return to the Earth to protect them from contamination. In contrast to the Earth, large portions of the lunar crust appear to be composed of rocks with high concentrations of the mineral
       <a href="/wiki/Anorthite" title="Anorthite">
        anorthite
       </a>
       . The mare basalts have relatively high
       <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
        iron
       </a>
       values. Furthermore, some of the mare basalts have very high levels of
       <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">
        titanium
       </a>
       (in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">
        ilmenite
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20151228_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151228-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Primary igneous rocks in the lunar highlands compose three distinct groups: the ferroan anorthosite suite, the magnesian suite, and the alkali suite.
      </p>
      <p>
       Lunar breccias, formed largely by the immense basin-forming impacts, are dominantly composed of highland
       <a href="/wiki/Lithology" title="Lithology">
        lithologies
       </a>
       because most mare basalts post-date basin formation (and largely fill these impact basins).
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        ferroan anorthosite suite
       </i>
       consists almost exclusively of the rock
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">
         anorthosite
        </a>
       </i>
       (&gt;90% calcic plagioclase) with less common
       <i>
        anorthositic
        <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">
         gabbro
        </a>
       </i>
       (70-80% calcic plagioclase, with minor pyroxene). The ferroan anorthosite suite is the most common group in the highlands, and is inferred to represent plagioclase flotation cumulates of the lunar magma ocean, with interstitial mafic phases formed from trapped interstitial melt or rafted upwards with the more abundant plagioclase framework. The plagioclase is extremely calcic by terrestrial standards, with molar anorthite contents of 94-96% (An94-96). This reflects the extreme depletion of the bulk moon in alkalis (Na, K) as well as water and other volatile elements. In contrast, the mafic minerals in this suite have low Mg/Fe ratios that are inconsistent with calcic plagioclase compositions. Ferroan anorthosites have been dated using the internal isochron method at "circa" 4.4 Ga.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        magnesian suite
       </i>
       (or "
       <i>
        mg suite
       </i>
       ") consists of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dunite" title="Dunite">
         dunites
        </a>
       </i>
       (&gt;90% olivine),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Troctolite" title="Troctolite">
         troctolites
        </a>
       </i>
       (olivine-plagioclase), and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">
         gabbros
        </a>
       </i>
       (plagioclase-pyroxene) with relatively high Mg/Fe ratios in the mafic minerals and a range of plagioclase compositions that are still generally calcic (An86-93). These rocks represent later intrusions into the highlands crust (ferroan anorthosite) at round 4.3-4.1 Ga. An interesting aspect of this suite is that analysis of the trace element content of plagioclase and pyroxene require equilibrium with a
       <a href="/wiki/KREEP" title="KREEP">
        KREEP
       </a>
       -rich magma, despite the refractory major element contents.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        alkali suite
       </i>
       is so-called because of its high alkali content—for moon rocks. The alkali suite consists of
       <i>
        alkali
        <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">
         anorthosites
        </a>
       </i>
       with relatively sodic plagioclase (An70-85),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Norite" title="Norite">
         norites
        </a>
       </i>
       (plagioclase-orthopyroxene), and
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gabbronorite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gabbronorite (page does not exist)">
         gabbronorites
        </a>
       </i>
       (plagioclase-clinopyroxene-orthopyroxene) with similar plagioclase compositions and mafic minerals more iron-rich than the magnesian suite. The trace element contents of these minerals also indicates a KREEP-rich parent magma. The alkali suite spans an age range similar to the magnesian suite.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Lunar granites
       </i>
       are relatively rare rocks that include
       <a href="/wiki/Diorite" title="Diorite">
        diorites
       </a>
       , monzodiorites, and
       <a href="/wiki/Granophyre" title="Granophyre">
        granophyres
       </a>
       . They consist of quartz, plagioclase, orthoclase or alkali feldspar, rare mafics (pyroxene), and rare zircon. The alkali feldspar may have unusual compositions unlike any terrestrial feldspar, and they are often Ba-rich. These rocks apparently form by the extreme fractional crystallization of magnesian suite or alkali suite magmas, although liquid immiscibility may also play a role. U-Pb date of
       <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">
        zircons
       </a>
       from these rocks and from lunar soils have ages of 4.1-4.4 Ga, more or less the same as the magnesian suite and alkali suite rocks. In the 1960s, NASA researcher John A. O'Keefe and others linked lunar granites with tektites found on Earth although many researchers refuted these claims. According to one study, a portion of lunar sample 12013 has a chemistry that closely resembles javanite tektites found on Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Lunar breccias
       </i>
       range from glassy vitrophyre melt rocks, to glass-rich breccia, to regolith breccias. The vitrophyres are dominantly glassy rocks that represent impact melt sheets that fill large impact structures. They contain few clasts of the target lithology, which is largely melted by the impact. Glassy breccias form from impact melt that exit the crater and entrain large volumes of crushed (but not melted) ejecta. It may contain abundant clasts that reflect the range of lithologies in the target region, sitting in a matrix of mineral fragments plus glass that welds it all together. Some of the clasts in these breccias are pieces of older breccias, documenting a repeated history of impact brecciation, cooling, and impact. Regolith breccias resemble the glassy breccias but have little or no glass (melt) to weld them together. As noted above, the basin-forming impacts responsible for these breccias pre-date almost all mare basalt volcanism, so clasts of mare basalt are very rare. When found, these clasts represent the earliest phase of mare basalt volcanism preserved.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mare_basalts">
        Mare basalts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mare basalts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Mare basalts
       </i>
       are named as such because they frequently constitute large portions of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_maria" title="Lunar maria">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       . They are similar to terrestrial basalts, but have many important differences; for example, mare basalts show a large negative
       <a href="/wiki/Europium_anomaly" title="Europium anomaly">
        europium anomaly
       </a>
       . Certain mare basalts (the so-called
       <i>
        VHK (Very High K) basalt
       </i>
       s) have extraordinary potassium content.
      </p>

      <p>
       The main repository for the Apollo Moon rocks is the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Sample_Laboratory_Facility" title="Lunar Sample Laboratory Facility">
        Lunar Sample Laboratory Facility
       </a>
       at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Space_Center" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center">
        Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">
        Houston, Texas
       </a>
       . For safe keeping, there is also a smaller collection stored at
       <a href="/wiki/White_Sands_Test_Facility" title="White Sands Test Facility">
        White Sands Test Facility
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Las_Cruces,_New_Mexico" title="Las Cruces, New Mexico">
        Las Cruces, New Mexico
       </a>
       . Most of the rocks are stored in nitrogen to keep them free of moisture. They are handled only indirectly, using special tools.
      </p>
      <p>
       Moon rocks collected during the course of lunar exploration are currently considered priceless. In 2002, a safe was stolen from the Lunar Sample Building that contained minute samples of lunar and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_meteorite" title="Mars meteorite">
        Martian material
       </a>
       . The samples were recovered, and NASA estimated their value during the ensuing court case at about $1 million for 10 oz. (285 g.) of material.
      </p>
      <p>
       Naturally transported Moon rocks in the form of lunar meteorites are sold and traded among private collectors.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Goodwill_Moon_rocks">
        Goodwill Moon rocks

       <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">

         [11]
        </a>

         [12]
        </a>

       </a>
       .

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discoveries">
        Discoveries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Discoveries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Three minerals were discovered from the Moon. These include
       <a href="/wiki/Armalcolite" title="Armalcolite">
        armalcolite
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tranquillityite" title="Tranquillityite">
        tranquillityite
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Pyroxferroite" title="Pyroxferroite">
        pyroxferroite
       </a>
       . Armalcolite was named for the three
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        astronauts
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       mission:
       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        <b>
         Arm
        </b>
        strong
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
        <b>
         Al
        </b>
        drin
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_(astronaut)" title="Michael Collins (astronaut)">
        <b>
         Col
        </b>
        lins
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stolen_and_missing_Moon_rocks">
        Stolen and missing Moon rocks
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Stolen and missing Moon rocks">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Stolen_and_missing_moon_rocks" title="Stolen and missing moon rocks">
        Stolen and missing moon rocks
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Because of their rarity on Earth, and the difficulty of obtaining more, Moon rocks have been frequent targets of
       <a href="/wiki/Theft" title="Theft">
        theft
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">
        vandalism
       </a>
       , and many have gone missing or were stolen.
      </p>
