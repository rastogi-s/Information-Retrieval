      <p>
       The
       <b>
        umbra
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        penumbra
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        antumbra
       </b>
       are three distinct parts of a
       <a href="/wiki/Shadow" title="Shadow">
        shadow
       </a>
       , created by any light source after impinging on an
       <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">
        opaque
       </a>
       object. For a point source only the umbra is cast.
      </p>
      <p>
       These names are most often used for the shadows cast by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">
        celestial bodies
       </a>
       , though they are sometimes used to describe levels of darkness, such as in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">
        sunspots
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Umbra">
        Umbra
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbra,_penumbra_and_antumbra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Umbra">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antumbra.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="614" data-file-width="819" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Antumbra.jpg/200px-Antumbra.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Antumbra.jpg/300px-Antumbra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Antumbra.jpg/400px-Antumbra.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antumbra.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Umbra, penumbra, and antumbra formed through windows and shutters
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        umbra
       </b>
       (Latin for "shadow") is the innermost and darkest part of a shadow, where the light source is completely blocked by the occluding body. An observer in the umbra experiences a total eclipse. The umbra of a round body occluding a round light source forms a
       <a href="/wiki/Cone" title="Cone">
        right circular cone
       </a>
       ; to a viewer at the cone's apex, the two bodies are equal in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_size" title="Apparent size">
        apparent size
       </a>
       . The distance from the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       to the apex of its umbra is roughly equal to that between the Moon and
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       . Because the Earth is 3.70 times wider than the Moon, its umbra extends correspondingly farther, roughly 1.4 million kilometers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Penumbra">
        Penumbra
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbra,_penumbra_and_antumbra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Penumbra">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       "Penumbra" redirects here. For other uses, see
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Penumbra_(disambiguation)" title="Penumbra (disambiguation)">
        Penumbra (disambiguation)
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        penumbra
       </b>
       (from the Latin
       <i>
        paene
       </i>
       "almost, nearly") is the region in which only a portion of the light source is obscured by the occluding body. An observer in the penumbra experiences a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_eclipse" title="Partial eclipse">
        partial eclipse
       </a>
       . An alternative definition is that the penumbra is the region where
       <i>
        some or all
       </i>
       of the light source is obscured (i.e., the umbra is a subset of the penumbra). For example,
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's Navigation and Ancillary Information Facility defines that a body in the umbra is also within the penumbra.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Antumbra">
        Antumbra
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbra,_penumbra_and_antumbra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Antumbra">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        antumbra
       </b>
       (from Latin
       <i>
        ante
       </i>
       , "before") is the region from which the occluding body appears entirely contained within the disc of the light source. An observer in this region experiences an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annular_eclipse" title="Annular eclipse">
        annular eclipse
       </a>
       , in which a bright ring is visible around the eclipsing body. If the observer moves closer to the light source, the apparent size of the occluding body increases until it causes a full umbra.
      </p>
