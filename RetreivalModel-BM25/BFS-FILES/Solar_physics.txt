   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Solar physics
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       This article is about stellar physics of the Sun. For the physics of stars, see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_physics" title="Stellar physics">
        stellar physics
       </a>
       . For the physics journal, see
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Physics_(journal)" title="Solar Physics (journal)">
        Solar Physics (journal)
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Solar physics
       </b>
       is the branch of astrophysics that specializes in the study of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       . It deals with detailed measurements that are possible only for our closest star. It intersects with many disciplines of pure
       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
        physics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
        astrophysics
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">
        computer science
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">
        fluid dynamics
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_physics" title="Plasma physics">
        plasma physics
       </a>
       including
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">
        magnetohydrodynamics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">
        seismology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">
        particle physics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_physics" title="Atomic physics">
        atomic physics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">
        nuclear physics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">
        stellar evolution
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Space_physics" title="Space physics">
        space physics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectroscopy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Radiative_transfer" title="Radiative transfer">
        radiative transfer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">
        applied optics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Signal_processing" title="Signal processing">
        signal processing
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_vision" title="Computer vision">
        computer vision
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Computational_physics" title="Computational physics">
        computational physics
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_physics" title="Stellar physics">
        stellar physics
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_astronomy" title="Solar astronomy">
        solar astronomy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Because the Sun is uniquely situated for close-range observing (other stars cannot be resolved with anything like the spatial or temporal resolution that the Sun can), there is a split between the related discipline of observational astrophysics (of distant stars) and observational solar physics.
      </p>
      <p>
       The study of solar physics is also important as it is believed that changes in the solar atmosphere and solar activity can have a major impact on Earth's climate. The Sun also provides a "physical laboratory" for the study of plasma physics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_times">
        Ancient times
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient times">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Babylonians were keeping a record of solar eclipses, with the oldest record originating from the ancient city of Ugarit, in modern-day Syria. This record dates to about 1300 BC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ancient Chinese astronomers were also observing solar phenomena (such as solar eclipses and visible sunspots) with the purpose of keeping track of calendars, which were based on lunar and solar cycles. Unfortunately, records kept before 720 BC are very vague and offer no useful information. However, after 720 BC, 37 solar eclipses were noted over the course of 240 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ancient_china_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ancient_china-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_times">
        Medieval times
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medieval times">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Astronomical knowledge flourished in the Islamic world during medieval times. Many observatories were built in cities from Damascus to Baghdad, where detailed astronomical observations were taken. Particularly, a few solar parameters were measured and detailed observations of the Sun were taken. Solar observations were taken with the purpose of navigation, but mostly for timekeeping. Islam requires its followers to pray five times a day, at specific position of the Sun in the sky. As such, accurate observations of the Sun and its trajectory on the sky were needed. In the late 10th century, Iranian astronomer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Khujandi" title="Al-Khujandi">
        Abu-Mahmud Khojandi
       </a>
       built a massive observatory near Tehran. There, he took accurate measurements of a series of meridian transits of the Sun, which he later used to calculate the obliquity of the ecliptic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Following the fall of the Western Roman Empire, Western Europe was cut from all sources of ancient scientific knowledge, especially those written in Greek. This, plus de-urbanisation and diseases such as the Black Death led to a decline in scientific knowledge in Medieval Europe, especially in the early Middle Ages. During this period, observations of the Sun were taken either in relation to the zodiac, or to assist in building places of worship such as churches and cathedrals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_period">
        Renaissance period
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Renaissance period">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In astronomy, the renaissance period started with the work of
       <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">
        Nicolaus Copernicus
       </a>
       . He proposed that planets revolve around the Sun and not around the Earth, as it was believed at the time. This model is known as the heliocentric model.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His work was later expanded by
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">
        Johannes Kepler
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
        Galileo Galilei
       </a>
       . Particularly, Galilei used his new telescope to look at the Sun. In 1610, he discovered sunspots on its surface. In the autumn of 1611,
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Fabricius" title="Johannes Fabricius">
        Johannes Fabricius
       </a>
       wrote the first book on sunspots, "De Maculis in Sole Observatis" (On the spots observed in the Sun).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_times">
        Modern times
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern times">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Modern day solar physics is focused towards understanding the many phenomena observed with the help of modern telescopes and satellites. Of particular interest are the structure of the solar photosphere, the coronal heat problem and sunspots.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Research">
        Research
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Research">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Physics_Division" title="Solar Physics Division">
        Solar Physics Division
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Astronomical_Society" title="American Astronomical Society">
        American Astronomical Society
       </a>
       boasts 555 members (as of May 2007), compared to several thousand in the parent organization.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A major thrust of current (2009) effort in the field of solar physics is integrated understanding of the entire
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">
        solar system
       </a>
       including the Sun and its effects throughout interplanetary space within the
       <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">
        heliosphere
       </a>
       and on
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       and planetary
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
        atmospheres
       </a>
       . Studies of phenomena that affect multiple systems in the heliosphere, or that are considered to fit within a heliospheric context, are called
       <a href="/wiki/Heliophysics" title="Heliophysics">
        heliophysics
       </a>
       , a new coinage that entered usage in the early years of the current millennium.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Space_based">
        Space based
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Space based">

       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SDO">
        SDO
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: SDO">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
        Solar Dynamics Observatory
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Dynamics_Observatory_1.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1280" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory_1.jpg/220px-Solar_Dynamics_Observatory_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory_1.jpg/330px-Solar_Dynamics_Observatory_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory_1.jpg/440px-Solar_Dynamics_Observatory_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Dynamics_Observatory_1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The SDO satellite
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO) was launched by NASA in February 2010 from Cape Canaveral. The main goals of the mission are understanding how solar activity arises and how it affects life on Earth by determining how the Sun's magnetic field is generated and structured and how the stored magnetic energy is converted and released into space.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SOHO">
        SOHO
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: SOHO">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NASA_SOHO_spacecraft.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1984" data-file-width="1889" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/NASA_SOHO_spacecraft.png/220px-NASA_SOHO_spacecraft.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/NASA_SOHO_spacecraft.png/330px-NASA_SOHO_spacecraft.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/NASA_SOHO_spacecraft.png/440px-NASA_SOHO_spacecraft.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NASA_SOHO_spacecraft.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Image of SOHO spacecraft
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Solar &amp; Heliospheric Observatory, SOHO, is a joint project between
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       and ESA that was launched in December 1995. It was launched to probe the interior of the Sun, make observations of the solar wind and phenomena associated with it and investigate the outer layers of the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="HINODE">
        HINODE
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: HINODE">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">
        Hinode
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A publicly funded mission led by the Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency, the HINODE satellite, launched in 2006, consists of a coordinated set of optical, extreme ultraviolet and X-ray instruments. These investigate the interaction between the solar corona and the Sun's magnetic field.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="ATST">
        ATST
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: ATST">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Technology_Solar_Telescope" title="Advanced Technology Solar Telescope">
        Advanced Technology Solar Telescope
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Advanced Technology Solar Telescope (ATST) is a solar telescope facility that is under construction in Maui. Twenty-two institutions are collaborating on the ATST project, with the main funding agency being the National Science Foundation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Big_Bear">
        Big Bear
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Big Bear">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Big_Bear_Solar_Observatory" title="Big Bear Solar Observatory">
        Big Bear Solar Observatory
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Big Bear Solar Observatory in California houses several telescopes including the New Solar Telescope(NTS) which is a 1.6 meter, clear-aperture, off-axis Gregorian telescope. The NTS saw first light in December 2008. Until the ATST comes on line, the NTS remains the largest solar telescope in the world. The Big Bear Observatory is one of several facilities operated by the Center for Solar-Terrestrial Research at New Jersey Institute of Technology (NJIT).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="EUNIS">
        EUNIS
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: EUNIS">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The Extreme Ultraviolet Normal Incidence Spectrograph (EUNIS)is a two channel imaging spectrograph that first flew in 2006. It observes the solar corona with high spectral resolution. So far, it has provided information on the nature of coronal bright points, cool transients and coronal loop arcades. Data from it also helped calibrating SOHO and a few other telescopes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
