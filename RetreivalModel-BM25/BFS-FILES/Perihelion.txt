      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Perihelion_and_aphelion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The words perihelion and aphelion were coined by
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">
        Johannes Kepler
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to describe the orbital motion of the planets. The words are formed from the prefixes
       <i>
        peri-
       </i>
       (Greek:
       <i>
        περί
       </i>
       , near) and
       <i>
        apo-
       </i>
       (Greek:
       <i>
        ἀπό
       </i>
       , away from) affixed to the Greek word for the sun, ἥλιος.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Perihelion and aphelion are sometimes incorrectly used for the orbits of objects about bodies other than the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       . The correct terms are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        For orbits around the
        <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
         Earth
        </a>
        :
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
         perigee
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
         apogee
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        For orbits around a star: periastron and apastron.
       </li>
       <li>
        For orbits around any center of mass: periapsis (or pericenter) and apoapsis (or apocenter).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomical_meaning">
        Astronomical meaning
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Perihelion_and_aphelion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Astronomical meaning">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion#First_law" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">
        Kepler's first law of planetary motion
       </a>
       , all planets, comets, and asteroids in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       have approximately
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        elliptical
       </a>
       orbits around the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsu_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wsu-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is only approximate because of
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        perturbations
       </a>
       due to the gravity of other bodies. Every ellipse has two focus points, and the Sun is at one of these focus points for the elliptical orbits of its satellites. Hence, an orbiting body has a closest and a farthest point from its parent object, that is, a perihelion and an aphelion. Each extreme is known as an
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        apsis
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        Orbital eccentricity
       </a>
       measures the flatness (departure from a perfect
       <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">
        circle
       </a>
       ) of the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Application_to_Earth">
        Application to Earth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Perihelion_and_aphelion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Application to Earth">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis#Perihelion_and_aphelion_of_the_Earth" title="Apsis">
        Apsis § Perihelion and aphelion of the Earth
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       is about 147.1 million kilometers (91.4 million miles) from the Sun at perihelion around January 3, in contrast to about 152.1 million kilometers (94.5 million miles) at aphelion around July 4 — a difference of about 5.0 million kilometers (3.1 million miles). (These dates change over time due to precession and other orbital factors, which follow cyclical patterns known as
       <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">
        Milankovitch cycles
       </a>
       . For a table of these dates for various years, see
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis#Perihelion_and_aphelion_of_the_Earth" title="Apsis">
        Apsis
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       Because of the increased distance at aphelion, only 93.55% of the solar radiation from the Sun falls on a given area of land as does at perihelion. However, this fluctuation does not account for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seasons" title="Seasons">
        seasons
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as it is
       <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">
        summer
       </a>
       in the northern hemisphere when it is
       <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">
        winter
       </a>
       in the southern hemisphere and
       <i>
        vice versa.
       </i>
       Instead, seasons result from the
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        tilt of Earth's axis
       </a>
       , which is 23.4 degrees away from perpendicular to the plane of Earth's orbit around the sun. Winter falls on the hemisphere where sunlight strikes least directly, and summer falls where sunlight strikes most directly, regardless of the Earth's distance from the Sun.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the northern hemisphere, summer occurs at the same time as aphelion. Despite this, there are larger land masses in the northern hemisphere, which are easier to heat than the seas. Consequently, summers are 2.3 °C (4 °F) warmer in the northern hemisphere than in the southern hemisphere under similar conditions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earth_at_Aphelion_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Earth_at_Aphelion-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
