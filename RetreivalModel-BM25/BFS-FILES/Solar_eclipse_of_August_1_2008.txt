      <p>
       A total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       occurred on August 1, 2008. A
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       occurs when the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       passes between
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , thereby totally or partly obscuring the image of the Sun for a viewer on Earth. A total solar eclipse occurs when the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_diameter" title="Apparent diameter">
        apparent diameter
       </a>
       is larger than the Sun's, blocking all direct sunlight, turning day into darkness. Totality occurs in a narrow path across Earth's surface, with the partial solar eclipse visible over a surrounding region thousands of kilometres wide. It had a
       <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_of_eclipse" title="Magnitude of eclipse">
        magnitude
       </a>
       of 1.0394
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eclipse_plot_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-eclipse_plot-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that was visible from a narrow corridor through northern
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">
        Nunavut
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       , central
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russia
       </a>
       , eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">
        Kazakhstan
       </a>
       , western
       <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">
        Mongolia
       </a>
       and China.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_eclipse_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa_eclipse-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Occurring north of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_circle" title="Arctic circle">
        arctic circle
       </a>
       , it belonged to the so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">
        midnight sun
       </a>
       eclipses. The largest city in the path of the eclipse was
       <a href="/wiki/Novosibirsk" title="Novosibirsk">
        Novosibirsk
       </a>
       in Russia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heraldsun_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-heraldsun-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The total eclipse lasted for 2 minutes, and covered 0.4% of the Earth's surface in a 10,200 km long path. It was the 47th eclipse of the 126th
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saros_cycle" title="Saros cycle">
        Saros cycle
       </a>
       , which began with a partial eclipse on March 10, 1179 and will conclude with a partial eclipse on May 3, 2459.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_eclipse_bulletin_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa_eclipse_bulletin-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A partial eclipse could be seen from the much broader path of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penumbra" title="Penumbra">
        penumbra
       </a>
       , including northeastern North America and most of Europe and Asia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_eclipse_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa_eclipse-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was described by observers as "special for its colours around the horizon. There were wonderful oranges and reds all around, the clouds lit up, some dark in silhouette, some golden, glowing yellowy-orange in the distance. You could see the shadow approaching against the clouds and then rushing away as it left."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The eclipse began in the far north of Canada in
       <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">
        Nunavut
       </a>
       at 09:21 UT, the zone of totality being 206 km wide, and lasting for 1 minute 30 seconds. The path of the eclipse then headed north-east, crossing over northern
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       and reaching the northernmost latitude of 83° 47′ at 09:38 UT before dipping down into Russia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_eclipse_bulletin_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa_eclipse_bulletin-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The path of totality touched the northeast corner of
       <a href="/wiki/Kvit%C3%B8ya" title="Kvitøya">
        Kvitøya
       </a>
       , an uninhabited Norwegian island in the
       <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">
        Svalbard
       </a>
       archipelago, at 09:47 UT.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Greatest_eclipse:_Russia">
        Greatest eclipse: Russia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [

      </h2>
      <p>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_eclipse_bulletin_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa_eclipse_bulletin-4">
         [4]
        </a>

       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>

           citation needed
          </span>

        ]
       </sup>
       The greatest eclipse occurred shortly after, at 10:21:07 UT at coordinates
       <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
        <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Solar_eclipse_of_August_1,_2008&amp;params=65_39_N_72_18_E_">

         <span class="geo-nondefault">
          <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           65.650°N 72.300°E
          </span>
          <span style="display:none">
           ﻿ /
           <span class="geo">
            65.650; 72.300
           </span>
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       (close to
       <a href="/wiki/Nadym" title="Nadym">
        Nadym
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Megion" title="Megion">

        Novosibirsk

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusion:_China">
        Conclusion: China
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_August_1,_2008&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conclusion: China">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The path of the eclipse then moved south-east, crossing into Mongolia and just clipping Kazakhstan at around 10:58 UT. The path here was 252 km wide, but the duration was decreased to 2 minutes 10 seconds. The path then ran down the China-Mongolia border, ending in China at 11:18 UT, with an eclipse lasting 1 minute 27 seconds at sunset.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The total eclipse finished at 11:21 UT. The total eclipse passed over
       <a href="/wiki/Yiwu_County" title="Yiwu County">
        Yiwu County
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jiuquan" title="Jiuquan">
        Jiuquan
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xi%E2%80%99an" title="Xi’an">
        Xi’an
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_eclipse_bulletin_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa_eclipse_bulletin-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Around 10,000 people were gathered to watch the eclipse in Yiwu.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heraldsun_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-heraldsun-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Partial_eclipse">
        Partial eclipse
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_August_1,_2008&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Partial eclipse">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A partial eclipse was seen from the much broader path of the Moon's penumbra, including the north east coast of North America and most of Europe and Asia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_eclipse_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa_eclipse-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In London, England, the partial eclipse began at 09:33
       <a href="/wiki/British_Summer_Time" title="British Summer Time">
        BST
       </a>
       , with a maximum eclipse of 12% at 10:18 BST, before concluding at 11:05 BST. At
       <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">
        Edinburgh
       </a>
       the partial eclipse was 23.5% of the sun, whilst it was 36% in
       <a href="/wiki/Lerwick" title="Lerwick">
        Lerwick
       </a>
       in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shetland_Isles" title="Shetland Isles">
        Shetland Isles
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ras_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ras-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="LTU_1111">
        LTU 1111
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_August_1,_2008&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: LTU 1111">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       German charter airline
       <a href="/wiki/LTU_International" title="LTU International">
        LTU
       </a>
       , now trading as
       <a href="/wiki/Air_Berlin" title="Air Berlin">
        Air Berlin
       </a>
       , operated a special flight from
       <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">
        Düsseldorf
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       to observe the eclipse. Flight number LT 1111 spent over 11 hours in the air, returning to base at 6pm after flying a planeload of eclipse chasers, scientists, journalists and TV crews to watch the celestial event. The route also included a low-level sightseeing tour of
       <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">
        Svalbard
       </a>
       before the eclipse and the magnetic pole afterwards.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saros_126">
        Saros 126
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_August_1,_2008&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Saros 126">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       It is a part of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Saros_126" title="Solar Saros 126">
        Saros cycle 126
       </a>
       , repeating every 18 years, 11 days, containing 71 events. The series started with partial solar eclipse on March 10, 1179. It contains annular eclipses from June 4, 1323 through April 4, 1810 and hybrid eclipses from April 14, 1828 through May 6, 1864. It contains total eclipses from May 17, 1882 through August 23, 2044. The series ends at member 72 as a partial eclipse on May 3, 2459. The longest duration of central eclipse (annular or total) was 5 minutes, 46 seconds of annularity on November 22, 1593. The longest duration of totality was 2 minutes, 36 seconds on July 10, 1972.
