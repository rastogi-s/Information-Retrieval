      <p>
       <b>
        James Simmons
       </b>
       (1933–2001) was a poet,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_critic" title="Literary critic">
        literary critic
       </a>
       and songwriter from
       <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">
        Derry
       </a>
       , Northern Ireland.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
        Biography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Simmons_(poet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       He was born into a middle-class
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">
        Protestant
       </a>
       family in Derry in 1933 and attended
       <a href="/wiki/Campbell_College" title="Campbell College">
        Campbell College
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">
        Belfast
       </a>
       before moving to the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">
        University of Leeds
       </a>
       to read for a degree in English. He married Laura Stinson and returned to Northern Ireland to teach at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friends%27_School_Lisburn" title="Friends' School Lisburn">
        Friends' School Lisburn
       </a>
       for five years. His final foreign excursion was a position at
       <a href="/wiki/Ahmadu_Bello_University" title="Ahmadu Bello University">
        Ahmadu Bello University
       </a>
       in Nigeria, where he worked for three years. During this time they had five children: Rachel, Sarah, Adam, Helen and Penelope. He returned to Northern Ireland in 1968, accepting a position at the recently opened
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Ulster_at_Coleraine" title="University of Ulster at Coleraine">
        New University of Ulster
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Coleraine" title="Coleraine">
        Coleraine
       </a>
       , where he remained until his retirement in 1984.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the early '70s – the bloodiest times of all in NI – he was the inspiration and leading light for The Resistance Cabaret, a satirical revue combining song, poetry and political comment on 'the troubles' and life in general, written and performed by Simmons and some of his students. Arguably, Simmons – whose passion for poetry was equalled only by his yearning to make it accessible to all the people – felt most at home in this setting, connecting with an audience that was moved to talk back.
      </p>
      <p>
       Near the end of his teaching career at the University of Ulster, Simmons and his first wife Laura divorced. He married Imelda Foley, the sister of Derry poet and fiction writer
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Foley_(writer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Michael Foley (writer) (page does not exist)">
        Michael Foley
       </a>
       , and had one child, Anna. After this marriage to Imelda ended, he had a son Ben with his third wife, fellow-poet
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Janice_Fitzpatrick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Janice Fitzpatrick (page does not exist)">
        Janice Fitzpatrick
       </a>
       . Simmons and Fitzpatrick started
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Poets%27_House&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Poets' House (page does not exist)">
        The Poets' House
       </a>
       , initially in
       <a href="/wiki/Islandmagee" title="Islandmagee">
        Islandmagee
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">
        County Antrim
       </a>
       , later in
       <a href="/wiki/Falcarragh" title="Falcarragh">
        Falcarragh
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">
        County Donegal
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
        Career
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Simmons_(poet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When Simmons returned to
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">
        Northern Ireland
       </a>
       he took part in
       <a href="/wiki/The_Belfast_Group" title="The Belfast Group">
        The Belfast Group
       </a>
       , together with such notables as
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Longley" title="Michael Longley">
        Michael Longley
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Seamus_Heaney" title="Seamus Heaney">
        Seamus Heaney
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Derek_Mahon" title="Derek Mahon">
        Derek Mahon
       </a>
       . In 1968, with his nephew Michael Stephens, Simmons went on a tour of universities in England. When he returned to Ireland, he established
       <a href="/wiki/The_Honest_Ulsterman" title="The Honest Ulsterman">
        The Honest Ulsterman
       </a>
       , the most important Irish
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_journal" title="Literary journal">
        literary journal
       </a>
       of the next 35 years
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . Simmons served as the editor for 17 of the first 19 issues; he then passed control of the magazine onto a series of younger editors. The Honest Ulsterman published a series of more than 30 poetry chapbooks, including the first collections of work by
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Muldoon" title="Paul Muldoon">
        Paul Muldoon
       </a>
       ("Knowing My Place"), Michael Foley ("The Acne and the Ecstasy"), and Michael Stephens ("Blues for Chocolate Doherty"). Members of the Belfast Group frequently published in The Honest Ulsterman.
      </p>
      <p>
       Whereas John Hewitt, the Ulster poet whom Simmons called 'the grandaddy of us all', ground out his truth by placing himself in the mortar and pestle of nature, Simmons ground his out by placing himself under a self-imposed public scrutiny. Using his own life for material, he explored his frailties in his poetry with the clinical detachment of a laboratory technician, the humour of a big soul, and the vulnerability of a lover. Perhaps his best epitaph is his own:
      </p>
      <p>
       "Hiding in humility, In irony, and wit, It would be very hard to prove That Simmons is a shit"
      </p>
      <p>
       He won several prizes for his poetry including the Gregory and
       <a href="/wiki/Cholmondeley_Award" title="Cholmondeley Award">
        Cholmondeley Awards
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       He also wrote a critical biography of
       <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_O%27Casey" title="Seán O'Casey">
        Seán O'Casey
       </a>
       (London: Macmillan).
      </p>
      <p>
       Throughout his career Simmons wrote and performed exquisitely provocative, yet hilarious and humane, songs about every aspect of contemporary life. In 1970 he founded a new platform for bringing these to a wider audience, the satirical revue – The Resistance Cabaret – with
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.garvincrawford.co.uk" rel="nofollow">
        Garvin Crawford
       </a>
       , Victor Thompson, David Templeton, Eithne Murphy, Jim Brown, Mike Graves, Jon Marshall and Heather Hutchinson. In varying line-ups, they performed their unique repertoire regularly at venues throughout Northern Ireland until 1976. His poetry collection, West Strand Visions, contains some of the repertoire. He recorded three collections of his own songs – City &amp; Eastern, Love In The Post, The Rostrevor Sessions – and produced a Resistance Cabaret album with the other members. He also set a number of Yeats' poems to music which he released on a tape cassette. The album was called Women's Company and included original songs and a selection of jazz standards.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since his death Simmons' work has been increasingly marginalised – few anthologies include him – and a 'Collected Poems' is yet to appear. His songs, however, continue to challenge and delight appreciative audiences of The Resistance Cabaret around Northern Ireland - most often sung by
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.garvincrawford.co.uk" rel="nofollow">
        Garvin Crawford
       </a>
       in
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.dufferincoachinginn.com/" rel="nofollow">
        The Dufferin Arms
       </a>
       , Killyleagh.
      </p>
