      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       is the largest
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       of and the closest major
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">
        astronomical object
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       . The Moon may be
       <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">
        observed
       </a>
       by using a variety of
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_instrument" title="Optical instrument">
        optical instruments
       </a>
       , ranging from the
       <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">
        naked eye
       </a>
       to large
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescopes
       </a>
       . The Moon is the only celestial body upon which surface features can be
       <a href="/wiki/Visual_acuity" title="Visual acuity">
        discerned
       </a>
       with the unaided eyes of most people.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Optimal_viewing_times">
        Optimal viewing times
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Optimal viewing times">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:72px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ComputerHotline_-_Grande-lune_(by).JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6429" data-file-width="3668" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/ComputerHotline_-_Grande-lune_%28by%29.JPG/70px-ComputerHotline_-_Grande-lune_%28by%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/ComputerHotline_-_Grande-lune_%28by%29.JPG/105px-ComputerHotline_-_Grande-lune_%28by%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/ComputerHotline_-_Grande-lune_%28by%29.JPG/140px-ComputerHotline_-_Grande-lune_%28by%29.JPG 2x" width="70"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ComputerHotline_-_Grande-lune_(by).JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Shadow" title="Shadow">
          Shadows
         </a>
         provide a sense of depth.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Contrary to popular belief, the Moon should ideally not be viewed at its full
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        phase
       </a>
       . During a full moon, rays of sunlight are hitting the visible portion of the moon
       <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">
        perpendicular
       </a>
       to the surface. As a result, there is less surface detail visible during a full moon than during other phases (such as the quarter and crescent phases) when sunlight hits the Moon at a much shallower angle. The brightness of a full moon as compared to a phase where a smaller percentage of the surface is illuminated tends to wash out substantial amounts of detail and can actually leave an
       <a href="/wiki/Afterimage" title="Afterimage">
        afterimage
       </a>
       on an observer's eye that can persist for several minutes. First quarter (six to nine days past
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       ) is generally considered the best time to observe the Moon for the average stargazer.
       <a href="/wiki/Shadow" title="Shadow">
        Shadows
       </a>
       and detail are most pronounced along the "
       <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">
        terminator
       </a>
       ", the dividing line between the illuminated (day side) and dark (night side) of the Moon.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Suggested_viewing_tools">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Naked_eye">
        Naked eye
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naked eye">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="floatright">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_names.svg" title="Lunar nearside with major maria and craters labeled">
        <img alt="Lunar nearside with major maria and craters labeled" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="512" height="375" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Moon_names.svg/500px-Moon_names.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Moon_names.svg/750px-Moon_names.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Moon_names.svg/1000px-Moon_names.svg.png 2x" width="500"/>
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earthshine_Karonen.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Earthshine_Karonen.jpg/200px-Earthshine_Karonen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Earthshine_Karonen.jpg/300px-Earthshine_Karonen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Earthshine_Karonen.jpg/400px-Earthshine_Karonen.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

         </div>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthshine" title="Earthshine">
          Earthshine
         </a>
         reflecting off the Moon. The bright region at left is directly illuminated by sunlight, while the rest of the Moon is faintly lit by sunlight reflected off the Earth.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Generally, the Moon can be viewed even with the naked eye, however it may be more enjoyable with optical instruments. The primary lunar surface features detectable to the naked eye are the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_maria" title="Lunar maria">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       or "seas", large
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basaltic
       </a>
       plains which form imaginary figures as the traditional "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_Rabbit" title="Moon Rabbit">
        Moon Rabbit
       </a>
       " or familiar "
       <a href="/wiki/Man_in_the_Moon" title="Man in the Moon">
        Man in the Moon
       </a>
       ". The maria cover about 35% of the surface. The contrast between the less reflective dark gray maria and the more reflective gray/white lunar highlands is easily visible without optical aid. Under good viewing conditions, those with keen eyesight may also be able to see some of the following features:
      </p>

       <li>

         Copernicus
        </a>

       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Mare_Nectaris" title="Mare Nectaris">

        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Mare_Humorum" title="Mare Humorum">
         Mare Humorum
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        Bright region around
        <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(lunar_crater)" title="Kepler (lunar crater)">
         Kepler
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Gassendi_(crater)" title="Gassendi (crater)">
         Gassendi

        region
       </li>
       <li>

         Plinius
        </a>
        region
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Mare_Vaporum" title="Mare Vaporum">
         Mare Vaporum
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Lubiniezky_(crater)" title="Lubiniezky (crater)">
         Lubiniesky
        </a>
        region
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Sinus_Medii" title="Sinus Medii">
         Sinus Medii
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        Faintly shaded area near
        <a href="/wiki/Sacrobosco_(crater)" title="Sacrobosco (crater)">
         Sacrobosco

       </li>
       <li>
        Dark spot at foot of
        <a href="/wiki/Mons_Huygens" title="Mons Huygens">

         Riphean Mountains

       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Another interesting phenomenon visible with the naked eye is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthshine" title="Earthshine">
        Earthshine
       </a>
       . Best visible shortly before or after new a moon (during the waning and waxing crescent phases respectively), Earthshine is the faint glow of the non-illuminated (night) side of the Moon caused by sunlight reflecting off the surface of Earth (which would appear nearly full to an observer situated on the Moon at this time) and onto the night side of the Moon. By the time the Moon reaches first its quarter however, the sunlight illuminated portion of the Moon becomes far too bright for Earthshine to be seen with the naked eye, however it can still be observed telescopically.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Binoculars_and_telephoto_camera_lenses">
        Binoculars and telephoto camera lenses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Binoculars and telephoto camera lenses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Binoculars are commonly used by those just beginning to observe the Moon and many experienced
       <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">
        amateur astronomers
       </a>
       prefer the view in binoculars to a telescopic view due to the larger
       <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">
        field of view
       </a>
       . Their high level of portability makes them the simplest device used to see more detail on the surface beyond naked eye observing.
      </p>
      <p>
       The primary disadvantage of binoculars is that they cannot be held very steady unless one utilizes a commercial or homemade binocular tripod. The recent introduction of
       <a href="/wiki/Image-stabilized_binoculars" title="Image-stabilized binoculars">
        image-stabilized binoculars
       </a>
       has changed this to some extent; however, cost is still an issue.
      </p>
      <p>
       The average pair of binoculars will bring the moon approximately as close as can be observed with a 200mm camera lens. The photos below were shot with a 200mm lens. The first photo was taken on 13 November, 2016 at 6:20pm PST, observing the new supermoon just hours before it would officially become the largest
       <a href="/wiki/Supermoon" title="Supermoon">
        supermoon
       </a>
       since 1948. The second photo was shot 24 hours later, and the contrast was enhanced to bring out details such as mountainous terrain. The next supermoon will not occur this large until the year 2034.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Telescopes">
        Telescopes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Telescopes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       To some it may be more desirable to utilize a telescope in which case far more options for observing the Moon exist. Even a small, well-made telescope will show the observer much greater detail than is visible with the naked eye or small binoculars. As the
       <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">
        aperture
       </a>
       of the telescope mirror (in the case of a
       <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">
        reflecting telescope
       </a>
       ) or lens (in the case of a
       <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">
        refracting telescope
       </a>
       ) increases, smaller and smaller features will begin to appear. With large amateur telescopes, features as small as 0.6 miles (1 km) in diameter can be observed depending on atmospheric conditions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most astronomers use different kinds of filters in order to bring out the contrast of certain surface features. Simple
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutral_density_filter" title="Neutral density filter">
        neutral density filters
       </a>
       are also common as they can cut down the amount of light reaching the eye by 60–95%, something that is helpful especially when observing a full or gibbous moon so the surface does not appear as washed out.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_occultations">
        Lunar occultations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lunar occultations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OccultJupiter_6-7-2005_6-07PM_Nelson_NZ.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="285" data-file-width="259" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/OccultJupiter_6-7-2005_6-07PM_Nelson_NZ.jpg/200px-OccultJupiter_6-7-2005_6-07PM_Nelson_NZ.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/OccultJupiter_6-7-2005_6-07PM_Nelson_NZ.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OccultJupiter_6-7-2005_6-07PM_Nelson_NZ.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
          Jupiter
         </a>
         (the bright object in the upper right) a few minutes before being occulted by the Moon on June 16, 2005
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       An
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occultation
       </a>
       is an astronomical event where a celestial object appears completely hidden by another, closer body (with a greater
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        angular diameter
       </a>
       ) due to the passage of the closer object directly between the more distant object and the observer. Due to the large apparent size of the Moon, lunar occultations are quite common and when a bright celestial object is involved, the result is an event that can be easily observed using the naked eye. The Moon almost constantly occults faint stars as it orbits the Earth but because even a young Moon appears immensely brighter than these stars, these events are difficult to observe using amateur telescopes. However, the Moon does frequently occult brighter stars and even
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">
        planets
       </a>
       due to its close proximity to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       . Four first
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        magnitude
       </a>
       stars,
       <a href="/wiki/Regulus" title="Regulus">
        Regulus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spica" title="Spica">
        Spica
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Antares" title="Antares">
        Antares
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Aldebaran" title="Aldebaran">
        Aldebaran
       </a>
       , are sufficiently close to the ecliptic that they may be occulted by the Moon. In addition, two
       <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">
        star clusters
       </a>
       visible to the naked eye, the
       <a href="/wiki/Beehive_Cluster" title="Beehive Cluster">
        Beehive Cluster
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">
        Pleiades
       </a>
       , are often occulted. Depending on one's location on the Earth, there are usually several occultations involving naked eye objects every year and many more that can be observed using binoculars or a telescope.
      </p>
      <p>
       Accurate timings (accuracy within a few tenths of a second) of lunar occultations are scientifically useful in fields such as lunar topography,
       <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">
        astrometry
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">
        binary star
       </a>
       studies. These timings are regularly obtained by amateur astronomers using readily available instruments to observe the Moon.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transient_Lunar_Phenomena">
        Transient Lunar Phenomena
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transient Lunar Phenomena">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A transient lunar phenomenon (TLP) or "Lunar Transient Phenomena" (LTP), refers to short-lived lights, colors, or changes in appearance of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_surface" title="Lunar surface">
        lunar surface
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Claims of these phenomena go back at least 1,000 years, with some having been observed independently by multiple witnesses or some in the scientific community. Nevertheless, the majority of transient lunar phenomena reports are irreproducible and do not possess adequate control experiments that could be used to distinguish among alternative
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypotheses" title="Hypotheses">
        hypotheses
       </a>
       . Few reports concerning these phenomena are ever published in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peer_reviewed" title="Peer reviewed">
        peer reviewed
       </a>
       scientific journals, and rightfully or wrongfully, the lunar scientific community rarely discusses these observations.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most lunar scientists will acknowledge that transient events such as
       <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">
        outgassing
       </a>
       and impact cratering do occur over
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_time" title="Geologic time">
        geologic time
       </a>
       : the controversy lies in the frequency of such events.
      </p>
      <p>
       A number of astronomical societies around the world have implemented their own TLP watch programs and TLP alert networks.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Blinking_devices">
        Blinking devices
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Blinking devices">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A number of observers employ different colored
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(optics)" title="Filter (optics)">
        filters
       </a>
       to determine colored transient events on the Moon. By quickly alternating filters of opposing colors in the telescopic light path, faintly colored areas on the Moon can stand out more by appearing to flicker on and off. A red area will appear brighter when viewed through a red filter and darker when seen through a blue filter. It is possible to alternate the filters manually however, this requires a certain dexterity of the hand and good coordination. A purpose built filter wheel is much more viable alternative, and this can be motorized, so the observer can devote all of their concentration to what is going on through the eyepiece. There are, however a number of features on the Moon that will appear to blink naturally, among them being the southwestern part of
       <a href="/wiki/Fracastorius_(crater)" title="Fracastorius (crater)">
        Fracastorius (crater)
       </a>
       , and a section of the western wall of
       <a href="/wiki/Plato_(crater)" title="Plato (crater)">
        Plato (crater)
       </a>
       . A special filter wheel called a "crater extinction device" is capable of measuring the brightness of an individual lunar feature to be measured according to the point where it ceases to be visible.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_of_the_crescent_Moon">
        Observation of the crescent Moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Observation of the crescent Moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       During the first two weeks, the Moon is called 'crescent' (when the illuminated portion increases) while it is 'falling' for the next two weeks. The Crescent Moon is seen before &amp; after the conjunction, or Change of Moon; that is, Waning crescent or Waxing Crescent. The Moon when other than Crescent or Dark, is called Gibbous, Waxing or Waning, either side of the Full Moon.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_items_of_interest">
        Other items of interest
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other items of interest">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because the Moon is so bright, it is especially interesting to see objects "superimposed" on it. One particular point of interest is an ISS (
       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">
        International Space Station
       </a>
       ) transit.
      </p>
