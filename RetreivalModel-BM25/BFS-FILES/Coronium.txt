      <p>
       <b>
        Coronium
       </b>
       , also called
       <b>
        newtonium
       </b>
       , was the name of a suggested
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
        chemical element
       </a>
       , hypothesised in the 19th century. The name, inspired by the solar
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       , was given by A. Gruenwald in 1887.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A new atomic thin green line in the solar corona was then considered to be emitted a new element unlike anything else seen under laboratory conditions. Because of this it was also mis-classified as
       <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
        Iron
       </a>
       Line Number 1474.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosmos_Portal_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cosmos_Portal-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_spectroscopy">
        Solar spectroscopy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Solar spectroscopy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       During the total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_August_7,_1869" title="Solar eclipse of August 7, 1869">
        solar eclipse of 7 August 1869
       </a>
       , a green
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">
        emission line
       </a>
       of wavelength 530.3 nm was independently observed by
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Augustus_Young" title="Charles Augustus Young">
        Charles Augustus Young
       </a>
       (1834–1908) and
       <a href="/wiki/William_Harkness" title="William Harkness">
        William Harkness
       </a>
       (1837–1903) in the
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        coronal
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">
        spectrum
       </a>
       . Since this line did not correspond to that of any known material, it was proposed that it was due to an unknown element, provisionally named coronium.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1902, in an attempt at a chemical conception of the
       <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">
        aether
       </a>
       , the Russian chemist and chemical educator
       <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">
        Dmitri Mendeleev
       </a>
       hypothesized that there existed two inert chemical elements of lesser atomic weight than hydrogen. Of these two, he thought the lighter to be an all-penetrating, all-pervasive gas, and the slightly heavier one to be coronium. Later he renamed coronium as newtonium.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was not until the 1930s that
       <a href="/wiki/Walter_Grotrian" title="Walter Grotrian">
        Walter Grotrian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bengt_Edl%C3%A9n" title="Bengt Edlén">
        Bengt Edlén
       </a>
       discovered that the spectral line at 530.3 nm was due to
       <a href="/wiki/Highly_charged_ion" title="Highly charged ion">
        highly ionized
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
        iron
       </a>
       (Fe
       <sup>
        13+
       </sup>
       ); other unusual lines in the coronal spectrum were also caused by highly charged ions, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">
        nickel
       </a>
       , the high ionization being due to the extreme temperature of the
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        solar corona
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morison_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morison-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
