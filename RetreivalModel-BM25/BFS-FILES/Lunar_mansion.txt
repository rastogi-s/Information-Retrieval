   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunar mansion
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        lunar mansion
       </b>
       is a segment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       (often called a station or house) through which the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       passes in
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        its orbit
       </a>
       around
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , often used by ancient cultures as part of their
       <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">
        calendar
       </a>
       system. In general, though not always, the
       <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">
        zodiac
       </a>
       is divided into 27 or 28 segments relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">
        fixed stars
       </a>
       – one for each day of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">
        lunar month
       </a>
       . (A
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Sidereal_month" title="Lunar month">
        sidereal month
       </a>
       lasts about 27.3 days.) The Moon's position is charted with respect to those fixed segments. Since the Moon's position at given stage will vary according to Earth's position in
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">
        its own orbit
       </a>
       , the lunar mansions are an effective system for keeping track of the passage of seasons.
      </p>
      <p>
       Various cultures have used sets of lunar mansions
       <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">
        astrologically
       </a>
       ; for example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Jyotisha" title="Jyotisha">
        Jyotisha
       </a>
       astrological
       <a href="/wiki/Nakshatra" title="Nakshatra">
        Nakshatras
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">
        Hindu
       </a>
       culture, the
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Manzils&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Manzils (page does not exist)">
        manzils
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Manazil_al-qamar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Manazil al-qamar (page does not exist)">
         manazil al-qamar
        </a>
       </i>
       ), the
       <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Eight_Mansions" title="Twenty-Eight Mansions">
        28 mansions
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">
        Chinese astronomy
       </a>
       , and the 36
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decans" title="Decans">
        decans
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_astronomy" title="Egyptian astronomy">
        Egyptian astronomy
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Western_astrology" title="Western astrology">
        Western astrology
       </a>
       does not use mansions, but each
       <a href="/wiki/Astrological_sign" title="Astrological sign">
        zodiac sign
       </a>
       covers 2 or 3 mansions. The Chinese system groups mansions into four groups related to the seasons.
      </p>
      <p>
       The concept of the lunar mansions are considered to be
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">
        Babylonian
       </a>
       in origin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In his
       <i>
        A History of Western Astrology
       </i>
       , Jim Tester explains that they appear in
       <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_astrology" title="Hellenistic astrology">
        Hellenistic astrology
       </a>
       in the 2nd-century list of fixed stars in the
       <i>
        Katarchai
       </i>
       by Maximus (the poet and astrologer, not to be confounded with
       <a href="/wiki/Maximus_of_Tyre" title="Maximus of Tyre">
        Maximus of Tyre
       </a>
       ), the Arabic lists by Alchandri and
       <a href="/wiki/Haly_Abenragel" title="Haly Abenragel">
        Haly Abenragel
       </a>
       , and a similar
       <a href="/wiki/Copts" title="Copts">
        Coptic
       </a>
       list with Greek names.
      </p>
      <p>
       Tester believes that though they were known in
       <a href="/wiki/Vedic_period" title="Vedic period">
        Vedic India
       </a>
       , all lists "seem to betray" transmission through Greek sources. Though pointing out that the Babylonians had well established lunar groupings by the
       <a href="/wiki/6th_century_BC" title="6th century BC">
        6th century BC
       </a>
       , he also notes that the 28-mansion "scheme was derived via Egyptian magic by the linking of the lists of lucky and unlucky days of the lunar month with the hemerologies and with the zodiac."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_mansions">
        Chinese mansions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_mansion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chinese mansions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Eight_Mansions" title="Twenty-Eight Mansions">
        Twenty-Eight Mansions
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        28 Lunar Mansions
       </b>
       (Chinese:
       <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text" xml:lang="zh">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%BA%8C%E5%8D%81" title="wikt:二十">
         二十
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%85%AB" title="wikt:八">
         八
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AE%BF" title="wikt:宿">
         宿
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        Èrshí-Bā Xiù
       </i>
       ) are the
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">
        Chinese
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Asian_culture" title="East Asian culture">
        East Asian
       </a>
       form of the lunar mansions. They can be considered as the equivalent to the Western
       <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">
        zodiac
       </a>
       , although the 28 Mansions reflect the movement of the Moon through a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_month" title="Sidereal month">
        sidereal month
       </a>
       rather than the Sun in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_year" title="Solar year">
        solar year
       </a>
       . In their final form, they embodied the astral forms of the
       <a href="/wiki/Four_Symbols_(China)" title="Four Symbols (China)">
        Four Symbols
       </a>
       : two real and two legendary animals important in
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_culture" title="Chinese culture">
        traditional Chinese culture
       </a>
       such as
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fengshui" title="Fengshui">
         fengshui
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_mansions">
        Indian mansions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_mansion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Indian mansions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Nakshatra" title="Nakshatra">
        Nakshatra
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Nakshatras
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Nákṣatras
       </b>
       (
       <small>
        lit.
       </small>
       "stars") are the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_astronomy" title="Indian astronomy">
        Indian
       </a>
       form of the lunar mansions. They usually number 27 but sometimes 28 and their names are related to the most prominent constellations in each sector. They start from a point on the ecliptic precisely opposite the star
       <a href="/wiki/Spica" title="Spica">
        Spica
       </a>
       (Sanskrit:
       <i>
        Chitrā
       </i>
       ) and develop eastwards. In classical
       <a href="/wiki/Hindu_mythology" title="Hindu mythology">
        Hindu mythology
       </a>
       , the creation of the nakshatras is attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Daksha" title="Daksha">
        Daksha
       </a>
       . They were wives of
       <a href="/wiki/Chandra" title="Chandra">
        Chandra
       </a>
       , the moon god. The nakshatras of traditional
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bhartiya_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bhartiya astronomy (page does not exist)">
        bhartiya astronomy
       </a>
       are based on a list of 28 asterisms found in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Atharvaveda" title="Atharvaveda">
         Atharvaveda
        </a>
       </i>
       (AVŚ 19.7) and also in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Shatapatha_Brahmana" title="Shatapatha Brahmana">
         Shatapatha Brahmana
        </a>
       </i>
       . The first astronomical text that lists them is the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vedanga_Jyotisha" title="Vedanga Jyotisha">
         Vedanga Jyotisha
        </a>
       </i>
       . The mansions are important parts of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_astrology" title="Indian astrology">
        Indian astrology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic_mansions">
        Arabic mansions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_mansion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arabic mansions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the traditional
       <a href="/wiki/Astrology_in_medieval_Islam" title="Astrology in medieval Islam">
        Arabic astrological system
       </a>
       , which was derived from the Indian
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Nakshatra" title="Nakshatra">
         nakshatra
        </a>
       </i>
       system,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the moon was seen to move through 28 distinct
       <i>
        manazil
       </i>
       (singular:
       <i>
        manzil
       </i>
       = house) during the normal solar year, each
       <i>
        manzil
       </i>
       lasting, therefore, for about 13 days. One or more
       <i>
        manazil
       </i>
       were then grouped into a
       <i>
        nawaa
       </i>
       (plural
       <i>
        anwaa
       </i>
       ) which were tied to a given weather pattern. In other words, the yearly pattern was divided in the following manner:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A year was divided into
       <i>
        anwaa
       </i>
       , each of which was made up of one more
       <i>
        manazil
       </i>
       , which were associated with a dominant star or constellation. These stars and constellations were sometimes, but not always, connected in some way to constellations in the
       <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">
        Zodiac
       </a>
       . Moreover, as the
       <i>
        anwaa' repeat on a regular, solar cycle, they can be correlated to fixed points on the Gregorian calendar.
       </i>
      </p>

       (Islamic view of angels - equivalent to rank of the "Watcher" or "Guardian Angel") is the Angel that rules the corresponding Arabic alphabet (rhythm of the alphabet in
       <a href="/wiki/Abjad_numerals" title="Abjad numerals">
        numerology of the Abjad system
       </a>
       ), manazilu ʾl-Qamar (lunar mansion) and constellation (i.e. zodiac sign). Generally speaking, the four Archangels in Islam is
       <a href="/wiki/Gabriel" title="Gabriel">
        Jibrāʾīl
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_(archangel)" title="Michael (archangel)">
        Mīkāʾīl
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Israfil" title="Israfil">
        ʾIsrāfīl
       </a>
       and Malaku ʾl-Maut (
       <a href="/wiki/Azrael" title="Azrael">
        ‘Azrāʾīl
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       3) the alphabetical orders follows the sequence of the original
       <i>
        abjadī
       </i>
       order (أَبْجَدِي), used for
       <a href="/wiki/Lettering" title="Lettering">
        lettering
       </a>
       , derives from the order of the
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet" title="Phoenician alphabet">
        Phoenician alphabet
       </a>
       , and is therefore similar to the order of other Phoenician-derived alphabets, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_alphabet" title="Hebrew alphabet">
        Hebrew alphabet
       </a>
       . In this order, letters are also used as numbers,
       <a href="/wiki/Abjad_numerals" title="Abjad numerals">
        Abjad numerals
       </a>
       , and possess the same alphanumeric code/
       <a href="/wiki/Cipher" title="Cipher">
        cipher
       </a>
       as Hebrew
       <a href="/wiki/Gematria" title="Gematria">
        gematria
       </a>
       and Greek
       <a href="/wiki/Isopsephy" title="Isopsephy">
        isopsephy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       4) those angel name with an "asterisk" needs source citation upon Arabic transliteration but the given is the closest pronunciation based upon uttering the consonants.
      </p>
