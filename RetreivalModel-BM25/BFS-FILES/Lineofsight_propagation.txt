   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Line-of-sight propagation
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>

    </div>

      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Propagation_VHF.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="587" data-file-width="1359" height="95" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Propagation_VHF.JPG/220px-Propagation_VHF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Propagation_VHF.JPG/330px-Propagation_VHF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Propagation_VHF.JPG/440px-Propagation_VHF.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Propagation_VHF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Line of sight propagation to an antenna
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Line-of-sight propagation
       </b>
       is a characteristic of
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
        electromagnetic radiation
       </a>
       or acoustic
       <a href="/wiki/Wave_propagation" title="Wave propagation">
        wave propagation
       </a>
       which means waves travel in a direct path from the source to the receiver. Electromagnetic
       <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(telecommunications)" title="Transmission (telecommunications)">
        transmission
       </a>
       includes light emissions traveling in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight_line" title="Straight line">
        straight line
       </a>
       . The rays or waves may be
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">
        diffracted
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">
        refracted
       </a>
       , reflected, or absorbed by the atmosphere and obstructions with material and generally cannot travel over the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       or behind obstacles.
      </p>
      <p>
       In contrast to line-of-sight propagation, at
       <a href="/wiki/Low_frequency" title="Low frequency">
        low frequency
       </a>
       (below approximately 3
       <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">
        MHz
       </a>
       ) due to
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">
        diffraction
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">
        radio waves
       </a>
       can travel as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_wave" title="Ground wave">
        ground waves
       </a>
       , which follow the contour of the Earth. This enables
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AM_radio" title="AM radio">
        AM radio
       </a>
       stations to transmit beyond the horizon. Additionally, frequencies in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortwave" title="Shortwave">
        shortwave
       </a>
       bands between approximately 1 and 30 MHz, can be reflected back to Earth by the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
        ionosphere
       </a>
       , called
       <a href="/wiki/Skywave" title="Skywave">
        skywave
       </a>
       or "skip" propagation, thus giving radio transmissions in this range a potentially global reach.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, at frequencies above 30 MHz (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VHF" title="VHF">
        VHF
       </a>
       and higher) and in lower levels of the atmosphere, neither of these effects are significant. Thus, any obstruction between the transmitting antenna (
       <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">
        transmitter
       </a>
       ) and the receiving antenna (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Receiver_(radio)" title="Receiver (radio)">
        receiver
       </a>
       ) will block the signal, just like the
       <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
        light
       </a>
       that the eye may sense. Therefore, since the ability to visually see a transmitting antenna (disregarding the limitations of the eye's resolution) roughly corresponds to the ability to receive a radio signal from it, the propagation characteristic at these frequencies is called "line-of-sight". The farthest possible point of propagation is referred to as the "radio horizon".
      </p>
      <p>
       In practice, the propagation characteristics of these radio waves vary substantially depending on the exact frequency and the strength of the transmitted signal (a function of both the transmitter and the antenna characteristics). Broadcast
       <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation" title="Frequency modulation">
        FM
       </a>
       radio, at comparatively low frequencies of around 100 MHz, are less affected by the presence of buildings and forests.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Impairments_to_line-of-sight_propagation">
        Impairments to line-of-sight propagation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Line-of-sight_propagation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Impairments to line-of-sight propagation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fresnel_zone_disrupted.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="512" height="309" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Fresnel_zone_disrupted.png/220px-Fresnel_zone_disrupted.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Fresnel_zone_disrupted.png/330px-Fresnel_zone_disrupted.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Fresnel_zone_disrupted.png/440px-Fresnel_zone_disrupted.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fresnel_zone_disrupted.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Objects within the
         <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_zone" title="Fresnel zone">
          Fresnel zone
         </a>
         can disturb line of sight propagation even if they don't block the geometric line between antennas.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Low-powered
       <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">
        microwave
       </a>
       transmitters can be foiled by tree branches, or even heavy rain or snow. The presence of objects not in the direct line-of-sight can cause diffraction effects that disrupt radio transmissions. For the best propagation, a volume known as the first
       <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_zone" title="Fresnel zone">
        Fresnel zone
       </a>
       should be free of obstructions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Reflected radiation from the
       <a href="/wiki/Ground_plane" title="Ground plane">
        surface of the surrounding ground or salt water
       </a>
       can also either cancel out or enhance the direct signal. This effect can be reduced by raising either or both antennas further from the ground: The reduction in loss achieved is known as
       <i>
        height gain
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It is important to take into account the curvature of the Earth for calculation of line-of-sight paths from maps, when a direct visual fix cannot be made. Designs for microwave formerly used ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         4
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
       </span>
       earth radius to compute clearances along the path.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_telephones">
        Mobile telephones
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Line-of-sight_propagation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mobile telephones">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although the frequencies used by
       <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">
        mobile phones
       </a>
       (cell phones) are in the line-of-sight range, they still function in cities. This is made possible by a combination of the following effects:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        ​
        <span class="frac nowrap">
         <sup>
          1
         </sup>
         ⁄
         <sub>
          <i>
           r
          </i>
          <sup>
           4
          </sup>
         </sub>
        </span>
        propagation over the rooftop landscape
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
           <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2016)">
            clarification needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        diffraction into the "street canyon" below
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multipath_fading" title="Multipath fading">
         multipath
        </a>
        reflection along the street
       </li>
       <li>
        diffraction through windows, and attenuated passage through walls, into the building
       </li>
       <li>
        reflection, diffraction, and attenuated passage through internal walls, floors and ceilings within the building
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The combination of all these effects makes the mobile phone propagation environment highly complex, with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multipath_fading" title="Multipath fading">
        multipath
       </a>
       effects and extensive
       <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_fading" title="Rayleigh fading">
        Rayleigh fading
       </a>
       . For mobile phone services, these problems are tackled using:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        rooftop or hilltop positioning of base stations
       </li>
       <li>
        many
        <a href="/wiki/Base_station" title="Base station">
         base stations
        </a>
        (usually called "cell sites"). A phone can typically see at least three, and usually as many as six at any given time.
       </li>
       <li>
        "sectorized" antennas at the base stations. Instead of one antenna with
        <a href="/wiki/Omnidirectional_antenna" title="Omnidirectional antenna">
         omnidirectional
        </a>
        coverage, the station may use as few as 3 (rural areas with few customers) or as many as 32 separate antennas, each covering a portion of the circular coverage. This allows the base station to use a directional antenna that is pointing at the user, which improves the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signal_to_noise_ratio" title="Signal to noise ratio">
         signal to noise ratio
        </a>
        . If the user moves (perhaps by walking or driving) from one antenna sector to another, the base station automatically selects the proper antenna.
       </li>
       <li>
        rapid
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handoff" title="Handoff">
         handoff
        </a>
        between base stations (roaming)
       </li>
       <li>
        the radio link used by the phones is a digital link with extensive
        <a href="/wiki/Error_detection_and_correction" title="Error detection and correction">
         error correction and detection
        </a>
        in the digital protocol
       </li>
       <li>
        sufficient operation of mobile phone in tunnels when supported by split cable antennas
       </li>
       <li>
        local repeaters inside complex vehicles or buildings
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Faraday_cage" title="Faraday cage">
        Faraday cage
       </a>
       is composed of a conductor that completely surrounds an area on all sides, top, and bottom. Electromagnetic radiation is blocked where the wavelength is longer than any gaps. For example, mobile telephone signals are blocked in windowless metal enclosures that approximate a Faraday cage, such as elevator cabins, and parts of trains, cars, and ships. The same problem can affect signals in buildings with extensive steel reinforcement.
      </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Repeater-schema.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="245" data-file-width="341" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Repeater-schema.svg/220px-Repeater-schema.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Repeater-schema.svg/330px-Repeater-schema.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Repeater-schema.svg/440px-Repeater-schema.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Repeater-schema.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Two stations not in line-of-sight may be able to communicate through an intermediate
         <a href="/wiki/Radio_repeater" title="Radio repeater">
          radio repeater
         </a>
         station.
        </div>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_horizon">
        Radio horizon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        radio horizon
       </i>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Locus_(mathematics)" title="Locus (mathematics)">
        locus
       </a>
       of points at which direct rays from an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antenna_(electronics)" title="Antenna (electronics)">
        antenna
       </a>
       are tangential to the surface of the Earth. If the Earth were a perfect sphere without an atmosphere, the
       <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
        radio
       </a>
       horizon would be a circle.
      </p>
      <p>
       The radio horizon of the transmitting and receiving antennas can be added together to increase the effective communication range.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">
        Radio wave propagation
       </a>
       is affected by atmospheric conditions,
       <a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_absorption" title="Ionospheric absorption">
        ionospheric absorption
       </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_bulge_and_atmosphere_effect">
        Earth bulge and atmosphere effect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Line-of-sight_propagation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Earth bulge and atmosphere effect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Earth bulge
       </i>
       is a term used in
       <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">
        telecommunications
       </a>
       . It refers to the circular segment of earth profile that blocks off long distance communications. Since the geometric line of sight passes at varying heights over the Earth, the propagating radio wave encounters slightly different propagation conditions over the path. The usual effect of the declining pressure of the atmosphere with height is to bend radio waves down towards the surface of the Earth, effectively increasing the Earth's radius, and the distance to the radio horizon, by a factor around ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         4
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       -factor can change from its average value depending on weather.
      </p>

       But in stormy weather,
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       may decrease to cause

       </a>
       in transmission. (In extreme cases
       <i>
        k
       </i>
