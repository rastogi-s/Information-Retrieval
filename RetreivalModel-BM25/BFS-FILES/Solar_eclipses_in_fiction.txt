      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Films">
        Films
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipses_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Films">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_featuring_eclipses" title="List of films featuring eclipses">
        List of films featuring eclipses
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       There is a body of films featuring
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       . Compared to other astronomical events featured in films, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moons
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       strikes, solar eclipses are less commonly seen. When they have featured in films, they often drive the plot and have a portentous presence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hickman_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hickman-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">
        NPR
       </a>
       's Glen Weldon said that films use eclipses "to signal to audiences that the normal rules have temporarily lifted, and things are about to get weird". The first film to feature a solar eclipse was the 1907 silent film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Eclipse,_or_the_Courtship_of_the_Sun_and_Moon" title="The Eclipse, or the Courtship of the Sun and Moon">
         The Eclipse, or the Courtship of the Sun and Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       that featured a solar eclipse as a fantastical consummation between the sun and the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weldon_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-weldon-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Eclipses have been seen as bad omens throughout history, so filmmakers leverage that belief "as visual cues or key plot points", according to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Oregonian" title="The Oregonian">
         The Oregonian
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">
        '
       </span>
       s Amy Wang.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wang_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wang-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The most accurate depiction of a solar eclipse in film is seen in the 1961 religious epic film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Barabbas_(1961_film)" title="Barabbas (1961 film)">
         Barabbas
        </a>
       </i>
       due to the filming of an actual solar eclipse during its crucifixion scene.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-white_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-white-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1984_eclipse_and_Witness">
        1984 eclipse and
        <i>
         Witness
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipses_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1984 eclipse and Witness">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       While director
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Weir" title="Peter Weir">
        Peter Weir
       </a>
       was filming
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Witness_(1985_film)" title="Witness (1985 film)">
         Witness
        </a>
       </i>
       (1985) in the
       <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">
        Amish
       </a>
       region of Pennsylvania, a genuine solar eclipse occurred on
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_May_30,_1984" title="Solar eclipse of May 30, 1984">
        May 30, 1984
       </a>
       at his location. Weir filmed several of his actors in costume, responding to the eclipse, with a vague intention of revising the film's storyline to include a solar eclipse.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Ultimately, however, the eclipse footage was not included in the final edit.
      </p>

       <li>
        In the game
        <a href="/wiki/Castlevania:_Aria_of_Sorrow" title="Castlevania: Aria of Sorrow">
         Castlevania: Aria of Sorrow
        </a>
        , the story begins with a total solar eclipse in the year 2035 where is supposed to be sealed Dracula's castle.
       </li>
       <li>
        The game
        <a href="/wiki/Terraria" title="Terraria">
         Terraria
        </a>
        features a solar eclipse as a rare event that causes monsters inspired by popular
        <a href="/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">
         horror fiction
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">
         films
        </a>
        to attack the player.
       </li>
       <li>
        The Japanese
        <a href="/wiki/Visual_novel" title="Visual novel">
         visual novel
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virtue%27s_Last_Reward" title="Virtue's Last Reward">
         Virtue's Last Reward
        </a>
        has both a lunar and solar eclipse as a plot point.
       </li>
       <li>
        The Japanese Berserk manga features an eclipse.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Love_and_Rockets_(comics)" title="Love and Rockets (comics)">
         Love and Rockets (comics)
        </a>
        features a solar eclipse in Palomar at the beginning of
        <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Hernandez" title="Gilbert Hernandez">
         Gilbert Hernandez
        </a>
        's "Heartbreak Soup", during which a pair of fraternal twins (Israel &amp; Aurora) are playing and Aurora disappears. This affects the storyline of not only Gilbert's stories going forward, but sheds light on a major character in
        <a href="/wiki/Jaime_Hernandez" title="Jaime Hernandez">
         Jaime
        </a>
        's work as well.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the graphic novel Batman The Return Of Bruce Wayne, eclipses play an important role, as it is usually during those events that Bruce Wayne jumps through time.
       </li>
