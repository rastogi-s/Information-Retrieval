   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Saros (astronomy)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Saros
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/ɛər/: 'are' in 'bare'">
            ɛər
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
        <small class="nowrap">
         (
         <span class="unicode haudio">
          <span class="fn">
           <span style="white-space:nowrap">
            <a href="/wiki/File:Saros.ogg" title="About this sound">
             <img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/>
            </a>
           </span>
           <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b2/Saros.ogg" title="Saros.ogg">
            listen
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         )
        </small>
       </span>
       ) is a period of approximately 223
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_month" title="Synodic month">
        synodic months
       </a>
       (approximately 6585.3211 days, or 18 years, 11 days, 8 hours), that can be used to predict
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipses
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . One saros period after an eclipse, the Sun,
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , and Moon return to approximately the same relative geometry, a near straight line, and a nearly identical eclipse will occur, in what is referred to as an
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_cycle" title="Eclipse cycle">
        eclipse cycle
       </a>
       . A
       <b>
        sar
       </b>
       is one half of a saros.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A series of eclipses that are separated by one saros is called a
       <b>
        saros series
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saros_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The earliest discovered historical record of what is known as the saros is by
       <a href="/wiki/Neo-Babylonian_Empire" title="Neo-Babylonian Empire">
        Chaldean
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy" title="Babylonian astronomy">
        (neo-Babylonian) astronomers
       </a>
       in the last several centuries BC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was later known to
       <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">
        Hipparchus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">
        Pliny
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The name "saros" (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        σάρος
       </span>
       ) was applied to the eclipse cycle by
       <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Halley" title="Edmond Halley">
        Edmond Halley
       </a>
       in 1691, who took it from the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Suda" title="Suda">
         Suda
        </a>
       </i>
       , a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">
        Byzantine
       </a>
       lexicon of the 11th century. The Suda says, "[The saros is] a measure and a number among
       <a href="/wiki/Chaldea" title="Chaldea">
        Chaldeans
       </a>
       . For 120 saroi make 2220 years (years of 12 lunar months) according to the Chaldeans' reckoning, if indeed the saros makes 222 lunar months, which are 18 years and 6 months (i.e. years of 12 lunar months)."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The information in the
       <i>
        Suda
       </i>
       in turn was derived directly or otherwise from the
       <i>
        Chronicle
       </i>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">
        Eusebius of Caesarea
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       which quoted
       <a href="/wiki/Berossus" title="Berossus">
        Berossus
       </a>
       . (
       <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Le_Gentil" title="Guillaume Le Gentil">
        Guillaume Le Gentil
       </a>
       claimed that Halley's usage was incorrect in 1756,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       but the name continues to be used.) The Greek word apparently comes from the Babylonian word "sāru" meaning the number 3600.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to Tony Freeth, in an article for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">
         Scientific American
        </a>
       </i>
       , mechanical calculation of the saros cycle is built into the
       <a href="/wiki/Antikythera_mechanism" title="Antikythera mechanism">
        Antikythera mechanism
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saros_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunareclipsediagram.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="481" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Lunareclipsediagram.svg/240px-Lunareclipsediagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Lunareclipsediagram.svg/360px-Lunareclipsediagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Lunareclipsediagram.svg/480px-Lunareclipsediagram.svg.png 2x" width="240"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunareclipsediagram.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Lunar eclipses occurring near the Moon's ascending node are given
         <i>
          odd
         </i>
         saros series numbers. The first eclipse in such series passes through the southern edge of the Earth's shadow, and the Moon's path is shifted northward each successive saros.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The saros, a period of 6585.3211 days (14 common years + 4 leap years + 11.321 days, or 13 common years + 5 leap years + 10.321 days), is useful for predicting the times at which nearly identical eclipses will occur. Three periodicities related to lunar orbit, the
       <a href="/wiki/Month#Types_of_months_in_astronomy" title="Month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Month#Types_of_months_in_astronomy" title="Month">
        draconic month
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Month#Types_of_months_in_astronomy" title="Month">
        anomalistic month
       </a>
       coincide almost perfectly each saros cycle. For an eclipse to occur, either the Moon must be located between the Earth and Sun (for a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       ) or the Earth must be located between the Sun and Moon (for a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       ). This can happen only when the Moon is
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full
       </a>
       , respectively, and repeat occurrences of these
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        lunar phases
       </a>
       result from solar and lunar orbits producing the Moon's
       <i>
        synodic period
       </i>
       of 29.53059 days. During most full and new moons, however, the shadow of the Earth or Moon falls to the north or south of the other body. Eclipses occur when the three bodies form a nearly straight line. Because the plane of the lunar orbit is inclined to that of the earth, this condition occurs only when a full or new Moon is near or in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_of_the_ecliptic" title="Plane of the ecliptic">
        ecliptic plane
       </a>
       , that is when the moon is at one of the two
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">
        nodes
       </a>
       (the ascending or descending node). The period of time for two successive lunar passes through the ecliptic plane (returning to the same node) is termed the
       <i>
        draconic month
       </i>
       , a 27.21222 day period. The three-dimensional geometry of an eclipse, when the new or full moon is near one of the nodes, occurs every 5 or 6 months when the Sun is in conjunction or opposition to the Moon and coincidentally also near a node of the Moon's orbit at that time, or twice per
       <a href="/wiki/Year#Draconic_year" title="Year">
        eclipse year
       </a>
       . Two Saros eclipses have the same appearance and duration due to the distance between the Earth and Moon being nearly the same for each event because the Saros eclipse cycle is also an integer of the
       <i>
        anomalistic month
       </i>
       , the period of the eccentricity of lunar orbit, 27.5545 days.
      </p>

      </div>
      <p>
       After one saros, the Moon will have completed roughly an integer number of synodic, draconic, and anomalistic periods (223, 242, and 239) and the Earth-Sun-Moon geometry will be nearly identical: the Moon will have the same phase and be at the same node and the same distance from the Earth. In addition, because the saros is close to 18 years in length (about 11 days longer), the earth will be nearly the same distance from the sun, and tilted to it in nearly the same orientation (same season).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-totality_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-totality-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Given the date of an eclipse, one saros later a nearly identical eclipse can be predicted. During this 18-year period, about 40 other solar and lunar eclipses take place, but with a somewhat different geometry. One saros equaling 18.03 years is not equal to a perfect integer number of lunar orbits (earth revolutions with respect to the fixed stars of 27.32166 days
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_month" title="Sidereal month">
        sidereal month
       </a>
       ), therefore, even though the relative geometry of the Earth–Sun–Moon system will be nearly identical after a saros, the Moon will be in a slightly different position with respect to the stars for each eclipse in a Saros series. The axis of rotation of the Earth–Moon system exhibits a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_precession" title="Lunar precession">
        precession
       </a>
       period of 18.59992 years.
      </p>
      <p>
       The saros is not an integer number of days, but contains the fraction of ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
       </span>
       of a day. Thus each successive eclipse in a saros series occurs about 8 hours later in the day. In the case of an eclipse of the Sun, this means that the region of visibility will shift westward about 120°, or about one third of the way around the globe, and the two eclipses will thus not be visible from the same place on Earth. In the case of an eclipse of the Moon, the next eclipse might still be visible from the same location as long as the Moon is above the horizon. Given three saros eclipse intervals, the local time of day of an eclipse will be nearly the same. This three saros interval (19,755.96 days) is known as a
       <b>
        triple saros
       </b>
       or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Exeligmos" title="Exeligmos">
         exeligmos
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        <i>
         Greek
        </i>
       </a>
       : "turn of the wheel") cycle.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saros_series">
        Saros series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saros_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Saros series">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saros_136_animation.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="278" data-file-width="240" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Saros_136_animation.gif/220px-Saros_136_animation.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bf/Saros_136_animation.gif 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saros_136_animation.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Solar eclipses occurring near the Moon's descending node are given
         <i>
          even
         </i>
         saros series numbers. The first eclipse of each series starts at the southern limb of the Earth and the eclipse's path is shifted northward with each successive saros.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Each saros series starts with a partial eclipse (Sun first enters the end of the node), and each successive saros the path of the Moon is shifted either northward (when near the descending node) or southward (when near the ascending node) due to the fact that the saros is not an exact integer of draconic months (about one hour short). At some point, eclipses are no longer possible and the series terminates (Sun leaves the beginning of the node). An arbitrary solar saros series was designated as solar saros series 1 by compilers of eclipse statistics. This series has finished, but the eclipse of November 16, 1990 BC (
       <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">
        Julian calendar
       </a>
       ) for example is in solar saros series 1. There are different saros series for solar and lunar eclipses. For lunar saros series, the lunar eclipse occurring 58.5 synodic months earlier (February 23, 1994 BC) was assigned the number 1. If there is an eclipse one
       <a href="/wiki/Inex" title="Inex">
        inex
       </a>
       (29 years minus about 20 days) after an eclipse of a particular saros series then it is a member of the next series. For example, the eclipse of October 26, 1961 BC is in solar saros series 2. Saros series, of course, went on before these dates, and it is necessary to extend the saros series numbers backwards to negative numbers even just to accommodate eclipses occurring in the years following 2000 BCE (up till the last eclipse with a negative saros number in 1367 BC). For solar eclipses the statistics for the complete saros series within the era between 2000 BCE and 3000 CE are given in this article's references.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meeus-Mathematical_Astronomy_Morsels_III_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Meeus-Mathematical_Astronomy_Morsels_III-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Espenak-Five_Millennium_Canon_of_Solar_Eclipses_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Espenak-Five_Millennium_Canon_of_Solar_Eclipses-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It takes between 1226 and 1550 years for the members of a saros series to traverse the Earth's surface from north to south (or vice versa). These extremes allow from 69 to 87 eclipses in each series (most series have 71 or 72 eclipses). From 39 to 59 (mostly about 43) eclipses in a given series will be central (that is, total, annular, or hybrid annular-total). At any given time, approximately 40 different saros series will be in progress.
      </p>
      <p>
       Saros series, as mentioned, are numbered according to the type of eclipse (lunar or solar).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In odd numbered series (of either kind) the sun is near the ascending node, whereas in even numbered series it is near the descending node. Generally, the ordering of these series determines the time at which each series peaks, which corresponds to when an eclipse is closest to one of the lunar nodes. For solar eclipses, the 40 series numbered between
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Saros_117" title="Solar Saros 117">
        117
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Saros_156" title="Solar Saros 156">
        156
       </a>
       are active (series 117 will end in 2054), whereas for lunar eclipses, there are now 41 active saros series (these numbers can be derived by counting the number of eclipses listed over an 18-year (saros) period from the eclipse catalog sites
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_Solar_eclipses_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA_Solar_eclipses-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_lunar_eclipses_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA_lunar_eclipses-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>

This eclipse series began in AD 1427 with a partial eclipse at the southern edge of the Earth's shadow when the Moon was close to its descending node. In each successive saros, the Moon's orbital path is shifted northward with respect to the Earth's shadow, with the first total eclipse occurring in 1950. For the following 252 years, total eclipses occur, with the central eclipse in 2078. The first partial eclipse after this will occur in the year 2220, and the final partial eclipse of the series will occur in 2707. The total lifetime of lunar saros series 131 is 1280 years.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because of the ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
       </span>

For the lunar saros series 131, the first total eclipse of 1950 had its best visibility for viewers in Eastern Europe and the Middle East because mid-eclipse was at 20:44 UT. The following eclipse in the series occurred about 8 hours later in the day with mid-eclipse at 4:47 UT, and was best seen from North America and South America. The third total eclipse occurred about 8 hours later in the day than the second eclipse with mid-eclipse at 12:43 UT, and had its best visibility for viewers in the Western Pacific, East Asia, Australia and New Zealand. This cycle of visibility repeats from the start to the end of the series, with minor variations.

      <h2>
       <span id="Relationship_between_lunar_and_solar_saros_.28sar.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_between_lunar_and_solar_saros_(sar)">
        <span id="sar">
        </span>
        Relationship between lunar and solar saros (sar)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saros_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationship between lunar and solar saros (sar)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       After a given lunar or solar eclipse, after 9 years and 5.5 days (a half saros) an eclipse will occur that is lunar instead of solar, or vice versa, with similar properties.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, if the moon's penumbra partially covers the southern limb of the earth during a solar eclipse, 9 years and 5.5 days later a lunar eclipse will occur in which the moon is partially covered by the southern limb of the earth's penumbra. Likewise, 9 years and 5.5 days after a total solar eclipse occurs, a total lunar eclipse will also occur. This 9-year period is referred to as a
       <b>
        sar
       </b>
       . It includes 111.5 synodic months, or 111 synodic months plus one
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_cycle#Eclipse_cycles" title="Eclipse cycle">
        fortnight
       </a>
       . The fortnight accounts for the alternation between solar and lunar eclipse. For a visual example see
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_season#Examples" title="Eclipse season">
         this chart
        </a>
       </i>
       (each row is one sar apart).
      </p>
