         <div class="magnify">

         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
          Sun
         </a>
         <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_motion" title="Diurnal motion">
          appears to move
         </a>
         with respect to the
         <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">
          fixed stars
         </a>
         , as seen from the orbiting
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         . The ecliptic is the yearly path the Sun appears to follow on the
         <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
          celestial sphere
         </a>
         . This process repeats itself in a cycle lasting a little over
         <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">
          365 days
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        ecliptic
       </b>
       is the circular path on the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       that the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       appears to follow over the course of a
       <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">
        year
       </a>
       ; it is the basis of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">
        ecliptic coordinate system
       </a>
       . The term also refers to the
       <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_plane_(spherical_coordinates)" title="Fundamental plane (spherical coordinates)">
        plane
       </a>
       of this path, which is
       <a href="/wiki/Coplanarity" title="Coplanarity">
        coplanar
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">
        Earth's orbit
       </a>
       around the Sun (and hence the Sun's apparent orbit around
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA2010_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AA2010-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The ecliptic is not normally noticeable from Earth's surface because
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
        Earth rotates
       </a>
       , carrying the observer through the cycles of
       <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">
        sunrise
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">
        sunset
       </a>
       , which obscure the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_motion" title="Diurnal motion">
        apparent motion
       </a>
       against the background of
       <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">
        fixed stars
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id="Sun.27s_apparent_motion">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sun's_apparent_motion">
        Sun's apparent motion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecliptic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sun's apparent motion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The motions as described above are simplifications. Due to the movement of Earth around the Earth–Moon
       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
        center of mass
       </a>
       , the apparent path of the Sun wobbles slightly, with a period of about
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_moon" title="Orbit of the moon">
        one month
       </a>
       . Due to further
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        perturbations
       </a>
       by the other
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       , the Earth–Moon
       <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">
        barycenter
       </a>
       wobbles slightly around a mean position in a complex fashion. The
       <b>
        ecliptic
       </b>
       is actually the apparent path of the Sun throughout the course of a
       <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">
        year
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because Earth takes one year to orbit the Sun, the apparent position of the Sun takes one year to make a complete circuit of the ecliptic. With slightly more than 365 days in one year, the Sun moves a little less than 1° eastward
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-celes_direc_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-celes_direc-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       every day. This small difference in the Sun's position against the stars causes any particular spot on Earth's surface to catch up with (and stand directly north or south of) the Sun about four minutes later each day than it would if Earth would not orbit; a day on Earth is therefore 24 hours long rather than the approximately 23-hour 56-minute
       <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_time" title="Sidereal time">
        sidereal day
       </a>
       . Again, this is a simplification, based on a hypothetical Earth that orbits at uniform speed around the Sun. The actual speed with which Earth orbits the Sun varies slightly during the year, so the speed with which the Sun seems to move along the ecliptic also varies. For example, the Sun is north of the celestial equator for about 185 days of each year, and south of it for about 180 days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The variation of orbital speed accounts for part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_time" title="Equation of time">
        equation of time
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_the_celestial_equator">
        Relationship to the celestial equator
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecliptic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relationship to the celestial equator">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        Axial tilt
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earths_orbit_and_ecliptic.PNG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="926" data-file-width="906" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Earths_orbit_and_ecliptic.PNG/260px-Earths_orbit_and_ecliptic.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Earths_orbit_and_ecliptic.PNG/390px-Earths_orbit_and_ecliptic.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Earths_orbit_and_ecliptic.PNG/520px-Earths_orbit_and_ecliptic.PNG 2x" width="260"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earths_orbit_and_ecliptic.PNG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">
          plane
         </a>
         of
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         's
         <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
          orbit
         </a>
         projected in all directions forms the reference plane known as the ecliptic. Here, it is shown projected outward (gray) to the
         <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
          celestial sphere
         </a>
         , along with Earth's
         <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
          equator
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
          polar axis
         </a>
         (green). The plane of the ecliptic intersects the celestial sphere along a
         <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">
          great circle
         </a>
         (black), the same circle on which the Sun seems to move as Earth orbits it. The intersections of the ecliptic and the equator on the celestial sphere are the vernal and autumnal
         <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
          equinoxes
         </a>
         (red), where the Sun seems to cross the celestial equator.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Because
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
        Earth's rotational axis
       </a>
       is not
       <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">
        perpendicular
       </a>
       to its
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy)" title="Orbital plane (astronomy)">
        orbital plane
       </a>
       , Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equatorial plane
       </a>
       is not
       <a href="/wiki/Coplanarity" title="Coplanarity">
        coplanar
       </a>
       with the ecliptic plane, but is inclined to it by an angle of about 23.4°, which is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        obliquity of the ecliptic
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If the equator is projected outward to the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       , forming the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">
        celestial equator
       </a>
       , it crosses the ecliptic at two points known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinoxes
       </a>
       . The Sun, in its apparent motion along the ecliptic, crosses the celestial equator at these points, one from south to north, the other from north to south.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-celes_direc_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-celes_direc-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The crossing from south to north is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        vernal equinox
       </a>
       , also known as the
       <i>
        first point of Aries
       </i>
       and the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Orbital_node" title="Orbital node">
         ascending node
        </a>
        of the ecliptic
       </i>
       on the celestial equator.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The crossing from north to south is the
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        autumnal equinox
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_node" title="Orbital node">
        descending node
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axial_precession_(astronomy)" title="Axial precession (astronomy)">
        Axial precession (astronomy)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The orientation of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
        Earth's axis
       </a>
       and equator are not fixed in space, but rotate about the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecliptic_pole" title="Ecliptic pole">
        poles of the ecliptic
       </a>
       with a period of about 26,000 years, a process known as
       <i>
        lunisolar
        <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
         precession
        </a>
       </i>
       , as it is due mostly to the gravitational effect of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">
        Earth's equatorial bulge
       </a>
       . Likewise, the ecliptic itself is not fixed. The gravitational
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        perturbations
       </a>
       of the other bodies of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       cause a much smaller motion of the plane of Earth's orbit, and hence of the ecliptic, known as
       <i>
        planetary
        <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
         precession
        </a>
       </i>
       . The combined action of these two motions is called
       <i>
        general
        <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
         precession
        </a>
       </i>
       , and changes the position of the equinoxes by about 50
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">
        arc seconds
       </a>
       (about 0.014°) per year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_nutation" title="Astronomical nutation">
        Astronomical nutation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Once again, this is a simplification. Periodic motions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and apparent periodic motions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       (actually of Earth in its orbit) cause short-term small-amplitude periodic oscillations of Earth's axis, and hence the celestial equator, known as
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_nutation" title="Astronomical nutation">
        nutation
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This adds a periodic component to the position of the equinoxes; the positions of the celestial equator and (vernal) equinox with fully updated precession and nutation are called the
       <i>
        true equator and equinox
       </i>
       ; the positions without nutation are the
       <i>
        mean equator and equinox
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Obliquity_of_the_ecliptic">
        Obliquity of the ecliptic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecliptic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Obliquity of the ecliptic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt#Earth" title="Axial tilt">
        Axial tilt § Earth
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Obliquity of the ecliptic is the term used by astronomers for the inclination of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       with respect to the ecliptic, or of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
        Earth's rotation axis
       </a>
       to a
       <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">
        perpendicular
       </a>
       to the ecliptic. It is about 23.4° and is currently decreasing 0.013 degrees (47 arcseconds) per hundred years due to planetary
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        perturbations
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The angular value of the obliquity is found by observation of the motions of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        other planets
       </a>
       over many years. Astronomers produce new
       <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_ephemeris" title="Fundamental ephemeris">
        fundamental ephemerides
       </a>
       as the accuracy of
       <a href="/wiki/Observational_astronomy" title="Observational astronomy">
        observation
       </a>
       improves and as the understanding of the
       <a href="/wiki/Analytical_dynamics" title="Analytical dynamics">
        dynamics
       </a>
       increases, and from these ephemerides various astronomical values, including the obliquity, are derived.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Obliquity_of_the_ecliptic_laskar.PNG">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">

       <a href="/wiki/Newcomb%27s_Tables_of_the_Sun" title="Newcomb's Tables of the Sun">
        work of Newcomb
       </a>
       , who analyzed positions of the planets until about 1895:
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="texhtml">
        <i>
         ε
        </i>

        <i>

        − 0″.0059
        <i>

        <sup>

       where

       <span class="texhtml">

       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">

       <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory_Development_Ephemeris" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory Development Ephemeris">
        Jet Propulsion Laboratory's DE series
       </a>
       of computer-generated ephemerides took over as the
       <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_ephemeris" title="Fundamental ephemeris">

      </p>

       These expressions for the obliquity are intended for high precision over a relatively short time span, perhaps
       <span class="texhtml">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">

      </p>

       All of these expressions are for the
       <i>
        mean
       </i>
       obliquity, that is, without the nutation of the equator included. The
       <i>
        true

       or instantaneous obliquity includes the nutation.

        <a href="#cite_note-17">

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plane_of_the_Solar_System">

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">

       </a>
      </div>

      <p>
       Most of the major bodies of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       orbit the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       in nearly the same plane. This is likely due to the way in which the Solar System formed from a
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">
        protoplanetary disk
       </a>
       . Probably the closest current representation of the disk is known as the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Invariable_plane" title="Invariable plane">
         invariable plane
        </a>
        of the Solar System
       </i>
       . Earth's orbit, and hence, the ecliptic, is inclined a little more than 1° to the invariable plane, and the other major planets are also within about 6° of it. Because of this, most Solar System bodies appear very close to the ecliptic in the sky. The ecliptic is well defined by the motion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       . The invariable plane is defined by the
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       of the entire Solar System, essentially the
       <a href="/wiki/Summation" title="Summation">
        summation
       </a>
       of all of the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbital
       </a>
       motions and
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation#Astronomy" title="Rotation">
        rotations
       </a>
       of all the bodies of the system, a somewhat uncertain value that requires precise knowledge of every object in the system. For these reasons, the ecliptic is used as the reference plane of the Solar System out of convenience.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div style="clear:both;">
      </div>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Celestial_reference_plane">
        Celestial reference plane
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecliptic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Celestial reference plane">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">
        Celestial equator
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">
        Ecliptic coordinate system
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ecliptic_vs_equator_small.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="359" data-file-width="300" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Ecliptic_vs_equator_small.gif/220px-Ecliptic_vs_equator_small.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a3/Ecliptic_vs_equator_small.gif 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ecliptic_vs_equator_small.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The apparent motion of the
         <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
          Sun
         </a>
         along the ecliptic (red) as seen on the inside of the
         <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
          celestial sphere
         </a>
         .
         <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">
          Ecliptic coordinates
         </a>
         appear in (red). The
         <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">
          celestial equator
         </a>
         (blue) and the
         <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_coordinate_system" title="Equatorial coordinate system">
          equatorial coordinates
         </a>
         (blue), being inclined to the ecliptic, appear to wobble as the Sun advances.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The ecliptic forms one of the two fundamental
       <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">
        planes
       </a>
       used as reference for positions on the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       , the other being the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">
        celestial equator
       </a>
       . Perpendicular to the ecliptic are the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecliptic_pole" title="Ecliptic pole">
        ecliptic poles
       </a>
       , the north ecliptic pole being the pole north of the equator. Of the two fundamental planes, the ecliptic is closer to unmoving against the background stars, its motion due to planetary
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        precession
       </a>
       being roughly 1/100 that of the celestial equator.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-montenbruck_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-montenbruck-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system" title="Spherical coordinate system">
        Spherical coordinates
       </a>
       , known as ecliptic longitude and latitude or celestial longitude and latitude, are used to specify positions of bodies on the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       with respect to the ecliptic. Longitude is measured positively eastward
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-celes_direc_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-celes_direc-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       0° to 360° along the ecliptic from the vernal equinox, the same direction in which the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       appears to move. Latitude is measured perpendicular to the ecliptic, to +90° northward or -90° southward to the poles of the ecliptic, the ecliptic itself being 0° latitude. For a complete spherical position, a distance parameter is also necessary. Different distance units are used for different objects. Within the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical units
       </a>
       are used, and for objects near
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">
        Earth radii
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">
        kilometers
       </a>
       are used. A corresponding right-handed
       <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">
        rectangular coordinate system
       </a>
       is also used occasionally; the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       -axis is directed toward the vernal equinox, the
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       -axis 90° to the east, and the
       <i>
        z
       </i>
       -axis toward the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecliptic_pole" title="Ecliptic pole">
        north ecliptic pole
       </a>
       ; the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical unit
       </a>
       is the unit of measure. Symbols for ecliptic coordinates are somewhat standardized; see the table.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable" style="float: left;margin:0em 1em .5em 0em;">
       <caption>
        <br/>
        <b>

      <p>
       Ecliptic coordinates are convenient for specifying positions of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       objects, as most of the planets'
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbits
       </a>
       have small
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclinations
       </a>
       to the ecliptic, and therefore always appear relatively close to it on the sky. Because Earth's orbit, and hence the ecliptic, moves very little, it is a relatively fixed reference with respect to the stars.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ecliptic_inclination_dziobek.PNG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="825" data-file-width="1213" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Ecliptic_inclination_dziobek.PNG/290px-Ecliptic_inclination_dziobek.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Ecliptic_inclination_dziobek.PNG/435px-Ecliptic_inclination_dziobek.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Ecliptic_inclination_dziobek.PNG/580px-Ecliptic_inclination_dziobek.PNG 2x" width="290"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ecliptic_inclination_dziobek.PNG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Inclination of the ecliptic over 200,000 years, from Dziobek (1892).
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
          <a href="#cite_note-24">
           [23]
          </a>
         </sup>
         This is the inclination to the ecliptic of 101,800 CE. Note that the ecliptic rotates by only about 7° during this time, whereas the
         <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">
          celestial equator
         </a>
         makes several complete cycles around the ecliptic. The ecliptic is a relatively stable reference compared to the celestial equator.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Because of the
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        precessional motion of the equinox
       </a>
       , the ecliptic coordinates of objects on the celestial sphere are continuously changing. Specifying a position in ecliptic coordinates requires specifying a particular equinox, that is, the equinox of a particular date, known as an
       <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy)" title="Epoch (astronomy)">
        epoch
       </a>
       ; the coordinates are referred to the direction of the equinox at that date. For instance, the
       <i>
        Astronomical Almanac
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       lists the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliocentric#Modern_use_of_geocentric_and_heliocentric" title="Heliocentric">
        heliocentric
       </a>
       position of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       at 0h
       <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_Time" title="Terrestrial Time">
        Terrestrial Time
       </a>
       , 4 Jan 2010 as: longitude 118° 09' 15".8, latitude +1° 43' 16".7, true heliocentric distance 1.6302454 AU,
       <b>
        mean equinox and ecliptic of date
       </b>
       . This specifies the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic#Relationship_to_the_equator" title="Ecliptic">
        mean equinox
       </a>
       of 4 Jan 2010 0h TT
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic#Relationship_to_the_equator" title="Ecliptic">
        as above
       </a>
       , without the addition of nutation.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Eclipses">
        Eclipses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecliptic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eclipses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        Eclipse
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Because the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        orbit of the Moon
       </a>
       is inclined only about 5.145° to the ecliptic and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       is always very near the ecliptic,
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipses
       </a>
       always occur on or near it. Because of the inclination of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       , eclipses do not occur at every
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy_and_astrology)" title="Conjunction (astronomy and astrology)">
        conjunction
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(planets)" title="Opposition (planets)">
        opposition
       </a>
       of the Sun and Moon, but only when the Moon is near an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_node" title="Orbital node">
        ascending or descending node
       </a>
       at the same time it is at conjunction or opposition. The ecliptic is so named because the ancients noted that eclipses only occurred when the Moon crossed it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The exact instants of equinoxes or solstices are the times when the apparent ecliptic longitude (including the effects of
       <a href="/wiki/Aberration_of_light" title="Aberration of light">
        aberration
       </a>
       and nutation) of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       is 0°, 90°, 180°, or 270°. Because of
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        perturbations
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">
        Earth's orbit
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        peculiarities of the calendar
       </a>
       , the dates of these are not fixed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astrology">
        Astrology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecliptic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Astrology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">
        Astrology
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The ecliptic forms the center of a band about 20° wide called the
       <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">
        zodiac
       </a>
       , on which the Sun, Moon, and planets are seen always to move.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Traditionally, this region is divided into 12
       <a href="/wiki/Astrological_sign" title="Astrological sign">
        signs
       </a>
       of 30° longitude, each of which approximates the Sun's motion through one month.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In ancient times the signs corresponded roughly to 12 of the constellations that straddle the ecliptic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These signs give us some of the terminology used today. The
       <i>
        first point of Aries
       </i>
       was named when the vernal equinox was actually in the constellation Aries; it has since moved into Pisces.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
