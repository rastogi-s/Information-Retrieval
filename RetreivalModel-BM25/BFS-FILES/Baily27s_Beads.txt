      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Baily's beads
       </b>
       effect, or
       <b>
        diamond ring
       </b>
       effect, is a feature of total and annular
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       . As the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       covers the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       during a solar eclipse, the rugged topography of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_limb" title="Lunar limb">
        lunar limb
       </a>
       allows beads of
       <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
        sunlight
       </a>
       to shine through in some places while not in others. The effect is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Francis_Baily" title="Francis Baily">
        Francis Baily
       </a>
       , who explained the phenomenon in 1836.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The diamond ring effect is seen when only one bead is left, appearing as a shining "diamond" set in a bright ring around the lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Silhouette" title="Silhouette">
        silhouette
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Topography_of_the_Moon" title="Topography of the Moon">
        Lunar topography
       </a>
       has considerable
       <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">
        relief
       </a>
       because of the presence of
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_mountains_on_the_Moon" title="List of mountains on the Moon">
        mountains
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        craters
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_valleys_on_the_Moon" title="List of valleys on the Moon">
        valleys
       </a>
       , and other topographical features. The irregularities of the lunar limb profile (the "edge" of the Moon, as seen from a distance) are known accurately from observations of grazing
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occultations
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       . Astronomers thus have a fairly good idea which mountains and valleys will cause the beads to appear in advance of the eclipse. While Baily's beads are seen briefly for a few seconds at the center of the eclipse path, their duration is maximized near the edges of the path of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
        umbra
       </a>
       , lasting 1–2 minutes.
      </p>
      <p>
       After the diamond ring effect has diminished, the subsequent Baily's beads effect and totality phase are safe to view without the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_filter#_Solar_filter" title="Astronomical filter">
        solar filters
       </a>
       used during the partial phases. By then, less than 0.001% of the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       is visible.
      </p>
      <p>
       Observers in the path of totality of a solar eclipse see first a gradual covering of the Sun by the lunar silhouette for over an hour, followed by the diamond ring effect (visible without filters) as the last bit of photosphere disappears. As the burst of light from the ring fades, Bailey's beads appear as the last bits of the bright photosphere shine through valleys aligned at the edge of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As the Baily’s beads disappear behind the advancing lunar edge (the beads also reappear at the end of totality), a thin reddish edge called the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       (the Greek
       <i>
        chromos
       </i>
       meaning "color") appears. Though the reddish hydrogen radiation is most visible to the
       <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">
        unaided eye
       </a>
       , the chromosphere also emits thousands of additional
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
        spectral lines
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baily%27s_beads&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observational history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although Baily is often said to have discovered the cause of the feature which bears his name, Sir
       <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Halley" title="Edmond Halley">
        Edmond Halley
       </a>
       made the first recorded observations of Baily's beads during the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_May_3,_1715" title="Solar eclipse of May 3, 1715">
        solar eclipse of 3 May 1715
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pasa_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pasa-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [Note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Halley described and correctly ascertained the cause of the effect
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pasa_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-pasa-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in his "Observations of the late Total Eclipse of the Sun[...]" in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_Transactions_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society">
         Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society
        </a>
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        About two Minutes before the Total Immersion, the remaining part of the Sun was reduced to a very fine Horn, whose Extremeties seemed to lose their Acuteness, and to become round like Stars ... which Appearance could proceed from no other Cause but the Inequalities of the Moon's Surface, there being some elevated parts thereof near the Moon's Southern Pole, by whose Interposition part of that exceedingly fine Filament of Light was intercepted.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pasa_6-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-pasa-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_media">
        In media
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baily%27s_beads&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In media">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmas_Damian_Asam" title="Cosmas Damian Asam">
        Cosmas Damian Asam
       </a>
       was probably the earliest realistic painter to depict a total solar eclipse and diamond ring.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His painting was finished in 1735.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Baily's beads phenomenon is seen during the credit opening sequence of the NBC TV show
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Heroes_(TV_series)" title="Heroes (TV series)">
         Heroes
        </a>
       </i>
       , while the Diamond Ring effect is seen during the credit opening sequence of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_Voyager" title="Star Trek: Voyager">
         Star Trek: Voyager
        </a>
       </i>
       , albeit from a fictitious extrasolar body, seen from space.
      </p>
