      <p>
       <b>
        Einstein Observatory
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        HEAO-2
       </b>
       ) was the first fully imaging
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-ray
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       put into space and the second of
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's three High Energy Astrophysical Observatories. Named
       <b>
        HEAO B
       </b>
       before launch, the observatory's name was changed to honor
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Albert Einstein
       </a>
       upon its successfully attaining orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfa-harvard_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cfa-harvard-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">
        Launch
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Einstein_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Launch">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Einstein Observatory, HEAO-2, was launched on November 13, 1978, from
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral,_Florida" title="Cape Canaveral, Florida">
        Cape Canaveral, Florida
       </a>
       , on an Atlas-Centaur SLV-3D booster rocket into a near-circular orbit with an initial altitude slightly above 500 km. Its orbital inclination orbit was 23.5 degrees. The Einstein Observatory satellite re-entered the Earth's atmosphere and burned up on March 25, 1982.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecuip-lib_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ecuip-lib-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation">
        Instrumentation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Einstein_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Instrumentation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Einstein Observatory carried a single large
       <a href="/wiki/Wolter_telescope" title="Wolter telescope">
        grazing-incidence
       </a>
       focusing X-ray telescope that provided unprecedented levels of sensitivity (hundreds of times better than previously achieved) and arc-second angular resolution of point sources and extended objects. It had instruments sensitive in the 0.2 to 3.5
       <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">
        keV
       </a>
       energy range. A collection of four focal-plane instruments was installed in the satellite:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adsabs-harvard_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-adsabs-harvard-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        HRI, or High Resolution Imaging camera, 0.15-3 keV
       </li>
       <li>
        IPC, or Imaging Proportional Counter, 0.4 to 4 keV
       </li>
       <li>
        SSS, or Solid State Spectrometer, 0.5 to 4.5 keV
       </li>
       <li>
        FPCS, or Bragg Focal Plane Crystal Spectrometer
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       There was also a coaxial instrument 'MPC', the Monitor Proportional Counter, working in the 1-20 keV range, and two filters that could be used with the imaging detectors:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        BBFS, Broad Band Filter Spectrometer (aluminium and beryllium filters than can be placed into the X-ray beam, to change the spectral sensitivity)
       </li>
       <li>
        OGS, Objective grating spectrometer (transmission gratings with a spectral resolution of about 50)
       </li>
      </ul>
