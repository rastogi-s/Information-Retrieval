    Earthlight (astronomy)
   </h1>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earthshine_Moon.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="1805" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Earthshine_Moon.jpg/220px-Earthshine_Moon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Earthshine_Moon.jpg/330px-Earthshine_Moon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Earthshine_Moon.jpg/440px-Earthshine_Moon.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earthshine_Moon.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         When the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         is at
         <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
          crescent phase
         </a>
         , the darker side is illuminated by indirect
         <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
          sunlight
         </a>
         reflected from Earth, while the other side by direct sunlight.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Earthlight
       </b>
       is the dim illumination of the otherwise dark side of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
        sunlight
       </a>
       reflected from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's surface and
       <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">
        clouds
       </a>
       . It is also known as
       <b>
        earthshine
       </b>
       (see also
       <a href="/wiki/Planetshine" title="Planetshine">
        planetshine
       </a>
       ) or the
       <b>
        Moon's ashen glow
       </b>
       . Earthlight on a waxing crescent is called "the old Moon in the new Moon's arms", while that on a waning crescent is called "the new Moon in the old Moon's arms".
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This phenomenon is most visible from Earth at
       <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">
        night
       </a>
       (or astronomical
       <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">
        twilight
       </a>
       ) a few days before or after the day of
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       when the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        lunar phase
       </a>
       is a thin crescent. On these nights, the entire lunar disk is both directly and indirectly sunlit, and is thus unevenly bright enough to see. Earthlight on a waxing crescent Moon is most clearly seen in the western sky after
       <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">
        dusk
       </a>
       , while that on a waning crescent Moon in the eastern sky before
       <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">
        dawn
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        earthlight
       </i>
       would also be suitable for an observer on the Moon seeing Earth during the lunar night, or for an
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        astronaut
       </a>
       inside a
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       looking out the window.
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
        Arthur C. Clarke
       </a>
       uses it in this sense in his novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Earthlight" title="Earthlight">
         Earthlight
        </a>
       </i>
       . The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
         Oxford English Dictionary
        </a>
       </i>
       recognizes the word.
      </p>
