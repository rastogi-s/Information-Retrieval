      <p>
       A
       <b>
        space probe
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">
        robotic spacecraft
       </a>
       that does not orbit the Earth, but, instead, explores further into
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">
        outer space
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A space probe may approach the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       ; travel through
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_space" title="Interplanetary space">
        interplanetary space
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_flyby" title="Planetary flyby">
        flyby
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       , or land on other
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">
        planetary
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">
        bodies
       </a>
       ; or enter
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_space" title="Interstellar space">
        interstellar space
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       See
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_active_Solar_System_probes" title="List of active Solar System probes">
        List of active Solar System probes
       </a>
       for a list of active probes; the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_agencies" title="Space agencies">
        space agencies
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_program" title="Soviet space program">
        USSR
       </a>
       (now
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federal_Space_Agency" title="Russian Federal Space Agency">
        Russia
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Space_Agency_of_Ukraine" title="National Space Agency of Ukraine">
        Ukraine
       </a>
       ), the
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        United States
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        European Union
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency">
        Japan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/China_National_Space_Administration" title="China National Space Administration">
        China
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Space_Research_Organisation" title="Indian Space Research Organisation">
        India
       </a>
       have in the aggregate launched probes to several
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moons" title="Moons">
        moons
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       as well as to a number of
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comets
       </a>
       . Approximately fifteen missions are currently operational.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interplanetary_trajectories">
        Interplanetary trajectories
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Interplanetary trajectories">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Once a probe has left the vicinity of Earth, its trajectory will likely take it along an
       <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">
        orbit around the
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       similar to the Earth's orbit. To reach another planet, the simplest practical method is a
       <a href="/wiki/Hohmann_transfer_orbit" title="Hohmann transfer orbit">
        Hohmann transfer orbit
       </a>
       . More complex techniques, such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_slingshot" title="Gravitational slingshot">
        gravitational slingshots
       </a>
       , can be more fuel-efficient, though they may require the probe to spend more time in transit. Some high
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta-V" title="Delta-V">
        Delta-V
       </a>
       missions (such as those with high
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination_change" title="Orbital inclination change">
        inclination changes
       </a>
       ) can only be performed, within the limits of modern propulsion, using gravitational slingshots. A technique using very little propulsion, but requiring a considerable amount of time, is to follow a trajectory on the
       <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_Transport_Network" title="Interplanetary Transport Network">
        Interplanetary Transport Network
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       is a 733-kilogram probe launched September 5, 1977. It is currently
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_probe&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       still operational, making it the longest-lasting mission of the U.S.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration" title="National Aeronautics and Space Administration">
        National Aeronautics and Space Administration
       </a>
       (NASA). It visited
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       and was the first probe to provide detailed images of the moons of these planets.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Voyager 1
       </i>
       is the farthest human-made object from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , traveling away from both the Earth and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       at a relatively faster speed than any other probe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of September 12, 2013,
       <i>
        Voyager 1
       </i>
       is about 12
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">
        billion
       </a>
       miles (19 billion kilometers) from the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On August 25, 2012,
       <i>
        Voyager 1
       </i>
       became the first human made object to enter interstellar space.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_Spacecraft_Embarks_on_Historic_Journey_Into_Interstellar_Space_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA_Spacecraft_Embarks_on_Historic_Journey_Into_Interstellar_Space-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Voyager 1
       </i>
       has not had a functioning plasma sensor since 1980, but a solar flare in 2012 allowed scientists from NASA to measure vibrations of the plasma surrounding the craft. The vibrations allowed scientists to measure the plasma to be much denser than measurements taken in the far layers of our
       <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">
        heliosphere
       </a>
       , thus concluding the craft had broken beyond the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">
        heliopause
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">
