      <p>
       The
       <b>
        moon rabbit
       </b>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">
        folklore
       </a>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">
        rabbit
       </a>
       that lives on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , based on
       <a href="/wiki/Pareidolia" title="Pareidolia">
        pareidolia
       </a>
       that identifies the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        markings
       </a>
       of the Moon as a rabbit. The folklore originated in China. Then the story spread to other cultures, prominently in
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asian
       </a>
       folklore and
       <a href="/wiki/Aztec_mythology" title="Aztec mythology">
        Aztec mythology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greathare_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-greathare-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In East Asia, it is seen pounding with a
       <a href="/wiki/Mortar_and_pestle" title="Mortar and pestle">
        mortar and pestle
       </a>
       , but the contents of the mortar differ among Chinese, Japanese, and Korean folklore. In
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_folklore" title="Chinese folklore">
        Chinese folklore
       </a>
       , it is often portrayed as a companion of the Moon goddess
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e" title="Chang'e">
        Chang'e
       </a>
       , constantly pounding the
       <a href="/wiki/Elixir_of_life" title="Elixir of life">
        elixir of life
       </a>
       for her; but in Japanese and Korean versions, it is pounding the ingredients for
       <a href="/wiki/Rice_cake" title="Rice cake">
        rice cake
       </a>
       . In some
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        Chinese
       </a>
       versions the rabbit pounds medicine for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mortals" title="Mortals">
        mortals
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_rabbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       An early mention that there is a rabbit on the moon appears in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Chu_Ci" title="Chu Ci">
         Chu Ci
        </a>
       </i>
       , a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Han" title="Western Han">
        Western Han
       </a>
       anthology of Chinese poems from the
       <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">
        Warring States period
       </a>
       , which notes that along with a
       <a href="/wiki/Toad" title="Toad">
        toad
       </a>
       , there is a rabbit on the Moon who constantly pounds herbs for the
       <a href="/wiki/Xian_(Taoism)" title="Xian (Taoism)">
        immortals
       </a>
       . This notion is supported by later texts, including the
       <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">
        Song
       </a>
       -era
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taiping_Imperial_Reader" title="Taiping Imperial Reader">
         Taiping Imperial Reader
        </a>
       </i>
       . Han Dynasty poets call the rabbit on the Moon the "Jade Rabbit" (玉兔) or the "Gold Rabbit" (金兔), and these phrases were often used in place of the word for the Moon. A famous poet of Tang China,
       <a href="/wiki/Li_Bai" title="Li Bai">
        Li Bai
       </a>
       , relates how "[t]he rabbit in the Moon pounds the medicine in vain" in his poem "The Old Dust."
      </p>

          </a>
         </i>
         .
        </div>

      <p>
       In the Buddhist
       <a href="/wiki/Jataka_tales" title="Jataka tales">
        Jataka tales
       </a>
       (Tale 316),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a monkey, an otter, a jackal, and a rabbit resolved to practice charity on the day of the full moon (
       <a href="/wiki/Uposatha" title="Uposatha">
        Uposatha
       </a>
       ), believing a demonstration of great virtue would earn a great reward. When an old man begged for food, the monkey gathered fruits from the trees and the otter collected fish, while the jackal wrongfully pilfered a lizard and a pot of milk-curd. The rabbit, who knew only how to gather grass, instead offered its own body, throwing itself into a fire the man had built. The rabbit, however, was not burnt. The old man revealed himself to be
       <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Aakra_(Buddhism)" title="Śakra (Buddhism)">
        Śakra
       </a>
       and, touched by the rabbit's virtue, drew the likeness of the rabbit on the Moon for all to see. It is said the lunar image is still draped in the smoke that rose when the rabbit cast itself into the fire. A version of this story can be found in the Japanese anthology
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Konjaku_Monogatarish%C5%AB" title="Konjaku Monogatarishū">
         Konjaku Monogatarishū
        </a>
       </i>
       , where the rabbit's companions are a fox, instead of a jackal, and a monkey. The legend is popular and part of local folklore throughout Asia in China, Japan, Korea, Sri Lanka, Thailand, Vietnam, Cambodia and Myanmar.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The legend also gave rise to the
       <a href="/wiki/Mid-Autumn_Festival" title="Mid-Autumn Festival">
        Mid-Autumn Festival
       </a>
       of China and Vietnam,
       <a href="/wiki/Tsukimi" title="Tsukimi">
        Tsukimi
       </a>
       of Japan and
       <a href="/wiki/Chuseok" title="Chuseok">
        Chuseok
       </a>
       of Korea which all celebrate the legend of the moon rabbit.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_American_folklore">
        Indigenous American folklore
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_rabbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Indigenous American folklore">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Similar legends occur in Mexican folklore, where people also identified the markings on the Moon as a rabbit. According to an
       <a href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">
        Aztec
       </a>
       legend, the god
       <a href="/wiki/Quetzalcoatl" title="Quetzalcoatl">
        Quetzalcoatl
       </a>
       , then living on Earth as a man, started on a journey and, after walking for a long time, became hungry and tired. With no food or water around, he thought he would die. Then a rabbit grazing nearby offered herself as food to save his life. Quetzalcoatl, moved by the rabbit's noble offering, elevated her to the Moon, then lowered her back to Earth and told her, "You may be just a rabbit, but everyone will remember you; there is your image in light, for all people and for all times."
      </p>
      <p>
       Another
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoamerican" title="Mesoamerican">
        Mesoamerican
       </a>
       legend tells of the brave and noble sacrifice of
       <a href="/wiki/Nanahuatzin" title="Nanahuatzin">
        Nanahuatzin
       </a>
       during the creation of the fifth sun. Humble Nanahuatzin sacrificed himself in fire to become the new sun, but the wealthy god
       <a href="/wiki/Tecciztecatl" title="Tecciztecatl">
        Tecciztecatl
       </a>
       hesitated four times before he finally set himself alight to become the Moon. Due to Tecciztecatl's cowardice, the gods felt that the Moon should not be as bright as the sun, so one of the gods threw a rabbit at his face to diminish his light. It is also said that Tecciztecatl was in the form of a rabbit when he sacrificed himself to become the Moon, casting his shadow there.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
        Native American
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Cree" title="Cree">
        Cree
       </a>
       ) legend tells a different variation, about a young rabbit who wished to ride the Moon. Only the
       <a href="/wiki/Crane_(bird)" title="Crane (bird)">
        crane
       </a>
       was willing to take him. The trip stretched the crane’s legs as the heavy rabbit held them tightly, leaving them elongated as cranes' legs are now. When they reached the Moon, the rabbit touched the crane’s head with a bleeding paw, leaving the red mark cranes wear to this day. According to the legend, on clear nights, Rabbit can still be seen riding the Moon.
      </p>

         Bowser
        </a>
        has hired as wedding planners for his scheme to marry
        <a href="/wiki/Princess_Peach" title="Princess Peach">
         Princess Peach
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In the video game
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Destiny_(video_game)" title="Destiny (video game)">
          Destiny
         </a>
        </i>
        , there is a weapon called
        <i>
         The Jade Rabbit
        </i>
        , which was created using parts from a former lunar colony within in-game lore, in reference to the myth.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        One of the celestial brush gods in
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ckami" title="Ōkami">
          Ōkami
         </a>
        </i>
        , Yumigami, is a rabbit who hid herself in the reflection of the moon on Agata Forest's lake's surface, which was swallowed by the legendary giant catfish Whopper. Instead of a mortar, Yumigami carries a mochi-pounding mallet, with which she makes mochi with
        <a href="/wiki/Amaterasu_(%C5%8Ckami)" title="Amaterasu (Ōkami)">
         Amaterasu
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
