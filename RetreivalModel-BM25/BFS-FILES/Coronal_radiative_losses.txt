   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Coronal radiative losses
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       and in
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
        astrophysics
       </a>
       , for
       <b>
        radiative losses of the solar corona
       </b>
       , it is meant the
       <a href="/wiki/Energy_flux" title="Energy flux">
        energy flux
       </a>
       irradiated from the external
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       (traditionally divided into
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_transition_region" title="Solar transition region">
        transition region
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       ), and, in particular, the processes of production of the
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       coming from the solar corona and transition region, where the plasma is optically-thin. On the contrary, in the chromosphere, where the temperature decreases from the photospheric value of 6000 K to the minimum of 4400 K, the
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">
        optical depth
       </a>
       is about 1, and the radiation is thermal.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Latest_xrt_soft_x-ray.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1024" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Latest_xrt_soft_x-ray.gif/320px-Latest_xrt_soft_x-ray.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Latest_xrt_soft_x-ray.gif/480px-Latest_xrt_soft_x-ray.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Latest_xrt_soft_x-ray.gif/640px-Latest_xrt_soft_x-ray.gif 2x" width="320"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Latest_xrt_soft_x-ray.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The Sun in the soft X-rays as seen by the
         <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">
          Hinode
         </a>
         X-ray Telescope (XRT) on 15 Oct 2009.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       extends much further than a solar radius from the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       and looks very complex and inhomogeneous in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-rays" title="X-rays">
        X-rays
       </a>
       images taken by satellites (see the figure on the right taken by the XRT on board
       <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">
        Hinode
       </a>
       ). The structure and dynamics of the
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       are dominated by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_magnetic_field" title="Solar magnetic field">
        solar magnetic field
       </a>
       . There are strong evidences that even the heating mechanism, responsible for its high temperature of million degrees, is linked to the magnetic field of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Energy_flux" title="Energy flux">
        energy flux
       </a>
       irradiated from the corona changes in
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stellar_active_region&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stellar active region (page does not exist)">
        active regions
       </a>
       , in the quiet Sun and in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_holes" title="Coronal holes">
        coronal holes
       </a>
       ; actually, part of the energy is irradiated outwards, but approximately the same amount of the energy flux is conducted back towards the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       , through the steep
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_transition_region" title="Solar transition region">
        transition region
       </a>
       . In active regions the energy flux is about 10
       <sup>
        7
       </sup>
       erg cm
       <sup>
        −2
       </sup>
       sec
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       , in the quiet Sun it is roughly 8 10
       <sup>
        5
       </sup>
       – 10
       <sup>
        6
       </sup>
       erg cm
       <sup>
        −2
       </sup>
       sec
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       , and in coronal holes 5 10
       <sup>
        5
       </sup>
       - 8 10
       <sup>
        5
       </sup>
       erg cm
       <sup>
        −2
       </sup>
       sec
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       , including the losses due to the solar wind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The required power is a small fraction of the total flux irradiated from the Sun, but this energy is enough to maintain the plasma at the temperature of million degrees, since the density is very low and the processes of radiation are different from those occurring in the photosphere, as it is shown in detail in the next section.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Processes_of_radiation_of_the_solar_corona">
        Processes of radiation of the solar corona
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_radiative_losses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Processes of radiation of the solar corona">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e_it.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2200" data-file-width="3000" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e_it.png/320px-EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e_it.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e_it.png/480px-EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e_it.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e_it.png/640px-EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e_it.png 2x" width="320"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e_it.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">
          effective temperature
         </a>
         of the Sun. The grey area represents the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody_radiation" title="Blackbody radiation">
          blackbody radiation
         </a>
         with the same radiative flux of the solar spectrum (yellow area).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The electromagnetic waves coming from the solar
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       are emitted mainly in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-rays" title="X-rays">
        X-rays
       </a>
       . This radiation is not visible from the Earth because it is filtered by the atmosphere. Before the first rocket missions, the corona could be observed only in white light during the eclipses, while in the last fifty years the solar corona has been photographed in the EUV and X-rays by many satellites (
       <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_program" title="Pioneer program">
        Pioneer
       </a>
       5, 6, 7, 8, 9,
       <a href="/wiki/Helios" title="Helios">
        Helios
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">
        Skylab
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Maximum_Mission" title="Solar Maximum Mission">
        SMM
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/NIXT" title="NIXT">
        NIXT
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">
        Yohkoh
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
        TRACE
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">
        Hinode
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The emitting plasma is almost completely ionized and very light, its density is about 10
       <sup>
        −16
       </sup>
       - 10
       <sup>
        −14
       </sup>
       g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       . Particles are so isolated that almost all the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">
        photons
       </a>
       can leave the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's surface without interacting with the matter above the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       : in other words, the corona is transparent to the radiation and the emission of the
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       is optically-thin. The Sun's atmosphere is not the unique example of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_X-ray_source" title="Astronomical X-ray source">
        X-ray source
       </a>
       , since hot plasmas are present wherever in the Universe: from stellar coronae to thin
       <a href="/wiki/Galactic_halo" title="Galactic halo">
        galactic halos
       </a>
       . These stellar environments are the subject of the
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_astronomy" title="X-ray astronomy">
        X-ray astronomy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In an optically-thin plasma the matter is not in thermodynamical equilibrium with the radiation, because collisions between particles and photons are very rare, and, as a matter of fact, the square root mean velocity of photons, electrons, protons and ions is not the same: we should define a temperature for each of these particle populations. The result is that the
       <a href="/wiki/Emission_spectrum" title="Emission spectrum">
        emission spectrum
       </a>
       does not fit the spectral distribution of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody_radiation" title="Blackbody radiation">
        blackbody radiation
       </a>
       , but it depends only on those collisional processes which occur in a very rarefied plasma.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FraunhoferLinesDiagram.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="165" data-file-width="381" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/FraunhoferLinesDiagram.jpg/320px-FraunhoferLinesDiagram.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9f/FraunhoferLinesDiagram.jpg 1.5x" width="320"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FraunhoferLinesDiagram.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines" title="Fraunhofer lines">
          Fraunhofer lines
         </a>
         in the solar spectrum.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       While the
       <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines" title="Fraunhofer lines">
        Fraunhofer lines
       </a>
       coming from the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_lines" title="Absorption lines">
        absorption lines
       </a>
       , principally emitted from ions which absorb photons of the same frequency of the transition to an upper energy level, coronal lines are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_lines" title="Emission lines">
        emission lines
       </a>
       produced by metal ions which had been excited to a superior state by collisional processes. Many spectral lines are emitted by highly ionized atoms, like calcium and iron, which have lost most of their external electrons; these emission lines can be formed only at certain temperatures, and therefore their individuation in solar
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        spectra
       </a>
       is sufficient to determine the temperature of the emitting plasma.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some of these spectral lines can be forbidden on the Earth: in fact, collisions between particles can excite ions to metastable states; in a dense gas these ions immediately collide with other particles and so they de-excite with an allowed transition to an intermediate level, while in the corona it is more probable that this ion remains in its metastable state, until it encounters a photon of the same frequency of the forbidden transition to the lower state. This photon induces the ion to emit with the same frequency by
       <a href="/wiki/Stimulated_emission" title="Stimulated emission">
        stimulated emission
       </a>
       . Forbidden transitions from metastable states are often called as satellite lines.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        Spectroscopy
       </a>
       of the corona allows the determination of many physical parameters of the emitting plasma. Comparing the
       <a href="/wiki/Luminous_intensity" title="Luminous intensity">
        intensity
       </a>
       in lines of different ions of the same element, temperature and density can be measured with a good approximation: the different states of ionization are regulated by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saha_equation" title="Saha equation">
        Saha equation
       </a>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">
        Doppler shift
       </a>
       gives a good measurement of the velocities along the
       <a href="/wiki/Line-of-sight_propagation" title="Line-of-sight propagation">
        line of sight
       </a>
       but not in the perpendicular plane. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Line_width" title="Line width">
        line width
       </a>
       should depend on the
       <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution" title="Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution">
        Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution
       </a>
       of velocities at the temperature of line formation (thermal line broadening), while it is often larger than predicted. The widening can be due to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_broadening" title="Pressure broadening">
        pressure broadening
       </a>
       , when collisions between particles are frequent, or it can be due to
       <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
        turbulence
       </a>
       : in this case the line width can be used to estimate the macroscopic velocity also on the Sun's surface, but with a great uncertainty. The magnetic field can be measured thanks to the line splitting due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Zeeman_effect" title="Zeeman effect">
        Zeeman effect
       </a>
       .
      </p>
