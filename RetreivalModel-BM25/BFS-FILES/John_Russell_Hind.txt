      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_work">
        Life and work
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Russell_Hind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and work">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       John Russell Hind was born in 1823 in
       <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">
        Nottingham
       </a>
       , the son of lace manufacturer John Hind and Elizabeth Russell,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was educated at
       <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_High_School" title="Nottingham High School">
        Nottingham High School
       </a>
       . At age 17 he went to
       <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
        London
       </a>
       to serve an apprenticeship as a civil engineer, but through the help of
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wheatstone" title="Charles Wheatstone">
        Charles Wheatstone
       </a>
       he left engineering to accept a position at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Greenwich_Observatory" title="Royal Greenwich Observatory">
        Royal Greenwich Observatory
       </a>
       under
       <a href="/wiki/George_Biddell_Airy" title="George Biddell Airy">
        George Biddell Airy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cox2008_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cox2008-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hind remained there from 1840 to 1844, at which time he succeeded
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W._R._Dawes" title="W. R. Dawes">
        W. R. Dawes
       </a>
       as director of the private
       <a href="/wiki/George_Bishop%27s_Observatory" title="George Bishop's Observatory">
        George Bishop's Observatory
       </a>
       . In 1853 Hind became Superintendent of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nautical_Almanac" title="Nautical Almanac">
        Nautical Almanac
       </a>
       , a position he held until 1891.
      </p>
      <p>
       Hind is notable for being one of the early discoverers of
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       . He also discovered and observed the
       <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">
        variable stars
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/R_Leporis" title="R Leporis">
        R Leporis
       </a>
       (also known as Hind's Crimson Star),
       <a href="/wiki/U_Geminorum" title="U Geminorum">
        U Geminorum
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri" title="T Tauri">
        T Tauri
       </a>
       (also called Hind's Variable Nebula), and discovered the variability of
       <a href="/wiki/Mu_Cephei" title="Mu Cephei">
        μ Cephei
       </a>
       . Hind discovered
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nova_Ophiuchi_1848&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nova Ophiuchi 1848 (page does not exist)">
        Nova Ophiuchi 1848
       </a>
       (
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=V841_Ophiuchi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="V841 Ophiuchi (page does not exist)">
        V841 Ophiuchi
       </a>
       ), the first nova of modern times (since the supernova
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SN_1604" title="SN 1604">
        SN 1604
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Hind's naming of the asteroid
       <a href="/wiki/12_Victoria" title="12 Victoria">
        12 Victoria
       </a>
       caused some controversy. At the time, asteroids were not supposed to be named after living persons. Hind somewhat disingenuously claimed that the name was not a reference to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">
        Queen Victoria
       </a>
       , but the mythological figure
       <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(mythology)" title="Victoria (mythology)">
        Victoria
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       He was elected a
       <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">
        Fellow of the Royal Society
       </a>
       in June 1863
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and President of the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">
        Royal Astronomical Society
       </a>
       in 1880.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He died in 1895 in
       <a href="/wiki/Twickenham" title="Twickenham">
        Twickenham
       </a>
       , London. Hind had married Fanny Fuller in 1846; he and his wife had six children.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">
        Notes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Russell_Hind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some sources give his name as
       <b>
        John Russel Hind
       </b>
       with only one "L". However, civil records
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and 19th century British astronomical magazines consistently spell his name with two "L"s.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the table of discovered asteroids, mpc links to the
       <a href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center" title="Minor Planet Center">
        Minor Planet Center
       </a>
       database for more information about the asteroid, along with the background on its name.
      </p>
