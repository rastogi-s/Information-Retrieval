      <p>
       <b>
        Radio Television of Serbia
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">
        Serbian
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="sr">
        Радио-телевизија Србије (PTC)/
        <i>
         Radio-televizija Srbije (
         <b>
          RTS
         </b>
         )
        </i>
       </span>
       ) is the
       <a href="/wiki/Public_broadcasting" title="Public broadcasting">
        public broadcaster
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">
        Serbia
       </a>
       . It broadcasts and produces news, drama, and sports programming through radio, television and the Internet. RTS is a member of the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Broadcasting_Union" title="European Broadcasting Union">
        European Broadcasting Union
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Radio Television of Serbia has four organizational units - radio, television, music production, and record label (
       <a href="/wiki/PGP-RTS" title="PGP-RTS">
        PGP-RTS
       </a>
       ). It is financed primarily through monthly subscription fees and
       <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">
        advertising
       </a>
       revenue.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span id="Radio_Belgrade_.281929.E2.80.931958.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_Belgrade_(1929–1958)">
        Radio Belgrade (1929–1958)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Television_of_Serbia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Radio Belgrade (1929–1958)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Radio Belgrade began its broadcasts in 1929. The first news announcer in 1929 was Jelena Bilbija. The first radio program in Serbia was broadcast in February 1924, when released radio signal was transmitted from the transmitter in Belgrade suburb of
       <a href="/wiki/Rakovica,_Belgrade" title="Rakovica, Belgrade">
        Rakovica
       </a>
       . After five years, on March 24, 1929 Radio Belgrade began with regular broadcasting of its program from the building of the
       <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Academy_of_Sciences_and_Arts" title="Serbian Academy of Sciences and Arts">
        Serbian Academy of Sciences and Arts
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Second World War interrupted the broadcasting of the national radio program in Belgrade. During the war, there was a German radio station, which broadcast programs in Serbian language. The emergence of the current Radio Belgrade followed the liberation from the Nazis on November 10, 1944.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Radio_Television_Belgrade_.281958.E2.80.931991.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_Television_Belgrade_(1958–1991)">
        Radio Television Belgrade (1958–1991)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Television_of_Serbia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radio Television Belgrade (1958–1991)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Radio Television Belgrade (RTB), consisting of Radio Belgrade and Television Belgrade (TVB) was established as a result of the decision by the Executive Council of the
       <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Republic_of_Serbia" title="Socialist Republic of Serbia">
        Socialist Republic of Serbia
       </a>
       on 13 February 1958. This came after the
       <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">
        Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia
       </a>
       's government decision of 1956 to invest in a television network.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first televised broadcast was on 23 August 1958, an edition of the
       <i>
        Dnevnik
       </i>
       (Journal) news programme with Miloje Orlović, Branislav Surutka, Olga Nađ, Olivera Živković and Vera Milovanović. The first RTB program was broadcast from the
       <a href="/wiki/Belgrade_Fair" title="Belgrade Fair">
        Belgrade Fair
       </a>
       and from a new TV Studio build there. From 1961, RTS began to use
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2_inch_Quadruplex_videotape" title="2 inch Quadruplex videotape">
        quadruplex
       </a>
       video tape recording equipment. The Sixties saw dramatic development in all genres of TV programs. TVB became famous by its sitcoms (directed and written by Radivoje-Lola Djukić, Novak Novak and others (unfortunately, only a small proportion is preserved, owing to implicit censorship and shortage of tapes). Also, TVB had excellent documentary programs (series
       <i>
        Karavan
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Reflektor
       </i>
       and others) and quizzes. By 1970, the entire territory of Serbia was covered by the RTS signal. On 31 December 1971, TVB started broadcasting in
       <a href="/wiki/PAL" title="PAL">
        PAL
       </a>
       color system on its second network. A new AM (radio) broadcast equipment in Zvečka, Obrenovac, with 2000 kW transmitter was erected in 1976.
      </p>
      <p>
       After the political turmoil in the 1970s (against the "liberals") the program of RTB became more sterile, however, in the 1980s it reached the zenith.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1989, preparation for the formation of the RTS system officially began. That same year, 3K TVB started broadcasting as the youth, alternative TV channel. Along with it, Radio 101 started broadcasting in Belgrade and Vojvodina. Radio 101 was the more commercial youth radio, carrying pop and turbo-folk hits. It was intended to complement the more alternative Belgrade 202.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1990, a few regional studios (Niš, Kragujevac, Jagodina, Šabac) officially started
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_variation" title="Regional variation">
        broadcasting regional programming
       </a>
       via a window in place of "Beogradska hronika".
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1991, all public broadcasters started their merger into RTS.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Radio_Television_of_Serbia_.281990s.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_Television_of_Serbia_(1990s)">
        Radio Television of Serbia (1990s)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Television_of_Serbia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Radio Television of Serbia (1990s)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Role_of_the_media_in_the_Yugoslav_wars" title="Role of the media in the Yugoslav wars">
        Role of the media in the Yugoslav wars
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The establishment of
       <a href="/wiki/Slobodan_Milo%C5%A1evi%C4%87" title="Slobodan Milošević">
        Slobodan Milošević
       </a>
       's regime led to one of RTB's most troubled eras in its history. Hundreds of journalists were fired for not complying with Milošević's propaganda. During the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9_March_1991_protest" title="9 March 1991 protest">
        protests of 9 March 1991
       </a>
       the regime's
       <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(politics)" title="Opposition (politics)">
        opposition
       </a>
       attacked the RTB building, calling it a "TV
       <a href="/wiki/Bastille" title="Bastille">
        Bastille
       </a>
       ". In 1992
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_Television_Belgrade" title="Radio Television Belgrade">
        Radio Television Belgrade
       </a>
       , together with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_Television_Novi_Sad" title="Radio Television Novi Sad">
        Radio Television Novi Sad
       </a>
       (RTNS),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_Television_Pristina" title="Radio Television Pristina">
        Radio Television Pristina
       </a>
       (RTP) and many local government owned radio stations, became part of Radio Television of Serbia, a centralized and closely monitored media network intended to be a propaganda tool for Milošević and his government. The most notorious of the network's TV programming during the Milošević era was
       <i>
        Dnevnik
       </i>
       (Daily news), which was used to glorify the "wise politics of Slobodan Milošević" and to attack "servants of Western powers, forces of chaos and despair", or the Serbian
       <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(politics)" title="Opposition (politics)">
        opposition
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ruins_of_the_Radio_Television_of_Serbia,_destroyed_during_1999_NATO_aggression.jpg">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ruins_of_the_Radio_Television_of_Serbia,_destroyed_during_1999_NATO_aggression.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         RTS headquarters damaged after NATO bombing
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Serbian media during Milošević's era was accused by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       of embracing
       <a href="/wiki/Serbian_nationalism" title="Serbian nationalism">
        Serbian nationalism
       </a>
       and promoting
       <a href="/wiki/Xenophobia" title="Xenophobia">
        xenophobia
       </a>
       toward other ethnic groups in the former Yugoslavia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Serbian state media during the wars featured reports which demonized Bosnians and Croats. The negative media depictions of said ethnic groups are examples of Milošević's state media promoting hysteria and to anger Serbs to support the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yugoslav_wars" title="Yugoslav wars">
        Yugoslav wars
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hague.bard.edu_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hague.bard.edu-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The station drew little attention to Serbian war crimes committed against Croats and Serbs. Reports about Serbs being massacred by Bosniaks and/or Croats were broadcast daily in order to create anger among the Serb and Montenegrin populace.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaufman_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kaufman-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Examples include the American embassy reporting of falsified stories created by state media of Bosnians and Croats killing nuns and babies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaufman_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kaufman-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnian_war" title="Bosnian war">
        Bosnian war
       </a>
       , when
       <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Sarajevo" title="Siege of Sarajevo">
        Sarajevo was under siege
       </a>
       , Radio Television of Serbia newscasts showed a still photo from
       <a href="/wiki/Sarajevo" title="Sarajevo">
        Sarajevo
       </a>
       in the 1980s, untouched, in order to create the image that Sarajevo was not under siege.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rozen_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rozen-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brosse_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brosse-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosovo_war" title="Kosovo war">
        Kosovo war
       </a>
       , Serbian state media denied the
       <a href="/wiki/Gornje_Obrinje_massacre" title="Gornje Obrinje massacre">
        Gornje Obrinje massacre
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Vu%C4%8Ditrn_massacre" title="Vučitrn massacre">
        Vučitrn massacre
       </a>
       , committed by Serb paramilitaries and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_of_the_Republic_of_Serbia" title="Police of the Republic of Serbia">
        Serbian Police
       </a>
       against Albanian civilians on 26 September 1998 and 2 and 3 May 1999, respectively. A Serbian television report described the
       <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
        BBC
       </a>
       accounts of atrocities committed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_of_the_Republic_of_Serbia" title="Police of the Republic of Serbia">
        Serbian police
       </a>
       in Kosovo as "lies and manipulation".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition, Radio Television of Serbia never reported the 800,000
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosovar_Albanians" title="Kosovar Albanians">
        Kosovar Albanians
       </a>
       expelled by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_of_the_Republic_of_Serbia" title="Police of the Republic of Serbia">
        Serbian police
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramilitaries" title="Paramilitaries">
        paramilitaries
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Operation_Horseshoe" title="Operation Horseshoe">
        Operation Horseshoe
       </a>
       ), except when a convoy of fleeing Kosovars was killed by NATO bombs.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rozen_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rozen-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 23 April 1999,
       <a href="/wiki/NATO_bombing_of_the_Radio_Television_of_Serbia_headquarters" title="NATO bombing of the Radio Television of Serbia headquarters">
        NATO bombed the RTS headquarters
       </a>
       in downtown
       <a href="/wiki/Belgrade" title="Belgrade">
        Belgrade
       </a>
       , killing 16 people, mostly technicians. In 2002, Dragoljub Milanović, the general manager of RTS, was sentenced to 10 years in prison because he had failed to order the workers in the building to evacuate, despite knowing that the building would be bombed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">
        Amnesty International
       </a>
       has described the
       <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">
        NATO
       </a>
       attack as a
       <a href="/wiki/War_crime" title="War crime">
        war crime
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 5 October 2000, the same RTS building was demolished and partly burned during the
       <a href="/wiki/Overthrow_of_Slobodan_Milo%C5%A1evi%C4%87" title="Overthrow of Slobodan Milošević">
        riots against Slobodan Milošević
       </a>
       . For some time after 5 October 2000, RTS was officially called
       <i>
        Nova RTS
       </i>
       <i>
        (New RTS)
       </i>
       to symbolize liberation from control from Milošević's regime and to symbolize a rebirth in the broadcaster's history.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span id="Radio_Television_of_Serbia_.282000s.29">

        Radio Television of Serbia (2000s)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

       <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_2008" title="Eurovision Song Contest 2008">

       </a>

      <p>
       After Milošević's removal from power, RTS underwent reconstruction in order to regain respect amongst much of its audience which the network had lost during the '90s. Particular emphasis was put on news programming which suffered greatly during the '90s. In 2006 RTS became the most viewed television network in Serbia and has retained this position since then. Early that year, RTS decided to shut down one of its television channels.
       <i>
        3K
       </i>
       (Treći kanal RTS-a) was a channel dedicated to the youth, which, however, became the main film, series and sports channel in the late 1990s and the early 2000s..
      </p>
      <div style="float:right; width:250px; background:#f0f0f0; margin-left:8px; padding:4px; border:1px solid black;">

      <p>
       In 2007, the
       <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
        BBC
       </a>
       World Service Trust launched an extensive training programme at Serbia's national broadcaster. This 30-month project, which was funded by the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
        European Union
       </a>
       , provided extensive journalism, craft and management training to all levels of staff at the broadcaster.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2008, RTS underwent major changes as it celebrated 50 years of existence. The network launched its digital network which uses DTT
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_terrestrial_television" title="Digital terrestrial television">
        Digital terrestrial television
       </a>
       via several
       <a href="/wiki/DVB-T" title="DVB-T">
        DVB-T
       </a>
       transmitters. It has also invested millions in new technology. The new high-definition television system was first put in place in May for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_Eurovision_Song_Contest" title="2008 Eurovision Song Contest">
        2008 Eurovision Song Contest
       </a>
       while on 26 November 2008, RTS began airing its new channel ‘’RTS Culture and Arts’’ which is a DTT-only channel, transmitted in 16:9 standard definition format, with stereo and 5.1 digital audio.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During 2008 the networks web presentations was greatly improved. On 23 August 2008, the 50th anniversary of
       <i>
        Dnevnik
       </i>
       (the RTS news bulletin) was celebrated. A special edition of the 19.30
       <i>
        Dnevnik
       </i>
       was aired with Mića Orlović, the first newsreader to host the news in Serbia, hosted the special addition helped by Dušanka Kalanj, the first female newsreader in Serbia. The theme of the evening's news included a reflection on the past 50 years a projection of the future as well as the news of the day. The weather was read out by Kamenko Katić, the first weather forecaster. All babies born on 23 August 2008, received a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flat_screen" title="Flat screen">
        flat screen
       </a>
       television set from RTS. On 9 September 2009, at 21.00
       <a href="/wiki/Central_European_Time" title="Central European Time">
        CET
       </a>
       , RTS launched its first high definition channel – RTS HD.
      </p>
      <p>
       RTS was the host broadcaster of the semi-final and finals of the
       <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_2008" title="Eurovision Song Contest 2008">
        Eurovision Song Contest 2008
       </a>
       . Serbia gained the rights to host the contest after
       <a href="/wiki/Marija_%C5%A0erifovi%C4%87" title="Marija Šerifović">
        Marija Šerifović
       </a>
       's 2007 victory in
       <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">
        Helsinki
       </a>
       , Finland. The Eurovision Song Contest 2008 was held in
       <a href="/wiki/Belgrade" title="Belgrade">
        Belgrade
       </a>
       . RTS broadcast the event as usual (since 2004) on RTS1. The host couple were
       <a href="/wiki/Jovana_Jankovi%C4%87" title="Jovana Janković">
        Jovana Janković
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDeljko_Joksimovi%C4%87" title="Željko Joksimović">
        Željko Joksimović
       </a>
       . The rating of the final of Eurovision was overwhelming with 4.560.000 people tuning in to watch making it the most watched event on Serbian television as well as on RTS.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2011, RTS issued a written apology to the citizens of Serbia and former Yugoslavia for its actions during the regime of Slobodan Milošević and the break up of Yugoslavia. The letter apologises for the network’s senseless reporting and the hurt it caused to the public. It vows never to let history repeat itself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 23 August 2014, at the 56th anniversary of the broadcaster, RTS got a new visual identity: focusing on new on-screen logos introduced on 18 February for their terrestrial channels. At the same day, the watermarks changed themselves to fit into the 16:9 format.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Television">
        Television
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Television_of_Serbia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Television">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/RTS1" title="RTS1">
        RTS1
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/RTS2" title="RTS2">

       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/RTS_SAT" title="RTS SAT">

      <p>
       RTS has two TV centers: in addition to the main TV production center within RTS headquarters complex in the downtown
       <a href="/wiki/Belgrade" title="Belgrade">
        Belgrade
       </a>
       , there is also TV production center in
       <a href="/wiki/Ko%C5%A1utnjak" title="Košutnjak">
        Košutnjak
       </a>
       (housing two largest studios: Studio 8 and Studio 9). RTS offers live programming on its website.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Channels">
        Channels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Television_of_Serbia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Channels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are currently five channels:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/RTS1" title="RTS1">
          RTS1
         </a>
        </b>
        is the oldest television station in Serbia, launched on 23 August 1958. It is available nationally free-to-air and is the most watched television channel in the country beating the other two most popular television networks in Serbia,
        <a href="/wiki/RTV_Pink" title="RTV Pink">
         RTV Pink
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prva" title="Prva">
         Prva
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
        RTS1 offers viewers political shows and debates and domestic and international shows. RTS1 prides itself for being the network with the most domestic shows which it funds itself. RTS1 airs a range of locally produced dramas which are highly successful with viewers, constantly ranking as the most watched television shows in Serbia.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/RTS2" title="RTS2">
          RTS2
         </a>
        </b>
        is first colour television station in Serbia, launched on 31 December 1971. Available nationally free-to-air, it focuses on educational programmes, in addition to offering and sporting events. Parliamentary sittings are also broadcast live on RTS2. Though on average it doesn't draw high viewer ship, RTS2 often may win the viewing day with special sporting events and special parliamentary debates. RTS2 also reruns of old Serbian drama and comedy programmes.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=RTS3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="RTS3 (page does not exist)">
          RTS3
         </a>
        </b>
        is first digital-only channel which began broadcasting on 26 November 2008 as RTS Digital. The channel, available nationally free-to-air, mainly airs cultural programmes, with emphasize on music (classical musical and jazz performances, in particular) broadcasting various concerts as well as ballet performances. Among other things, the channel broadcasts the
        <a href="/wiki/Vienna_New_Year%27s_Concert" title="Vienna New Year's Concert">
         Vienna New Year's Concert
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest" title="Eurovision Song Contest">
         Eurovision Song Contest

        <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Bosilj%C4%8Di%C4%87" title="Ivan Bosiljčić">

The show’s final episode on 19 January 2009 was watched by 3,277,000 people, making it the most watched television show in Serbia. Due to viewer requests, once the show ended it was immediately reprised in primetime, making it the fastest repeated show on RTS. In its repeats it also managed to produce extremely high ratings.

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">
        Radio
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Television_of_Serbia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Radio">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_Belgrade" title="Radio Belgrade">
        Radio Belgrade
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       RTS operates 4 radio stations, under the name Radio Belgrade.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Radio Belgrade 1
        </b>
        is flagship channel which provides comprehensive news and current affairs programmes to the public as well as entertainment and culture shows.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Radio Belgrade 2
        </b>
        is a cultural station based around social dialogue which constantly broadcasts thoughts about life and creation abroad and in Serbia. The station is known for documentary reporting, religious discussions classical music, evergreen, jazz and satire. Radio Belgrade 2 shares the same radio waves as Radio Belgrade 3 and is broadcast from 6:00 until 20:00.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Radio Belgrade 3
        </b>
        focuses on classical music and radio dramas. Radio Belgrade 3 shares the same radio waves as Radio Belgrade 2 and broadcasts from 20.00 until 06.00.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Radio Belgrade 202
        </b>
        broadcasts short news segments, rock and pop music. Hosts of various music programmes on the radio often ask listeners to send in their thoughts via
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_message_service" title="Short message service">
         SMS
        </a>
        or the Internet. Belgrade 202 also has a special morning programme broadcast from 06.00 until 09.00. which is based around current cultural, social and political trends.
       </li>
      </ul>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       RTS has an archive of its TV programmes. In addition to 5000+ video tapes in the long obsolete
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2_inch_Quadruplex_videotape" title="2 inch Quadruplex videotape">
        quadruplex
       </a>
       format, the archive contains tapes in
       <a href="/wiki/Type_C_videotape" title="Type C videotape">
        C-type
       </a>
       helical scan,
       <a href="/wiki/U-matic" title="U-matic">
        U-matic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Betacam" title="Betacam">
        beta-SP
       </a>
       and digital formats. Also, the archive contains an extensive collection of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newsreels" title="Newsreels">
        newsreels
       </a>
       , short filmed stories, and feature films on 16 mm and 35 mm tapes.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/PGP-RTS" title="PGP-RTS">
        PGP-RTS
       </a>
       is a music production company owned by the television network, starting with production in 1958 under the name
       <a href="/wiki/PGP-RTB" title="PGP-RTB">
        PGP-RTB
       </a>
       and used to be one of two largest record labels in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Former_Yugoslavia" title="Former Yugoslavia">
        former Yugoslavia
       </a>
       . Today, it is the third largest record label in Serbia (after
       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Production" title="Grand Production">
        Grand Production
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/City_Records" title="City Records">
        City Records
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
