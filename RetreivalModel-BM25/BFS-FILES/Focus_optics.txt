      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Geometrical_optics" title="Geometrical optics">
        geometrical optics
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        focus
       </b>
       , also called an
       <b>
        image point
       </b>
       , is the point where
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_(optics)" title="Ray (optics)">
        light rays
       </a>
       originating from a point on the object converge.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although the focus is conceptually a point, physically the focus has a spatial extent, called the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_confusion" title="Circle of confusion">
         blur circle
        </a>
       </i>
       . This non-ideal focusing may be caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_aberration" title="Optical aberration">
        aberrations
       </a>
       of the imaging optics. In the absence of significant aberrations, the smallest possible blur circle is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airy_disc" title="Airy disc">
        Airy disc
       </a>
       , which is caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">
        diffraction
       </a>
       from the optical system's
       <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">
        aperture
       </a>
       . Aberrations tend to get worse as the aperture diameter increases, while the Airy circle is smallest for large apertures.
      </p>
      <p>
       An image, or image point or region, is
       <i>
        <b>
         in focus
        </b>
       </i>
       if light from object points is converged almost as much as possible in the image, and
       <i>
        <b>
         out of focus
        </b>
       </i>
       if light is not well converged. The border between these is sometimes defined using a
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_confusion" title="Circle of confusion">
        circle of confusion
       </a>
       criterion.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        principal focus
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        focal point
       </b>
       is a special focus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        For a
        <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">
         lens
        </a>
        , or a
        <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
         spherical
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">
         parabolic
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">
         mirror
        </a>
        , it is a point onto which
        <a href="/wiki/Collimated_light" title="Collimated light">
         collimated light
        </a>
        parallel to the axis is focused. Since light can pass through a lens in either direction, a lens has two focal points—one on each side. The distance in air from the lens or mirror's
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principal_plane" title="Principal plane">
         principal plane
        </a>
        to the focus is called the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">
          focal length
         </a>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
         Elliptical
        </a>
        mirrors have two focal points: light that passes through one of these before striking the mirror is reflected such that it passes through the other.
       </li>
       <li>
        The focus of a
        <a href="/wiki/Hyperbola" title="Hyperbola">
         hyperbolic
        </a>
        mirror is either of two points which have the property that light from one is reflected as if it came from the other.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Diverging (negative) lenses and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convex_mirrors" title="Convex mirrors">
        convex mirrors
       </a>
       do not focus a collimated beam to a point. Instead, the focus is the point from which the light appears to be emanating, after it travels through the lens or reflects from the mirror. A convex parabolic mirror will reflect a beam of collimated light to make it appear as if it were radiating from the focal point, or conversely, reflect rays directed toward the
       <a href="/wiki/Focus_(geometry)" title="Focus (geometry)">
        focus
       </a>
       as a collimated beam. A convex elliptical mirror will reflect light directed towards one focus as if it were radiating from the other focus, both of which are behind the mirror. A convex
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperbola" title="Hyperbola">
        hyperbolic
       </a>
       mirror will reflect rays emanating from the
       <a href="/wiki/Focus_(geometry)" title="Focus (geometry)">
        focal point
       </a>
       in front of the mirror as if they were emanating from the focal point behind the mirror. Conversely, it can focus rays directed at the focal point that is behind the mirror towards the focal point that is in front of the mirror as in a
       <a href="/wiki/Cassegrain_reflector" title="Cassegrain reflector">
        Cassegrain telescope
       </a>
       .
      </p>
